# Superior Essex Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Superior Essex Inc. - Company Profile, Information, Business Description, History, Background Information on Superior Essex Inc.](https://www.referenceforbusiness.com/history2/85/Superior-Essex-Inc.html)  ·  `page_010434`

## Facts

- Headquartered in Atlanta, Georgia.
- Leading manufacturer of cable and wire with a 75-year heritage.
- Among the largest industry players in North America with a significant global presence.
- Operates 23 production facilities in the United States, Mexico, and Europe.
- Has an established global distribution network.
- Customer base includes cable television and telephone companies, distributors, and manufacturers in various industries.
- Offers more than 500 magnet wire and winding wire products.
- Products marketed through Essex Group Inc. in North America and China.
- Serves the European market through EssexNexans joint venture.
- Magnet wire products are critical components in millions of items, including automobiles, home appliances, generators, motors, transformers, and commercial machinery.
- Has business units devoted to specific end markets.
- World's largest producer of outside plant copper communications cables via Superior Essex Communications LP.
- Serves original equipment manufacturers and the motor repair market via Essex Brownell, Essex Express, and Active Industries units offering electrical insulation products.
- Formed in 1998 by the combination of Essex Group Inc. and Superior TeleCom.
- Roots trace to Detroit, Michigan, where Addison E. Holton established Essex Wire Corp. in 1930.
- Early workforce produced battery cables, wiring harnesses, and other wire items for the Ford Model A from leased space in Ford Motor Company's Highland Park plant.
- Acquired Logansport, Indiana-based RBM Manufacturing Company, a producer of electrical switches and automotive components.
- Bought a rubber insulation plant in Jonesboro, Indiana in 1932 known for Paranite building wire.
- Acquired a vacant industrial facility in Fort Wayne, Indiana in 1936 formerly housing Dudlo Manufacturing Company.
- Produced enough magnet wire to build millions of transformers used by America and its allies during World War II.
- Plants produced thousands of miles of field telephone wire for the Army Signal Corps and wiring harnesses for B-24 bombers.
- Awarded the 'E' Award by the Secretary of War.
- Relocated headquarters to Fort Wayne in 1954.
- Walter F. Probst succeeded Addison Holton as president in 1958; Holton retired in 1962 and Probst became board chairman.
- By 1965, sales totaled $355 million with seven divisions, 41 plants throughout North America, and 26 warehouses.
- First listed on the New York Stock Exchange in 1965 with 5 million shares.
- Unveiled an $8 million expansion initiative in 1965 including $3.2 million for Essex Wire and Cable division, $2.5 million for Essex Automotive division, and $2.3 million for acquisition/expansion of other facilities.
- Paul W. O'Malley named company president in early 1966; Probst continued as chairman and CEO.
- By 1968, building a new 30,000-square-foot facility in Fort Wayne for Metal Products division; had 67 plants in 30 states and Canada.
- Acquired Transport Motor Express Inc. in a $12 million stock deal in 1968.
- Acquired 14 other companies in 1969.
- Essex Wire Corporation operated under name Essex International Inc. by 1970 with an employee base of 25,700.
- Operated a facility in Northern Ireland at the end of the 1970s.
- Divisions included Magnet Wire; IWI; Wire and Cable; Controls; Metal Products and Plastics; Power Conductors; Communications and CATV; Industrial Laboratory Products; Wire Assembly; and Electro-Mechanical.
- Sales reached approximately $845 million in 1973 with about 100 plants.
- Merged with East Hartford, Connecticut-based United Aircraft Corporation (United Technologies Corporation) in 1974 creating the nation's 30th largest company with combined 1973 sales of $3.13 billion.
- Walter Probst retired as chairman and CEO in 1974, succeeded by Paul O'Malley.
- James A. O'Connor succeeded O'Malley in 1981 after 31 years at Essex.
- Faced criticism from labor leaders in the late 1970s for being anti-labor, encouraging multiple unions, scattering production, paying low wages, and providing poor benefits.
- U.S. Justice Department charged Essex and five other wire manufacturers with price fixing in September 1978.
- Founder Addison Holton died at age 83 in 1980.
- Announced plans to invest $3 million in its Fort Wayne facility (world headquarters) in 1982, scheduled for completion in 1984.
- Employed 8,982 workers worldwide with an annual payroll of $26.8 million by 1983.
- Became a privately owned firm in 1988 when MS/Essex Holdings bought it from United Technologies; John M. Bruce was president and CEO.
- Purchased by Bessemer Holdings in 1992, remaining privately owned.
- Became publicly traded again in 1997.
- Acquired magnet wire and electrical motor distributor Brownell in 1995, merged into distribution arm as Essex Brownell.
- Acquired Active Industries (converter/fabricator of electrical insulation products) in 1998.
- Announced acquisition by Superior Telecom Inc. in September 1998 in a deal valued at $1.4 billion in cash, stock, and debt; finalized in 1999 making Essex a subsidiary and creating North America's largest cable and wire manufacturer.
- Steven Abbott became Superior's president and chief operating officer following the deal.
- Superior Telecom started in 1954 in Hickory, North Carolina as Superior Cable Corporation producing plastic insulated twisted pair cable and wire.
- Production took place in a new 32,000-square-foot plant authorized by Hickory Development Corp. at a cost of $160,000.
- First reel of cable produced in August, delivered to Hickory Telephone Company.
- Walker Geitner was first president; explained cables transfer intelligence for telephone, fire alarms, traffic control, and pipeline systems.
- Geitner named chairman in 1959; James L. Robb succeeded him as president.
- Established second manufacturing plant in Rocky Mount, North Carolina in 1960.
- Expanded headquarters plant three times by 1962 to 150,000 square feet; production expanded to coaxial cables and wire for missile, aircraft, and industrial applications.
- Rolled out industry's first all-plastic drop wire in 1963.
- Erected new cable plant in Brownwood, Texas in 1964.
- By 1965, home plant had 125,000 square feet of manufacturing space, 35,000 square feet of office space, and 35,000-square-foot warehouse; annual sales reached nearly $20 million.
- Acquired by Continental Telephone in 1967, renamed Superior Continental Corp.
- Annual sales reached $90 million in 1969 and approximately $125 million in 1971.
- Announced plans to build an eight-story corporate office facility in Hickory for $3 million in 1971.
- Divested by Continental Telephone in 1976; purchased by Robert C. Pittman and A. L. Viles for $17.1 million in cash and stock.
- Deal included Hickory Brand Telephone Cords, Keller, Texas-based Communication Apparatus Company, and a non-operating plant in Angier, North Carolina.
- Acquired by Siecor Corporation in 1980; Siecor relocated from Horseheads, New York to Hickory in 1981.
- Divested by Siecor five years later (1986); went independent and marketed fiber optic cable.
- Alpine Group Inc. acquired Superior in 1993; added premises data cables to lineup.
- Went public in 1996 on NYSE; annual sales reached $500 million in 1997.
- Changed name to Superior Essex following acquisition of Essex International in 1999.
- Held about 30 percent of market for copper building wire by August 1999.
- Announced closure of three wire manufacturing plants and layoff of 469 employees in 1999 to optimize financial returns amid oversupply.
- Closed plants in Glendale, Arizona; Pauline, Kansas; and Tiffin, Ohio; continued production at sites in Sikeston, Missouri; Florence, Alabama; Anaheim, California; Lafayette, Indiana; and Columbia City, Indiana.
- Awarded three-year contract to supply cable and wire to Alltel Communications Products Inc. in 2000 serving 8.5 million customers in 25 states.
- Launched e-commerce Web site in late 2000 for order entry, shipment tracking, and industry news.
- Occupied a 228,000-square-foot warehousing and distribution facility in northeast Indiana by December 2001 previously occupied by Raytheon.
- Maintained workforce of 5,000 in 2003 while giving employees better benefits without increasing out-of-pocket contributions.
- Stephen M. Carter was CEO as of early 2004.
- Announced plans to acquire North American outside plant communications cable and wire operations of Belden Inc. in April 2004 for approximately $95 million.
- Carter stated acquisition aligns capacity with demand which declined 50 percent in North America since 2000, requiring consolidation beyond internal solutions.
- Confident in transitioning Belden's customer base which generated approximately $200 million in revenues last year.
- Announced revenues reached $1.8 billion in 2005, up from $1.4 billion in 2004.
- Carter attributed increased revenues to consolidating acquisitions and noted increased earnings despite skyrocketing energy costs.
- Principal Subsidiaries: Superior Essex Communications LP; Essex Group, Inc.
- Principal Competitors: Alcatel; APA Cables and Networks Inc.; General Cable Corporation.
