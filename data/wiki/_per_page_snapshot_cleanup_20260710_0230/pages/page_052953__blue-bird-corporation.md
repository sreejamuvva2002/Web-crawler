## Facts

- One of North America's top three school bus manufacturers, alongside Thomas-Built (Daimler) and IC (Navistar).
- Headquartered in Fort Valley, Georgia.
- Traces its origins to the Houston County Motor Co., a small Georgia Ford distributor founded in 1920 by Albert L. Luce.
- Founder Albert Laurence Luce was born June 26, 1888, in La Grange, Cook County, Illinois, and died November 16, 1962.
- Luce served in Company I of the 126th Infantry during WWI, embarking for France on February 19, 1918, and earned the French 'Croix de Guerre' medallion.
- After returning to the US in May 1919, Luce secured a Ford Motor Co. franchise for Fort Valley, Georgia.
- Luce's Fort Valley Ford dealership was located on North Macon St. (Georgia Route 49/Macon Hwy., now North Camellia Blvd.).
- Luce opened a second Ford distributorship, Houston Valley Motor Co., in Perry, Georgia, at the corner of Ball and Commerce streets.
- On June 23, 1920, Luce married Helen Tryphena Mathews of Fort Valley, Georgia.
- In 1925, Penn Dixie Cement Co. requested a vehicle to transport workers, prompting Luce to equip a Model T chassis with a rudimentary open-sided wooden bus body.
- Dissatisfied with the coachwork quality, Luce collaborated with a local blacksmith to construct a better bus using steel angles, channels, sheets, wood, and canvas on a 1927 Ford Model T chassis.
- The first Blue Bird bus was sold to Frank Slade of Marshallville, Georgia, for rural school transport.
- Only one Luce-built bus body was delivered in 1930, with new Ford car sales dropping to 57 in Fort Valley and 150 in Perry that year.
- In 1931, only 10 new Fords were delivered (3 in Fort Valley, 7 in Perry), but Luce sold 7 bus bodies that year.
- By the end of 1931, Luce closed the Fort Valley branch and consolidated auto sales and body-building in Perry.
- In 1932, Luce sold his Perry Ford dealership and invested his last $12,000 to construct 25 bus bodies, officially starting the school bus manufacturing business.
- Luce named the company Blue Bird after a student called a blue and yellow scale model a 'pretty little blue bird.'
- Initially, Luce assembled bodies in a rented peach-packing shed before moving operations in 1934 to a former livery stable in Fort Valley.
- In 1934, Joe Twombly installed a rudimentary assembly line inside the stables where bodies were moved on skids constructed of 4x4-in timbers.
- By year-end 1934, Luce completed 87 bus bodies and purchased land in Five Points, Fort Valley, on Georgia Route 49.
- Minutes from February 21, 1935, show A.L. Luce planning to build a factory for school bus bodies.
- Blue Bird's first permanent plant was a 1-story, 27,000 sq. ft. structure erected by Luce's employees using lumber purchased on credit from J. Mead Tolleson in Perry, Georgia.
- Early Blue Bird buses used composite bodies with sheet metal panels nailed to ash wood frameworks, with wrought iron braces from a Fort Valley blacksmith and parts from a sheet-metal shop at 197-199 Church St.
- In early July 1935, sales manager Carlyle Irby helped deliver 100 school buses, generating an estimated $25,000 profit for the year.
- Religious services were offered at the factory's lunchroom every other Monday in the mid-1930s.
- In the mid-1930s, fatal school bus-train accidents prompted Luce to convert the factory to manufacturing all-steel school bus bodies.
- Blue Bird debuted its first all-steel bus body in late 1937 for the 1938 school season.
- A 1939 incident highlighted Luce's cost management, where he lent his sons $500 each to be repaid through studying at rates of $1/hour before dinner and $0.75/hour after dinner.
- An April 1939 conference in New York City chaired by Columbia University professor Frank W. Cyr established national standards for school bus construction, including the adoption of National School Bus Chrome yellow.
- At the start of WWII, Blue Bird converted factories to military goods, winning contracts for lightweight military buses limited to 1,000 lbs. of metal.
- During WWII, Blue Bird engineers Johnny Wells and Bob Peeples modified chassis by fishplating and sistering oak timbers to handle overhung rear bodies for a Maine Naval shipyard.
- During the War, Blue Bird constructed several hundred 4-stretcher ambulance bodies for major metropolitan areas.
- George E. Luce married Willouise Butts on June 29, 1946.
- A.L. 'Buddy' Luce Jr. enlisted in the US Army Reserves at Fort McPherson, Atlanta, Georgia, on March 15, 1943.
- Joseph P. Luce trained as a gunner with an Aviation Ordinance Flight Crew and attended Emory, Georgia Tech, Le Tourneau Tech, and graduated from Asbury College in 1950.
- By the end of WWII, Blue Bird ranked seventh out of twelve bus manufacturers accounted for.
- In 1945, a fire caused by a blocked vent pipe on a coal stove destroyed the entire Blue Bird plant; Luce escaped but the building collapsed 45 minutes after ignition.
- Most equipment and buses were saved due to employee efforts, and operations moved to a temporary assembly line across the road under make-shift tents.
- Johnny Wells located 175,000 lbs. of steel beams and trusses from a Knoxville, Tennessee scrap dealer for the new factory structure.
- Galvanized sheet metal for the new building was sourced from Tennessee Coal and Iron in Birmingham, Alabama.
- New buses began exiting the new factory in Spring 1946.
- Luce's sons restored his original 1927 bus, which was exhibited at the 1947 National School Administrators Show in Atlantic City.
- Blue Bird opened a satellite facility in Mount Pleasant, Iowa.
- In fall 1948, Luce and his wife visited Europe, touring the Paris Auto Salon and the Opel plant in Antwerp, Belgium.
- The Luces purchased a complete motor coach from a Belgian coachbuilder (Van Hool or Jonckheere) with a GM-chassised front-engine design.
- Blue Bird engineers discovered the chassis was a modified Chevrolet bus chassis and developed an all-new remote control shifter in-house by engineer Wilbur Rumph.
- The new Blue Bird 'All American' debuted on the road in early 1949, initially using GM-sourced chassis before switching to in-house built chassis for production deliveries in 1950.
- A dedicated 'All American' school bus officially debuted on May 9, 1952.
- The All American was substantially redesigned in 1957 with an enlarged panoramic windshield and taller roof.
- Quad headlights were added in 1958 following federal legislation, and a rear-engined 'All American' (RE) became available in 1961 using a GMC chassis.
- Buddy Luce took over as Blue Bird president in 1950 after his father's heart attack.
- During the 1950s, school districts began operating their own fleets and buying buses directly through public bidding processes.
- Blue Bird salesman 'Red Willie' used tactics like 'dummy bids' and 'pigeon drops' to win contracts before being replaced by a professional sales manager.
- In April 1961, the San Antonio Express and News reported that Blue Bird and its Austin, Texas distributor were sued for price rigging.
- Senior Luce explored foreign market expansion in South America, offering consignment demonstrator buses to Venezuela's Wayne Works distributor.
