# Lehigh Technologies

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** 2018-05-29

**Source:** [How Michelin is finding a second life for tires | Trellis](https://trellis.net/article/how-michelin-finding-second-life-tires/)  ·  `page_057041`

## Facts

- French tire giant Michelin acquired Lehigh Technologies in late 2017.
- Lehigh Technologies reincarnates end-of-life tires into a specialty material called micronized rubber powder (MRP).
- MRP is already used by more than 60 companies, including seven of the world’s 10 biggest tire makers.
- MRP serves as a feedstock for high-performance tires sold by Michelin.
- MRP is sold to more than 42 plants in 15 countries.
- Lehigh operates as an independent subsidiary of Michelin.
- Lehigh's second manufacturing plant in Murillo del Fruto, Spain, was set to come online in July.
- Lehigh's process uses liquid nitrogen to turn rubber scraps stripped of metals and fibers cold enough to be pulverized into a fine composite.
- Lehigh sources material from tire recyclers or through closed-loop arrangements with tire manufacturers.
- The MRP material helps save energy in the tire production process.
- The new European facility has an initial annual capacity of 10,000 metric tons.
- The European facility is located on the same site as a local tire recycler to ensure a steady stream of feedstock.
- Lehigh’s first plant is located in Tucker, Georgia.
- The Tucker, Georgia plant is the world’s MRP manufacturing site with an annual capacity of 140 million pounds.
- Lehigh’s technologies are becoming increasingly integral to Michelin’s shift toward a closed-loop production cycle that uses fewer raw or virgin inputs.
- Lehigh CEO Alan Barton stated the company has been looking for ways to have a significantly higher presence in recycling of materials than in the past, which fits their strategic direction.
- As of April, MRP has been used in more than 500 million new tires and retreads.
- Roughly 1 billion tires are headed for disposal annually worldwide, including more than 300 million in the United States.
- Traditionally, the biggest non-fuel markets for tire scraps have included civil engineering projects like embankment fill or as an additive for asphalt.
- Lehigh is actively developing new applications for MRP beyond markets traditionally associated with scrap rubber.
- MRP is finding its way into plastics, consumer goods, coatings, sealants, construction materials, and car parts.
- The durability and longevity of MRP make it appropriate for use in the foam that’s part of car headrests.
- The carpet and flooring industry represents a potentially large market for MRP as part of underlays.
- Mohawk actively embraces the use of recycled materials, repurposing 42 million pounds of old tires into welcome mats last year.
- Testing and introducing new material takes significant time, often requiring a half-year in the laboratory to produce samples followed by several months for a production trial before test marketing.
