# Murata Electronics North America Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Murata revs production to meet wireless boom - EDN](https://www.edn.com/murata-revs-production-to-meet-wireless-boom/)  ·  `page_047102`

## Facts

- Murata Electronics North America Inc. is located in Smyrna, Ga., and is repositioning itself in the passives market by building stronger relationships with its OEM and distribution partners.
- The company aims to dominate the component count of a typical cellular phone or other wireless device by getting involved early in its customers' product-design cycles.
- Tom Kuhl, director of sales, stated, “We're evolving from a supplier of commodity products to becoming a high-end design partner.”
- Murata is a supplier of ceramic capacitors, microwave components, and crystal oscillators.
- Sales for the supplier surged over the past two years, reaching $425 million in 1998 and climbing to $590 million last year as a result of the wireless and telecom boom.
- Parent company Murata Manufacturing Co. Ltd., Kyoto, Japan, had sales in 1998 and 1999 of $3 billion and $4 billion, respectively.
- The company is looking to increase sales by 35% this year.
- To achieve growth, the company is increasingly finding itself in meetings with OEMs, contract electronics manufacturers, and distributors during the initial stages of an end-product's design.
- This strategy involves identifying OEM design requirements earlier and developing products to meet those demands.
- In the wireless and telecom markets, there is a push for increasingly smaller end-use products such as cookie-thin cell phones, PDAs, and pagers.
- The movement toward component miniaturization is driving much of Murata's product-development efforts.
- Murata is about to bring to market high-frequency chip inductors that have a 0201 footprint, which is about the size of pepper.
- The company is currently using thin-film, wire-wound, and monolithic designs on its chip inductors.
- A typical cell phone contains 25 to 30 inductors.
- Murata is developing smaller, surface-mount crystal oscillator products that offer a longer life span.
- John Switalski, product manager of crystal oscillators, noted that technology is moving away from through-hole packages to surface-mount devices that feature digital tuning as well as inverted crystals.
- Sales for these types of products have jumped from nearly $4 million more than a year ago to almost $10 million this year.
- Crystal oscillator sales are expected to climb to $20 million next year, according to Switalski.
- Nearly 7% of Murata's revenue has been earmarked for R&D.
- The company plans to raise capacity by 40% over the next year or so.
- Manufacturing of 0402-size components and high-capacitance MLCCs will take the lion's share of the investments.
