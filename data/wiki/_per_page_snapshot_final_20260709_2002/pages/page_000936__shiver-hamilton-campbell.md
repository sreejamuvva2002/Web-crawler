## Facts

- Lithium-ion battery fires occupy a narrow but consequential space in Georgia product liability law.
- Battery fire cases routinely involve manufacturing defect, design defect, and failure to warn theories simultaneously.
- Manufacturing defect claims focus on contaminated electrodes, misaligned separators, or incomplete welds in the cell casing.
- Design defect claims challenge the fundamental architecture of the battery management system and question whether thermal runaway protection was adequate under foreseeable operating conditions.
- Failure to warn theory carries unusual weight because thermal runaway is invisible to consumers until it becomes catastrophic.
- The supply chain for batteries in recalled hoverboards or e-bikes is global and deeply fragmented.
- Cells may be manufactured in one country, assembled into a pack in another, imported by a distributor, and sold by a retailer.
- Identifying which link in the supply chain caused a failure requires forensic analysis of the battery’s internal structure, manufacturing records, and quality control documentation.
- Thermal runaway begins when a cell’s internal temperature rises past a threshold, triggering exothermic reactions that generate more heat than the cell can dissipate.
- Within seconds, a single cell failure can cascade through an entire battery pack.
- Post-fire evidence is often severely degraded because thermal runaway consumes much of the battery itself.
- Battery fire experts typically include electrochemical engineers who distinguish between internal short circuits, overcharging caused by defective chargers, and mechanical damage.
- Lithium-ion batteries sold in the United States are subject to oversight from the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC), the Department of Transportation (DOT), and the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA).
- The CPSC enforces safety standards under the Consumer Product Safety Act and has authority to issue recalls when a product presents a substantial product hazard.
- A battery recall serves as powerful evidence of a manufacturer’s knowledge of a defect in Georgia civil litigation.
- DOT regulations governing lithium battery shipment create liability avenues if improper packaging, labeling, or shipping violates federal hazardous materials regulations.
- Atlanta is a major distribution hub where significant volumes of lithium-ion products move through warehouses and freight facilities in the metro area.
- E-bikes and electric scooters are responsible for a significant and growing share of battery fire injuries in urban areas including Atlanta.
- Hoverboards became subject to major CPSC scrutiny beginning in the mid-2010s.
- Vaping devices, power tools, laptop computers, and home energy storage systems have also generated substantial litigation.
- Under Georgia law (O.C.G.A. Section 51-1-11), any seller in the chain of distribution can face strict liability, including cell manufacturers, battery pack assemblers, finished product brands, importers, distributors, and retailers.
- Georgia’s general personal injury statute of limitations requires claims to be filed within two years of the date of injury.
- Georgia’s product liability statute of repose generally bars claims brought more than ten years after the product was first sold.
- Atlanta is one of the largest distribution and logistics centers in the southeastern United States.
- Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport handles enormous volumes of air freight, including lithium battery products subject to FAA and DOT shipping regulations.
- The metro area’s concentration of warehouses, e-commerce fulfillment centers, and last-mile delivery operations means improperly handled or stored battery products are a real and recurring risk.
