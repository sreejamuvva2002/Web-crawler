# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-10-14

**Source:** [ICE raid leaves ripple effects across Coastal Georgia communities - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2025/10/14/ice-raid-leaves-ripple-effects-across-coastal-georgia-communities/)  ·  `page_001205`

## Facts

- A Korean-speaking real estate agent moved to Pooler in 2023 to provide temporary housing for hundreds of workers building Georgia’s largest economic development project.
- For nearly two years, temporary housing for Korean construction workers, technicians, and engineers near the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America site in Bryan County included mobile homes on rural plots, rented rooms in owner-occupied homes, and a makeshift bed and breakfast.
- In September, the U.S. Department of Homeland Security raided the Ellabell electric vehicle factory and detained more than 300 Korean workers.
- Construction on the EV battery plant halted alongside the raid, as many specialized employees worked there.
- The real estate agent's temporary housing business came to a halt following the September raid.
- Ripple effects from the federal raid impacted at least a half dozen small businesses in counties surrounding the metaplant.
- Local business owners reported double-digit percentage drops in revenue after their Korean customers were detained.
- Some local entrepreneurs who capitalized on the Korean workforce's arrival are now facing bankruptcy.
- Hyundai Motor Group leadership stated commitment to expanding production and work opportunities at the metaplant despite acknowledging short-term struggles to increase output.
- Bianca Johnson, HMGMA manager of public relations, declined to comment on disruptions to local businesses following the ICE raid.
- Georgia and southeast coastal counties are subsidizing Hyundai and its suppliers with $2.1 billion in tax breaks, construction costs, discounted land, and other perks.
- Governor Brian Kemp announced the subsidy deal would produce good-paying jobs for Georgians.
- Hyundai Metaplant, the LG joint-venture battery facility, and five affiliated Hyundai suppliers must collectively invest $5.545 billion and hire at least 8,500 workers by 2031.
- The hired workers must earn an average annual salary of $58,105.
- The created jobs must be retained until 2048.
- Chip Johnson, CEO of the Turnstone Group, invested in the only hotel at the I-16 exit leading to the Hyundai plant, expecting steady customers during construction.
- Since opening in July 2025, the hotel has received only one booking from a metaplant worker.
- Last year, owners of Savannah's Viet Huong grocery store purchased an old Piggly Wiggly in Ellabell across the highway from the Hyundai plant to stock goods for the Korean workforce.
- For nearly a year, the Ellabell grocery store served as a preferred meeting spot for Korean workers buying snacks and lunch in the parking lot before work and groceries for dinner afterward.
- Following the federal raid, the Ellabell grocery store's customer base dropped to a daily trickle.
- Local Bryan County residents showed no interest in Korean food, leaving the store with little cash flow to restock.
- A Korean American-owned restaurant in Pooler experienced spoiled inventory after visiting Korean engineers and managers stopped coming following the raid.
- By the end of September, the anonymous Pooler restaurant owner's weekly income dropped by 18%.
- The restaurant owner, who originally came to Georgia as a Hyundai worker, is uncertain about keeping his business open.
- Fear from the raid caused additional Korean workers to return home, and new arrivals from Korea stopped coming entirely.
- A second Pooler restaurant previously hosted groups of 20 to 30 Korean workers daily but now rarely sees groups larger than five.
- The second Pooler restaurant owner noted that provided food is going to waste because customers stopped eating there.
- Hispanic construction workers and their families also stopped visiting the second Pooler restaurant, likely due to fear from the raid.
- A Hispanic employee at the second Pooler restaurant was shaken after a traffic stop where police requested her immigration documents.
- Hyundai announced a goal to increase metaplant production from 300,000 to 500,000 vehicles annually.
- Current metaplant output is limited to one shift per day.
- The LG joint-venture battery plant will not open until next year at the earliest due to difficulties finding qualified personnel to finish the high-tech production line build-out.
- Partnered construction companies are struggling to replace specialized Korean labor with local hires.
- A supplier site manager confirmed the battery plant has no backup plan for completing construction.
- On Oct. 1, Reuters reported that the U.S. agreed to allow South Korean workers with existing temporary visas to work on equipment at U.S. investment sites like the Hyundai metaplant.
- Korean suppliers are working to improve their reputation for compliance with U.S. labor laws and environmental requirements.
- Multiple Korean suppliers and subcontractors previously failed to report workplace accidents at the metaplant in violation of U.S. law.
- On Oct. 8, Chris Susock, Hyundai Motor North America’s chief manufacturing officer, suggested creating a steering committee for the Joint Development Board of county officials.
- The proposed steering committee would include CEOs and senior leadership from all metaplant companies to improve communication and planning.
- Chris Susock stated the committee's purpose is to set policy, guidance, and procedures for site management, covering safety, security, operations, communications, and public relations.
- It remains unclear how the new steering committee will impact employment recruitment across the Hyundai supply chain.
- One month after the raid, HMGMA hosted a job fair at Savannah Technical College.
- More than 350 job seekers attended the HMGMA job fair at Savannah Technical College seeking stability amid recent International Paper mill closures and timber industry upheaval.
- Cody Bennington, a truck driver from Millen, attended the job fair seeking a stable career.
- Dasia Mosley, recently laid off from Daniel Defense (located across I-16 from the metaplant), is willing to commute an hour to work at the plant for a good salary.
- Hyundai Glovis employee Jazzlyn Javis reported severe retention issues, noting she trained nearly 500 people over four years but only five remain employed there.
- Jazzlyn Javis stated they lack sufficient staff to complete work and cannot achieve stability while constantly losing employees.
- Bruce Hall, a 62-year-old veteran from Pooler, viewed the job fair as evidence of Hyundai's long-term commitment to the community.
- The real estate agent moved back to Atlanta at the end of September due to a foreseeable long-term regional slowdown tied to Hyundai's operations.
- The real estate agent retains hope for a second golden period in the next couple of years if visa regulations relax and Hyundai resumes flying in an immigrant workforce from South Korea.
- The real estate agent stated that all revenue plans in Coastal Georgia have stopped with no known timeline for resumption.
