# Atlanta 2025 EV Readiness Ordinance (25-O-1011)

**Type:** Policy / Incentive  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[government-support|Government Support]]  ·  **Location:** Atlanta, Georgia, United States

## Overview

A landmark ordinance enacted by the City of Atlanta that mandates electric vehicle charging infrastructure in new construction and major renovations across commercial, multifamily, and residential developments.

## EV supply-chain relevance

Directly shapes EV charging deployment throughout the city by requiring a minimum percentage of installed and capable chargers in new buildings and major renovations.

## Timeline

- **2025** — City of Atlanta enacts the 2025 EV Readiness Ordinance (25-O-1011).
- **January 1, 2026** — Ordinance becomes effective, mandating EV infrastructure in new construction and major renovations.
- **2023** — Georgia Power transitions to kilowatt-hour based pricing for EV charging.

## Key facts

- The ordinance introduces three defined tiers: EVSE Capable, EVSE Ready, and EVSE Installed.
- Commercial and multifamily developments must include at least 20% EVSE Installed spaces and 20% EVSE Capable spaces.
- City-owned projects must meet higher thresholds: 25% EVSE Installed and 50% EVSE Capable.
- Fire safety provisions are included, such as emergency shutoff switches, equipment spacing, and ventilation requirements for enclosed garages.
- Georgia law allows energy dispensed by chargers to be priced based on kilowatt-hour usage rather than time.
- Georgia Power's off-peak rate is $0.09/kWh versus a peak rate of $0.20/kWh, offering up to 56% savings when charging overnight.
- The state received a $24.4 million grant to build 26 new charging stations as part of the NEVI Program.
- Six private companies will design, install, finance, operate, and maintain DC fast-charging sites for at least five years, each site featuring four simultaneous DCFC ports.
- Goal: public EV fast charging every 50 miles along I-85 to reduce range anxiety on long trips.
- Georgia can continue spending $135 million in federal funds allocated for EV charging infrastructure on Interstates 16 and 95.
- Atlanta hosts 2,334 total charging stations: 2,153 Level 2 (92%) and 181 Level 3 (8%).
- 22% of all stations (514) are free; free stations include Colony Square and the Atlanta Botanical Garden.
- Major networks operating in Atlanta are ChargePoint, EVgo, Tesla Supercharger, Electrify America, Blink, and EnviroSpark.
- Level 2 charging adds 11-54 miles per hour; Level 3 DC fast charging provides rapid top-ups for drivers with limited time.
- Adapters for CCS-to-NACS cost $100-$200 to enable non-Tesla EVs to use Tesla Superchargers.
