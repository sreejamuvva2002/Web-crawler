# EV Battery Plant Construction Industry

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-02-20

**Source:** [EV battery plant construction booms even as automakers hit the brakes | Construction Dive](https://www.constructiondive.com/news/ev-battery-plant-construction-booms-demand/707853/)  ·  `page_000715`

## Facts

- General Motors and Ford slammed the brakes on electric vehicle production late last year as consumer adoption lagged behind expectations.
- GM scrapped plans to produce 400,000 electric vehicles through mid-2024 due to lower demand.
- Ford cut back production on its electric pickup.
- Electrical machinery construction, which includes EV battery plants, hit $35.2 billion in 2023, according to Dodge Construction Network.
- Electrical machinery construction accounted for roughly 47% of overall manufacturing construction in 2023.
- Economists anticipate the manufacturing boom to continue in 2024 despite automaker pullbacks.
- Dodge Construction Network expects a 15% increase in overall manufacturing construction in 2024.
- American consumers bought 15.6 million vehicles in 2023.
- About 9% of 2023 vehicle sales came from EVs.
- GM expects EV sales to increase to at least 10% in 2024, according to CEO Mary Barra's recent earnings call statements.
- S&P Global Mobility forecasts EV market share for new vehicles will jump to 40% by 2030.
- Meeting the projected 2030 demand would require about 60 billion batteries and 40 large-scale gigafactories, according to Anthony Johnson.
- Kansas City, Missouri-based JE Dunn plans to remain busy with manufacturing-related work in 2024, particularly in the EV-related space.
- JE Dunn will deliver Dongwha Electrolyte’s first project in North America: a $70 million electric vehicle battery production facility in Clarksville, Tennessee.
- Brent Strength serves as senior vice president and manufacturing market leader at JE Dunn.
- Strength expects manufacturing construction in 2024 to surpass its 2023 levels.
- Contractors currently have a healthy backlog from previous years.
- There is significant spending of Inflation Reduction Act, CHIPS Act, and similar dollars right now.
- Chicago-based Clayco expects to remain busy with advanced manufacturing construction in 2024.
- Clayco recently won the contract to build Rivian’s $5 billion EV plant in Stanton Springs, Georgia.
- Anthony Johnson serves as president of the industrial business unit at Clayco.
- Johnson expects the advanced manufacturing segment to see significant investment expanding at very high rates in 2024.
- Contractors acknowledge maintaining record activity levels indefinitely is not guaranteed due to factors outside the onshoring boom.
- Hurdles to sustained growth include changes to EV tax breaks for consumers and persistently high interest rates, according to Strength.
- The overall growth trajectory might not be as steep as previously projected, given slower consumer adoption, higher interest rates and changes to tax credits, said Strength.
- The number of EV types qualifying for clean vehicle tax credits up to $7,500 under the Inflation Reduction Act dropped from 35 to 14 this year, per the U.S. Department of Energy and IRS.
- The tax credit change stipulates that vehicles sourcing EV battery components from foreign-based entities, such as firms in China or Korea, will no longer be eligible for the credit in 2024.
- Without the incentive, 2024 will provide a clear sense of where EV demand truly is in the United States, according to Dodge chief economist Richard Branch.
- Johnson expects EV adoption to continue making slow headway, which will translate into a longer-term boom of work for contractors in the sector.
- The 2024 election year has made EVs a political topic regarding climate change and government subsidies, creating segment volatility, according to Johnson.
- Individual automakers will experience ups and downs along the way, but the overall EV growth curve continues to go upwards over time.
