# Hyundai Metaplant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [The Hyundai Metaplant: A New Era in EV Manufacturing - IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/hyundai-metaplant)  ·  `page_000281`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group’s Metaplant is located in Ellabell, Ga.
- The facility represents a US $12.6 billion investment in U.S.-based manufacturing of EVs and batteries.
- Ioniq 5 and Ioniq 9 SUVs are assembled on surgically spotless assembly lines at the plant.
- Consumer adoption of EVs had started slowing as of the reporting period.
- The U.S. federal government’s $7,500 clean-car tax credit was being phased out.
- The Ioniq 9 is a three-row SUV tailored to the United States’ plus-size tastes.
- Each vehicle features a 110.3-kilowatt-hour battery pack that sits below the floor.
- The battery pack is shielded to prevent or limit damage in a collision.
- The battery is part of an advanced 800-volt architecture for ultrafast DC charging.
- A $4.3 billion joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution is building a battery plant near the Savannah-area factory.
- The joint venture battery plant is on track to produce lithium-ion cells for Hyundai, Kia, and Genesis models in 2026.
- The battery facility encompasses 11 low-roofed buildings covering 697,000 square meters (70 hectares) with pale green walls designed to blend into the Georgia countryside.
- The Metaplant is backed by $2.1 billion in state subsidies.
- It is the largest public development project in Georgia’s history.
- The plant covers 70 hectares and is the centerpiece of Hyundai’s $12.6 billion total investment in the state.
- U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents raided the battery factory in September, detaining and deporting more than 300 South Korean workers.
- The plant's creation was accelerated by the Biden administration’s pro-EV policies.
- It was the centerpiece of Republican Gov. Brian Kemp’s bid to make Georgia “the electric mobility capital of the country.”
- Hyundai executives call the Metaplant North America’s most automated car factory and the most advanced full-scale factory among Hyundai Motor Co.’s 12 global manufacturing facilities.
- The facility rivals or surpasses Japan’s most advanced plants, such as those operated by Toyota.
- Automakers are enlisting armies of AI-controlled robots that can potentially work 24/7 without asking for raises or lunch breaks to compete with China.
- The factory may eventually employ 8,500 people directly and 7,000 satellite workers.
- The plant has an annual capacity of 500,000 cars, which is more than Tesla’s Texas Gigafactory but less than Tesla’s Shanghai plant.
- During the past summer, 1,340 humans were sufficient to operate the assembly lines for two Ioniq models.
- “Meta Pros” working on the assembly lines earn an average of $58,100 a year, which is 35 percent higher than the average in Bryan County, Ga.
- The cavernous welding hall houses about 475 robots piecing together car chassis.
- A steel stamping plant uses robots to stamp roofs and body panels and stow them in overhead racks without requiring ear protection due to its quiet operation.
- Human workers park their cars beneath solar roofs that generate up to 5 percent of the plant’s electricity.
- A fleet of 21 Hyundai-owned Xcient hydrogen fuel-cell trucks carries parts from suppliers with zero tailpipe emissions.
- Hyundai’s goal is to obtain 100 percent of the Metaplant’s energy from renewables by 2030.
- The body build line adapts to changing production mixes to meet customer orders and has built-in flexibility to assemble future models.
- The Metaplant’s welding shop houses about 500 industrial robots that piece together the “body-in-white” prior to adding subassemblies like suspension, power train, body trim, and interior.
- Hyundai is experimenting with humanoid robots to perform welding tasks to reduce labor costs and scrapped materials.
- “Body-complete” robots mount front doors onto Ioniq 5s using machine vision and laser-measurement systems to ensure exact fit and install mounting bolts to exact torque specifications validated for safety and quality standards.
- The factory utilizes 850 robots to unload parts from trucks at the docks.
- About 300 automated guided vehicles (AGVs) glide silently across the factory floor without tracks, trained to stop for humans.
- AGVs use cameras and sensors to navigate, slow or stop to avoid collisions, and emit spoken warnings to human workers in their path.
- An AI-based procurement-and-logistics system allows AGVs to allocate and deliver parts to workstations for “just in time” delivery.
- Adjustable-height carriers elevate Ioniq 5s for access to central fasteners and plugs for suspension components and the high-voltage battery prior to the “marriage” of upper and lower vehicle sections.
- On the final assembly line, an Ioniq 9’s “top hat” is married to a lower “skateboard” structure containing electric motors, battery, and suspension.
- Finished cars undergo testing including a water bath for leaks and a quick road test outdoors.
- A “collaborative” robot is being used for the first time at a Hyundai factory to install bulky, heavy doors on the assembly line without needing fencing or isolation.
- Spot, robotic quadrupeds from Hyundai-owned Boston Dynamics use camera vision, sensors, and “athletic intelligence” to autonomously recharge and inspect body welds for defects.
- Atlas, a humanoid robot from Boston Dynamics, is being trained to sense environments and manipulate parts in complex sequences for future deployment in global factories.
- A backup station allows high-voltage battery fasteners to be installed manually if automated systems require servicing, ensuring the assembly line keeps running.
- Freshly painted Ioniq 5s and 9s move along a conveyor on a windowed bridge visible from Interstate 16 in Ellabell, Ga., as an intentional glimpse of factory operations for drivers seeing their tax dollars at work.
- Hyundai is already building a second battery plant in Georgia and a steel plant in Louisiana as part of an expanded pledge of $21 billion in U.S. investment through 2028.
- The first Ioniq 5 rolled off the Metaplant line on 3 October 2024.
- Production of the larger Ioniq 9 kicked off in March 2025.
- Immediately after final inspection, a human driver takes the car for its first road test on a track just outside the factory.
