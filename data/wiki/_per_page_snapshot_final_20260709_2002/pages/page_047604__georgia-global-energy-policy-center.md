# Georgia Global Energy Policy Center

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2023-07-14

**Source:** [Powering Ahead: How Georgia Can Become a Global Energy Thought Leader  - Global Atlanta](https://www.globalatlanta.com/powering-ahead-how-georgia-can-become-a-global-energy-thought-leader/)  ·  `page_047604`

## Facts

- Article authored by Craig Lesser (Chairman of The Pendleton Group, former Georgia Commissioner of Economic Development 2004-07) and Jorge Fernandez (Senior Consultant at The Pendleton Group).
- Authors propose creating a new "Georgia Global Energy Policy Center" to leverage state assets and partner internationally for energy innovation.
- The proposed center would be funded by state government, private industry, federal grants, and foundations.
- The center would leverage the expertise of the entire University System of Georgia.
- The Georgia Institute of Technology would serve as the hub for the Center of Energy Supply Research.
- The University of Georgia would form the Center for Rural Energy Research and Policy.
- Georgia Southern University would house the Center of Air and Sea Energy Research and Policy.
- Georgia State University would become the Center of Urban Energy Research and Policy.
- Georgia aims to compete on the value of its intellectual capacity and connections rather than solely on job counts and inward investment figures.
- The proposed center would connect global players to address pressing energy issues and form partnerships with international energy think tanks.
- Companies with energy interests in Georgia include AGCO, Delta Air Lines, Fieldale Farms, Georgia Power Co., Gulfstream, Hyundai, Kia, Lockheed, Mercedes Benz, Porsche, and Southern Co.
- These companies could utilize the proposed center to partner with university researchers and cooperate on joint energy projects to attract international partners and funding.
- Energy-related organizations like AEE and INPO could cooperate with the policy center to pool resources and knowledge.
- The existing Center of Innovation for Energy is led by the Georgia Department of Economic Development.
- This center works with companies like Stryten Energy, Mitsubishi Turbines, and Siemens Energy & Automation to advance energy technologies for generation, storage, and consumption.
- The innovation center has worked with partners for about 10 years to lay groundwork for the EV mobility industry in Georgia.
- Georgia has positioned itself as a hub for electric transportation with 35 EV-related projects investing in the state since 2018.
- Hyundai Motor Group’s fully dedicated EV and battery manufacturing “meta plant” near Savannah is expected to ramp up to full production in 2025.
- Rivian’s largest manufacturing facility in the U.S. is slated to start production in Georgia in 2026.
- Georgia is rapidly expanding its full EV supply chain with related manufacturing and solutions.
- NVH Korea announced a $72 million battery parts factory in Henry County.
- Woory Industrial Co., Ltd. plans to open an EV parts R&D center and manufacturing facility in Dublin, Georgia.
- Both NVH Korea and Woory Industrial are coming to support the Hyundai Meta Plant.
- LG Energy Solution Ltd. is partnering on a $4.3 billion battery operation at the Hyundai Meta Plant.
- Anovion Technologies is building an $800 million plant to manufacture synthetic graphite anode materials for lithium-ion batteries in Bainbridge, Georgia.
- Georgia’s ambitions to become a hub for clean vehicles underscore its goal to be a clearinghouse for clean energy expertise in EV infrastructure, battery development, commercialization, and charging stations.
- Georgia’s existing energy innovation center is focusing on identifying EV charging equipment manufacturers, such as Netherlands-based Heliox.
- The state is especially interested in attracting global players in hydrogen energy technology and storage to invest in Georgia.
- Georgia could work with Hydrogen Europe on innovation and investment for deploying hydrogen power.
- The Center of Innovation for Energy hopes to develop demonstration hydrogen projects and potentially the first hydrogen living laboratory.
- This hydrogen lab initiative is similar to the state's net-zero transportation initiative The Ray and the smart city Curiosity Lab.
- Georgia is bidding for funding to become a center for hydrogen expertise through a coalition of regional utilities, including Southern Company.
- Georgia Power is conducting research into hydrogen fuels.
- Suppliers like Atlanta Cylinders Inc., whose parent company is in the Czech Republic, are lining up to support Georgia’s hydrogen initiatives.
- Automakers Hyundai and Toyota Motor Corp. are looking to hydrogen as the fuel of the future.
- Success with hydrogen initiatives could attract broader industry attention to Georgia similar to the EV mobility industry's focus.
- Georgia’s previous initiatives to coalesce expertise around fintech and global health demonstrate its ability to garner local and international industry support.
- The proposed policy center would encourage public education programs around new energy technology to engage youth and cultivate students for universities and technical schools.
- A growing community of educated professionals would help attract talented entrepreneurs and innovators from around the world to Georgia.
- The Georgia Research Alliance has been instrumental in accelerating university research and development growth in the state.
- The Georgia Research Alliance supports startup companies and creates jobs by bringing private and public research investment to Georgia.
- Alignment between the Georgia Research Alliance and a new energy policy center could result in further economic impact throughout the state.
- The Georgia Global Energy Policy Center would be a nonpartisan effort benefiting the entire state.
- Research and development on this scale could benefit every Georgian by creating new jobs or inventing efficiencies that impact gas and electric bills.
