# Volvo Cars

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-05-26

**Source:** [Trump administration permits Volvo to keep selling connected cars in the US | TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/26/trump-administration-permits-volvo-to-keep-selling-connected-cars-in-the-us/)  ·  `page_014088`

## Facts

- Volvo Cars reached an agreement with the Trump administration that allows the automaker to continue selling vehicles in the U.S., avoiding a ban on Chinese-connected vehicle technology.
- The Swedish automaker is majority owned by China’s Geely Holding.
- Volvo received specific authorization from the U.S. Department of Commerce to continue importing and selling its vehicles in the United States.
- The Biden administration finalized rules in January 2025 that blocked vehicles equipped with software and hardware developed and maintained by Chinese companies over national security concerns.
- Connected car technology involves software covering everything from syncing with phones to some automated driving features.
- The rules initially apply to 2027 model-year vehicles equipped with software developed and maintained by Chinese companies or companies with Chinese ties.
- A separate ban prohibiting the import of vehicle connected hardware begins with 2030 model-year vehicles.
- Volvo vehicles are primarily manufactured in Sweden and imported to the United States, with the exception of the EX90 which is assembled at the company’s factory in South Carolina.
- Volvo’s ties to China’s Geely and its manufacturing operations in China meant it would have been banned under the new rules.
- The approval followed constructive discussions with the Commerce department and other U.S. officials regarding the company’s governance, technology, and data security.
- Volvo stated it can now move forward with its expansion plans in the United States.
- In September 2025, Volvo announced plans to bring two additional vehicles—the XC60 midsize SUV and a new hybrid vehicle—into production at its South Carolina factory.
- In March, Volvo announced it will bring all production of the Polestar 3 EV from its sister company Polestar to its U.S. factory.
- The Polestar 3 is currently also produced in Chengdu, China.
- The rule governing these restrictions is titled “Securing the Information and Communications Technology and Services Supply Chain: Connected Vehicles.”
- Under the rules, Chinese companies are prohibited from testing autonomous vehicles in the United States.
- Several Chinese-affiliated companies, including Baidu’s Apollo Autonomous Driving LLC, Pony.ai, and WeRide, currently hold permits to test their autonomous vehicle technology in California with a human safety operator behind the wheel.
- The article was updated to clarify that the approval was a special authorization rather than an exemption from the rules.
