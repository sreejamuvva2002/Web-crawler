# Hyundai-LG Savannah Battery Plant

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-04

**Source:** [Georgia EV Battery Plant Raid Fallout Impacts Industry](https://www.batterytechonline.com/industry-outlook/maybe-we-should-hold-off-on-us-investments-battery-industry-reacts-to-georgia-plant-raid)  ·  `page_000255`

## Facts

- On September 4, 2025, an immigration raid occurred at the Hyundai-LG battery plant under construction near Savannah, Georgia.
- The raid resulted in the detention of 475 workers, including more than 300 South Korean nationals.
- LG Energy Solution announced that construction at the plant will remain on hold until the first half of 2026.
- The facility's opening is delayed by at least two to three months to early 2026.
- The plant is a cornerstone of Hyundai's $12.6 billion investment in Georgia.
- Plans for the facility include producing batteries for up to 500,000 hybrid and electric vehicles across Hyundai's brand portfolio.
- Hyundai Motor CEO José Muñoz confirmed that specialized South Korean workers deported in the raid were essential to the joint venture battery plant.
- The company had to move workers from other plants to make up for the lost labor.
- Approximately 400 state and federal law enforcement personnel participated in the operation.
- The initial search warrant targeted just four Mexican nationals as 'targeted persons.'
- ICE agents arrested South Korean workers after determining they entered on B-1 visas or through ESTA.
- Immigration attorney Charles Kuck, representing 11 individuals, noted that ICE agents did not bring Korean language interpreters to the raid.
- One detained South Korean worker had just arrived the night before and was in a business suit attending a meeting when arrested.
- Multiple sources indicate a White House-imposed arrest quota drove the mass detention.
- Stephen Miller, White House deputy chief of staff and homeland security adviser, publicly stated a goal of 3,000 immigrant arrests per day.
- Charles Kuck told Forbes that ICE considered the operation successful because it met Miller's quota, despite arresting people eligible for their work type.
- NBC News confirmed the raid was part of a broader Trump administration deportation drive to fulfill campaign promises.
- Top US diplomat Christopher Landau expressed regret over the raid during meetings with South Korean counterparts.
- Washington promised that South Korean workers would face no disadvantages in reentering the US and that similar incidents would be prevented.
- South Korean President Lee Jae Myung called the incident 'bewildering' and an 'unjust infringement on the activities of our people.'
- President Lee warned the incident could discourage future foreign investment in the United States.
- A South Korean editorial, cited by Paul Krugman, compared the sight of detained workers being led out in chains to images of African slaves in the 18th and 19th centuries.
- Charles Kuck reported that companies in Asia and Europe are considering holding off on big US investments for at least three years due to the raid.
- South Korean tech giants Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix have been investing billions into US facilities as part of reshoring efforts.
- On September 19, Hyundai confirmed it is moving forward with expanding its Georgia plant.
- The expansion commitment includes $2.7 billion and 3,000 new jobs for phase two of the complex.
- The US H-1B visa program allows temporary hiring of foreign workers in 'specialty occupations' but is highly selective due to an annual cap and lottery system.
- On Friday, Sept. 19, President Trump signed a proclamation imposing a $100,000 fee for H-1B visas granted to skilled foreign workers.
- The administration announced on Saturday that the new fee would only apply to new H-1B applicants.
- South Korean President Lee urged the US to normalize the visa process related to investment by securing sufficient quotas or creating a new visa category.
- LGES North America President and Chief Strategy Officer Bob Lee noted that difficulty obtaining visas has long been a challenge for battery companies, but increased attention on the issue will be beneficial.
- Georgia Governor Brian Kemp suggested the raid may ultimately lead to improvements in the visa system, smoothing the way for South Korean employees to more easily obtain legal permission to work in the US.
