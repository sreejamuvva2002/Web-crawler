# Rivian Automotive

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2021-12-16

**Source:** [Anti-Rivian Residents Stand Strong as Gov. Kemp Touts GA as Nation’s EV Hub | UncoverDC | uncoverdc.com](https://uncoverdc.com/2023/01/16/anti-rivian-residents-stand-strong-as-gov-kemp-touts-ga-as-nations-ev-hub)  ·  `page_059156`

## Facts

- On December 16, 2021, Gov. Brian Kemp announced that Rivian Automotive would invest $5 billion in a 20 million square foot carbon-conscious campus in Georgia to produce electric adventure vehicles.
- Financing for the project is set to come from a bond-funded stimulus package of at least $1.5 billion and up to $15 billion.
- The nearly 2,000-acre location of the Rivian complex is known as the East Atlanta Megasite.
- The site is represented by the Joint Development Authority (JDA) of Jasper, Morgan, Newton, and Walton counties.
- The JDA was formed in 1999 and bought the land, which includes residential areas, forests, and historic farmland.
- Non-disclosure and non-disparagement clauses prevented critical landowners in Morgan County from sharing alleged intimidation tactics used to encourage them to sign contracts.
- Rivian, the JDA, and other government officials had been in secret talks for months crafting the deal before the December 2021 announcement.
- A study by the Northeast Georgia Regional Commission (NEGRC) concluded the Rivian development is generally inconsistent with Morgan County's 2017-2036 Comprehensive Plan prioritizing rural living, conservation, and gradual development for its less than 20,000 residents.
- Morgan County's comprehensive plan lists desired land uses to include undeveloped land left in its natural state, agriculture, and low-density, large-lot residential homes.
- Once up and running, Rivian's plant is scheduled to produce 400,000 electric vehicles annually.
- The facility will generate at least 6,700 tons of toxic, hazardous wastes annually that will need to go to a landfill.
- The proposed site contains at least 29 acres of wetlands and an additional 232 acres of wetlands are within a mile of the site.
- 92 percent of the proposed Rivian site lies within a Conservation area.
- On September 29, 2022, Morgan County Superior Court Judge Brenda Trammell denied the $15 billion in bonds, describing the bond structure as a mechanism to obtain tax abatement for a private company for which there is no ability legislatively or constitutionally.
- Instead of paying property taxes, the arrangement had Rivian set to make at least $300 million in PILOT payments to the JDA for 25 years by asserting that the company's rental agreement would qualify as a usufruct.
- Under the terms of Kemp's agreement, the local JDA would issue bonds that Rivian would purchase, with rent paid by Rivian used to repay the principal and interest due on the bonds.
- Judge Trammell ruled that the terms of Rivian's rental agreement did not qualify as a usufruct, leaving the company responsible for standard property taxes.
- A December 27, 2022 report by the Center for Economic Accountability (CEA) declared the deal Georgia's Rivian Plant Subsidy Recognized as 2022's Worst Economic Development Deal of the Year.
- CEA President John C. Mozena proclaimed that Georgia made a massive, speculative investment of taxpayer money in an early-stage company without doing basic due diligence.
- In February 2022, Rivian investor George Soros lost nearly $1 billion after a prolonged plunge in the company's share price.
- Amazon invested $1.3 billion in Rivian in 2019 as part of a plan to go green and took a $7.6 billion loss on its shares in April 2022.
- In late October 2022, the GDEcD and JDA submitted a notice of appeal to the Georgia Court of Appeals challenging Judge Trammell's ruling, with a calendar date of February 2023.
- On December 28, 2022, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers issued a federal Clean Water Act Section 404 permit allowing the Rivian assembly plant project to move forward on the entire 2,000-acre site.
- JoEllen Artz, president of No2Rivian, called the 404 permit devastating and urged Gov. Kemp to pause the Project until the legal system resolves the bond issuance case.
- Rivian stated in a statement to the Atlanta Journal Constitution that it remains excited to work with Georgia to bring good paying manufacturing jobs to the state while respecting the natural environment.
- Felton Jenkins III, an anti-Rivian member, claimed his family was threatened with land condemnation to sell off property for the project and called the industrialization a violation of Morgan County zoning regulations.
- Rivian was founded in 2009 by Robert J. Scaringe as a spinoff of Mainstream Engineering, a Florida-based developer that received significant funding from partnerships with the U.S. government.
- In 2017, Rivian purchased a former $100 million Mitsubishi factory in Illinois.
- Rivian received $1.5 million from the state-funded Space Florida organization and retained a $2 million grant from the Florida Office of Energy to build a lightweight modular prototype vehicle delivered to NASA in 2012.
- In September 2019, Amazon agreed to purchase 100,000 electric vehicles from Rivian, marking the largest order of EV delivery vehicles ever at the time.
- In July 2020, Rivian raised $2.5 billion in funding led by existing investors T. Rowe Price, Amazon, and Ford Motor.
- Cox Enterprises holds a 4.7 percent stake in Rivian and supplies services to the company.
- Sandy Schwartz, a Cox executive who oversees the AJC, is on Rivian's board of directors and holds stock personally.
- On December 8, 2022, Gov. Kemp announced that Hyundai Motor Group and SK On selected a site in Bartow County for a new electric vehicle battery manufacturing facility to supply Hyundai plants, wooed by an incentive package valued at more than $1.8 billion.
- Gov. Kemp announced that Korean solar panel maker QCells would invest over $2.5 billion in new solar panel plants in Georgia, described as the largest solar investment in U.S. history.
- Since 2020, more than 30 EV-related projects have announced more than $21 billion in investment and announced more than 26,300 jobs in Georgia.
