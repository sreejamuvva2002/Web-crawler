# Hyundai-LG Energy Solution Georgia EV Battery Plant Joint Venture

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-08

**Source:** [Trump trade, immigration agendas collide in Hyundai raid](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/economy/trump-trade-immigration-agendas-collide-hyundai-raid-rcna229890)  ·  `page_021108`

## Facts

- Federal authorities launched an immigration raid on a Georgia construction site for a Hyundai electric vehicle battery plant.
- The raid led to the arrests of nearly 500 workers, about 300 of whom were South Korean nationals.
- Seoul is working with U.S. law enforcement to return those detained workers home.
- Hyundai stated it is "committed to full compliance with all laws and regulations" regarding the raid.
- Hyundai noted that "none of those detained is directly employed by Hyundai Motor Company."
- In the United States, Hyundai is the nation's fourth bestseller of new vehicles, behind GM, Toyota and Ford.
- Hyundai stated it will invest in American manufacturing and create thousands of jobs in full accordance with U.S. law.
- Hyundai declined to say specifically if it would alter its plans because of last week’s raid.
- LG Energy Solution, which will operate the factory in a joint venture with Hyundai, has reportedly decided to delay the start of production until the first half of 2026.
- South Korean Foreign Minister Cho Hyun told lawmakers that if the factory completion is delayed, the United States will suffer significant losses.
- Trump dismissed concerns about the raid straining relations with South Korea, stating Sunday: "No, we have a great relationship with South Korea."
- The South Korean government has been working with the Trump administration to finalize a framework trade agreement under which South Korea would buy $100 billion of U.S. energy products and invest $350 billion in the United States.
- Trump said those $350 billion investment funds would be his to use any way he sees fit.
- Days after the raid, Trump admonished foreign companies on Truth Social, encouraging them to legally bring smart people with great technical talent to build World Class products.
- Other projects touted by Trump that could require visas for similar workers include semiconductor factories, high-powered AI data centers, pharmaceutical plants, energy infrastructure, and aircraft assembly lines.
- An ICE spokesperson told NBC News the investigation was focused on ensuring accountability for law violators, protecting jobs for Georgians, ensuring a level playing field for compliant businesses, safeguarding economic integrity, and protecting workers from exploitation.
- Trump’s border czar, Tom Homan, told CNN the administration plans "a lot more worksite operations."
- Homan cited his personal experience hiring a roofing company, noting he had to call five different companies before finding one that guaranteed a legal workforce.
- The Labor Department reported a slowdown in jobs growth on Friday, indicating a cooling broader economy.
- The administration touted a decline in foreign-born workers despite the jobs report.
- Farms across California, meatpacking plants in Nebraska, and businesses in other states received ICE/Border Patrol visits in May and June, leading to protests and complaints from industries losing reliable workers.
- Farmers were already under pressure from Trump’s tariff policy, which hurt demand for American products overseas.
- Trump posted in June that aggressive immigration policy is taking good, long-time workers away from farmers and the hotel/leisure business, making jobs almost impossible to replace.
- Trump stated: "We must protect our Farmers, but get the CRIMINALS OUT OF THE USA. Changes are coming!"
- ICE initially paused visits to farms, hotels, restaurants, fisheries, and other workplaces, but DHS walked that back after about a week and visits resumed.
- Trump conceded the administration will have to grant visas to overseas workers, especially those who could train U.S. workers in specialty industries like establishing battery factories.
- Trump stated: "We’re going to have to train people. And the way you train people is [to] bring people in that know what they’re doing and let them stay for a little while and help."
- Trump added: "So, I’m going to look at that. It’s a very interesting situation that took place in Georgia."
- Trump has pointed to the Hyundai factory and related U.S. investments as evidence that his administration’s economic agenda is working, even though such facilities take years to begin producing tangible output.
- Wells Fargo economists wrote Friday that stricter immigration enforcement has likely reduced new entrants to the workforce, following a jobs report showing just 22,000 jobs added in August.
- The August jobs report included revisions that showed a labor market contraction in June.
- EY’s chief economist concurred, noting that "tighter immigration policies are increasingly restraining labor supply."
