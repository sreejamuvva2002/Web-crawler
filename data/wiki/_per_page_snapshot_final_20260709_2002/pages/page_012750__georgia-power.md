# Georgia Power

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Georgia Power plans additional nuclear capacity - World Nuclear News](https://www.world-nuclear-news.org/articles/georgia-power-plans-additional-nuclear-capacity)  ·  `page_012750`

## Facts

- Georgia Power filed its 2025 Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) on January 31 with the Georgia Public Service Commission (PSC).
- The IRP proposes power uprates at four units at the Hatch and Vogtle nuclear power plants to meet long-term energy needs.
- The plan details necessary investments in the generation fleet and transmission system to support Georgia's expected extraordinary growth.
- Georgia Power projects approximately 8200 MW of electrical load growth over the next six years.
- Peak demand is projected to increase by more than 2200 MWe by the end of 2030 compared to the 2023 IRP Update approved in April 2024.
- The company identified opportunities to upgrade existing nuclear units to provide additional baseload capacity without requiring incremental transmission system investment.
- Georgia Power is proposing extended power uprates (EPU) at Vogtle units 1 and 2, and Hatch units 1 and 2.
- Between 2028 and 2034, the company plans to add a total of 112 MWe of capacity across the four units: 27 MWe each at Vogtle 1 and 2; 30 MWe at Hatch 1 and 28 MWe at Hatch.
- Extended power uprates involve significant modifications to major plant equipment to increase reactor thermal output.
- For the Hatch units, Georgia Power plans a boiler water reactor (BWR) upgrade called the 'Maximum Extended Load Line Limit (MELLA+)' enhancement to allow higher thermal power without increasing core flow.
- The company is considering transitioning Vogtle units 1 and 2 outage windows to a 24-month cycle to extend unit runtimes and decrease refueling outages.
- Georgia Power is working with the US Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) for the required review and licensing process, which includes reviewing license amendment requests for each facility.
- The company believes additional nuclear power will be needed long-term to reliably and economically serve customer energy needs.
- Nuclear power provides a pathway to reduce carbon emissions and mitigate cost pressures from potential future environmental regulations on fossil-fired fleets.
- Expansion planning uses multiple views of future technology costs, electricity consumption, and fuel prices, with B2025 scenarios selecting nuclear generation in six of nine scenarios over a 20-year horizon as early as 2037.
- The company acknowledges that new nuclear construction carries substantial risks and believes risk mitigation solutions must be developed before proposing additional new nuclear generation.
- Georgia Power is prioritizing viable new nuclear options by performing site assessments, evaluating emerging technologies, and engaging stakeholders to improve deployment methods.
- Kim Greene, chairman, president and CEO of Georgia Power, stated that the state is well-positioned for the future thanks to proactive planning and policies like the IRP.
- Kim Greene added that the 2025 IRP provides a comprehensive plan to support Georgia's continued economic growth and serve Georgians with clean, safe, reliable, and affordable energy into the future.
