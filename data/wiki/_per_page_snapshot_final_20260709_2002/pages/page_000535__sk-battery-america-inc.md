# SK Battery America Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [SK Battery America Inc. lays off 958 workers at Georgia plant | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/georgia-electric-vehicle-battery-manufacturing-layoffs-workers-79a4ec7a5b7f2816f64033f8b3d4898d)  ·  `page_000535`

## Facts

- Battery company SK Battery America Inc. laid off nearly 1,000 workers at a manufacturing plant northeast of Atlanta on Friday amid automakers’ changing electrification plans and uncertain consumer demand for EVs.
- Friday marked the last working day for 958 plant employees, about 37% of its workforce, according to a Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) notice filed by human resources chief Chuck Moore.
- Impacted workers will be paid through May 6.
- The plant will continue to employ about 1,600 workers.
- SK opened the $2.6 billion battery plant in Commerce, Georgia, in January 2022.
- The Korean company notably supplied the Ford F-150 Lightning electric pickup truck.
- Ford announced plans to cancel the fully electric version of the truck in December.
- The U.S. electric vehicle market is at a standstill amid the Trump administration steering federal support away from electrification in favor of more lax automotive emissions policies and a broader agenda supporting oil and gas industries.
- SK Americas spokesperson Joe Guy Collier said the workforce reduction was made to align operations to market conditions.
- Collier stated SK Battery America remains committed to Georgia and building a robust U.S. supply chain for advanced battery manufacturing.
- SK is pursuing future customers, including in the Battery Energy Storage System arena.
- The City of Commerce and Jackson County commission chair did not immediately respond to requests for comment.
- Ford said in December it would scrap the fully-electric version of its iconic pickup truck and opt for an extended-range version.
- A Ford spokesperson could not comment on supplier personnel actions.
- SK and Ford had together previously invested $11.4 billion in joint battery plants in the U.S.
- The battery maker ended the joint venture with Ford in December.
- SK is also a supplier to Volkswagen.
- Georgia U.S. Sen. Jon Ossoff (Democrat) stated: “Let’s be clear: these were battery manufacturing jobs and now they’re gone.”
- Ossoff added: “As predicted, Trump’s war on electric vehicles is hurting Georgia’s economy. We were booming and building new plants. Now Georgians are losing their jobs.”
- SK has invested significantly in Jackson County, Georgia, in recent years as automakers shored up plans to spend billions to develop and build EVs with federal support under former President Joe Biden.
- In June 2020, SK announced plans to pour $940 million to expand its battery manufacturing presence in Atlanta.
- Gov. Brian Kemp’s office said the expansion would create 600 jobs.
- SK and Hyundai are still jointly building a $5 billion battery factory near Cartersville, northwest of Atlanta.
- Georgia has attracted other massive EV manufacturing investments, including Rivian’s $5 billion factory and Hyundai’s own $7.6 billion factory complex.
- Few states benefited more than Georgia from Biden’s Inflation Reduction Act, which accelerated a rush of green energy projects.
- The 33 additional projects announced by the end of 2024 were the most nationwide, according to E2.
- Projects in Georgia topped $20 billion, pledging more than 25,000 jobs.
- Qcells, a unit of South Korea’s Hanwha Solutions, resumed normal production on Friday after temporarily reducing hours and pay last year because U.S. customs officials detained imported components needed to make solar panels.
- A spokesperson for Kemp’s office said it is grateful for the presence of workers remaining at SK’s factory.
- The statement noted Georgia’s job market remains robust, with over 219,700 new jobs announced since 2019.
- EV demand has not met automakers’ ambitious expectations in recent years.
- EVs accounted for about 8% of new vehicle sales in the U.S. in 2025, much the same as a year earlier.
- Automakers have been reevaluating multibillion-dollar electrification plans as financial losses mount and demand shifts.
- Manufacturers including Ford, General Motors, Stellantis and others have reneged on factory, investment and product plans, laid off workers, and pivoted to hybrid and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles.
- Hybrids and more efficient gasoline-powered vehicles are becoming more palatable for mainstream buyers concerned about EV driving range and charging infrastructure availability.
- Under President Donald Trump, Congress eliminated tax credits of up to $7,500 for consumers’ purchases of new or used EVs.
- The administration announced plans to weaken fuel economy and greenhouse gas emissions rules for automakers, essentially eliminating any federal incentive for auto companies to make their vehicle fleets cleaner.
