# Saft America

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Saft America: half a century in the United States | Saft | Batteries to energize the world](https://saft.com/en/media-resources/our-stories/saft-america-half-century-united-states)  ·  `page_059200`

## Facts

- Saft has been operating in America for half of its 100-year history.
- The company operates five key locations across the US, comprising four manufacturing plants and a distribution center.
- Saft produces a range of battery chemistries for customers including the military and energy companies.
- In 1954-1957, Saft entered the US market with the ambition of bringing nickel cadmium technology to the US airline industry.
- In 1951, Saft began talks with the Bureau of Aeronautics through Hazard Reeves for a license to produce Saft batteries in the US.
- After submitting Alcabloc and Voltabloc batteries to the Bureau of Standards for approval, the Bureau of Aeronautics signed a $500,000 contract.
- In May 1957, Saft Corporation of America was formed in Lodi, New Jersey as a joint enterprise with Reeves Soundcraft Corporation.
- The new company struggled to win against American lead-acid battery competitors and closed in October 1957.
- By 1974, Saft determined it needed its own local production site to develop rapidly in the US.
- In May 1973, Saft increased its holding in Gulton Industries' aircraft-battery manufacturing division to become the largest stakeholder.
- The company was renamed Saft America, with Gulton manufacturing nickel-based prismatic cells for aircraft from a New Jersey facility.
- In 1975, Saft America relocated operations to a brand-new plant in Valdosta, Georgia, producing portable cells for the commercial market and prismatic cells for aviation.
- The Valdosta facility remains operational today, with some original staff members still working there more than 40 years later.
- In 1978, Saft purchased SCORE, a small company in Cockeysville, Maryland that made thermal batteries for the space and defense market.
- The SCORE acquisition provided Saft with lithium production facilities that became increasingly important in later years.
- Within two years of the acquisition, Saft made the previously loss-making business profitable.
- In 1983, Saft purchased Gould National Batteries, which operated facilities in St Paul, Minnesota and Tijuana, Mexico.
- A couple of years after the Gould deal, Saft relocated the Minnesota facility to Valdosta while retaining the Tijuana site for assembly and warehousing.
- The Tijuana site closed in June 2001, with remaining production moved to France.
- In the late 1980s, Saft purchased Duracell’s business-to-business operations in Valdese, North Carolina.
- The Duracell deal expanded Saft's lithium sulfur dioxide (Li-SO2) capabilities and added a new US site.
- During the 1980s, the US Army was the world’s largest consumer of lithium-based batteries, mostly for radio applications.
- Saft decided to manufacture Li-SO2 in the US because the market was about 10 times larger than in Europe.
- After the Duracell deal, Valdese became the center for both Li-SO2 and Li-SOCI2 production, with Cockeysville's capability relocated there.
- At around the same time, Saft America purchased Catalyst Research, which brought lithium-anode technology to its Cockeysville portfolio.
- In 1991, Operation Desert Storm created massive demand for batteries for GPS and night-vision applications, leading to a $120m order, the largest in Saft’s history.
- To meet the Desert Storm order, the Valdese plant quadrupled in size and grew again a dozen years later with the launch of the second Gulf War.
- In 1995, Saft signed a contract to provide the battery for the Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor (Advanced Tactical Fighter), which had its first flight in 1997 and final delivery in 2012.
- The F-22 was the first application for Saft’s Aircraft Maintenance-Free Battery (AMFB).
- In 1993, Cockeysville ceased production after Saft stopped making unprofitable thermal cells, becoming an R&D-only facility for the next 10 years.
- During the 1990s, Saft developed in-house lithium-ion technology and industrial processes, securing major discoveries and patents.
- The Cockeysville R&D team built lithium-ion prototypes quickly, especially for defense applications, making lithium-ion one of Saft’s core technologies.
- In 1996, Saft began developing its own electronic battery-control systems because lithium-ion cells could not be used safely without them.
- Over the next decade, the Cockeysville team developed complex electrical and software engineering systems ranging from small 28V batteries up to electronics for the Joint Strike Fighter 270V battery qualified to aviation standards.
- In 1991, Saft bought NIFE in Greenville, North Carolina, along with the worldwide NIFE group, transferring pocket-plate cell and battery assembly, industrial primary battery recycling, and bring-back recycling for nickel-based batteries to Valdosta.
- In 1993, Saft acquired Gates Aerospace Batteries, bringing on Annie Sennet, who later became Saft’s Executive Vice President for Space & Defense and Saft America’s President and CEO.
- In 2001, Saft bought Hawker Eternacell, increasing Li-SO2 capabilities and making Saft a major world player in that market.
- During the 1990s, the R&D Center performed funded research into molten-salt secondary batteries under contract with the US Advanced Battery Consortium backed by the US Department of Energy.
- The molten-salt research did not lead to a rechargeable battery but maintained the capability to build thermal batteries.
- Saft established a thermal-battery production operation in Cockeysville as part of a new subsidiary with EADS (now Airbus) called Advanced Thermal Batteries.
- In 2011, Saft opened a state-of-the-art facility in Jacksonville, Florida, over 100 miles from the Valdosta plant, to enhance lithium-ion battery production for renewable energy, electric vehicles, and telecom applications.
- In 2012, Valdese began producing lithium manganese dioxide (Li-MnO2) cells for new metering applications as demand in the sector grew.
- Saft America currently represents 36 percent of the company's total sales and is a major contributor to its overall customer base.
- The five US locations employ close to 1,000 people and produce batteries for all of Saft's major markets.
- At the US R&D center in Cockeysville, 14 employees work on projects including lithium titanate (LTO) cells for 12V start-stop batteries, Formula One car cells operating at high rate and elevated temperature, and manganese phosphate providing safe and high energy density cells.
