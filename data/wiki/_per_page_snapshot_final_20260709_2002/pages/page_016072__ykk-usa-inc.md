# YKK (USA) Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2024-06-07

**Source:** [YKK Celebrates 50 Years Linking Macon With Japan - Global Atlanta](https://www.globalatlanta.com/ykk-celebrates-50-years-linking-macon-with-japan/)  ·  `page_016072`

## Facts

- YKK (USA) Inc. is marking 50 years in Macon, Georgia, commemorating its first foray into U.S. manufacturing in 1974.
- The company opened a New York sales office in 1960 before searching for sites in the South to enter the textile industry.
- Cloyd Hall, a Macon native and advisor to then-Georgia Gov. Jimmy Carter, introduced YKK founder Tadao Yoshida to the governor, helping clinch the deal for Georgia due to its location, port, and airport.
- Georgia Gov. Jimmy Carter set up the state’s trade office in Tokyo in 1973.
- Over time, a facility that started with zippers tailored to the U.S. apparel market has significantly adapted, producing a varied portfolio of solutions for the automotive, medical, and government sectors.
- The Macon facility currently employs 600 people, down from its peak but reflective of its long-term commitment.
- John Smith, president of YKK (USA) Inc., stated that the company expresses deep gratitude to dedicated employees and the city of Macon for their unwavering support over the past 50 years and is enthusiastic about extending this legacy into the future.
- As part of yearlong celebrations, YKK has honored employees celebrating their 45th and 50th years with the company.
- YKK re-dedicated the “Heart of Friendship” sculpture in Macon that was donated by the company in 1974 and relocated to the city’s Carolyn Crayton Park.
- In 1990, YKK donated $300,000 for the Macon Cherry Blossom Festival headquarters building.
- Chairman Tadahiro Yoshida, son of founder Tadao Yoshida and recently awarded the Order of the Rising Sun with Gold and Silver Stars by the Japanese government, was on hand for the sculpture dedication.
- YKK supported the establishment of a sister-city relationship between Macon and Kurobe, Japan, which has led to at least 1,100 Maconites visiting Japan since 1977 as well as partnerships between the cities’ hospital systems.
