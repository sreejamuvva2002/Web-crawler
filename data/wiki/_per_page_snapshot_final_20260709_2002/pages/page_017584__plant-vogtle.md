# Plant Vogtle

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [The first US nuclear reactor built from scratch in decades enters commercial operation in Georgia | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/georgia-power-nuclear-reactor-vogtle-9555e3f9169f2d58161056feaa81a425)  ·  `page_017584`

## Facts

- Unit 3 at Plant Vogtle, located southeast of Augusta, Georgia, has completed testing and entered commercial operation.
- Unit 3 is the first American nuclear reactor to be built from scratch in decades.
- Construction of Unit 3 was seven years late and $17 billion over budget.
- At full output, Unit 3 generates 1,100 megawatts of electricity.
- The power from Unit 3 can supply 500,000 homes and businesses.
- Electricity from the unit is distributed to utilities in Georgia, Florida, and Alabama, as well as Southern Co. subsidiary Georgia Power's 2.7 million customers.
- Chris Womack serves as CEO of Atlanta-based Southern Co.
- A fourth reactor at Plant Vogtle is nearing completion.
- The Nuclear Regulatory Commission approved loading radioactive fuel into Unit 4 before the end of September.
- Unit 4 is scheduled to enter commercial operation by March.
- The third and fourth reactors were originally budgeted at $14 billion but are now on track to cost their owners $31 billion.
- Original contractor Westinghouse paid $3.7 billion to the owners to walk away from the project.
- Total spending on the Vogtle project is almost $35 billion.
- Construction for the third reactor began in 2009, with power generation originally slated for 2016.
- Government officials and utilities are looking to nuclear power to alleviate climate change by generating electricity without burning natural gas, coal, and oil.
- Most U.S. focus is currently on smaller nuclear reactors to avoid the cost and schedule overruns that plagued Vogtle.
- Southern Co. does not plan to add any more reactors to its fleet.
- Georgia Power owns a 45.7% share of the reactors.
- Oglethorpe Power Corp., which provides electricity to member-owned cooperatives, holds a smaller ownership share.
- The Municipal Electric Authority of Georgia and the city of Dalton hold smaller ownership shares.
- Oglethorpe and MEAG plan to sell power to cooperatives and municipal utilities across Georgia, Jacksonville, Florida, parts of Alabama, and the Florida Panhandle.
- Almost every electric customer in Georgia will pay for Vogtle through an ongoing finance charge.
- Residential customers are projected to pay more than $926 apiece as part of the finance charge.
- Elected public service commissioners approved a rate increase that adds $4 per month to residential bills starting as soon as the third unit begins generating power, potentially in August.
- This follows a previous $16-a-month rate increase for higher fuel costs.
- Experts testified that high construction costs have wiped out any future benefit from low nuclear fuel costs.
- Tom Newsome, director of utility finance for the commission, testified that cost increases and schedule delays completely eliminated any life-cycle cost benefit.
- Opponents argue that power generated from solar and wind would be cheaper than Vogtle.
- Southern Environmental Law Center staff attorney Bob Sherrier stated that capital-intensive projects benefit Georgia Power's shareholders but are not the least-cost option for Georgians facing repeated bill increases.
- Commissioners will later decide who pays for the remainder of Vogtle costs, including the fourth reactor.
- Customers will pay for spending deemed prudent, while the company and its shareholders will pay for spending deemed wasteful.
- Georgia Power CEO Kim Greene stated the company has not made a final determination on how much to ask customers to pay pending a prudence filing.
