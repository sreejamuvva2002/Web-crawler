## Facts

- Volvo Cars is funneling additional capital into its Ridgeville, South Carolina plant.
- The investment builds on a $1.3 billion investment over the past decade.
- The goal is to fully utilize the facility’s 150,000-vehicle annual capacity.
- The Ridgeville plant has been operational since 2015.
- The plant currently produces the electric EX90 SUV and Polestar 3 but runs below potential.
- Volvo will introduce XC60 mid-size SUV production from late 2026.
- The XC60 model sold over 27,000 units in the US in the first eight months of 2025.
- XC60 sales are up nearly 20 percent year-on-year.
- This production shift may counter rising tariffs on Chinese imports, which have disrupted global automotive trade since 2024.
- Before 2030, a US-specific hybrid model, likely a next-generation XC90, will join the lineup.
- The upcoming hybrid model will blend petrol and electric power to appeal to buyers hesitant about full EVs.
- This reflects Volvo’s regionalization strategy, tailoring products and production to local markets to mitigate risks from geopolitical tensions and supply bottlenecks.
- Håkan Samuelsson, chief executive, stated that the investment plans reinforce long-term commitment to the US market and manufacturing operations in South Carolina.
- Volvo Cars is celebrating 70 years of presence in the United States this year.
- Volvo has sold over 5 million cars in the US and plans to sell many more in years to come.
- South Carolina’s status as a US automotive powerhouse leading in vehicle exports is bolstered by Volvo’s plans.
- The state’s skilled workforce and port access have drawn over $2 billion in auto-related investments since 2020.
- Volvo’s expansion is poised to add jobs and supplier activity, though exact figures remain undisclosed.
- Nearby, Magna made a $200 million investment that created 200 roles.
- The shift to electrification demands new skills in battery systems and software, areas where South Carolina lags behind tech hubs like California.
- Henry McMaster, Governor of South Carolina, stated that Volvo's decision reinforces South Carolina's position as a national leader in automotive manufacturing.
- Henry McMaster highlighted the strength of the state's workforce and the confidence top companies have in it, expressing pride in continuing the partnership with Volvo Cars.
- The plant’s ability to handle multiple platforms and technologies offers agility in a turbulent market.
- South Carolina’s reliance on foreign manufacturers like Volvo and BMW, whose exports exceed $10 billion annually, raises questions about economic sovereignty if trade policies harden.
- Volvo’s US strategy aligns with its global network, including plants in Europe and China, to deliver region-specific products while pursuing net-zero emissions by 2040.
- Volvo recently reported a $1 billion loss in Q2 2025.
- Volvo now targets 50 to 60 percent electrified sales by year-end, down from earlier ambitions.
- EV demand is waning amid sparse charging networks and reduced subsidies.
- Hybrids are outpacing pure EVs in US sales, making Ridgeville’s planned hybrid a pragmatic bet.
- Luis Rezende, Volvo Cars’ president of Americas, stated that the Charleston plant is foundational to the strategic growth plan for the US.
- Luis Rezende added that adding another model into production will take a big step toward realizing the full potential of local manufacturing investments and workforce, accelerating progress toward commercial targets.
