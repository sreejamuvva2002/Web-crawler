# Georgia Institute of Technology

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2024-09-27

**Source:** [Georgia Tech’s New Cathode Technology Could Revolutionize Electric Vehicles and Energy Storage](https://scitechdaily.com/georgia-techs-new-cathode-technology-could-revolutionize-electric-vehicles-and-energy-storage/)  ·  `page_000332`

## Facts

- Georgia Tech researchers developed a new iron chloride cathode that could slash lithium-ion battery costs and revolutionize electric vehicles and energy storage.
- A research team from multiple institutions, led by Hailong Chen of Georgia Tech, developed the cost-effective cathode with potential to significantly enhance lithium-ion batteries.
- Hailong Chen is an associate professor with appointments in the George W. Woodruff School of Mechanical Engineering and the School of Materials Science and Engineering at Georgia Tech.
- The new iron chloride (FeCl3) cathode costs a mere 1-2% of typical cathode materials and can store the same amount of electricity.
- Cathode materials affect capacity, energy, efficiency, performance, lifespan, and affordability of a battery.
- Hailong Chen stated that their cathode could be a game-changer that would greatly improve the EV market and the whole lithium-ion battery market.
- Batteries are currently responsible for about 50% of an EV’s total cost, which makes clean-energy cars more expensive than internal combustion vehicles.
- Only four types of cathodes have been successfully commercialized for lithium-ion batteries so far; Chen's would be the fifth.
- The development represents a big step forward in battery technology toward creating an all-solid-state lithium-ion battery (LIB).
- Conventional LIBs use liquid electrolytes that have hard limits on energy storage and can leak or catch fire, while all-solid-state LIBs use solid electrolytes to dramatically boost efficiency, reliability, safety, and energy capacity.
- All-solid-state LIB technology is currently still in the development and testing phase.
- With the FeCl3 cathode, a solid electrolyte, and a lithium metal anode, the cost of their whole battery system is 30-40% of current LIBs.
- Hailong Chen noted that this technology could make EVs much cheaper than internal combustion cars, provide large-scale energy storage to enhance grid resilience, and improve sustainability and supply chain stability for the EV market.
- Chen's interest in FeCl3 as a cathode material originated with his lab's research into solid electrolyte materials starting in 2019.
- The lab initially tried making solid-state batteries using chloride-based solid electrolytes with traditional commercial oxide-based cathodes, but the cathode and electrolyte materials were incompatible.
- Researchers determined that a chloride-based cathode could provide a better pairing with the chloride electrolyte to offer better battery performance.
- Hailong Chen explained that FeCl3 was chosen because its crystal structure is potentially suitable for storing and transporting lithium ions, and it functioned as expected.
- The most popularly used EV cathodes are oxides requiring a gigantic amount of costly nickel and cobalt, which are heavy elements that can be toxic and pose an environmental challenge.
- The Chen team’s cathode contains only iron (Fe) and chlorine (Cl), which are abundant, affordable, widely used elements found in steel and table salt.
- Initial tests showed FeCl3 performed as well as or better than other, much more expensive cathodes.
- The new cathode has a higher operational voltage than the popularly used LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) cathode.
- This technology may be less than five years from commercial viability in EVs.
- The research work was led by Chen and postdoc Zhantao Liu, who is the lead author of the study.
- Collaborators included Ting Zhu and Yuanzhi Tang from Georgia Tech, Jue Liu from Oak Ridge National Laboratory, and Shuo Chen from the University of Houston.
- Hailong Chen stated that while they want to perfect the materials in the lab and understand their underlying functioning mechanisms, they are open to opportunities to scale up the technology toward commercial applications.
- The study titled “Low-cost iron trichloride cathode for all-solid-state lithium-ion batteries” was published on 23 September 2024 in Nature Sustainability.
- The study was funded by the National Science Foundation under Grant Nos. 1706723 and 2108688.
