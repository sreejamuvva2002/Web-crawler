# Rivian Automotive

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-03

**Source:** [EXCLUSIVE: 'Rivian wanted what Georgia had': How the Peach State became Rivian's $5B match](https://www.teslarati.com/rivian-wanted-what-georgia-had-pat-wilson-ga-economic-development-commissioner-interview/)  ·  `page_009034`

## Facts

- A 2,000-acre slice of land in Northern Georgia, about 45 miles east of Atlanta, was pitched for years by state officials to various automakers before being secured by Rivian.
- Pat Wilson has served as Commissioner of the State of Georgia’s Economic Development Department since November 2016.
- Wilson previously showed the property to Volvo and Toyota/Mazda but could not come to terms with them on the land.
- Large-scale automakers were unwilling to join Kia, which operates a massive $1.8 billion, 2.2 million square foot factory just miles away from the Georgia-Alabama border.
- Rivian Automotive requested information for the site after rolling its first production units off an assembly line in Normal, Illinois and completing its first deliveries earlier this year.
- The project represents Georgia’s single most significant investment in state history at $5 billion.
- CEO RJ Scaringe drove around the 2,000-acre site in a Rivian R1T to plot ideas for his company’s second U.S.-located automotive assembly plant.
- RJ Scaringe was genuinely concerned about keeping the area environmentally stable and prioritized preserving its natural beauty.
- Rivian wanted to preserve the landscape's beauty and integrate its future automotive facility into the topography.
- The facility is expected to hit an employment of 7,500 people in 2028.
- Local residents gave their blessing for the Economic Development Department to offer the area to large industries because the project would not intrude on them.
- Wilson clarified that the department does not propose sites unless they are invited to do so.
- Rivian’s elevated expectations and accelerated timeline scared off other regions competing for “Project Terra”.
- Construction is scheduled to begin in Georgia in Summer 2022, with production lines ramping up in 2024.
- Rivian hopes to launch production around two years after construction crews break ground to meet its speed-to-market needs.
- The company locked up the 2,000-acre land plot and announced it during its first quarterly earnings call as a publicly-traded entity.
- The plant sits in the Interstate 20 corridor, providing easy access to the Port of Savannah and the State’s 5,000 miles of railway for delivering manufacturing materials.
- Georgia’s unemployment rate was listed at just 2.8% for November 2021 by Department of Labor statistics, making it the fourth-lowest rate federally.
- Other states' unemployment rates mentioned were Nebraska (1.8%), Utah (2.1%), and Oklahoma (2.5%).
- Governor Brian Kemp kept the State’s workforce relatively operational through the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 and 2021 by creating structure for the State.
- Georgia committed to addressing Rivian’s employment concerns by securing plans for a Quick Start workforce training program facility at the future automotive plant.
- Quick Start is a State-sponsored program created in 1967 that provides customized workforce training for expanding industries.
- The program runs through the Georgia Technical College System and gives workers free, hands-on, in-depth training that contributes to the state’s economy.
- Wilson stated that the program allows taxpayer dollars to be funded back into local communities through job training.
- The program resulted in the first car ever built at the Kia Factory in West Point being fully operational rather than a prototype.
- Wilson stated that the Quick Start program retains talent in Georgia, keeping the State’s workforce and brilliant minds local.
- Wilson expressed hope that more EV makers will come to Georgia.
- Wilson noted there will be more change in the automotive industry in the next ten years than in the last 100.
- Wilson noted that these plants will create jobs 60 years down the road.
