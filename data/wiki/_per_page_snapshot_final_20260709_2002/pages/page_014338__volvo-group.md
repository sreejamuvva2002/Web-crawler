# Volvo Group

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Volvo CEO says plan for Mexico truck factory on track](https://www.rte.ie/news/business/2025/0109/1489824-volvo-ceo-says-plan-for-mexico-truck-factory-on-track/)  ·  `page_014338`

## Facts

- Sweden's Volvo Group is proceeding with its planned $700m heavy-truck factory in Monterrey, Mexico.
- CEO Martin Lundstedt confirmed the plan despite President-elect Donald Trump threatening to impose US tariffs on imports of vehicles and other goods.
- The maker of Volvo and Mack semi-trucks has targeted growth in North America as a strategic priority, with North America accounting for about 30% of company revenue.
- The Monterrey plant is expected to start operations in 2026.
- The factory will deliver vehicles to the US, Mexico, Canada, and Latin America.
- President-elect Donald Trump plans to sign documents immediately after his January 20 inauguration to begin charging a 25% tariff on imports from Mexico and Canada unless those countries clamp down on illegal immigration and fentanyl trafficking.
- The Mexico project is at an 'early stage that is continuing,' according to Lundstedt in a Reuters NEXT Newsmaker interview on the sidelines of CES 2025 in Las Vegas.
- Volvo has options to use the plant for sales outside the US.
- Lundstedt stated, 'This is not a replacement of our American facilities.'
- Depending on US tariff outcomes, the Mexico factory may or may not send trucks to the US.
- Volvo's US factories currently make 100% of the trucks it sells in the US.
- The company has been investing in plants in Virginia, Maryland, and Pennsylvania to expand that capacity.
- Erik Smith, a director in the automotive and industrial practice at AlixPartners, noted that Volvo, Daimler Trucks North America, PACCAR, and TRATON supply nearly all heavy-duty trucks in North America.
- Smith stated Volvo is the only one among those competitors without a footprint in Mexico, which is likely why they are looking to establish one.
- Trump's tariff proposals have sent a shockwave through the auto manufacturing sector, affecting passenger car makers like BMW, Toyota, and General Motors who have invested in Mexican factories for lower labor costs and business-friendly policies.
- Volvo has agreements to provide electric semi-trucks to customers including Amazon.com and Danish logistics firm DSV.
- Lundstedt said Volvo expects high-volume production at a planned battery plant in Sweden to begin a couple of years later than the original target of 2029-2030.
- In September, Volvo announced it would delay construction of the Swedish battery plant by one or two years, leaving uncertainty about the manufacturing start date.
- Volvo wants to be at the forefront of green investments, including zero-emissions trucking, but is refining its strategy to adjust for market factors.
- Lundstedt said Volvo does not want to be 'fighting all the windmills alone, like Don Quixote and Sancho Panza.'
