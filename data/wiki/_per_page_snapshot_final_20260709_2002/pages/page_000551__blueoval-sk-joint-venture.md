# BlueOval SK Joint Venture

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-12-12

**Source:** [SK On and Ford split US battery venture - electrive.com](https://www.electrive.com/2025/12/12/sk-on-and-ford-split-us-battery-venture/)  ·  `page_000551`

## Facts

- SK On and Ford Motor have agreed to unwind their BlueOval SK joint venture in the United States.
- The partnership was launched in 2022 to build large-scale battery manufacturing capacity for electric vehicles.
- The three battery plants developed under the BlueOval SK banner will be split between the two companies, with ownership and operations separated.
- SK On will assume full ownership of the battery plant under construction in Tennessee, located within Ford’s BlueOval City campus.
- Ford will take over the two plants in Kentucky through a wholly owned subsidiary.
- The Kentucky plant officially opened its doors in August.
- A second plant in Kentucky is still under development.
- The Tennessee plant was supposed to be completed this year; however, production there has yet to start.
- The companies aim to complete their parting by the end of the first quarter of 2026, subject to regulatory approvals and customary closing conditions.
- Once the transfer is finalized, the Tennessee site will become SK On’s second wholly owned battery factory in the US.
- SK On’s other US battery facility is SK Battery America in Commerce, Georgia.
- SK On’s standalone battery production capacity in the US is expected to rise from 22 gigawatt-hours to 67 gigawatt-hours annually.
- The company’s only remaining US joint venture will be its battery project with Hyundai Motor Group in Georgia.
- Despite the dissolution of the joint venture, SK On and Ford are expected to continue their supplier relationship.
- The Tennessee plant is set to provide batteries for Ford’s next-generation electric vehicles under existing agreements.
- The BlueOval SK project was originally announced as an investment of around $11 billion.
- The combined planned capacity is up to 120 gigawatt-hours per year.
- The planned capacity is sufficient to supply batteries for more than one million electric vehicles annually.
- Timelines for the Tennessee plant and the second Kentucky facility had been pushed back.
- In 2023, Ford announced that it would postpone its investment in the second Kentucky plant and thus the start of construction.
- The reason for the postponement was weaker-than-anticipated demand for EVs across the US.
- For that same reason, Ford agreed to let competitor Nissan use its battery production in Kentucky earlier this year.
- SK On currently focuses on nickel-cobalt-manganese battery chemistries for electric vehicles.
- SK On supplies cells to several automakers, including Hyundai Motor Group, Kia, Volkswagen, Nissan and US EV startup Slate.
- The Tennessee plant could support energy storage system production, reflecting rising demand for grid-scale storage in the US.
- A spokesperson told The Korea Herald: “With our 45-gigawatt-hour Tennessee plant, we aim to supply batteries for both electric vehicles and energy storage systems not only to Ford but to a wider range of customers, strengthening profitability and operational resilience in the North American market.”
- Ford published what is probably the shortest company statement ever: “We are aware of SK’s disclosure and we have nothing further to share at this time.”
