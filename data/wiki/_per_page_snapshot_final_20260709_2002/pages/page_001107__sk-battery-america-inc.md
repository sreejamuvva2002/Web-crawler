# SK Battery America Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-07

**Source:** [Nearly 1,000 Jobs Cut at SK Battery's Georgia Plant as EV Market Headwinds Intensify - Georgia Asian Times](https://gasiantimes.com/business/2026/03/09/nearly-1000-jobs-cut-at-sk-batterys-georgia-plant-as-ev-market-headwinds-intensify/)  ·  `page_001107`

## Facts

- SK Battery America Inc. laid off 958 workers, representing roughly 37 percent of its workforce, at its manufacturing plant northeast of Atlanta on Friday, March 7, 2026.
- The layoffs mark one of the most significant single-day job losses in Georgia's electric vehicle industry since the sector began its rapid expansion in the state.
- Affected employees' last working day was Friday, and they will continue to receive pay through May 6.
- Approximately 1,600 workers remain at the Commerce plant following the layoffs.
- The plant opened in January 2022 following a $2.6 billion investment by SK Battery America Inc.
- The facility served as a flagship example of SK Battery's U.S. ambitions and supplied lithium-ion battery cells for Ford's F-150 Lightning electric pickup truck.
- SK Battery America cited automakers' shifting electrification timelines and uncertain consumer demand as the primary drivers behind the layoff decision.
- Federal policy changes under the Trump administration eliminated the $7,500 federal EV tax credit and rolled back emissions standards.
- These policy changes removed two of the most powerful market incentives that had previously fueled EV demand growth.
- EV purchases in the United States accounted for roughly 8 percent of new vehicle sales in 2025, remaining flat compared to the prior year and falling short of projections used to justify multi-billion-dollar battery investments.
- In December 2025, Ford announced it would cancel the fully electric version of the F-150 Lightning.
- In the same month, SK Battery America Inc. and Ford ended their joint battery manufacturing venture, which had involved a combined pledge of $11.4 billion across multiple U.S. battery plants.
- Ford's step back from the all-electric segment removed a core pillar of production rationale for the Georgia plant.
- Law firm Strauss Borrelli announced it was investigating whether SK Battery violated the federal WARN Act, which mandates at least 60 days' written notification before major layoffs or plant closures.
- SK's spokesperson maintained that the company's actions comply with WARN guidelines, though the legal investigation remains ongoing.
- U.S. Senator Jon Ossoff placed direct blame on the White House, arguing that the Trump administration's stance on electric vehicles is damaging Georgia's economy.
- Senator Raphael Warnock expressed concern for the affected workers and their families.
- Governor Brian Kemp's office highlighted more than 219,700 new jobs announced in Georgia since 2019 as evidence of the state's overall economic resilience.
- SK Battery America Inc. stated it remains committed to its Georgia operations and is actively pursuing customers in the Battery Energy Storage System market for data centers and power grid backup applications.
- Demand in the Battery Energy Storage System sector continues to grow independently of passenger vehicle trends.
- A second Georgia battery plant contracted to supply Hyundai Motor is scheduled to begin production in the first half of 2026.
- A third SK facility located in Tennessee is expected to come online in 2028.
- Both upcoming plants reflect SK's ongoing commitment to the long-term trajectory of EV adoption despite an uncertain near-term outlook.
- Georgia benefited significantly from the Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act, with green energy projects topping $20 billion in announced investments and pledging more than 25,000 jobs.
- The Commerce plant was among the most prominent of these projects, symbolizing advocates' vision of Georgia emerging as a hub for clean energy manufacturing.
