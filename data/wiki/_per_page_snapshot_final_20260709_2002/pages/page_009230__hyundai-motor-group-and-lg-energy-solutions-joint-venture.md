# Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solutions Joint Venture

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [ICE Conducts Largest-Ever Raid at Georgia Hyundai Plant: 475 Arrested, Mostly Korean | Democracy Now!](https://www.democracynow.org/2025/9/8/hyundai_immigration_raid_south_korea)  ·  `page_009230`

## Facts

- Hyundai is investing over $12 billion in a record-setting economic development deal with the state of Georgia for an electric vehicle battery plant.
- State officials say the $12.6 billion project is the largest economic development deal in Georgia’s history.
- The facility is a joint venture by Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solutions.
- The construction site is located in Ellabell, Georgia, near Savannah, and is part of Hyundai’s massive 3,000-acre Metaplant campus.
- South Korea’s President Lee Jae-myung promised at least $50 billion of investments during a recent meeting with President Trump.
- The South Korean government recently agreed to invest hundreds of billions more in the U.S. in exchange for lower tariffs.
- Hyundai also has a factory in Alabama and plans to invest $5 billion in a steel plant in Louisiana.
- Federal agents arrested nearly 500 workers at the facility on Thursday, marking the largest-ever single-site immigration raid in U.S. history.
- Approximately 475 people were detained during the raid, which began around 9:00 or 10:00 a.m. and lasted until as late as 6:30 p.m.
- Some 300 of the 475 arrested workers are Korean nationals who were building the electric vehicle battery plant.
- Detained workers also included individuals from Guatemala, Colombia, Chile, Mexico, Ecuador, and Venezuela.
- Some U.S. citizens, mostly younger workers, were initially subjected to investigatory detention before being released.
- None of the detained workers have been charged, and many reportedly hold valid U.S. work permits or pending asylum applications.
- Over 500 federal, state, and local officials participated in the raid, including agents from ICE, Border Patrol, the Department of Labor’s Office of Inspector General, the FBI, the DEA, ATF, the IRS, and the Georgia State Patrol.
- The majority of detained workers were taken to the Folkston ICE Processing Center, a GEO Group-run facility in south Georgia near the Florida line that has operated as an ICE detention center since approximately 2017.
- DHS Secretary Tom Homan stated that companies hiring undocumented workers undercut competition paying U.S. citizen salaries and drive wages down.
- South Korean officials negotiated the release of detained workers, with Chief of Staff Kang Hun-sik confirming that a chartered plane would depart to repatriate them once administrative procedures were completed.
- The South Korean government will work with its Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy and related companies to review and improve residency status and visa systems for travelers on U.S.-related projects.
- Meredyth Yoon, litigation director for Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta, reported that heavily armed federal agents threatened workers, used tear gas, blocked facility exits, took phones, and pressured people to sign papers.
- Witnesses described hundreds of people being arrested, shackled at their waist and ankles, and loaded into buses.
- No criminal arrests of plant owners or company officials involved in the raid have been reported so far.
- The raid is viewed by advocates as a disincentive to companies wanting to invest in the U.S., running counter to Georgia Governor Kemp’s efforts to attract South Korean economic development.
- Local organizations are providing coordinated support for detained individuals, including food, financial assistance, and legal help.
- President Trump is quietly preparing to travel to South Korea next month (October) for the APEC summit, where he could meet with Chinese leaders.
