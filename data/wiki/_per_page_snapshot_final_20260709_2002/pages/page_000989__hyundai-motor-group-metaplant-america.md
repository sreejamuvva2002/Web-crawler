# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Hyundai Metaplant America reshapes EV and hybrid production with smart factory automation](https://www.automotivemanufacturingsolutions.com/smart-factory/hyundai-reshapes-vehicle-production-at-metaplant-america/2677700)  ·  `page_000989`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group is building a $7.6 billion manufacturing complex in Ellabell, Georgia.
- The facility is named Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America (HMGMA).
- HMGMA is a software-defined factory integrating AI and robotics automation for electric and hybrid vehicle assembly.
- The plant currently produces the Hyundai Ioniq 5 and Ioniq 9 EVs, and the Kia Sportage hybrid.
- At full capacity, the facility targets an annual production of 500,000 EV and hybrid vehicles.
- Production will cover a mix across 10 different models for the Hyundai, Genesis, and Kia brands.
- HMGMA officially opened in March last year.
- Euisun Chung, executive chair of Hyundai Motor Group, stated at the opening that the facility showcases the carmaker's advanced manufacturing capabilities and commitment to innovation.
- The plant uses cutting-edge robotics and vision systems designed to ensure high-quality and flexible vehicle production.
- Robots assist human workers to improve efficiency at the facility.
- Human workforce at HMGMA is projected to number 8,500 by 2031.
- An on-site battery manufacturing facility operates as a joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution called HLBMA (formerly HL-GA Company).
- The HLBMA joint venture represents a $4.3 billion investment in Georgia.
- Located on the HMGMA complex in Ellabell, the facility produces battery cells with an annual capacity of 40 GWh.
- HLBMA currently employs more than 500 people, mostly local hires.
- A spokesperson for HLBMA stated the facility's focus remains on safe, high-quality operations supported by comprehensive training and dedicated on-site emergency response capabilities.
- Hyundai committed to spending $6 billion to localize parts and strengthen logistics.
- The commitment includes investing in US steel production.
- Hyundai will use digital technology to connect and optimize processes from order collection, procurement, logistics, and production.
- Hyundai aims to strengthen its group logistics to ensure robust supply chains in the US.
- The investment comes despite a volatile market for battery electric vehicles (BEVs), notably following the removal of incentives by the Trump administration in September last year.
- HMGMA's flexible assembly line allows Hyundai to accommodate hybrid production with little interruption.
- Hyundai states its core strategy has not changed despite the removal of US EV tax incentives.
- The company is adjusting the mix of EVs, hybrids, and internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles to align with market demand and policy conditions.
- Hyundai maintains its long-term electrification investment outlook remains unaltered.
- HMGMA serves as the innovation flagship for Hyundai Motor Group.
- The plant scaled up advanced manufacturing technologies developed and validated at the Hyundai Motor Group Innovation Center Singapore (HMGICS) for mass production.
- HMGMA integrates AI, robotics, and data technologies into an automated production system connecting all processes from order collection to assembly.
- Employees at the plant are known as 'Meta Pros'.
- Automation and technology at HMGMA are used to boost efficiency, replace repetitive and hazardous tasks, enhance productivity, reduce physical injury, and improve the work environment.
- Automated vehicle assembly applications target operations requiring high precision or that are difficult for humans.
- Jerald Roach, head of department for general assembly at HMGMA, stated that robotics and vision systems lend themselves well to applications requiring vision data to precisely position components.
- Robotics and vision can perform repetitive tasks beyond human physical limitations, allowing HMGMA to integrate a high number of successful technologies in volume production.
- Hyundai is not sharing details of assembly equipment providers at HMGMA, noting many are group affiliates.
- Boston Dynamic’s Atlas humanoid robots are scheduled for deployment in January 2028.
- The Atlas humanoid has transitioned from prototype to production-ready industrial robot and is being built at Boston Dynamic's headquarters.
- At HMGMA, Atlas will initially be used in the external scheduling area for unloading and sorting inbound parts, focusing on packaging boxes with large or heavy components.
- In October last year, Hyundai tested the Atlas robot by having it sort roof racks for the assembly line.
- During testing, an engineer wearing a virtual reality headset took direct control of Atlas to guide its hands and arms until the task was learned, generating data to train the robot's AI models for autonomous operation.
- The Atlas robot features an externally positioned battery that allows it to replace its own battery, supported by fast-charging technology for a factory fleet.
- Atlas' camera technology and flexible head enable it to see its environment, its body parts, and its spatial position to comply with safety priorities when working alongside humans.
- EV battery cells are supplied from the on-site HLBMA facility.
- Battery cells are delivered to Mobis Battery System Assembly, which packages them onto skateboard chassis before moving to the HMGMA assembly area via conveyor.
- A second on-site Mobis facility, Mobis Module, produces the front end module for the vehicle.
- Jerald Roach stated that feeding and marrying battery sub-assemblies at HMGMA is done via a highly automated process ensuring repeatability and precision.
- Automation ensures critical components like front suspension, drive system, rear suspension, and battery subassembly are precisely positioned to the underbody with controlled fastening torque.
- Hyundai maintains common locating schemes for major components so automation can be easily modified to accept new models across ICE, BEV, or hybrid powertrains.
- HMGMA was specifically designed and built to optimize the assembly of BEVs, differing from traditional automotive assembly lines.
- Hyundai emphasizes that marrying a large battery to the underbody must be considered in both factory design and vehicle design upfront.
- Plants designed for ICE vehicles require significant structural modifications or compromises to address fundamental BEV differences.
- Hyundai's modular vehicle design enables the flexibility required to build various powertrain combinations in a single plant.
- Hyundai uses automated material delivery systems, including automated guided vehicles (AGVs) and automated trailer unloading systems, to avoid safety concerns associated with tuggers and forklifts.
- An aggressive kitting strategy distributes sequenced parts to the point of use, increasing flexibility for multiple models and reducing decisions required from Meta Pros.
- The lack of high numbers of parts displayed lineside improves overall visual management of assembly operations.
- AGVs move vehicles along the assembly line on programmed paths guided by QR codes on the factory floor instead of traditional rigid conveyors.
- HMGMA's sustainability measures include renewable energy sourcing, solar generation, energy-efficient facility design, reduced emissions through electrified production, and an advanced wastewater treatment system.
- Water conservation measures include reclaimed water for landscaping and stormwater collection with controlled release.
- Hyundai Motor Group signed a 15-year power purchase agreement with Matrix Renewables in 2024.
- Hyundai purchased 147 MW of generation capacity from the Stillhouse Solar Project in Texas via this agreement.
- Hyundai expects to secure approximately 378 GWh of electricity annually through the agreement.
- The company anticipates an annual reduction of 140,000 tons of carbon emissions across its US facilities.
- Jerald Roach stated that Hyundai remains confident in the future of BEV manufacturing in the US and will continue investing in local production capacity.
- The company views electrification as a long-term growth driver supported by localization, scale, and consumer choice, even as near-term policy dynamics evolve.
