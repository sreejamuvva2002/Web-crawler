# U.S. E-waste and Battery Recycling Industry

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Battery and hard drive waste becomes new battleground](https://cryptorank.io/news/feed/bc1f6-us-old-tech-in-rare-earth-fight-with-china)  ·  `page_001280`

## Facts

- The U.S. government is pouring real money into recycled electronics to cut reliance on China.
- The Pentagon acquired a significant equity stake in MP Materials, the only company currently mining rare earths domestically.
- President Trump imposed a 50% import tariff on global copper supplies, pushing prices to an all-time high.
- The U.S. is leveraging existing e-waste (dead phones, broken EV batteries, servers, laptops) as an alternative to new mining, which takes decades to scale.
- The strategy focuses on recycling metals like neodymium, praseodymium, and terbium from e-waste instead of waiting 30 years for new mines.
- Companies including Glencore, Full Circle Electronics, Cyclic Materials, and startup Illumynt are competing to process e-waste as raw material in the geopolitical competition with China.
- Recycled rare earth metals are critical components for fighter jets, power tools, MRI machines, wind turbines, and other high-end military and industrial technology.
- Kunal Sinha, global head of recycling at Glencore, noted that the recycling industry has only recently gained serious attention.
- Kunal Sinha stated that many industry participants underestimate the massive potential of e-waste recycling.
- Glencore has operated a smelter in Quebec for decades to process old electronics into copper slabs and extract precious metals like gold, silver, palladium, and platinum.
- Recycled electronics now constitute 15% of Glencore's Quebec smelter feedstock.
- Glencore entered a multi-year agreement with Cyclic Materials to supply recycled copper sourced from EV motors, data center components, and wind turbines.
- Cyclic Materials is investing over $20 million to construct a new facility in Mesa, Arizona.
- High demand for recycled materials is driving foreign companies to enter the U.S. recycling market.
- German company Wieland launched a $100 million recycling facility in Kentucky in 2022.
- Aurubis opened an $800 million multi-metal recycling plant in Georgia.
- Aurubis CEO Toralf Haag stated that the Richmond facility aims to retain strategically important metals domestically and reduce reliance on China.
- John Mitchell, president of the Global Electronics Association, noted that most U.S.-imported electronics contain significant amounts of copper, gold, and steel.
- John Mitchell suggested that current tariffs could serve as a catalyst for increased domestic recycling efforts.
- E-waste recycling offers a faster supply chain alternative to new mining, as waste streams are continuous and daily.
- Full Circle Electronics CEO Dave Daily reported a surge in corporate e-waste disposal as companies rush equipment upgrades before prices increase further.
- Dave Daily confirmed a noticeable influx of e-waste entering Full Circle Electronics' warehouses.
- Full Circle Electronics disassembles old electronics into 40 to 50 distinct material types for sale to metal recyclers.
- Lithium-ion batteries are increasingly used as feedstock due to their high content of lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese, and aluminum.
- Companies including Ascend Elements, Redwood Materials, Cirba Solutions, and American Battery Technology are recovering battery metals to supply EV battery manufacturing.
- The viability of these recyclers depends on the Biden-era 45X tax credit, which faces potential elimination under Trump's proposed spending plans.
- Kunal Sinha cautioned against building recycling business models solely around temporary tax credits.
- Startup Illumynt is extracting rare earth metals from decommissioned hard drives sourced from data centers.
- In April, Western Digital partnered with Microsoft, Critical Materials Recycling, and PedalPoint Recycling to recover copper, gold, aluminum, steel, and rare earths from legacy hard drives.
- The IT asset disposition (ITAD) sector is expanding rapidly, with companies like Waste Management, Clean Harbors, Sims Lifecycle Services, and Electronic Recyclers International processing and dismantling devices.
- Global e-waste production reached nearly 62 million metric tons in 2022, representing an 82% increase since 2010.
- The United States generated approximately 8 million metric tons of e-waste in 2022, with only 15–20% being recycled.
- The U.S. e-waste recycling market generated $28.1 billion in revenue last year and is expanding at an annual rate of 8%, according to IBISWorld.
- In April 2025, China imposed export restrictions on rare earth magnets as a retaliatory measure against new U.S. tariffs.
- Ford was forced to halt production at certain factories because of the resulting rare earth magnet shortages.
- In June, China issued several six-month export licenses for rare earth magnets to alleviate supply constraints, though volumes remain below normal levels.
- China maintains a 90% global market share across the entire rare earth supply chain, including mining, refining, and processing.
- The Biden administration previously provided MP Materials with a $45 million grant to support domestic rare earth development.
- In April, the U.S. Department of the Interior approved development of the Colosseum rare earth project located within California's Mojave National Preserve.
- The Colosseum site, owned by Australian firm Dateline Resources, has the potential to become the second operational rare earth mine in the United States.
- Kunal Sinha advised investors to focus on fundamental business metrics rather than industry hype or unproven technologies.
- Glencore invested $327.5 million into Toronto-based battery recycler Li-Cycle.
- Li-Cycle filed for Chapter 15 bankruptcy protection in May following the commencement of construction on a facility in Rochester, New York.
- Glencore is attempting to acquire Li-Cycle's failed project through a $40 million bid.
- Kunal Sinha predicted that current market conditions will spur increased startup formation and investment in the recycling sector, noting Glencore's own active investment strategy.
