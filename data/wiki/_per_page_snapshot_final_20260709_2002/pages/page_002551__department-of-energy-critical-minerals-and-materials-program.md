# Department of Energy Critical Minerals and Materials Program

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [What Are Critical Minerals and Materials? | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/cmm/what-are-critical-minerals-and-materials)  ·  `page_002551`

## Facts

- The Energy Act of 2020 defines a “critical mineral” as any mineral, element, substance, or material designated as critical by the Secretary of the Interior, acting through the director of the U.S. Geological Survey.
- The Energy Act of 2020 defines a “critical material” as any non-fuel mineral, element, substance, or material that the Secretary of Energy determines has a high risk of supply chain disruption and serves an essential function in one or more energy technologies, including technologies that produce, transmit, store, and conserve energy; or a critical mineral as defined by the Secretary of the Interior.
- In 2023, the Department of Energy published a comprehensive assessment of various materials for energy to evaluate potential supply risk and importance to energy technologies.
- DOE’s assessment considers supply risk factors including basic availability, competing technology demand, political, regulatory, and social factors, codependence on other markets, and producer diversity.
- The assessment evaluates importance to energy technologies based on two factors: energy demand and substitutability.
- The methodology incorporates material intensity of energy technologies for both currently widely deployed and innovative technologies that may reduce material requirements in the future.
- Pursuant to Section 7002(a) of the Energy Act of 2020, the Secretary of Energy determines the Critical Materials List.
- The current list includes critical materials from the Final 2023 Critical Materials List and the May 2025 amendment, as well as those on the 2025 final list published by the Secretary of the Interior acting through the director of the U.S. Geological Survey.
- Non-elemental critical minerals designated by the USGS include natural graphite, metallurgical coal, phosphate, potash, and synthetic graphite.
- Non-elemental critical materials designated by the DOE include natural graphite, metallurgical coal, electrical steel, and silicon carbide.
- Critical minerals and materials serve energy applications in batteries, nuclear energy, fuel cells, solar cells, displays & optics, catalysts, semiconductors & microconductors, magnets & motors, steel production & strengthening alloys for aerospace/defense/high performance, lighting, general electronics components, lasers, and atomic clocks & GPS.
- Elements and materials specifically listed for battery applications include aluminum, antimony, cobalt, copper, fluorine, graphite, lanthanum, lead, lithium, manganese, nickel, phosphate, silver, and vanadium.
- The results of the 2023 DOE Critical Materials Assessment are presented in a criticality matrix to evaluate how critical each material is for the future of energy.
