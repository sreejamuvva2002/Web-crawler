# Anovion Technologies

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Why EV battery makers are so hungry for clean energy - E&E News by POLITICO](https://www.eenews.net/articles/why-ev-battery-makers-are-so-hungry-for-clean-energy/)  ·  `page_001507`

## Facts

- Eric Stopka is the president of Anovion Technologies.
- Stopka evaluated 15 states while searching for a battery materials factory site.
- Three of the five most important site selection criteria were power-related.
- The plant required a vast amount of electricity, fast ramp-up capability, and clean power.
- Stopka stated he does not want to be the company dragging down its parent company's net-zero goals.
- Anovion rejected a potential site because its grid was largely fired by fossil fuels and the 10-year plan was outside their comfort range.
- In May, Anovion selected Bainbridge, Georgia (a town of fewer than 15,000 in the southwest corner) as its factory location.
- The Bainbridge site allows the factory to build a turnout on the local train line and ship by barge on the nearby Flint River.
- To staff the facility, Anovion is in talks with the local technical college for 400 employees.
- Georgia Power has promised to deliver more zero-carbon energy over time to support the site.
- Georgia has two large nuclear power plants, and regulators approved a plan last year for Georgia Power to add 2,300 MW of renewables by 2025.
- Anovion's site will require a new substation and transformers before production begins at scale.
- The company plans to build a second large factory and then a third facility in the future.
- Stopka noted that utilities willing to start and move quickly scored higher, emphasizing that breaking ground starts the clock ticking.
- Anovion makes synthetic graphite, one of the most voluminous ingredients of lithium-ion batteries.
- The company received a $117 million grant last fall from the Department of Energy funded by the 2021 bipartisan infrastructure law.
- Domestic synthetic graphite production aims to ease fears that availability could vanish during U.S.-China tensions, as China currently overwhelmingly controls graphite production.
- Anovion's manufacturing technique starts with needle coke, a petroleum waste material described as "the last 15 percent of the barrel."
- The needle coke is poured into metal and ceramic crucibles a meter or two long.
- A monster electric current heats the petroleum to more than 5,400 degrees Fahrenheit for nearly a month.
- The process yields synthetic graphite, a gray substance with the consistency of baking powder ready for EV battery integration.
- Anovion is one of dozens of EV-related companies flocking to the southeastern U.S., where local officials aggressively court them and a cultural aversion to labor unions may trim employment costs.
- Stopka said people do not typically think of southwest Georgia as a clean energy region but were pleasantly surprised by the site's attributes.
- The battery supply chain anticipates pressure from downstream customers, especially automakers, to make products with immaculate environmental standards.
- Europe passed new rules to track the energy footprint of battery materials back to the mine.
- The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission is drafting requirements for public companies to report carbon emissions.
- Venkat Srinivasan is a chemical engineer at Argonne National Laboratory and director of the Argonne Collaborative Center for Energy Storage Science.
- Srinivasan said in 10-20 years they want a different world and need to ensure batteries use clean energy as the linchpin to a clean energy future.
- Battery supply chain executives say electricity cleanliness is top of mind for factory siting.
- Stopka thinks the new battery and vehicle supply chain will transcend any growth seen in the U.S. in the last 150 years.
- The search for zero-carbon electricity acts as an invisible hand guiding where factories get built.
- Li-Bridge, a battery industry consortium, reported that shovel-ready sites for large electricity demands are in short supply and need access to large amounts of clean, reliable energy.
- Big wind and solar energy projects are constrained by a lack of transmission and clogged grid interconnection queues.
- Making 1 kilowatt-hour of battery requires an input of 47 kWh of energy, according to Argonne National Laboratory estimates.
- Seven years ago, Argonne researchers visited Chinese battery plants that used mostly natural gas.
- Ford Motor Co.’s F-150 Lightning has a battery of 131 kWh in its largest format.
- Synthesizing one F-150 Lightning battery would use more than 6 megawatts of energy per Argonne estimates.
- Celina Mikolajczak is a manufacturing veteran and chief battery technology officer at Lyten, a California maker of lithium-sulfur batteries.
- Anovion's synthetic graphite production process highlights the battery industry's extreme energy needs driven by temperature control.
- Downstream battery-making mixes powders and liquid into slurry, which is pasted onto metal sheets.
- Ovens remove water and chemicals from the slurry; almost 47 percent of the energy needed to make a battery cell comes from this drying process per a 2021 Worcester Polytechnic Institute and A123 Systems LLC study.
- Some materials like nickel are irreversibly changed by moisture, requiring entire assembly shops to be enclosed in dry rooms with constant air conditioning.
- The Worcester Polytechnic study said another 29 percent of battery manufacturing energy use is from air conditioners.
- More than three-quarters of a battery's energy use is controlling temperature.
- Heating produces huge energy bills and contributes substantially to the battery's finished cost.
- Srinivasan noted that "temperature is money."
- Automakers are seeking "dry cell" technology to fabricate electrodes without slurry, potentially slashing energy use nearly in half.
- Tesla considers dry cells key to continuing its EV dominance and acquired Maxwell Technologies in 2019 for this technology.
- Tesla cited dry-cell tech as a key innovation for its new 4680 battery cell to reduce the energy and footprint of making cells tenfold.
- Musk tweeted in 2021 that dry electrode is key to lowering lithium battery costs but required immense engineering to reach volume production.
- Tesla did not reply to a query about current plans to deploy dry cells.
- Volkswagen AG announced last month it is scaling up dry-cell technology, which could save hundreds of millions of euros a year.
- Battery makers in the U.S. do not have to use renewables or carbon-free power but feel urgency due to climate goals and business reasons.
- Gene Berdichevsky is co-founder and CEO of Sila Nanotechnologies, which is building a battery materials factory in Washington state.
- Berdichevsky said limits will be set on the CO2 footprint per kilowatt-hour of battery production over time.
- Carmakers needing to stay below emission caps will pay more for products that help them stay under limits.
- Sila's principal investor and customer is Mercedes-Benz AG.
- Recent EU regulations vow to rigorously track the carbon footprint of batteries used in Europe's EVs.
- Sila builds silicon-based anode powder by cooking materials in electric furnaces and sited its factory on a power grid in eastern Washington with abundant hydropower for a low carbon footprint.
- Mikolajczak said any executive looks at the carbon footprint of their factory because factories run for 30 years and must be cost-effective.
- Energy requirements drive battery startups' conversations with electric utilities and sway where factories get built.
- Aqua Metals Inc. is a battery recycler recovering high-purity critical minerals from spent batteries.
- Steve Cotton is president of Aqua Metals.
- Old batteries are chopped into black mass, immersed in proprietary liquid, and electroplated to deposit metals on a sheet.
- Aqua Metals' pilot plant in Reno, Nev., intends to soak 3,000 metric tons a year of black mass and produce $60 million worth of critical minerals including cobalt, manganese dioxide, lithium hydroxide, nickel, and copper.
- Electroplating uses tens of megawatts of electricity, giving the plant an energy footprint similar to a data center.
- Power for Aqua Metals comes from NV Energy, which promised 40 percent renewable power; the remaining 60 percent will come from renewable energy certificates.
- Cotton said by the end of the decade, net zero requires every electron from a clean source, so the plant will have massive solar (about a megawatt across roofs and parking canopies on a 5-acre campus).
- Amprius Technologies Inc. is a California battery cell maker that announced a new 1.3 million-square-foot factory outside Denver in March, located in a former Costco distribution warehouse.
- Amprius CEO Kang Sun said Denver's low humidity will lower energy bills and with 300 days of sunlight, they can take advantage of solar and wind energy.
