# Ascend Elements

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Kentucky’s EV Ecosystem Begins to Take Shape – Site Selection Magazine](https://siteselection.com/kentuckys-ev-ecosystem-begins-to-take-shape/)  ·  `page_052524`

## Facts

- Ascend Elements is a Massachusetts-based company with a billion-dollar plan for battery recycling using black mass (recyclable material from gigafactory scrap and end-of-life lithium-ion batteries).
- The company broke ground for a greenfield site in October 2022 in Christian County, Southwestern Kentucky.
- Roger Lin serves as vice president of global marketing and government relations for Ascend Elements and sits on the board of directors at the U.S. Energy Storage Association.
- Lin estimates that 6% to 10% of a lithium-ion battery factory's output is scrap.
- Any battery factory that creates scrap or requires cathode active materials (CAM) is considered a potential customer for Ascend Elements.
- The company worked with 50 different sites throughout the Southeast during its site selection process.
- A key requirement was proximity to Ascend Elements' "Base 1" facility in Covington, Georgia.
- Base 1 recently began operations and shreds 30,000 tons of batteries a year to create black mass.
- Site selection criteria included clear height, power, zoning, and access to labor.
- The company preferred a greenfield site because its factory model is unique in the country.
- The new Hopkinsville facility is dubbed "Apex 1".
- Community factors influencing the site selection included technical requirements, timing for availability, workforce access, and the welcoming attitude of local residents.
- Assistance during site selection came from the office of Gov. Andy Beshear, the Kentucky Cabinet for Economic Development, the Hopkinsville mayor's office, Christian County officials, and the South Western Kentucky Economic Development Council.
- In early February 2023, 12 county economic development organizations in Western Kentucky formed "Kentucky Cornerstone" to market the region near EV battery production centers in Kentucky and Tennessee.
- The Kentucky Cornerstone initiative started in late 2021 after Ford projects were announced.
- Partnered communities are located along the I-69 and I-24 corridors connecting BlueOval City in Tennessee and BlueOval SK Battery Park in Kentucky.
- Apex 1 is located on over 140 acres in Commerce Industrial Park II.
- The facility spans 500,000 square feet and is scheduled to begin operations in late 2023.
- With potential expansion phases, the facility will employ up to 400 people in roles including engineers, chemists, warehouse associates, and manufacturing operators.
- Michael O’Kronley serves as CEO of Ascend Elements.
- The Hopkinsville facility will produce sustainable active battery material (both CAM and engineered precursor/pCAM) for approximately 250,000 EVs per year.
- Ascend Elements operates facilities in Westborough, Massachusetts; Michigan; Georgia; and Kentucky.
- The company's intellectual property was developed more than a decade ago near Worcester Polytechnic Institute in Massachusetts.
- An IDTechEx analysis released in February 2023 projects that current plans for new cell production capacity will reach 3 TWh by 2030.
- U.S. battery manufacturing capacity is expected to grow to 18% of global capacity by 2027, up from approximately 7% in 2021.
- Plants within a close travel radius of the Hopkinsville site include SK On (Kentucky and Georgia), Freyr (Georgia), Envision AESC (Kentucky), LG partnered with GM (Tennessee), and Rivian (whose planned $5 billion plant is near Ascend Elements' Georgia facility).
- A division of SK was among several investors that helped Ascend Elements raise $300 million in equity and debt financing.
- As of November 2022, approximately 44 companies in Canada and the United States and 47 companies in Europe recycled or planned to recycle lithium batteries.
- Since 2020, Kentucky has seen roughly $10 billion in EV-related investments and more than 9,000 full-time jobs announced by sector companies.
- Martinrea Hopkinsville LLC, a Canada-based Tier 1 automotive supplier, recently announced a $31 million, 33-job expansion in Christian County for lightweight structural steel products for ICE and BEV vehicles.
- Ford and SK On updated their $5.8 billion, 1,500-acre BlueOval SK Battery Park project in Glendale, Kentucky.
- BlueOval SK Battery Park is slated to start production in 2025 and will create 5,000 new jobs in Kentucky.
- Two battery manufacturing facilities at the site will collectively produce more than 80 gigawatt-hours (GWh) annually.
- Ford targets an annual run rate of 2 million electric vehicles globally by the end of 2026.
- Jee Dong-seob serves as president and CEO of SK On.
- Kentucky Gov. Andy Beshear called BlueOval SK the largest economic development project in state history and part of Ford's biggest investment ever.
- Lisa Drake serves as vice president of Ford EV Industrialization.
- The complex will train 5,000 new workers at the Elizabethtown Community and Technical College (ECTC) BlueOval SK Training Center located on site.
- The training center spans 42,000 square feet, is the only co-branded learning facility in the Kentucky Community College System, and represents a $25 million investment by the Commonwealth of Kentucky.
- Lisa Slaven serves as Hardin County Schools Transition Readiness Director.
- Japan-based Envision AESC announced a $2 billion investment in April 2022 to build a gigafactory in the Kentucky Transpark in Bowling Green, Warren County.
- The 30-GWh plant will create 2,000 skilled jobs and produce battery cells and modules for multiple global automotive manufacturers.
- Jeff Deaton serves as Envision AESC U.S. Managing Director.
- Todd Alcott is the mayor of Bowling Green.
- New-generation battery cells produced at the facility will have 30% more energy density, reduced charging time, increased range and efficiency, and power up to 300,000 vehicles annually by 2027.
- Envision AESC will receive up to $116.8 million from state incentive programs and up to $5 million in grant-in-aid for skills training.
- The gigafactory will be powered by 100% renewable energy from onsite generation and purchased locally from the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA).
- Adam Murray serves as a target market specialist with TVA Economic Development.
- TVA's seven-state territory has welcomed $17.2 billion in EV-related capital investment creating 17,500 jobs.
- LOTTE Chemical announced a joint venture with LOTTE Aluminium in August 2022 to build a facility in Elizabethtown producing 36,000 tons of cathode foil annually.
- Cathode foil is a core material for EV batteries, and demand is anticipated to increase by an average of 32% annually by 2030.
- The Hardin County operation sits on 40 acres in the T.J. Patterson Industrial Park and will be the company's first U.S. aluminum foil facility, expected to begin operation in 2025.
- The site received $500,000 in state funding in 2020 through Kentucky's pilot Product Development Initiative for infrastructure improvements.
- JV partners are subsidiaries of South Korea's LOTTE Group.
- South Korea-based Advanced Nano Products (ANP) announced a $49.6 million investment creating 93 jobs in Hardin County to supply battery producers including BlueOval SK Battery Park.
- Dr. Changwoo Park serves as CEO of ANP.
- Firestone Airide, a Bridgestone Americas subsidiary, moved forward with a $50 million, 250-job expansion at its Williamsburg plant in Whitley County for automotive air springs supporting the EV market.
- In January 2022, Gov. Beshear announced Piston Automotive LLC would increase its Jefferson County expansion scope to create 117 full-time jobs with a $26.3 million-plus investment.
- Piston is a subsidiary of Michigan-based Piston Group LLC, led by former NBA player Vinnie Johnson.
- Toyota Motor Manufacturing Kentucky in Georgetown announced additional investment and jobs to support EV production.
- Rebecca Goodman serves as Kentucky Energy and Environment Cabinet Secretary.
- Kentucky received federal approval in September 2022 to develop a nearly $70 million electric vehicle charging network.
- Kentucky's Electric Vehicle Infrastructure Deployment Plan was developed by the Kentucky Transportation Cabinet (KYTC), Energy and Environment Cabinet (EEC), Public Service Commission, and Federal Highway Administration.
- The approval secured federal NEVI formula program funds, bringing total available funding to $86.9 million over five years with matching funds.
- Initial NEVI funding will build direct current fast-charging (DCFC) stations capable of fully charging a battery in 30 minutes or less at interchanges and parkways.
- In July 2022, the FHWA approved Kentucky's plan for Alternative Fuel Corridors (AFCs).
- All of Kentucky's 11 Interstates and eight parkways are designated as EV AFCs.
- Fast-charging stations must be located no more than 50 miles apart along AFCs, within one mile of the corridor, have four ports at 150-kilowatts each (600-kilowatt total), and cannot be proprietary.
- The goal is to have a statewide EV charger network by 2025.
- Jim Gray serves as KYTC Secretary.
