# Ascend Elements

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [One of the biggest battery recycling plants in the US… | Canary Media](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/recycling-renewables/the-biggest-ev-battery-recycling-plant-in-the-us-is-open-for-business)  ·  `page_001470`

## Facts

- Ascend Elements is a Massachusetts-based recycling startup.
- Ascend Elements opened a recycling plant in Covington, Georgia in late March.
- The company says its Covington facility is the largest electric-vehicle battery recycling facility in North America.
- The Covington plant can process 30,000 metric tons of input each year.
- The plant breaks down old batteries and preps the most valuable materials inside to be processed and turned into new batteries.
- This processing capacity equates to breaking down the battery packs from 70,000 electric vehicles annually, according to Ascend CEO Mike O’Kronley.
- Redwood Materials told Canary Media its Nevada facility is already processing 40,000 metric tons of input annually.
- The Redwood Nevada facility capacity is equivalent to around 100,000 battery packs.
- The Covington plant opening is an early example of a nationwide movement to cost-effectively recycle and repurpose EV batteries as more drivers go electric.
- In previous decades, companies had not invested much in lithium-ion recycling.
- Investment in lithium-ion recycling soared in the last few years to match spiking demand for battery materials.
- Recycling can deliver new battery materials without the expense and environmental impact of new mining.
- It is extremely hard to develop new mines in the U.S.
- The federal government is lavishing funds on new battery recycling plants.
- The revamped EV tax credits call for increasing shares of domestically sourced batteries and battery materials.
- Market and policy shifts made recycling sufficiently desirable that Ascend is paying other companies for their old batteries.
- At the moment, those deals are mostly with EV or battery makers that have high volumes to get rid of.
- Ascend CEO Mike O’Kronley stated that paying for spent batteries keeps them from going into the landfill and is better than throwing them away.
- Ascend also accepts used consumer electronics from battery-collection programs, such as Call2Recycle.
- Currently, 80 to 90 percent of what goes into Ascend’s Covington facility is scrap materials from battery factories.
- This includes scrap materials from SK Battery America’s plant in Commerce, Georgia.
- That relationship influenced Ascend’s choice of location, as Covington sits in the emerging “Battery Belt.”
- The Battery Belt is a swath of new battery factories and electric-vehicle plants opening up across the Midwest and the Carolinas, Georgia, Tennessee and Kentucky.
- Fellow battery-recycling startup Redwood Materials also chose South Carolina for a forthcoming $3.5 billion recycling facility.
- Ascend CEO Mike O’Kronley stated there will need to be a recycling plant within about an hour’s drive of every single new battery gigafactory because EV batteries are classified as Class 9 hazardous materials.
- Nearby battery factories send their scrap down the road to Ascend for “pre-processing.”
- The scrap and used batteries go through mechanical shredding and sieving, which produces “black mass.”
- Ascend extracts lithium carbonate from the black mass.
- The remaining mass contains materials such as graphite, nickel, cobalt and manganese.
- Currently, Ascend sells most of these substances to the market.
- Ascend also converts some black mass into cathode precursor and cathode active materials at its Massachusetts R&D center.
- The company is building a second commercial-scale facility in Hopkinsville, Kentucky.
- The Kentucky facility will take outputs from Covington and convert them into cathode precursor and cathode active material so they are ready for new battery manufacturing.
- That $1 billion plant received $480 million in grant funding from the Department of Energy as part of the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law’s investment in domestic supply for critical materials.
- The Covington plant operates similarly to existing battery recycling plants.
- The Kentucky location will introduce a brand-new technique for efficiently extracting cathode materials from black mass, which Ascend has dubbed “hydro to cathode.”
- Ascend CEO Mike O’Kronley stated those two facilities represent the investment being made in key infrastructure to recover batteries, retain critical elements in the United States, and return them into the supply.
- The surging popularity of EVs will produce far more worn-out batteries than the country’s recyclers can process.
- Ascend is already out raising money to build more plants, according to O’Kronley.
- Redwood Materials was founded by Tesla co-founder JB Straubel.
- Redwood Materials won a $2 billion conditional loan from the DOE for a Nevada plant to make new batteries from recycled materials.
- Canada-based Li-Cycle received a $375 million conditional DOE loan for its own facility to process lithium carbonate from a network of recycling plants.
- Cirba Solutions is expanding a battery-recycling plant in Ohio.
- All of these facilities tie into the Biden administration’s goal to make the U.S. more capable of supplying itself with batteries pivotal to electrifying transportation and decarbonizing the grid.
