# High-Pressure Hydrogen Tanks

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [High-Pressure Hydrogen Tank Testing | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/fuels/high-pressure-hydrogen-tank-testing)  ·  `page_029003`

## Facts

- High-pressure tanks operating at 3,600 psi have been used safely in compressed natural gas vehicles (NGVs) for many years.
- Improved high-strength composite tanks now store hydrogen at higher pressures of 5,000 and 10,000 psi to achieve greater driving range in hydrogen-fueled vehicles.
- High-pressure hydrogen tanks are designed not to rupture and must meet rigorous performance requirements through extensive testing.
- Standards compliance for 25 MPa (3.6 ksi) storage pressure follows NGV2-2000 (modified) and DOT FMVSS 304 (modified).
- Standards compliance for 35 MPa (5 ksi) storage pressure includes E.I.H.P./Rev 12B ISO 15869, EU 97/23/EG, NGV2-2000 (modified), DOT FMVSS 304 (modified), and Reijikijyun Betten 9.
- Standards compliance for 70 MPa (10 ksi) storage pressure includes E.I.H.P./Rev 12B ISO 15869, EU 97/23/EG, DOT FMVSS 304 (modified), and Betten 9 (modified).
- During testing, tanks are subjected to more than twice the maximum pressure they experience under normal service conditions to ensure they do not fail.
- At the end of their life, tanks are tested to approximately twice their working pressure according to SAE Journal 2579.
- Hydrogen filling stations utilize numerous redundant overpressure protection systems to prevent vehicle fuel system over-pressurization.
- Millions of high-pressure composite tanks are estimated to be in use worldwide across commercial and industrial applications with an excellent overall safety record.
- Tanks undergo cycling tests where they are pressurized and depressurized far more times than during a vehicle's lifetime.
- Advanced carbon-composite tanks have been cycled more than 500,000 times to maximum operating pressure without leaking.
- A typical vehicle tank filled once a week for 20 years undergoes slightly more than 1,000 cycles.
- Tanks are exposed to pressures above normal to simulate fault management and are dropped 6 feet when empty, shot with a rifle, burned, and exposed to acids, salts, and other road hazards.
- In the unlikely event of a leak, an advanced composite tank can be removed from service without incident and is highly unlikely to fail in a way that endangers vehicle occupants.
- These tanks have remained intact during collisions and vehicle fires, passing various pressure tests after such events.
- A tank's pressure relief device activates when temperatures exceed a set point of typically 102°C (~216°F), safely releasing hydrogen gas.
- This safety procedure is validated through performance tests conducted per the NGV2-2000 standard.
- Required manufacturing quality pressure tests include hydrostatic burst, drop test, extreme and ambient temperature pressure cycles, permeation, hydrogen cycle, chemical exposure, softening temperature, bonfire, tensile properties, gunfire penetration, resin shear, flaw tolerance, hydrogen-compatible material testing, accelerated stress, and numerous internal tests.
- Additional standards SAE J2579 and ISO 15869 are being developed and validated to further improve safety standards for high-pressure hydrogen tanks.
- Domestic and foreign vehicle manufacturers are collaborating with tank manufacturers and others to develop robust test procedures ensuring tank and vehicle safety.
- Key organizations providing hydrogen codes and standards include the American National Standards Institute/Canadian Standards Association (NGV2-2000), European Integrated Hydrogen Project (Rev 12B), Society of Automotive Engineers (J2579), and H2 Tools.
