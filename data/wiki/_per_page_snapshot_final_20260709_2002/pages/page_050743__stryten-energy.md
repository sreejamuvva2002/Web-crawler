## Facts

- U.S.-based Stryten Energy announced the expansion of its battery production capacity to 24GWh across the U.S.
- The strategic layout covers key areas such as military and grid energy storage.
- Stryten Energy intends to comprehensively upgrade the production capacity of its 11 battery assembly plants located in Arkansas, Georgia and other places.
- The company will lay out multi-technology routes including lead-acid, lithium-ion, and all-vanadium liquid current batteries.
- The technology routes aim to satisfy the differentiated needs of the military in terms of high-reliability and long-duration energy storage on the grid.
- Stryten Energy is investing in recycled plastic technology for used batteries.
- The company will use recycled materials for new battery production.
- Stryten Energy's new lithium battery assembly plant in Georgia has recently gone into production.
- The new Georgia plant utilizes patented lithium module technology.
- The new Georgia plant focuses on the military and industrial battery market.
- Stryten Energy's capacity expansion plans align with U.S. government policy supporting local supply chains and clean energy.
- Ravi Manghani, Senior Director of Strategic Sourcing at Anza Renewables, stated that local sourcing is still the least risky option for the U.S. energy storage industry at this stage.
- There has been an observed acceleration in local energy storage manufacturing.
- Companies that have traditionally focused on the electric vehicle market are beginning to shift to energy storage as the growth rate of electric vehicles has fallen short of expectations.
- Battery manufacturers are aggressively expanding their customer base due to growing demand for energy storage and favorable market conditions.
- This expansion is directly contributing to planning announcements for more local manufacturing facilities.
- Stryten Energy is using technology partnerships and vertical integration to gradually eliminate its dependence on key materials.
- The company's strategy aims to support the autonomous development of the U.S. energy storage industry.
