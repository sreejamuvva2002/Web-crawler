# SK Innovation

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [The Georgia GOP Is Begging Biden To Act On Battery Plant, While Dems Stay Silent | HuffPost Latest News](https://www.huffpost.com/entry/georgia-skinnovation_n_605a0947c5b6cebf58d2123a)  ·  `page_050003`

## Facts

- President Joe Biden campaigned on electric vehicles and green jobs, and Georgia voters elected him to the White House while handing the state’s two Senate seats and Congress control to Democrats.
- A U.S. International Trade Commission ruling threatens to shut down a giant electric vehicle battery factory set to employ upward of 2,600 workers in the suburbs north of Athens, Georgia.
- Georgia’s Republican lawmakers have emerged as the project’s most vocal defenders.
- President Biden has just 19 days left to veto trade authorities’ ruling.
- The situation is enormously important for jobs in Georgia and the administration’s plans to electrify the nation’s automobiles over the next decade.
- Last month, the ITC ruled in favor of South Korean battery giant LG Chem, finding rival SK Innovation guilty in a trade secrets case.
- The ruling bans SK Innovation from importing what it needs to run its planned Georgia factory for the next decade.
- There has been no sign from the White House that it will block the decision.
- SK Innovation has hired consultants to draft a plan to shut down its $2.6 billion plant in Georgia.
- Peach State officials are scrambling to save one of the largest economic development projects in the state’s history.
- Pat Wilson, the GOP commissioner of Georgia’s Department of Economic Development, said: “There’s nothing partisan about the jobs of the future.”
- Georgia’s Republican leaders have been very public in their call for Biden to act to save the Commerce, Georgia, plant.
- Democrats have been largely silent on the issue; none of the six Democrats representing Georgia in the House of Representatives or the Georgia Democratic Party responded to requests for comment.
- Five emails to Democratic Sen. Jon Ossoff’s office went unanswered, and former gubernatorial candidate Stacey Abrams declined an interview request.
- Sen. Raphael Warnock (D-Ga.) declined an interview but a spokesman provided a link to a March 4 confirmation hearing video where Warnock pressed Polly Trottenberg, Biden’s pick for the No. 2 job at the Department of Transportation, on whether the agency would provide President Biden an analysis of the ITC ruling’s effect on U.S. battery supplies.
- During the hearing, Warnock said: “It’s not a good position for U.S. automakers if they’re only relying on almost one cell supplier.”
- Warnock stated during the hearing (which took place a month after the ruling): “An adverse ruling by the International Trade Commission threatens seriously the future of that project, whether it’ll happen at all,” and noted it would be “one of the largest economic investments in Georgia’s history and a severe punch in the gut, if you will, for folks who are counting on those jobs, not to mention President Biden’s own goals.”
- Warnock faces reelection in two years amid mounting pressure from the right.
- Conservative Atlanta-based pundit Erick Erickson called the ITC’s decision “a bad day for all of us here in Georgia” and compared the potential job loss to construction workers laid off after Biden blocked the Keystone XL oil pipeline.
- Erickson wrote that Biden should want to salvage the project because Georgia is a state where Warnock and Ossoff were elected, warning that if they lack political clout to reverse the decision, it will contribute to the loss of over 2,000 jobs in Georgia ahead of the 2022 elections.
- Carol Browner, former Clinton-era EPA administrator, served as an expert witness for SK Innovation and testified before the ITC that eliminating its Georgia plant put the U.S. electric vehicle supply chain at risk.
- Former Obama-era deputy attorney general Sally Yates told The New York Times’ Dealbook that the case could “seriously undermine critical progress on the climate crisis” and risked “killing jobs and setting back the Georgia economy.”
- Civil rights leader and former UN ambassador Andrew Young warned in an opinion piece that the ITC ruling “imperils thousands of direct and indirect Georgia jobs and postpones the future of the new era auto industry” over what he called a technicality.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp (R) repeatedly urged Biden to “support thousands of hardworking Georgians and their communities.”
- On March 12, Kemp sent a letter to Biden asking him to “exercise the authority granted to you under law to disapprove the ITC ruling on grounds that it is contrary to the public interest and will seriously jeopardize your administration’s environmental and economic goals.”
- Kemp wrote: “Simply put: the livelihoods of thousands of Georgians are now in your hands,” and added, “This critical opportunity requires us to work together for the good of my state and our country.”
- GOP Rep. Buddy Carter hoped Biden was helping broker an agreement between LG Chem and SK Innovation, stating: “We don’t want to lose those jobs in Georgia and we don’t want to lose those jobs in the electric vehicle industry,” and expressed hope that the president and his staff are engaged in trying to broker a deal.
- White House U.S. Trade Representative spokesman Adam Hodge stated the administration is “currently reviewing the policy implications of the ITC determination” and will complete the review within the 60-day deadline outlined by federal law.
- The dispute started in 2019 when LG’s battery subsidiary, LG Energy Solutions, lodged a complaint with the ITC.
- The underlying dispute began in 2017 when SK Innovation poached engineers in Seoul, prompting LG to file lawsuits accusing them of violating noncompete contracts.
- Around two years later, as SK Innovation announced plans to open its massive Georgia factory to supply Ford Motor Company and Volkswagen with batteries, LG turned to the ITC.
- LG filed a separate lawsuit in federal courts in Delaware for monetary damages for alleged intellectual property theft, which is pending the conclusion of the ITC case.
- The ITC’s guilty verdicts typically carry trade bans that restrict what and whether a convicted defendant can import.
- SK Innovation denied stealing trade secrets but argued that South Korean laws do not require companies to retain the same documents as U.S. statutes.
- On Feb. 10, the ITC found SK Innovation guilty of destroying documents allegedly detailing what it learned from its LG hires.
- The commission ruled unanimously to ban SK Innovation from importing raw materials for the next 10 years, which is the period LG said it would take for a competitor to develop batteries independently.
- The ITC granted a carve-out allowing SK Innovation to build batteries for the next four years for Ford’s electric F-150 pickup truck and two years for Volkswagen’s Crossover series.
- The commission determined this timeframe should give automakers enough time to find alternative sources or for SK Innovation to reach a settlement with LG.
- Tesla is currently the largest battery maker in the U.S., but its Nevada Gigafactory only produces packs for its own vehicles.
- LG operates several projects across the Midwest that, when completed, will produce roughly 30 gigawatt-hours per year and provide batteries to General Motors and other automakers.
- SK Innovation’s Georgia plant would add another 22 gigawatt-hours of annual capacity.
- The rest of the battery market is made up of much smaller companies.
- LG sent a letter on March 10 to Warnock vowing to “do whatever we can to help the people and workers of Georgia,” hinting it was prepared to make investments in the state to offset the closure of SK Innovation’s factory, according to The Atlanta Journal-Constitution.
- David Callahan, a lawyer for LG, stated the company is preparing to invest $4.5 billion in the U.S., resulting in at least two more factories, one of which could be in Georgia.
- SK Innovation stated that LG is “using the ITC to try to muscle SK out of the US EV battery market, period.”
- A SK Innovation spokesperson said: “SK has always been open to good-faith settlement talks with LG,” but noted that “LG continues to be inflexible and unreasonable in its demands. It’s clear that LG doesn’t want competition.”
- James Frith, an analyst at BloombergNEF, stated that allowing LG to dominate the battery market would pose significant risks to producing electric vehicles at the speed and affordability the Biden administration wants.
- Frith noted that competition within the battery industry has been a key factor in driving down cell and pack prices over the past decade.
- Frith warned: “It’s not a good position for U.S. automakers if they’re only relying on almost one cell supplier, because that cell supplier could set prices as it liked.”
- Other manufacturers that could fill SK Innovation’s void include Japan’s Panasonic Corporation, South Korea’s Samsung, and China’s CATL or BYD.
- Callahan denied that LG was seeking a monopoly and accused SK Innovation of bluffing about its willingness to shutter the plant to avoid a costly settlement.
- Callahan said: “If SK Innovation intends to be in this hugely growing market in the long haul, which they have said they are, it would be suicide to leave Ford and Volkswagen in the lurch,” and added that threatening to walk away from the plant is an idle threat.
- Callahan remarked: “They’re threatening Georgia. If I were Georgia, I would be mad. I understand for political reasons not everyone is going to acknowledge that.”
- SK Innovation already started hiring hundreds of workers, and it could take years for another company to replace the plant or increase supply to levels needed to electrify the U.S. auto fleet.
- Wilson stated: “We need batteries in order to keep up with the demand, this was not only an investment into Georgia but an investment into electric vehicles in the United States and a diversification of the supply chain outside of Asia,” and added, “We saw this as a win-win for the state and the country.”
