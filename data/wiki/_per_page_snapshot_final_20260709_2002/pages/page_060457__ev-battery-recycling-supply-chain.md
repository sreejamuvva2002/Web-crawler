# EV Battery Recycling Supply Chain

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [India's EV Transition: Addressing Battery Recycling Supply Chain Risks](https://www.downtoearth.org.in/energy/as-ev-transition-takes-shape-in-india-it-is-time-to-derisk-battery-recycling-supply-chains)  ·  `page_060457`

## Facts

- Auto OEMs need an undisrupted flow of material and components to their assembly lines to meet growing automobile demand.
- Geopolitical factors pose risks to automotive supply chains, ranging from the Russia-Ukraine war to strikes in West Bengal or riots in South Africa.
- The EV industry faces several battery recycling supply chain risks classified into three main themes: demand planning, geopolitical risk, and underutilised capacity.
- By 2030, roughly 1.2 million end-of-life EV batteries will need to be recycled per year, with the number potentially rising to 14 million per year by 2040, according to the International Council on Clean Transportation.
- NITI Aayog’s 2022 report estimated the cumulative potential of lithium-ion batteries in India during 2022-30 will be around 600 GWh across all segments in the base case.
- Of that potential, 128 GWh will be available for recycling by 2030, with 46 per cent (59 GWh) estimated to come from EVs alone.
- Planning for this demand without locking in capital in underutilised assets is identified as the biggest challenge.
- Recent supply chain disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic and Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, combined with rapidly growing demand in the Global South, have dramatically increased material and energy costs.
- The average price of lithium was nearly four times higher in 2022 than in 2019, while cobalt and nickel prices doubled.
- Battery metal price hikes in early 2022 led to battery prices increasing nearly 10 per cent globally relative to 2021 after years of continuous decline.
- During 2019-20, the supply chain became a bottleneck due to COVID-19, prompting private and public entities to focus on local manufacturing and self-reliance.
- Cell and battery manufacturers currently struggle to ensure steady daily raw material supply through legally binding contracts due to highly uncertain geopolitical factors.
- A growing trend encourages global integration of localised supply chains, which may enable Just In Time (JIT) inventories in the future, though current evidence is limited to a vast diaspora of investors in the lithium supply chain.
- More than 200 businesses globally now have a combined capacity to recycle more than 1 million metric tonnes of end-of-life batteries per year, according to Circular Energy Storage (CES).
- Automotive OEMs are building their own EV battery recycling facilities because most materials in EV batteries are expensive but recyclable.
- Mercedes-Benz inaugurated its battery recycling facility in Germany in October 2024.
- Most existing recycling facilities represent locked-up capital that is far from a profitable rate of utilisation.
- Hans Eric Melin, founder and managing director of CES, stated: “On a global basis, we have much more capacity than we need,” as quoted in c&en.
- Capacity planning remains a significant bottleneck due to uncertainty in building robust supply chains.
- At least 2GWh of recycling capacity has come up in India, with 58.88 GWh of waste EV battery volumes expected by 2030.
- Current input volumes do not match the scale of CAPEX invested in recycling infrastructure.
- Currently, around half of recycling plants’ feedstock material consists of production scrap from battery factories, including electrode offcuts and faulty cells.
- Over the next decade, end-of-life batteries will come to dominate feedstock as battery production becomes more efficient and EOL battery availability grows.
- Underutilisation of capacity is expected to occur in India similar to China, with many Indian recyclers currently operating with underutilised capacities.
