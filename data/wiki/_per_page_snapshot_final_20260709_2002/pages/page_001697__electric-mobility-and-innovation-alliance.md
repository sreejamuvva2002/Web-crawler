# Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2021-07-23

**Source:** [Kemp launches effort to boost Georgia’s electric mobility industry |](https://capitol-beat.org/2021/07/kemp-launches-effort-to-boost-georgias-electric-mobility-industry/)  ·  `page_001697`

## Facts

- Kia Motors America operates an assembly plant in Troup County producing 340,000 vehicles a year.
- Kia recently dropped “Motors” from its name and is now known simply as “Kia America.”
- The Korean automaker has a $25 billion plan to transition to electric vehicles soon.
- Gov. Brian Kemp announced a new statewide initiative on July 20 aimed at strengthening Georgia’s status as a leader in the electric mobility industry.
- The Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance (EMIA) will bring industry executives together with state leaders in government, education, utilities and nonprofits to look for ways to build up an industry that already has enjoyed some major successes.
- Gov. Brian Kemp stated: “Georgia has a proven track record of investing early in the resources and infrastructure needed to connect it to the world and develop jobs of the future.”
- Gov. Brian Kemp stated: “The Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance will ensure that our state is positioned to continue leading the nation in the rapidly growing electric mobility industry.”
- Georgia already boasts two SK Innovation electric vehicle battery manufacturing facilities in Jackson County.
- SK Innovation's investment in the Jackson County facilities is nearly $2.6 billion.
- Dutch electric-vehicle charging company Heliox decided to base its North American headquarters in Atlanta.
- Turkish electric-vehicle parts manufacturer TEKLAS plans to open its first North American plant in Gordon County.
- German-owned GEDIA, a manufacturer of lightweight auto-body parts, plans to open a plant in Whitfield County.
- South Korea-based Duckyang, an electric mobility parts supplier, will build two plants near Braselton.
- Chris Clark is president and CEO of the Georgia Chamber of Commerce.
- Projections show electric vehicles in Georgia will balloon from just 7% of the market in 2025 to 30% by 2030, 58% by 2040 and 96% by 2050.
- Chris Clark stated: “Kia, SK and all these companies have to got to build out these vehicles.”
- Chris Clark stated: “We want Georgia to do it.”
- Pat Wilson is commissioner of the state Department of Economic Development.
- Pat Wilson stated that 55,000 jobs in Georgia are connected in some form to the auto industry.
- Many of those auto industry jobs involve manufacturing vehicles with traditional internal-combustion engines.
- Pat Wilson stated: “We’re probably going to see more change in the next 20 years in the auto industry than we’ve seen in the past 100 years.”
- Pat Wilson stated: “We’re focused on public policy that can help drive that transition.”
- Participants in the EMIA will be divided into five committees.
- The five committees will set objectives in five categories: supply chain, infrastructure, workforce development, innovation and policy.
- The Carl Vinson Institute at the University of Georgia will host committee meetings.
- The auto industry will be represented in the initiative by executives from Kia, Cox Automotive, Porsche, and Mercedes-Benz USA.
- Porsche has its North American headquarters in Hapeville.
- Mercedes-Benz USA is based in Sandy Springs.
- Executives from Georgia Power and the state’s electric membership cooperatives (EMCs) will be involved.
- The public sector will be represented by the state Department of Transportation and the Technical College System of Georgia.
- The Technical College System of Georgia will take part in discussions on workforce development.
- Pat Wilson stated: “We’re looking at this as a true public-private partnership.”
- Outcomes from the initiative could come in the form of legislation for the General Assembly to consider, new policy initiatives or a combination of the two.
- Lawmakers approved a tax credit for purchasers of electric vehicles back in the 1990s.
- The state eliminated the EV tax credit in 2015 as EV sales soared.
- Georgia Rep. Chuck Martin is from R-Alpharetta and was the chief sponsor of the 2015 bill.
- Rep. Chuck Martin stated the 2015 tax credit was mainly generating sales for the Nissan Leaf electric vehicle.
- Rep. Chuck Martin stated: “It wasn’t helping drive innovation.”
- Rep. Chuck Martin stated: “It was just subsidizing a purchase.”
- Chris Clark agreed a tax credit to stimulate sales of EVs is no longer needed.
- Chris Clark expects some form of tax credit proposal could come out of the new initiative that will neither incentivize EV sales or offer the jobs tax credit Georgia traditionally has provided industries.
- Chris Clark stated: “It’s a new industry, a new economy, and our old jobs tax credit is not going to move the needle for these guys.”
- Chris Clark stated: “We’re going to have to listen to the industry.”
- Chris Clark envisions new tax breaks that would incentivize gasoline stations to install charging stations for EVs.
- Chris Clark envisions new tax breaks that would encourage homebuilders to put chargers in new houses.
- Rep. Chuck Martin stated state government needs to be careful not to get ahead of the industry by pushing too aggressively for a transition to all electric vehicles.
- Rep. Chuck Martin stated: “Innovation will get us there.”
- Rep. Chuck Martin stated: “But in some places, there won’t be the electric grid or charging stations for that to happen.”
- Pat Wilson said it’s uncertain what solutions the committees will hit upon.
- Pat Wilson stated: “We want to go into this with a blank slate and really listen to the professionals.”
- The EMIA committees will meet this fall.
- The goal is to make recommendations in time for the 2022 General Assembly session starting in January.
