# Daimler Truck North America

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-03-14

**Source:** [Workers at Daimler Truck North America vote overwhelmingly to authorize strike  - World Socialist Web Site](https://www.wsws.io/en/articles/2024/03/14/dtna-m14.html)  ·  `page_053548`

## Facts

- Workers at Daimler Truck North America voted by 96 percent on March 8 to authorize a strike when the current contract expires on April 26 at 12:01 a.m.
- The strike authorization vote covers workers at six facilities in North Carolina, Georgia, and Tennessee.
- The company employs nearly 7,000 United Auto Workers members at facilities throughout the South.
- Daimler Truck manufactures Freightliner trucks, heavy-duty Western Star trucks, and Thomas Built Buses.
- Daimler Truck is the largest manufacturer of commercial vehicles in the world.
- The firm has over 100,000 employees across 35 countries.
- Daimler Truck was spun off from German auto giant Daimler AG (subsequently renamed the Mercedes-Benz Group) in 2021.
- Mercedes remains the largest shareholder of Daimler Truck.
- In 2023, Daimler Truck made a record €5.5 billion ($6 billion) in earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT), with the figure jumping 38 percent compared to a year earlier.
- The company announced that it expected to exceed earnings forecasts for 2024.
- Daimler Truck raised its dividend by nearly 50 percent, from €1.30 to €1.90 a share.
- The dividend increase translates to approximately a $1.5 billion windfall for the company’s biggest investors.
- Truck assemblers at plants in North Carolina start at just $19.25 an hour.
- Workers cite issues motivating the strike authorization vote including insurance, benefits, cost of living, raises, and health and safety issues.
- Workers were forced to accept massive concessions following the 2008-09 recession that have yet to be restored while inflation continued to erode their standard of living.
- A Freightliner worker in Mount Holly, North Carolina, stated there is a 100 percent chance of striking based on the mood of most UAW workers.
- The strike authorization vote marks the beginning of the first major battle by a section of autoworkers in 2024.
- It is the first major autoworker battle since the UAW’s “stand-up strikes” at the Big Three automakers last year.
- During the Big Three strikes, no more than 30 percent of UAW members were on strike at their largest point.
- Ford Motor Co. made $10.41 billion in profits in 2023, including $1.1 billion in the fourth quarter during the “stand up” strike period.
- General Motors profits rose to $10.1 billion in 2023, an increase of 1.9 percent compared to the previous year.
- Stellantis reported increased profits in 2023 at a total of $20 billion, an 11 percent increase over the previous year.
- UAW President Shawn Fain worked closely with the White House and President Biden during contract negotiations to contain and suppress autoworker strikes.
- Fain and the UAW Executive Board have endorsed President Biden’s reelection campaign.
- The Big Three contracts provided a 25 percent wage increase over the contract period, which is far below the 40 percent or more demanded by autoworkers.
- By the end of the Big Three contracts in 2028, top pay will still be less than what it was in 2007 when adjusted for inflation.
- The COLA formula in the Big Three contracts does not keep pace with real inflation.
- The contracts shortened the “progression” period from eight to three years rather than abolishing tiers as workers demanded.
- Company-paid pensions and retiree health benefits were not restored under the Big Three contracts.
- The UAW bureaucracy shut down limited “stand-up strikes” before workers voted on or saw the contracts.
- Fain falsely claimed that all temporary workers would eventually be rolled over into full-time positions.
- Stellantis carried out mass firings of up to 2,300 temporary workers and eliminated shifts at factories in Toledo and Detroit.
- Company executives at Ford, GM, and Stellantis planned to make up for pay increases by laying off tens of thousands of workers as part of the transition to electric vehicles.
- In October, workers overwhelmingly rejected a Mack Trucks contract presented to them by 73 percent after opposition was organized by the Mack Trucks Workers Rank-and-File Committee.
- The UAW subsequently called a strike over the Mack Trucks deal and presented a virtually identical contract within a few weeks.
- The UAW released a video titled “Not Like It Used to Be” during the run-up to the Daimler strike authorization vote.
- The UAW video attempts to foment anti-Mexican sentiment by pointing to plants Daimler has built in Mexico and stating, “We’re just fighting to keep jobs here, in America.”
- In 2022, the UAW made an unsuccessful effort to convince workers to agree to reopen the current contract, implement changes demanded by the company, and extend the agreement by three years.
- The International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees (IWA-RFC) is building a global movement with member committees among autoworkers at Ford, GM, and Stellantis.
