# SK Battery America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2020-08-19

**Source:** [Calls for ICE to investigate who's building Korean battery plant in Jackson County | FOX 5 Atlanta](https://www.fox5atlanta.com/news/calls-for-ice-to-investigate-whos-building-korean-battery-plant-in-jackson-county)  ·  `page_049965`

## Facts

- The company promised 2,600 jobs once its factory was up and running.
- No one made sure that at least some of the people actually building the plant would be from Georgia.
- Each morning vans drop off Korean construction workers at the SK Battery America plant in Jackson County.
- A FOX 5 I-Team investigation raised questions over who is currently benefiting from a $2.6 billion factory under construction in Jackson County.
- Korean-owned SK Innovation chose Georgia after receiving one of the biggest tax relief packages the state has ever awarded: around $300 million in tax abatements and grants.
- SK Battery America is building what will eventually be a $2.6 billion factory that will employ 2,600 workers.
- Jackson County donated the land for the plant.
- SK Battery America is a subsidiary of SKI.
- When it is finished in 2022, SK Battery America promised 2,600 people will eventually be hired to build batteries for electric cars.
- David Cagle with the Georgia Local Union 72 of plumbers, pipefitters, and HVACR technicians complained that Georgians have gotten the shaft over the SKI battery plant.
- Cagle said when his people tried applying for construction jobs to build the plant itself, they were told nothing was available.
- Cagle stated there are about 500 people out of work right now who could come up and be on this job and make a living, but instead Koreans are making a living.
- David Cagle works for local Union 72.
- He says it has been difficult for both union and non-union pipefitters, plumbers, electricians, or HVAC workers to get jobs at SK Battery.
- On multiple visits to the job site, FOX 5 saw what bothered Cagle: Korean labor was everywhere.
- When asked if SK Battery America needed Koreans to build something specific, Cagle answered no, stating electrical work, ductwork, pipework, pipe fitting, and pipe welding are all generic stuff.
- The only legal way to bring in a foreign construction worker is with a special visa indicating no American can do the job or no American wants the job.
- In May, Customs and Border Protection caught 33 Koreans trying to sneak into the country with fraudulent employment letters to work at a battery factory under construction.
- The fraudulent letters claimed they had a 'specific skill set for specialized wo'.
- In May, CBP announced 33 Koreans were caught at Hartsfield-Jackson International Airport trying to sneak into the country for the sole purpose of working illegally at a construction company and battery plant in Georgia.
- An SK Battery America spokesman confirmed the plant was theirs but pointed out they do not do the construction hiring.
- SK Battery America stated it requires all contractors to ensure their workers at the site meet proper state and federal regulations.
- After becoming aware of the issue in June, SK Battery America reiterated with its contractors that these guidelines must be followed and is cooperating with authorities to provide support in resolving any issues.
- SK Battery America said more than 1,000 Americans have been hired since construction began in 2019.
- Georgia congressman Doug Collins wrote a letter urging ICE to determine whether and to what extent there are foreign workers illegally employed at the battery plant.
- Congressman Doug Collins still supports SKI but wrote a letter asking Immigration and Customs Enforcement to fully investigate complaints raised by Cagle, who lives in Collins' district.
- Collins wrote that his understanding is that CBP determined this was not an isolated incident and that these Korean nationals were part of a larger scheme to illegally bring foreign workers into the United States.
- Customs said the Koreans caught at Hartsfield were using fraudulent employment papers and were immediately sent home.
- Congressman Doug Collins told the FOX 5 I-Team that this is not a matter of whether you are a good company or a bad company, but a matter of following the rules that apply to workers in the country.
- FOX 5 watched the job site and saw many Korean construction workers getting bused to and from work.
- Korean construction workers were seen sharing houses in various neighborhoods surrounding SK Battery.
- Some shared rental homes in a Braselton neighborhood under construction, piling out of vans at the end of the workday still wearing reflective work vests.
- In similar neighborhoods in Pendergrass and Jefferson, local union leaders recorded video of multiple Korean workers living in the same house.
- FOX 5 found five Korean men sharing one house in Braselton.
- When FOX 5 tried to ask them whether they were American or had a visa to work at the plant, none said they spoke English.
- The day after the visit, neighbors say all five men quickly moved out.
- Cagle complained that Georgia gave them a huge break and stated this is not a union-non-union issue but a Georgia issue about what is right for Georgians getting treated right.
- The plant is still under construction.
- The next phase, and another 430,000 square feet of space, is due to start later this year.
