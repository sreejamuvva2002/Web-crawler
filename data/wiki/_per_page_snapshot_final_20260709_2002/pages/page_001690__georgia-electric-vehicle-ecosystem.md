# Georgia Electric Vehicle Ecosystem

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** 2022-07-29

**Source:** [Electric Revolution - Georgia Trend Magazine](https://www.georgiatrend.com/2022/07/29/electric-revolution/)  ·  `page_001690`

## Facts

- Georgia is quickly becoming the hub for a completely new automotive industry.
- A remarkable and deliberate shift occurred in Georgia’s automotive industry over the past five years and accelerated in the last 24 months.
- Since 2020, the state has announced more than 20 EV-related projects, from original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) to suppliers that include lithium-ion battery (LIB) manufacturers and recyclers, green hydrogen producers, providers of EV charging infrastructure solutions, seat manufacturers and engine parts providers.
- All told, these projects will invest more than $13.54 billion in the state and create close to 18,000 jobs.
- Pat Wilson is commissioner of Georgia’s Department of Economic Development.
- Manufacturers are setting ambitious electrification timelines, including Cadillac targeting 100% electric by 2028, Kia introducing a new EV model every year for the next nine years, and Ford launching the electric F-150 Lightning.
- The U.S. supply chain generally did not exist to meet these OEM goals.
- More than 75% of the nation’s lithium-ion battery supply comes from China, along with more than 80% of anodes and more than 55% of cathodes.
- Georgia continually invested in its logistics network (interstates, rail, ports) to create a first-class business location.
- The Port of Savannah is the most efficient port in the U.S., allowing quicker transport of raw materials into the port and products out of plants compared to many other places.
- Georgia also invested in workforce development, especially the Quick Start program started in 1968.
- Workforce availability and training capability are what put businesses over the top; Georgia’s Quick Start program listens to the business community to train Georgians for current and future jobs.
- Hyundai Motor Group announced in May that it will open a $5.54 billion facility dedicated to making EV cars and batteries at the 2,923-acre Bryan County megasite.
- Combined with non-affiliated Hyundai suppliers expected to invest another $1 billion, 8,100 jobs will be created in coastal Georgia.
- In December 2021, EV maker Rivian Inc. revealed plans to invest $5 billion to build a manufacturing facility at the 2,000-acre East Atlanta megasite (Stanton Springs North) developed by the Joint Development Authority (JDA) of Jasper, Morgan, Newton and Walton counties.
- The Rivian project will include technology centers to support company R&D.
- For years, the JDA hoped for a high-tech or pharmaceutical firm to land at its site along the I-20 corridor.
- Jerry Silvio is chair of the JDA.
- The Rivian investment means a lot of jobs, estimated at 7,500 direct and about 8,000 indirect jobs from first- and second-tier suppliers to the campus.
- Economic modeling projections by the state indicate those 15,000-plus jobs will generate more than $1 billion in labor income annually.
- Rivian received nearly $1.5 billion in tax breaks and other incentives including free land and improvements, substantial highway improvements, a Quick Start training center and customized recruitment and training programs.
- Many inducements are contingent on the company creating 80% of promised jobs and investment by the end of 2028 and maintaining them through 2047 or facing claw-back provisions.
- The project sparked controversy over loss of quality of life and environmental issues, but the deal moved forward.
- Local community benefits will begin as early as 2023 via payment in lieu of taxes (PILOT) fees for 25 years, after which the company pays taxes based on real and personal property value.
- Rivian’s first PILOT payment of $1.5 million in 2023 is 18 times the existing taxes on that same 2,000 acres.
- An intergovernmental agreement between the four counties and Social Circle divides the money based on original development investment.
- About 60% of those funds will be redistributed to local school systems.
- Burke County experienced benefits from Plant Vogtle construction despite it being six years behind schedule and costs increasing from $14 billion to $30 billion; Southern Company payments funded a bypass road, new schools, and a fire station.
- Similar results are expected in the four Rivian counties.
- SK Battery America finalized a $1.67 billion deal to build two EV battery production plants in Jackson County in 2019.
- The company invested an additional $940 million in 2020 and began production in January 2022.
- Timothy Jeong is CEO of SK Battery America.
- SK On (headquarters of SK Battery America) is now one of the top five makers of EV batteries in the world.
- Georgia’s location, logistics network, and support from technical colleges/universities made it an appealing location for SK.
- SK Battery America is in early stages of a research project with Georgia Tech to advance all-solid-state batteries for longer driving ranges in more compact batteries.
- Cal Wray is president of Augusta Economic Development Authority.
- German-owned Aurubis is building a $340 million multimetal smelter and recycling plant, drawn by the Augusta region workforce.
- Augusta Technical College (ATC) has roughly 4,000 students and a manufacturing mindset, contributing to the community of manufacturers in Augusta.
- Many metals reclaimed from circuit boards, primarily copper, are used in EV manufacturing.
- The Aurubis project is set to create 125 middle-skill jobs in phase 1, with more expected over time.
- The company broke ground in mid-June and should be operational in the first quarter of 2024.
- Ascend Elements is investing $43 million to open its first commercial-scale battery-recycling plant in Covington, east of Atlanta.
- The project has capacity to process 30,000 metric tons of lithium-ion batteries and scrap annually, equivalent to 70,000 vehicle batteries each year.
- Batteries will be recycled into "black mass" containing lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese and graphite to make new batteries.
- Roger Lin is vice president of global marketing and government relations at Ascend Elements.
- The active materials are the most valuable in the lithium-ion battery supply chain; Ascend's process turns black mass back into active material more efficiently than any other technology currently in operation.
- Materials created by Ascend go directly back into lithium-ion battery gigafactories, closing the loop from grave to cradle.
- Ascend will process battery scrap from SK Battery and may soon find itself with other customers.
- They are talking to all major auto makers and battery manufacturers in the country, especially those in the Southeast around Covington.
- All EV investment requires charging infrastructure.
- Limited public and private charging infrastructure is available statewide.
- In November 2021, Congress passed the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law including $7.5 billion to fund the National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure (NEVI) Formula Program.
- These funds assist states in creating a network of EV charging stations along designated "alternative fuel corridors," particularly along the interstate highway system.
- Each state must submit an EV infrastructure deployment plan before accessing its share of NEVI funds, with state plans due by August 1, 2022.
- Jannine Miller is director of planning for the Georgia Department of Transportation (GDOT) and leader of Georgia’s EV infrastructure deployment plan effort.
- Out of a $5 billion allocation, Georgia was allocated about $135 million.
- Federal regulations call for charging infrastructure every 50 miles, including four high-powered (150 kilowatt) charging stations capable of operating simultaneously, with stations no more than one mile off the access point or interstate.
- Regulations highlight advisability of public-private partnerships.
- Planners are unsure how deployment will work but hypothetically could partner with existing exit infrastructure (e.g., at a restaurant or gas station off I-16 in Dublin) that doesn't meet federal guidelines, bringing in a utility partner to upgrade it using the allocated dollars.
- Issues ranging from cybersecurity to maintenance of charging infrastructure must be worked out.
- Miller’s stakeholder list is 85-deep and they are being consulted as the plan develops; planners expect to know much more five months from now.
- Innovation is critical to the EV ecosystem.
- The Ray in Newnan and the Curiosity Lab in Peachtree Corners are hubs for electrification research.
- The Ray recently released a data-driven analysis of truck movement patterns to assist state DOTs with EV infrastructure plans, using in-cab data from two routes: I-20 from Dallas, Texas, to Atlanta and from Savannah to the Atlanta airport.
- Allie Kelly is executive director of The Ray.
- They need to plan infrastructure for medium and heavy-duty trucks that will be either battery electric or hydrogen fuel cell, including high-powered EV charging stations and hydrogen infrastructure for fueling stations.
- Hydrogen fuel cell trucks run on electricity created by combining hydrogen and oxygen, emitting only warm air and water vapor as zero-emission vehicles.
- With 16 available chargers in its town center, Peachtree Corners has the largest DC fast-charging site for electric vehicles in metro Atlanta.
- In March, the Curiosity Lab received a $495,000 federal grant to build a mobility hub as a testing location for EV infrastructure.
- Brandon Branham is assistant city manager and chief technology officer of Peachtree Corners and director of the Curiosity Lab.
- The lab acts as a facilitator of infrastructure to stay ahead of consumer EV adoption and make Peachtree Corners EV friendly.
- James Chen is vice president of public policy for Rivian.
- He describes a major, once-a-generation shift in transportation technology from evolutionary internal combustion improvements to a revolutionary complete paradigm shift to a completely new power-train structure.
- The real benefit to Georgia is being on the forefront of this shift in terms of jobs, being seen as a technology leader, bringing up the economy of the entire state, and bringing high-tech, cutting-edge jobs for this century and beyond.
- EV Projects in Georgia since 2020 account for more than $13.54 billion in investment and nearly 18,000 jobs.
