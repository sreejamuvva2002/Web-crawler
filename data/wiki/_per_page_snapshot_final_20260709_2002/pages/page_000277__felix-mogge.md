# Felix Mogge

**Type:** person  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Automotive suppliers steer through turbulent waters  | Roland Berger](https://www.rolandberger.com/en/Insights/Publications/Automotive-suppliers-steer-through-turbulent-waters.html)  ·  `page_000277`

## Facts

- The first half of 2023 was better but far from good for the automotive year.
- Vehicle sales were up in many markets, with Europe sales about 20 percent higher in the first quarter than in the same period last year.
- The current market is still nowhere near the size it was back in 2019 due to supply bottlenecks, inflation, and a weak Chinese market.
- The Chinese market presents the biggest challenges for both OEMs and suppliers, with volumes currently falling and the growth curve expected to be significantly flatter upon recovery.
- China is getting closer to the kind of dynamics seen in Western automotive markets.
- Automotive manufacturers focusing on high-margin models have weathered the storm well, and this strategy is expected to continue.
- Lockdowns left manufacturers with a significant demand backlog while supply was limited, but these effects have now normalized to some extent.
- Supply is gradually improving while demand is decreasing mainly due to recent inflation, putting the market back in familiar territory where price competition will rise as volumes stagnate.
- High margins can currently only be realized on vehicles with internal combustion engines, while electric models are still nowhere near as profitable.
- Some car manufacturers have already taken products out of their portfolio, with premium OEMs especially focusing on luxury and exiting the low-margin volume segment.
- All OEMs are currently faced with the challenge of having a complex product portfolio, making it necessary to streamline as more electric models join combustion engine vehicles in coming years.
- The low-margin segment may tend to be where cuts happen, but this strategy is only available to a small number of OEMs.
- Suppliers are still navigating difficult waters, representing not an automotive industry crisis but a supplier crisis.
- EBIT margins were already below five percent in 2022 and are expected to fall by up to one percentage point in 2023.
- Production volumes are still below pre-pandemic levels almost everywhere, resulting in significant overcapacity, reduced plant utilization, and pressure on operational efficiency.
- Discussions around OEMs sharing suppliers' costs have become more challenging than last year, as many compensations were one-time payments that suppliers need to renegotiate.
- Suppliers have often agreed to long-term price increases with their upstream suppliers, compounding the ongoing crisis.
- Smaller suppliers are particularly vulnerable, as getting through the crisis requires a certain size and financial stability.
- Sustainability and transparency in the supply chain add maximum demands on suppliers that can lead to overwhelm.
- Hella and Faurecia have formed the top-10 supplier Forvia through a merger.
- Two-thirds of all acquisitions ultimately do not create significantly more value, making partnerships or collaborations potentially easier and more promising than complex integration processes for acquiring technological expertise.
- Consolidation in the supplier industry is expected to be driven more by OEMs concentrating on a few select suppliers when awarding contracts rather than massive merger movements.
- Growth areas for automotive suppliers include key technologies such as batteries, electric powertrains, and driver assistance systems and electronics.
- Only suppliers that are large, financially strong, and have technological expertise have a chance of gaining a relevant share of the market in these growth areas.
- Competition is extremely intense in these areas with significant price pressure already present even in the early stages of innovation.
- The development of new technologies is almost always more complicated, time-consuming, and expensive than originally planned, making it challenging to present a financially stable business case.
- Some car manufacturers are establishing their own capacities for components in the electric mobility sphere to master technologies, understand business impact, or protect jobs.
- Insourcing in the electric mobility sphere is not expected to increase significantly and will probably decrease long-term, as electric mobility remains a suppliers' market.
- Suppliers are generally more effective at generating cost advantages and working towards standardization than any single OEM would be.
- Many mechanical component suppliers will find things harder in an electrified world and may no longer do business directly with OEMs, instead becoming Tier-2 suppliers for big system suppliers instead.
- Big system suppliers are likely to emerge as winners from the transformation to electric mobility.
- A collaborative partnership between OEMs and suppliers is going to be more necessary than ever due to volatility and uncertainty that neither party can manage alone.
- OEMs need suppliers as a source of product innovations and to keep costs under control, though they are often still far from being a partnership of equals.
