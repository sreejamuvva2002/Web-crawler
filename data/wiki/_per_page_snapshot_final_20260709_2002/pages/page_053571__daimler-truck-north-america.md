# Daimler Truck North America

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-05-21

**Source:** [DTNA’s O’Leary: EV Infrastructure Buildout Still Lagging - TT](https://www.ttnews.com/articles/dtna-oleary-ev)  ·  `page_053571`

## Facts

- Battery-electric trucks are on the market and increasingly on the road as early adopters gradually deploy these zero-emission vehicles in their fleets.
- Daimler Truck North America CEO John O’Leary said the scarcity of charging infrastructure continues to constrain the pace of industry adoption.
- O’Leary said what’s lacking is an end-to-end plan to meet the power and charging demands of battery-electric truck fleets.
- The interview took place at the Advanced Clean Transportation Expo, held May 20-23 in Las Vegas.
- O’Leary's overall assessment of electric truck charging has changed little since he warned that infrastructure buildout efforts were “flailing” a year ago during his keynote speech at ACT Expo 2023.
- A year later, O’Leary said the situation has not improved significantly and could be considered worse due to the amount of time that has transpired without accelerating the adoption curve.
- DTNA combines autonomous driving with a battery-electric powertrain in its Autonomous eCascadia demonstrator truck.
- O’Leary noted that things are not as robust as he would have hoped 12 months ago, acknowledging slightly more charging capability but stating industry sales leveling off indicates little to no progress.
- O’Leary suggested a more coordinated approach to electric vehicle infrastructure planning is needed, involving utility providers, municipalities, and government regulators.
- Charging infrastructure development has generally proceeded on an individual project basis rather than a master plan.
- DTNA's Freightliner eM2 has a range of 180 to 250 miles on a single charge.
- Power demand is rising from other parts of the economy, including data centers supporting artificial intelligence capabilities, exacerbating charging challenges.
- DTNA and its competitors are investing in several technology pathways as the industry transitions toward low- and zero-emission vehicles.
- DTNA sees diesel, battery-electric, hydrogen combustion, and hydrogen fuel cell as the “four primary options for the future.”
- The manufacturer of Freightliner and Western Star trucks plans to continue offering diesel engines through 2039, although timing may depend on regulations and macro events impacting diesel vehicle costs.
- The long-term market for battery-electric commercial vehicles will be in light- and medium-duty applications and heavy-duty trucks running shorter routes like drayage and regional operations.
- O’Leary said it is unlikely that battery-electric trucks will reach 1,000 miles on a single charge in the foreseeable future.
- For longer distances required by coast-to-coast longhaul trucking and vocational operations in remote areas without charging access, DTNA sees hydrogen as the eventual answer.
- The transition to hydrogen will start with hydrogen combustion because it is easier to develop and industrialize near-term due to commonalities with the diesel engine platform.
- That market segment will eventually migrate to hydrogen fuel cell electric, likely starting in the early to mid 2030s as technology becomes higher volume, more affordable, and more efficient.
- DTNA’s current view is that the commercial truck market will increasingly shift toward battery-electric and hydrogen fuel cell electric as predominant technologies over time.
- Fleet operators face a growing challenge selecting the right equipment from an expanding menu of powertrain options.
- O’Leary stated DTNA aspires to have the best products out there to help fleet operators navigate the changing powertrain mix.
- Top industry priorities such as total cost of ownership, vehicle uptime, and service capabilities will remain important regardless of powertrain technology changes.
