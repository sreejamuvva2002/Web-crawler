# Hanon Systems

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** thermal_management  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-02

**Source:** [Hanon Systems Estimates TFA Levels](https://naturalrefrigerants.com/news/hanon-systems-estimates-tfa-levels-generated-by-automotive-f-gas-emissions/)  ·  `page_055727`

## Facts

- Hanon Systems is a major South Korean provider of automotive thermal management systems.
- Hanon Systems released a report estimating the amount of trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) generated in Europe by emissions of refrigerants HFC-134a and HFO-1234yf from car cooling and heating systems.
- The report describes the opportunity to limit TFA creation via the use of CO2 (R744) and propane (R290) as alternative refrigerants.
- Hanon calculated a cumulative TFA load of approximately 68,000 metric tons through 2025 in Europe.
- Using three separate regulatory scenarios, the company projected a cumulative TFA load from 2028 through 2058 of 240,000 metric tons under a no-regulations scenario.
- The company projected a cumulative TFA load of 143,000 metric tons under a regulations-with-exemptions scenario.
- The company projected a cumulative TFA load of 102,000 metric tons under a regulations-without-exemptions scenario.
- The report is titled “TFA contaminations caused by fluorinated gases inside mobile air-conditioning systems and already available alternatives”.
- The report was authored by Dr. Roman Heckt, Global Chief Engineer for Natural Refrigerant Systems, and Dr. Henning Freitag, Natural Refrigerant Systems Development Engineer.
- Hanon’s office in Germany is responsible for natural refrigerant-based thermal management components.
- Hanon operates factories in Portugal, Bulgaria, and the Czech Republic.
- The report outlines a model devised by Hanon to calculate and evaluate TFA created in the atmosphere from refrigerant leakages in passenger car cooling and heating systems.
- The model assumes an average refrigerant charge per vehicle of 500g.
- The model focuses on passenger cars in Europe, comprising the 27 EU member states, the European Free Trade Association (EFTA) countries, and the United Kingdom.
- The model covers internal combustion engine vehicles (ICE), hybrid electric vehicles (HEV), plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEV), and battery electric vehicles (BEV).
- The report noted that annual TFA emissions are rising strongly and will continue for the next ten years at least unless a PFAS restriction leads to an early switch to natural refrigerants.
- For systems with electric compressors, the model used an annual leakage rate of 3% of the nominal refrigerant charge.
- For systems with mechanical compressors, the model uses a 5% annual leakage rate.
- The model includes a further 2% leakage due to crash events, assuming the release of the full charge.
- The main goal of the report is to quantify current and future emissions and to show the impact of different future regulatory scenarios.
- The EU’s European Chemical Agency (ECHA) is currently considering a sweeping restriction of PFAS that would include certain f-gas refrigerants and TFA, with a possible enactment of regulations in 2028.
- Thermal management systems using CO2 are already established in battery electric vehicles.
- Designs using R290 are under active development and validation.
- Compliance with stricter PFAS regulation is technically achievable and already available for the mass market, according to the report.
- TFA is an ultrashort-chain example of PFAS formed in the atmosphere by the breakdown of certain f-gases.
- HFC-134a undergoes a 7–20% decomposition over approximately 14 years.
- HFO-1234yf undergoes a 100% decomposition over 12 days.
- Over the past 30 years, HFC-134a and HFO-1234yf have been introduced as refrigerants in car air-conditioning systems.
- HFC-134a is now being phased down as a high-GWP greenhouse gas.
- TFA formed in the atmosphere is delivered to Earth in rainfall and is also created by the breakdown of pesticides, pharmaceuticals, and other sources.
- TFA has proliferated in the global environment, accumulating as a “forever chemical” in surface water and entering drinking water, beverages, foods, and ultimately human blood serum.
- TFA has a short half-life in the body of about 32 days.
- The effect of TFA on human health is being investigated, with a 2025 study at the University of Milan pointing to an impact on the immune system.
- Germany has recommended to the EU that TFA be classified as reproductively toxic.
- The chemical industry asserts TFA is not harmful, citing reports from the UN Environment Programme (UNEP).
- In its report, Hanon calculated that the use of HFC-134a, starting in the mid-1990s in air-conditioned vehicles, led to the creation of roughly 2,000 metric tons per year of TFA by 2010.
- After 2014, a steady transition to HFO-1234yf took place as a result of the EU MAC Directive, which fully banned HFC-134A as of 2017.
- By 2025, annual TFA emissions exceed 6,000 metric tons per year.
- R1234yf accounts for more than 80% of the total emissions but only 44% of the vehicle fleet, according to the report.
- About half of the cumulative TFA load through 2025 derives from historical R134a use, while the other half has accumulated during the brief but rapidly expanding era of R1234yf.
- Under the baseline scenario with no PFAS-related EU restriction for passenger car refrigerants, emissions rise until 2036 and exceed 9,000 metric tons per year.
- At that time, the full EU car fleet is transferred to R1234yf, except for a smaller number of cars built by the Volkswagen Group that continue to use R744.
- After the peak in 2036, TFA emissions decrease slightly because of fewer leakages due to an increasing number of electric compressors replacing mechanical compressors and the corresponding lower leakage rate.
- The overall car fleet volume is assumed to decrease after the peak.
- Over the 30-year baseline evaluation period, cumulative TFA emissions reach 240,000 metric tons.
- This would be 3.6 times more than the cumulative TFA load caused by the EU passenger car fleet over the previous 50 years.
- The report considered a second scenario model, Restriction Option 1 (RO1), described in the ECHA PFAS restriction proposal documents.
- RO1 assumes a full PFAS ban after an 18-month transitional period.
- Only the service of existing vehicles would continue with R1234yf after the ban date under RO1.
- Under RO1, annual TFA emissions do not rise above 9,000 metric tons per year because the restriction becomes active before the full vehicle fleet has transitioned to R1234yf.
- Even under this strict restriction option, an accumulated TFA load of 102,000 metric tons would result during the evaluation period, which is 1.5 times more than the cumulative TFA load caused by the EU passenger car fleet over the previous 50 years.
- The third scenario is Restriction Option 2 (RO2), for which the PFAS ban has derogation periods of five years for BEV passenger cars and a derogation period of 12 years applied to all other passenger cars.
- Because of the derogations granted under RO2, annual TFA emissions continue to rise until 2036 even though the regulations enter into force in 2028.
- Under RO2, the TFA peak is nearly as high as in the baseline scenario without regulation.
- Cumulative TFA emissions over the relevant 30-year period reach 143,000 metric tons under RO2, which is 2.1 times higher than during the previous 50 years.
- A key finding in the report is the need to convert as many vehicles as possible to alternative refrigerants before the entire fleet is converted to R1234yf by 2036 to avoid the enormous TFA emissions peak of more than 9,000 metric tons per year.
- The report noted that its model is not a full life-cycle model and does not include aspects such as production of refrigerants, leakages during vehicle manufacturing, detailed servicing procedures, end-of-life refrigerant recovery, or a detailed representation of atmospheric processes.
- The rapidly increasing electrification of the EU vehicle fleet will cause a growing share of vehicles to be equipped with heat pumps, which often require considerably higher refrigerant charges.
- This means that the expected annual TFA peak may in reality be significantly higher than 9,000 metric tons per year, and the present model carries a risk of underestimating future TFA emissions.
- Passenger car thermal management systems based on PFAS-free CO2 refrigerant are ready for widespread application across the mobile air-conditioning market, except for pure ICE vehicles.
- This includes all hybrid vehicles using an electric refrigerant compressor.
- In 2020, Volkswagen introduced a thermal management system using CO2 on its first all-electric vehicle platform, the Modular Electrification Toolkit (MEB).
- The MEB system also integrated a heat pump, well-suited to battery electric vehicles that lack waste heat from a combustion engine.
- In winter conditions, the heat pump significantly improves heating efficiency, helping to reduce vehicle range losses.
- There are now more than one million VW MEB cars using CO2 refrigerant on the road in Europe.
- About 10–15% of newly registered battery electric vehicles are equipped with CO2 thermal management systems.
- Hanon is also developing and optimizing systems and components using R290 as a refrigerant.
- Due to its flammability, R290 was not initially considered during the early development phase of natural refrigerant systems for MAC applications.
- This solution is at an earlier stage of development compared to CO2.
- Hanon has found R290 suitable for thermal management of battery electric vehicles, including battery conditioning.
- A potential start of production with key R290 components is planned for 2029.
- The extent to which R290 is suitable for use in vehicles with hybrid drive systems would still need to be investigated.
- According to Benedikt Schauder, Thermal Management Development Lead at German automotive supplier ZF, automakers are showing high interest in R290-based thermal management systems for electric vehicles.
