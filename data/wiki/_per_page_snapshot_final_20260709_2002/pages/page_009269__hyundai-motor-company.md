# Hyundai Motor Company

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-19

**Source:** [Hyundai CEO says he learned about ICE raid on Georgia plant from the news](https://nypost.com/2025/09/19/business/hyundai-ceo-says-he-learned-about-ice-raid-on-georgia-plant-from-the-news/)  ·  `page_009269`

## Facts

- The ICE raid on Hyundai’s battery plant in Georgia was the largest single-site operation in the department’s history.
- Hyundai CEO José Muñoz said he only learned about the ICE raid from the news.
- José Muñoz took the helm at the South Korean company in January.
- Muñoz was working out of his California office when officials rounded up 475 workers at the Hyundai-LG factory on Sept. 4.
- Muñoz told CNN during a media roundtable that he could not believe what he saw because he would have known normally before the news.
- Muñoz likely wasn't notified immediately because the EV battery plant in Ellabell, Georgia, is operated by South Korea’s LG Energy Solution.
- More than 300 South Korean workers were detained by ICE during the raid and deported back to South Korea last week via a charter plane.
- Video footage released by US authorities showed some workers being shackled with chains around their hands, ankles and wrists.
- The shackling has put strain on the country’s relationship with South Korea.
- Muñoz said he has been in touch with the Trump administration since the massive deportation effort.
- Muñoz told reporters that both governments, South Korea and the US, are working actively to try to ensure that situations like this don’t happen again.
- When asked if he was worried about future raids, Muñoz added that nobody can say they are exempt from everything.
- Muñoz opened the company’s first US investor conference in New York City on Thursday with a message to those detained workers and their families.
- Muñoz expressed sincere empathy for the workers from supplier partner companies who were detained, acknowledging the stress and hardship caused to them and their families.
- Hyundai has funneled billions of dollars into US infrastructure, including the EV battery plant in Georgia.
- The EV battery plant in Georgia is one of the largest economic development projects in the state’s history.
- Muñoz said earlier this month that the battery plant, which is still under construction, will face a two to three month startup delay as a result of the ICE raid.
- During the investor conference on Thursday, Hyundai confirmed that it is still moving forward with plans to expand the factory.
- Hyundai will spend $2.7 billion to increase production capacity at the Ellabell site by 200,00 over the next three years.
- The expansion will bring total capacity to a total of 500,000 vehicles annually.
- Hyundai has had to move workers from other plants to the Georgia site to make up for specialized workers flown back to South Korea after the raid.
- Muñoz stated that activities in this particular battery factory require very specific expertise that is not available in the country.
- Muñoz said foreign specialized workers should be able to receive a special visa that allows them to enter and exit the US five, six, or seven times.
- Once the factory is finished, these specialized workers do not come back again.
- Such visas are offered to workers from nations like Canada, Mexico, Singapore, and Chile.
- This visa option is not available for workers from South Korea.
