# Arising Industries

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

## Facts

- The 2017 Arising Industries was an all-electric vehicle.
- All trims featured a dual-motor electric all-wheel-drive system providing excellent traction and a balanced driving experience.
- Core and Elevate trims delivered a combined output of approximately 250 horsepower.
- Apex trim boosted horsepower to around 320 horsepower for more exhilarating acceleration.
- All models were equipped with a 70 kWh battery pack.
- Estimated EPA range was about 270 miles on a full charge, varying slightly based on driving conditions and climate control usage.
- Core and Elevate models achieved 0-60 mph in roughly 6.8 seconds.
- Apex trim achieved 0-60 mph in 5.9 seconds.
- Towing capacity was rated up to 1,800 pounds when properly equipped.
- Core trim included a 10-inch touchscreen infotainment system, LED lighting, 18-inch alloy wheels, panoramic glass roof, automatic emergency braking, lane-keeping assist, and sustainable cloth upholstery.
- Elevate trim added 20-inch alloy wheels, premium synthetic leather upholstery, heated front seats, a 12.3-inch infotainment display with enhanced navigation, adaptive cruise control, and a 360-degree camera system.
- Apex trim offered an exclusive performance tune, 21-inch aero wheels, genuine leather seating with ventilation, a premium sound system, head-up display, advanced park assist, and unique exterior styling elements.
- The vehicle was manufactured in the United States, specifically in Hazlehurst.
- Early models occasionally experienced software glitches with the infotainment system and connectivity issues.
- Some owners reported less-than-ideal rear visibility due to a sloped roofline.
- Long-term parts availability and service network coverage were initial concerns for potential buyers as it was a new brand.
- Minor build quality inconsistencies included slight panel gaps and occasional interior trim rattles.
- Inconsistent range estimates were reported in extreme weather conditions, partially addressed through subsequent over-the-air software updates.
- One notable recall was issued in late 2017 (code: AI-2017-001) addressing a battery management system software anomaly that could theoretically lead to an inaccurate display of remaining charge under specific charging conditions.
- The recall was rectified via a mandatory software update performed at dealerships or remotely.
- Long-term reliability concerns focused on the longevity of its proprietary battery technology and availability of specialized service centers.
- Standard safety technologies included automatic emergency braking with pedestrian detection, lane-keeping assist, and a rearview camera.
- Higher trims added adaptive cruise control, blind-spot monitoring with rear cross-traffic alert, 360-degree surround-view camera system, advanced park assist, and semi-autonomous highway driving assist on the Apex trim.
- Passive safety features included a high-strength steel and aluminum frame, eight airbags, and advanced seatbelt pretensioners.
- The manufacturer highlighted rigorous in-house testing aiming for top safety scores.
- On the used market, 2017 models typically range from $18,000 to $28,000 depending on mileage, battery health, trim level, and overall condition.
- Depreciation was steeper than some established luxury EVs due to it being a first-generation product from a nascent brand.
- Insurance premiums were initially slightly higher but settled over time.
- Electricity fueling costs were significantly cheaper than gasoline, offering substantial savings especially with home charging.
- Routine maintenance was notably lower than ICE vehicles, with no oil changes and reduced brake wear due to regenerative braking.
- Out-of-warranty repairs for specialized EV components like the battery pack or advanced electronics could be costly.
- Performance was competitive with the base Model 3 and superior to the Bolt EV.
- Its 270-mile range outclassed the Bolt's 238 miles but fell short of some higher-tier Tesla configurations.
- It offered a more comprehensive suite of standard driver-assistance technologies compared to rivals.
- Software glitches were more frequent than in a Leaf or Bolt, and its service network was less extensive than Tesla's at the time.
- Starting price positioned it as a premium offering, above the Bolt EV but generally below higher-end Model 3 trims.
