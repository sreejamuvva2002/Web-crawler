# HL-GA Battery Co.

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-04

## Facts

- Construction at Hyundai’s EV manufacturing campus near Savannah, Georgia, was halted two months prior by a dramatic immigration raid before machinery and workers returned.
- On September 4, 2025, U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) detained approximately 300 South Korean nationals at the HL-GA Battery Co. construction site.
- The detained workers were found to be in the U.S. on expired visas or visa waivers that specifically prohibited employment.
- The immigration raid abruptly shut down work on the battery facility, which sits adjacent to Hyundai Motor Group’s new EV assembly plant.
- HL-GA Battery Co. announced on November 14, 2025, that construction had resumed using a mix of new hires and returning South Korean specialists.
- The company expressed gratitude to the U.S. and South Korean governments, as well as Georgia officials, for their collaboration in supporting a smooth and timely return.
- HL-GA Battery Co. stated it remains on track to start production in the first half of next year (2026).
- The company continues to actively hire local positions to operate the facility.
- Mary Beth Kennedy, spokesperson for HL-GA Battery, confirmed that some returning workers were among those detained in September but declined to specify how many had returned.
- LG Energy Solution confirmed that 'subject matter experts' were back at the HL-GA Battery work site to restart machinery installation and get the project back on schedule.
- LG Energy Solution did not disclose exactly how many of the 317 South Korean nationals detained by ICE had returned.
- Hyundai Motor Co. CEO José Muñoz predicted in September that the immigration action would delay the battery factory’s opening by two to three months.
- The manufacturing campus includes the new Hyundai EV assembly plant and six affiliated supplier facilities.
- The campus is expected to produce up to 500,000 vehicles annually once fully operational.
- The battery plant is designed to supply batteries not just for the Savannah assembly line but also for other North American EV and hybrid projects.
- Governor Brian Kemp and other Georgia leaders campaigned vigorously to attract the investment, touting it as a transformative moment for the region.
- According to The New York Times, most of the detained workers were in the country on short-term B-1 or H-1B visas, which allow business visits and certain skilled labor but have strict conditions and expiration dates.
- HL-GA Battery Co. and its partners worked closely with both the U.S. and South Korean governments to facilitate the return of essential personnel.
- An HL-GA Battery spokesperson told Fox News: 'We appreciate the efforts of our partners and government officials in ensuring business continuity and the creation of high-paying, hard-working jobs for Georgians.'
- The company reiterated in November that it remains on track to start production in the first half of 2026.
- The facility aims to ramp up toward full-scale battery production in 2026 while blending international expertise with homegrown local skills.
- Korean attorney Hee Kuk, based in Atlanta, told The New York Times that navigating visa categories and labor rules creates anxiety for foreign-born workers and employers.
- Much of the specialized knowledge required to install and calibrate advanced battery equipment was developed by LG Energy Solution in Korea.
- Production at Georgia’s EV hub is slated to begin in the first half of 2026.
