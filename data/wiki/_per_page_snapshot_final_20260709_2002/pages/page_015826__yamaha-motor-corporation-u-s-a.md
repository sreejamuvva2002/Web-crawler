# Yamaha Motor Corporation, U.S.A.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Yamaha USA Relocation to Georgia: Headquarters Leaves California](https://www.motorcycles.news/en/yamaha-usa-relocation-georgia-headquarters-cypress-kennesaw/)  ·  `page_015826`

## Facts

- Yamaha Motor Co., Ltd. announced it will relocate its US subsidiary Yamaha Motor Corporation, U.S.A. (YMUS) from Cypress, California to Kennesaw, Georgia.
- The relocation marks the end of nearly half a century at the Cypress location.
- Yamaha acquired the Cypress property in 1978 and opened its US headquarters there in 1979.
- All real estate at the current Cypress location will be sold, including office buildings, warehouses, and the entire ~25.1-acre (around 102,000 square meters) property.
- Yamaha has a history of shifting operations to Georgia: its marine division was relocated to Kennesaw in 1999.
- In 2018, a comprehensive reorganization moved the motorsports division (except testing and racing departments) to Georgia.
- In 2019, motorcycle and off-road operations were also based in Kennesaw.
- Yamaha relocated its golf cart, WaveRunner, ATV, and side-by-side divisions to Georgia.
- Yamaha currently employs around 2,600 people in Georgia.
- The manufacturing facility in Newnan, Georgia produces all full-size Yamaha ATVs and side-by-side vehicles as well as WaveRunners and golf carts for US and global markets.
- Only the corporate administration and financial services division remained at Cypress before this move.
- Relocation had been planned for some time; evidence includes the dismantling and shipping of the "Wall of Champions" to Georgia.
- Primary reasons for restructuring are rising costs from US tariffs and changing market conditions.
- Yamaha's Japanese parent company reported a 30.4 percent decline in operating profit in fiscal year 2025, attributed partly to US tariffs.
- The relocation is part of structural reforms aimed at improving profitability of Yamaha’s US operations.
- Yamaha aims for a medium-to-long-term profit structure not exclusively dependent on revenue growth, seeking a more resilient organization.
- Transition involves a sale-and-leaseback arrangement for the Cypress property to maintain operations during transition; sale price, buyer, and exact timeline are under evaluation.
- Move will occur in phases from late 2026 through late 2028.
- High Southern California real estate costs and California’s tax burden are key factors in the decision.
- Selling the campus will free up tied-up capital and immediately relieve Yamaha's balance sheet.
- Leadership change coincides with relocation: Eishin Chihana was appointed CEO of the US division in July 2025.
- Chihana previously headed Yamaha in India and has experience in Europe, India, and Southeast Asia.
- Consolidating all US functions at a single location aligns with strategic direction under new leadership.
- Yamaha remains committed to the US market at the same scale; no product withdrawals or dealer closures have been announced.
- A small group of employees will remain stationed in California for test rides and motorsport.
- Yamaha has three offices in Georgia and 13 additional locations in the USA, including in Wisconsin, Alabama, and Florida.
- With this move, Georgia will definitively become the center of all Yamaha US operations.
- No cuts to model lineup or dealer network are planned for US customers.
- The move illustrates economic pressure on the US motorcycle industry from tariffs, rising costs, and changing market conditions.
- Yamaha is attempting to create a foundation through cost reductions and organizational streamlining to withstand potential sales stagnation.
