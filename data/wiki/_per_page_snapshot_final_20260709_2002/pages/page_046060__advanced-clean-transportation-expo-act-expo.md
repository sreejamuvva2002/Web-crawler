# Advanced Clean Transportation Expo (ACT Expo)

**Type:** event  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Electric heavy-duty trucks are hitting the roads in… | Canary Media](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/electric-vehicles/electric-heavy-duty-trucks-are-hitting-the-roads-in-california-and-beyond)  ·  `page_046060`

## Facts

- Study after study indicates that electric trucks are hitting cost and range targets that put them in the running to handle a rising share of U.S. freight-moving needs for routes under a few hundred miles per day.
- The four-day Advanced Clean Transportation Expo was held in Long Beach, California, where companies unveiled several big orders for all-electric Class 8 trucks.
- Nationwide logistics company NFI Industries announced an order for 60 Volvo VNR Electric trucks, adding to a prior commitment of 20 VNRs and 80 Freightliner eCascadias for its Ontario, California logistics hub.
- Quality Custom Distribution ordered 30 additional Volvo VNRs on top of 14 ordered last year for daily resupply routes from La Puente and Fontana, California distribution centers.
- Quality Custom Distribution, a subsidiary of Golden State Foods, announced a $20 million project at its La Puente site featuring 16 chargers and a solar-, battery- and generator-powered microgrid.
- Penske Truck Leasing and Shell Recharge Solutions North America unveiled plans to deploy Level 2 chargers at 33 sites, with 23 located in California.
- WattEV announced plans for a charging plaza in partnership with the Port of Long Beach to support its electric-truck-as-a-service business across Southern and Central California.
- The WattEV depot will feature 26 360-kilowatt charging bays to serve 50 Volvo VNR electric trucks received in March, with bays also available for other fleets as part of the port’s zero-emissions fleet goal by 2030.
- About 20,000 trucks are registered to operate out of the ports of Long Beach and Los Angeles.
- Quality Custom Distribution operates a fleet of 700 Class 8 trucks running more than 36 million miles of deliveries across the U.S. annually.
- Jeffrey Osborne, managing director and senior research analyst at Cowen, noted that ACT Expo announcements seem to be moving from testing phases toward planning multiyear rollouts.
- Quality Custom Distribution has been road-testing its first Volvo VNR Electric for the past year, with senior director of operations Shane Blanchette stating testing gave them comfort to expand their electric fleet.
- Today’s electric heavy trucks still lack range for routes longer than a few hundred miles.
- WattEV’s model allows drivers and cargo to transfer from a depleted truck to another that has been charging, addressing range limitations.
- Quality Custom Distribution’s last-stage deliveries for Starbucks, Chick-fil-A and Chipotle fit the roughly 150-mile ranges of first-generation Volvo VNR Electrics in Southern California.
- QCD’s trucks only make deliveries at night, giving them all day to recharge.
- Blanchette noted a 100-mile radius around QCD’s La Puente site hits a majority of customers for the future home of 30 electric trucks.
- QCD’s Fontana site serves a more dispersed customer set, but enough are within range to be served by its starter electric fleet of 15 trucks.
- Both QCD sites are eligible for grants and incentives from California state programs and the South Coast Air Quality Management District.
- Blanchette stated that without grant opportunities, electrification would be very challenging given electric trucks cost about three times as much as diesel counterparts.
- Electric trucks promise significantly lower maintenance costs, downtimes, and fueling costs compared to internal combustion engines, with the cost delta widening due to recent diesel price increases.
- Supply-chain crunch has slowed QCD’s plans for getting electric trucks on the road and installing high-voltage direct-current chargers, but timing and strategy are working so far.
- Grant-making agencies have been flexible with deadlines for using funds, and slower deployment hasn’t threatened QCD’s core business.
- More predictable timelines for vehicle delivery and charger installation are needed to enable next steps in QCD’s electrification plans.
- Blanchette warned that unchargeable electric trucks represent capital tied up with no return for businesses reliant on fleet uptime.
- Mike Roeth, executive director of NACFE, noted other fleet operators face similar uncertainties regarding how fast and in what markets electrification will occur.
- Last fall, NACFE partnered with RMI and truck manufacturers/fleet operators to test if various electric truck models can reliably complete shorter-haul routes.
- Results indicated electric trucks can meet about half of North America’s current road freight-hauling needs, leading to significant reductions in carbon emissions and local air pollution.
- NACFE has followed up with reports on medium-duty vans, panel trucks, and short-haul port terminal tractors.
- NACFE’s most recent report covers heavy-duty electric tractor-trailers from Volvo, Peterbilt, BYD and Freightliner tested last fall, focusing on hub-and-spoke operations rather than long-haul electrification.
- PepsiCo and Anheuser-Busch have somewhere around 90 percent of their delivery trucks operating less than 100 miles per day.
- Heavy-duty trucks are responsible for about 70 percent of carbon emissions from the U.S. medium- and heavy-duty transportation sector, with about 22 percent of those emissions coming from vehicles driving less than 250 miles per day.
- These trucks are an important emissions-reduction target via electrification, lower-emissions fuels like natural gas or renewable diesel, or hydrogen fuel cells.
- Cowen’s Osborne noted increased interest in zero-emissions solutions relative to lower-emission solutions like propane, natural gas or biodiesel at the show.
- California’s Advanced Clean Trucks rule, passed in 2020, requires manufacturers to sell an increasing proportion of zero-emissions trucks starting in 2024, aiming for 100 percent by 2045.
- Several other states and D.C. have instituted versions of this rule, representing roughly half the U.S. economy, according to a report from ACT Expo host Gladstein, Neandross & Associates.
- California is working on an Advanced Clean Fleet rule expected in late 2022 or early 2023, requiring large fleet owners to increase zero-emissions trucks to 100 percent between 2035 and 2042.
- The South Coast Air Quality Management District has ordered about 3,000 large warehouses in its jurisdiction to meet increasingly stringent pollution-reduction rules over the coming decade.
- Billions of dollars in public funds are being used to encourage clean fuels and vehicles, with California leading, according to the GNA report.
- California’s Hybrid and Zero-Emission Truck and Bus Voucher Incentive Project made nearly $430 million in funding available for heavy-duty vehicles in its March annual reopening.
- California’s Low-Carbon Fuel Standard program marked $4.7 billion in transactions last year, offering zero-emissions vehicle owners credits for emissions reductions.
- Corporate sustainability goals from Amazon, Coca-Cola, Home Depot, Nestle, PepsiCo, Sysco, Target, Verizon and Walmart are pushing electric trucks onto roads.
- GNA surveyed nearly 250 early-adopter fleet companies, finding 57 percent have tested or bought electric trucks and two-thirds plan to order them in the next year.
- NACFE data shows credits, grants and incentives can reduce higher upfront costs of battery-electric heavy trucks to close to parity with diesel analogs.
- A new RMI report finds half of heavy-duty trucks could be cost-effectively electrified today based on incentive structures in California and New York.
- Electric trucks cut refueling costs nearly in half compared to diesel, allowing total cost of ownership savings to flow almost immediately, according to NACFE data.
- Andy Brown, product marketing manager for Volvo Trucks, noted the price of gas helps TCO and batteries can be charged at a fraction of diesel fueling costs.
- Electric drivetrains have far fewer moving parts and do not need motor oil to cool engines.
- Volvo’s one-pedal driving feature uses regenerative braking to slow the vehicle, recharging batteries and reducing brake wear and maintenance costs.
- Regenerative braking extends ranges for shorter-haul urban routes by capturing energy back into the system during frequent stops.
- A March U.S. National Renewable Energy Laboratory report suggests electric trucks for routes under 250 miles could reach TCO parity with diesel before the end of the decade without subsidies.
- Manufacturers are bringing better trucks and battery packs, with the 200-mile range being the current standard in 2022.
- NACFE highlighted key concerns for electrification shifts: upfront cost, range, lengthy charging times, and fleet operators’ responsibility for installing and maintaining charging networks.
- Fleet operations must be purpose-built to dedicate trucks to specific business parts to deliver on sustainability goals.
- Electric heavy-duty trucks cannot be used for long-haul overnight deliveries and local daytime deliveries simultaneously due to lack of charging time, reducing flexibility.
- Freight companies tend to lease or own vehicles for about a decade, but technology will look very different in range and capability in 10 years, according to Blanchette.
- The maximum range of a Volvo VNR Electric truck grew from about 150 miles to about 230 miles in the past year.
- Companies including Volvo are developing vehicle-as-a-service and infrastructure-as-a-service programs to consolidate maintenance and charging installation costs.
- QCD is working with Volvo on shorter lease terms and higher residual value to plan for switching out future truck or battery versions.
- QCD has a stated goal of zero emissions but hasn’t yet mapped out its strategy, emphasizing the need to take first steps.
