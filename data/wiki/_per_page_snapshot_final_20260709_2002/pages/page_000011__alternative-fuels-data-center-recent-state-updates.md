# Alternative Fuels Data Center: Recent State Updates

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-01-01

**Source:** [Alternative Fuels Data Center: Recent State Updates](https://afdc.energy.gov/laws/recent?_variant=ddcpreview)  ·  `page_000011`

## Facts

- Beginning Jan. 1, 2026, Kansas annual registration fee is $165 for EVs, $100 for PHEVs, and $70 for HEVs.
- Electric motorcycles in Kansas have an annual registration fee of $30 starting Jan. 1, 2026.
- Beginning Jan. 1, 2026, Kansas annual registration fee for trucks and truck tractors under 12,000 pounds gross weight is $125 for HEVs or PHEVs and $200 for EVs.
- Massachusetts requires certain commercial buildings with new parking spaces to provide a minimum number of EV-ready spaces based on total space count, ranging from 1 space for 2–6 total spaces to 10% (not more than 16) for 41 or more total spaces.
- An EV-ready space in Massachusetts is defined as a designated parking space with a dedicated branch circuit for EV chargers.
- North Dakota state policy under the 25x’25 initiative prioritizes agricultural, forestry, and other natural resources as sources of fuel.
- North Dakota Industrial Commission (NDIC) programs, including the Renewable Energy Program and Clean Sustainable Energy Authority (CSEA), provide matching grants and financial assistance for low-emission technology research, demonstration, development, or commercialization.
- South Carolina defines a low-speed vehicle as a four-wheeled motor vehicle capable of reaching speeds of at least 20 mph but not greater than 25 mph with a gross vehicle weight rating under 3,000 pounds that meets CFR Title 49 section 571.500 safety standards.
- Low-speed vehicles in South Carolina may only operate on secondary highways with posted speed limits up to 35 mph but may cross higher-speed highways at intersections.
- Golf cart owners in South Carolina must obtain a DMV permit decal and registration by providing proof of ownership, liability insurance, and a $5 fee, replacing the decal every 5 years or after an address change.
- Operators of golf carts in South Carolina must be at least 16 years old, hold a valid driver’s license, carry required documentation, and passengers under 12 must wear fastened safety belts.
- An entity operating an electric vehicle charger in South Carolina is not considered a public utility, and authorized utilities may charge for infrastructure supporting these stations.
- Utilities offering direct-current-fast-charging services in South Carolina must provide fair and nondiscriminatory rates to all operators.
- Tennessee defines low-speed vehicles as four-wheeled electric vehicles achieving speeds of at least 20 mph but not more than 25 mph, excluding golf carts.
- Tennessee defines medium-speed vehicles as four-wheeled electric or gasoline vehicles with a maximum speed of more than 30 mph but not more than 35 mph.
- Low- and medium-speed vehicles in Tennessee are classified as utility terrain vehicles and are prohibited on interstate highways or any road with a posted speed limit greater than 45 mph.
- Texas allows motor vehicles equipped with automated driving systems to operate regardless of whether a human driver is physically present, preempting local discriminatory rules.
- Automated commercial motor vehicles in Texas require TxDMV authorization and an emergency responder interaction plan filed with the Department of Public Safety for commercial transport.
- By Dec. 1, 2024, the Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation adopted standards for EV charger connectors or plugs to ensure wide compatibility with as many EVs as practicable.
- EV charging providers in Texas must disclose on the charger display the fee calculation method, current rate, and applicable surcharges, and provide itemized receipts upon request.
- After Dec. 1, 2024, publicly available EV chargers funded through public grants or state rebate programs in Texas must meet additional TDLR standards.
