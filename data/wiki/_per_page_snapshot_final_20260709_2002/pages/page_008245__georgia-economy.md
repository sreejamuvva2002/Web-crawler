# Georgia Economy

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-01-23

**Source:** [2026 Overall U.S. and Georgia Economic Outlook | CAES Field Report](https://fieldreport.caes.uga.edu/publications/AP130-4-01/2026-overall-u-s-and-georgia-economic-outlook/)  ·  `page_008245`

## Facts

- The 2026 forecast calls for the second straight year of slow economic growth in Georgia.
- Protectionist trade policies and stricter enforcement of immigration laws are the main problems slowing economic growth.
- The risk that a recession will begin before the end of 2026 is just under 50%, which is double the previous year's estimate.
- Georgia’s economy is expected to match the U.S. economy in terms of GDP and job growth in 2026.
- Inflation-adjusted GDP for Georgia is expected to increase by 1.5% in 2026, about the same as the 1.3% gain expected for U.S. GDP.
- Job growth has narrowed to almost a full stop with hiring slumped, leaving only healthcare seeing job growth.
- Georgia is expected to see 0.5% job growth in 2026.
- Georgia’s unemployment rate for 2026 will average 4.1%, up 0.5 percentage points from its 2025 rate of 3.6%.
- The effective tariff rate on imports is expected to gradually stabilize at about 15% in 2026, which is about six times higher than the rate at the beginning of 2025.
- Uncertainty about U.S. trade policy is keeping many decisions regarding capital projects and hiring on hold until policies stabilize.
- Net foreign immigration to Georgia is expected to be negative in 2026, reversing a previous positive migration trend.
- The Federal Reserve is expected to ease monetary policy, cutting short-term policy interest rates to lower the federal funds rate to 2.75% in 2026.
- Lower interest rates are expected to benefit Georgia more than the rest of the U.S. because low rates will encourage businesses to invest in more economic development projects.
- Georgia’s economic developers are among the best in the nation, with many projects in the pipeline either building out or recently becoming operational.
- Uncertainty and slow growth are expected to reduce the number of expansions, relocations, and startups in contention for 2026.
- Georgia ranks sixth in the United States for total Department of Defense employment, with Fort Benning near Columbus as its largest employer.
- Georgia manufacturing was in recession in 2025 primarily because of the trade war, but a manufacturing recovery is expected to begin in 2026.
- Production by Georgia’s manufacturers is likely to grow faster than production by U.S. manufacturers in 2026.
- Many economic development projects announced in Georgia between 2020–2025 continue to build out and ramp up production.
- Georgia’s transportation and distribution industry is expected to outperform the United States in 2026 due to the build-out of recently announced projects and substantial infrastructure investment.
- The 2026 opening of the Georgia Ports Authority’s second inland container port, the Blue Ridge Connector near Gainesville, will produce economic development benefits similar to the Appalachian Regional Port.
- Imports equal 17% of Georgia’s GDP compared to 11% of U.S. GDP, making tariffs and trade barriers a stronger economic headwind for Georgia businesses and supply chains.
- Tariffs increase production costs for Georgia businesses and disrupt their supply chains, making products less competitive.
- Commercial real estate development accounts for about 209,000 jobs in Georgia, or one job in 33, with the industry stuck in a prolonged recession trough.
- Georgia is the number one state in the country for data center activity, surpassing Virginia, driven by soaring demand for high-performance computing and AI.
- Data centers create thousands of jobs during their multiyear buildout phases and hundreds of permanent, high-paying jobs once operational.
- The main challenge for Georgia’s data center industry growth is ensuring ample supplies of reliable electricity and water.
- Unrestrained growth of Georgia’s data center industry will transition to managed growth due to increased community opposition, regulatory actions, and resource limitations.
- Home prices in Georgia are expected to decline in 2026, which will lower consumer confidence, curb spending, and limit small business formation and expansion.
- Annual federal budget deficits that exceed 6% of GDP increase the risk of recession and limit capacity to reverse a downturn using fiscal policy.
