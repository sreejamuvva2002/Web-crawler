# Georgia Institute of Technology

**Source:** [Can an Iron-Chloride Cathode Cut EV Battery Costs? - Tech Insights](https://eepower.com/tech-insights/can-an-iron-chloride-cathode-cut-ev-battery-costs/)  ·  `page_000334`

## Facts

- Georgia Tech researchers have discovered a novel iron chloride (FeCl3) cathode material designed to improve lithium-ion battery performance and reduce costs for EVs and large-scale energy storage systems.
- Commercial lithium-ion batteries predominantly use combustible organic electrolytes and rare metal cathodes, which risk safety and sustainability while driving up costs.
- Batteries account for about 50% of an electric vehicle's total cost, primarily due to reliance on expensive materials like nickel and cobalt.
- Nickel and cobalt pose environmental and supply chain concerns.
- The new FeCl3 cathode uses only 1-2% of traditional cathode materials like cobalt and nickel while offering comparable energy storage capacity.
- Combined with a solid halide electrolyte and lithium-indium alloy anode, the material creates a fully solid-state battery that offers higher energy density, increased safety, and reduced leakage risks over conventional liquid-electrolyte Li-ions.
- Early tests reveal the cathode performs as well as or better than existing commercial cathodes.
- The development reduces total battery system costs by 30-40%.
- The FeCl3 cathode shows stable voltage plateaus and maintains 83% capacity after 1000 cycles, with 99.95% Coulombic efficiency.
- The cathode has shown operational voltages superior to commonly used lithium-iron phosphate, delivering higher electrical force and battery efficiency.
- Lead researcher Hailong Chen states the technology could be ready for commercial use in electric vehicles in less than five years.
- The team remains focused on laboratory refinement, including a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms and further perfecting the material.
