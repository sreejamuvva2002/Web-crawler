# SK Battery America Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-03

**Source:** [SK lays off nearly 1K workers at Ga. plant amid cooling automaker EV plans – The North State Journal](https://nsjonline.com/article/2026/03/sk-lays-off-nearly-1k-workers-at-ga-plant-amid-cooling-automaker-ev-plans/)  ·  `page_000541`

## Facts

- SK Battery America Inc. laid off nearly 1,000 workers at a manufacturing plant northeast of Atlanta on Friday.
- The layoffs occurred amid automakers’ changing electrification plans and uncertain consumer demand for EVs.
- Friday marked the last working day for 958 plant employees, representing about 37% of its workforce.
- The layoff was reported via a Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) notice filed by human resources chief Chuck Moore.
- Impacted workers will be paid through May 6.
- The plant will continue to employ about 1,600 workers after the layoffs.
- SK opened the $2.6 billion battery plant in Commerce, Georgia, in January 2022.
- The Korean company notably supplied batteries for the Ford F-150 Lightning electric pickup truck.
- Ford announced plans to cancel the fully electric version of the F-150 Lightning in December.
- The U.S. electric vehicle market is currently at a standstill amid the Trump administration steering federal support away from electrification toward laxer automotive emissions policies and broader support for oil and gas industries.
- SK Americas spokesperson Joe Guy Collier stated the workforce reduction was made to align operations to market conditions.
- Collier said: “SK Battery America remains committed to Georgia and to building a robust U.S. supply chain for advanced battery manufacturing.”
- Collier added that the company is pursuing future customers, including in the Battery Energy Storage System arena.
- Ford scrapped the fully electric version of its pickup truck in December, opting instead for an extended-range version.
- A Ford spokesperson declined to comment on supplier personnel actions regarding the layoffs.
- SK and Ford previously invested $11.4 billion together in joint battery plants in the U.S.
- The joint venture between SK and Ford ended in December.
- SK is also a supplier to Volkswagen.
- Georgia U.S. Sen. Jon Ossoff (Democrat) stated: “Let’s be clear: these were battery manufacturing jobs and now they’re gone.”
- Ossoff claimed: “As predicted, Trump’s war on electric vehicles is hurting Georgia’s economy. We were booming and building new plants. Now Georgians are losing their jobs.”
- SK has invested significantly in Jackson County, Georgia, in recent years as automakers developed EV plans with support from the Biden administration's domestic supply chain efforts.
- In June 2020, SK announced plans to invest $940 million to expand its battery manufacturing presence in Atlanta.
- At the time of the 2020 announcement, Gov. Brian Kemp’s office said the expansion would create 600 jobs.
- SK and Hyundai are jointly building a $5 billion battery factory near Cartersville, northwest of Atlanta.
- Georgia has attracted other massive EV manufacturing investments, including Rivian’s $5 billion factory and Hyundai’s own $7.6 billion factory complex.
- Few states benefited more than Georgia from Biden’s Inflation Reduction Act, which accelerated a rush of green energy projects.
- By the end of 2024, Georgia announced 33 additional EV/green energy projects, the most nationwide according to environmental business group E2.
- Projects in Georgia topped $20 billion in pledges, promising more than 25,000 jobs.
- Qcells, a unit of South Korea’s Hanwha Solutions, resumed normal production on Friday after temporarily reducing hours and pay last year due to U.S. customs detaining imported components for solar panels.
- A spokesperson for Gov. Kemp’s office expressed gratitude for the remaining workers at SK’s factory.
- The statement noted Georgia’s job market remains robust with over 219,700 new jobs announced since 2019.
- EV demand has not met automakers’ ambitious expectations recently, though it is still growing.
- EVs accounted for about 8% of new vehicle sales in the U.S. in 2025, similar to the prior year.
- Automakers are reevaluating multibillion-dollar electrification plans as financial losses mount and demand shifts.
- Manufacturers including Ford, General Motors, Stellantis, and others have reneged on factory/investment/product plans, laid off workers, and pivoted some efforts to hybrid and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles.
- Hybrids and more efficient gasoline-powered vehicles are becoming more palatable for mainstream buyers concerned about EV driving range and charging infrastructure availability.
- Under President Donald Trump, Congress eliminated tax credits of up to $7,500 for consumers’ purchases of new or used EVs.
