# Rivian Automotive Georgia Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-03-07

**Source:** [Automaker Rivian pauses construction of its $5 billion electric truck plant in Georgia](https://www.click2houston.com/business/2024/03/07/automaker-rivian-pauses-construction-of-its-5-billion-electric-truck-plant-in-georgia/)  ·  `page_060560`

## Facts

- Rivian Automotive announced it is pausing construction of its $5 billion electric truck manufacturing plant in Georgia to speed production and save money.
- California-based Rivian had planned to start building its new R2 midsize SUVs at the Georgia site located east of Atlanta.
- State and local governments offered an incentive package as large as $1.5 billion, one of the largest ever offered for an American auto plant.
- CEO RJ Scaringe stated that production of the R2 will instead begin at Rivian's existing plant in Normal, Illinois.
- The shift in production location would allow Rivian to get the R2 to market more quickly, sometime in 2026.
- The move would save $2.25 billion in capital spending for now, according to a company news release.
- Rivian has been burning through accumulated cash quickly while failing to meet production targets at its Illinois plant.
- CEO RJ Scaringe stated that the Georgia site remains core to scaling across the R2, R3, and R3X vehicles and expressed appreciation for the partnership there.
- Rivian did not provide a timetable for restarting work on the Georgia plant, stating only that resuming construction is expected later.
- Rivian’s share price closed at $12.51, up 13%, after announcing new R3 and R3X crossover models alongside the R2.
- On its first day of trading in 2021, Rivian shares closed at $100.73, giving the company a total stock market valuation of almost $86 billion, which was bigger than Ford and slightly lower than General Motors at the time.
- The site near Social Circle was expected to eventually hire 7,500 workers and produce up to 200,000 vehicles upon completion of its first phase later this year.
- A second planned phase would boost capacity for an additional 200,000 vehicles per year by 2030.
- State and local governments were projected to spend more than $125 million to purchase the nearly 2,000-acre (810-hectare) site near Social Circle, clear trees, and grade land.
- The state has finished purchasing and preparing the site, turning it over to Rivian.
- The state has also completed most of a pledged $50 million in roadwork.
- Signs for Rivian Parkway at a new traffic signal on U.S. 278 were removed on Thursday.
- Rivian currently produces its R1T pickup truck, R1S SUV, and electric delivery vans for commercial users at its Illinois factory.
- The R1 vehicles currently cost $70,000 or more.
- The original plan was to produce R2 vehicles in Georgia with lower price tags aimed at the mass market.
- The first phase of Rivian’s Georgia factory is supposed to make 200,000 vehicles a year, with a second phase capable of making another 200,000 a year.
- The company stated last year that the first phase would start production this year.
- State and local governments offered Rivian an incentive package worth an estimated $1.5 billion.
- The deadline for Rivian to complete its investment and hiring under the incentive deal was extended to 2030.
- Rivian cited a slowed pace of capital investment and legal challenges mounted by neighbors opposing development as reasons for the pause.
- If the plant is not ultimately built, it would dent Governor Brian Kemp’s goal of making Georgia a center of the electric vehicle industry.
- The construction pause contrasts with rapid construction at Hyundai Motor Group’s $7.6 billion electric vehicle and battery complex near Savannah.
- Hyundai’s plant in Ellabell, announced in 2022, could grow to 8,500 employees.
- Hyundai recently stated it now aims to begin production later this year instead of in 2025.
- Governor Kemp’s office declined comment and referred reporters to the Georgia Department of Economic Development.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development stated that Rivian has restated its commitment to Georgia and that state and local economic development officials remain in steady communication with Rivian regarding its manufacturing plans.
- JoEllen Artz of Morgan Land, Sky & Water Preservation, a group backing legal battles opposing the Rivian project, said she hopes the pause means the plant will never be built.
- Artz stated she is happy with at least a pause in construction.
- Since the state cut down trees and compacted a building pad for the plant, drainage problems have worsened on the site, causing water to stand after heavy rains.
- Artz argued that the state and county should have consulted with local residents and studied the site before pushing through plans for Rivian.
- Artz questioned whether electric vehicle demand justifies the plant and said Kemp should have pulled the plug.
- Artz stated there were many times Kemp could have backed out gracefully and admitted the location was not suitable.
