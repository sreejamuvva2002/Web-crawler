# Georgia Senate Bill on Income Tax and Corporate Incentives

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-27

**Source:** [Georgia Tax Bill Would Take Billions in Breaks From Companies](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southeast/2026/03/27/863761.htm)  ·  `page_010195`

## Facts

- A Georgia state Senate bill would cut the state’s flat income-tax rate for individuals and corporations from 5.19% to 4.99% starting this year.
- The legislation would exempt the first $50,000 of earnings for individual filers and the first $100,000 for married couples from income tax beginning in 2027.
- If passed, Georgia could see a $5.1 billion decline in revenue in fiscal 2028, equivalent to about 13% of its budget.
- The bill proposes ending dozens of incentives and exemptions for businesses including data centers, cigarette exporters, medical-equipment manufacturers, and banks.
- Manufacturing giants Hyundai Motor Group, Rivian Automotive Inc., and Kia Corp. have accepted incentive packages with property-tax breaks, sales-tax exemptions, and job tax credits to open large warehouses and plants in Georgia.
- A Rivian spokesperson stated the EV maker will continue to work closely with state and local partners in Georgia and remains committed to building a state-of-the-art manufacturing facility in Stanton Springs.
- Kia declined to comment on the bill, and Hyundai did not respond to requests for comment.
- Amazon.com Inc., Meta Platforms Inc., and Microsoft Corp. operate data centers in Georgia that currently take advantage of sales and use tax exemptions on high-tech equipment.
- The Senate bill would phase out the sales and use tax exemptions currently used by Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft for their Georgia data centers.
- Republican Senator Blake Tillery, the measure’s sponsor, said the legislation targets corporations to avoid burdening residents with sales taxes typically used to offset income-tax cuts.
- Senator Tillery stated, “All we’re trying to do is value people over corporate credits,” and added, “You shouldn’t be getting the credit just because you were wealthy enough to afford a lobbyist.”
- Since the Inflation Reduction Act passed in 2022, state and federal incentives have positioned Georgia at the center of a surge in clean-energy manufacturing and AI infrastructure.
- Don Bolia, a lobbyist representing businesses opposed to the bill, warned that companies could simply leave or reduce their business in the state if exemptions are cut.
- Khara Boender, director of state policy at The Data Center Coalition, said the bill would discourage further investment and send a signal that Georgia is closed for business.
- State projections indicate data centers could see their current tax burden more than double if the proposal becomes law.
- A December report by Georgia’s Department of Audits and Accounts found data centers supported more than 5,000 jobs in 2025.
- Senate Majority Leader Jason Anavitarte noted lawmakers leaned on a 2025 University of Georgia Carl Vinson Institute of Government report stating 70% of state data centers would exist without tax exemptions.
- Anavitarte stated, “It’s our pro-business environment, historically low taxes, having a balanced budget, truly policies that allow the free market to thrive,” and believes companies will stay even if tax credits disappear.
- Dave Williams, chief policy officer at Metro Atlanta Chamber, urged lawmakers to reduce income and property taxes while maintaining business incentives.
- Critics charge Senator Tillery, who is running for lieutenant governor, and Republican legislators crafted the measure to claim credit for affordability measures heading into November’s midterm elections.
- Democratic Senate Minority Leader Harold Jones II criticized the shift, noting Republicans previously credited these tax credits with making Georgia the number one state to do business.
- The Georgia House has until April 2 to vote on the bill and can propose edits that could send it back to the Senate.
- If passed by both chambers, the bill would go to Republican Governor Brian Kemp for his signature.
- Governor Kemp’s spokesperson declined to comment while the legislation was being debated.
- Other Republican-led states including Florida, Missouri, South Carolina, Mississippi, Oklahoma, and Kentucky have advanced or proposed income and property-tax cuts over the past year.
