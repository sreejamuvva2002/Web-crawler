# Hyundai Motor Group

## Facts

- Hyundai initially failed to qualify for the federal EV tax credit.
- Hyundai is intensifying efforts to regain eligibility by ramping up U.S. production and sourcing more EV battery components domestically.
- Hyundai’s new Metaplant in Georgia is central to its strategy of manufacturing U.S.-made EVs.
- The automaker initially expected American-made vehicles, including the 2025 IONIQ 5 and IONIQ 9, to qualify for the federal tax credit.
- Hyundai’s EVs have been excluded from the federal tax credit since the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) was enacted in 2022.
- The Department of Energy (DOE) released its list of EVs eligible for the federal tax credit in mid-January.
- Most of Hyundai’s EV portfolio was notably absent from the DOE's eligibility list.
- Hyundai’s luxury subsidiary brand, Genesis, also did not meet the requirements to qualify for the credit.
- The only Hyundai Motor Group vehicles that made the cut on the DOE list were the 2025 Kia EV9 and EV6.
- The specific reasons for Hyundai’s exclusion have not been officially confirmed.
- It is widely believed that Hyundai’s exclusion stems from the IRA’s new battery-sourcing rules.
- These IRA regulations require a higher percentage of battery components to be sourced from the U.S. or its trade partner countries.
- Hyundai plans to increase U.S. production by mass-producing the 2025 IONIQ 5 at its Georgia-based Metaplant to regain eligibility.
- SK Battery America (SKBA), the U.S. subsidiary of SK On, will produce batteries for both Hyundai and Kia EVs.
- This battery production strategy further aligns with IRA requirements.
- Until Hyundai’s vehicles become eligible for the tax credit, the automaker will continue passing the $7,500 credit on to consumers through leasing options.
- Potential changes in federal policy, such as President Donald Trump’s proposed plan to scrap EV incentives, could disrupt Hyundai’s strategy and plans.
