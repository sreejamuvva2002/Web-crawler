# 2024 Industrial Cross-Sector Technologies Funding Opportunity

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2024-10-08

**Source:** [Industrial Funding Selections: 2024 Industrial Cross-Sector Technologies Funding Opportunity | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/ito/industrial-funding-selections-2024-industrial-cross-sector-technologies-funding)  ·  `page_003374`

## Facts

- On Tuesday, Oct. 8, 2024, the U.S. Department of Energy’s (DOE’s) Industrial Technologies Office (ITO) announced the selection of 16 projects as part of a $38 million funding opportunity on cross-sector technologies.
- The total federal funding allocated for these selected projects is $38,515,323.
- These projects will advance research, development, and pilot-scale demonstrations of cross-sector process and equipment technologies with wide applicability.
- DOE’s investments in cross-sector technologies address common energy challenges across the U.S. industrial sector and can be tailored to meet the unique needs of specific subsectors.
- Selected projects will advance DOE’s Industrial Heat Shot™ initiative, which aims to develop cost-competitive industrial heat technologies.
- Topics selected for funding include Electrification of Industrial Heat, Efficient Energy Use in Industrial Systems, and Optimizing Organic Wastewater and Wet Organic Waste Treatment.
- Topic Area 1 (Electrification of Industrial Heat) includes projects in Ames, Iowa ($2,600,000 lead by Ames National Laboratory); Worcester, Massachusetts ($2,750,000 lead by Worcester Polytechnic Institute); North Las Vegas, Nevada ($3,000,000 lead by Blue Mountain Energy); Alameda, California ($2,999,376 lead by Rondo Energy, Inc.); and Southfield, Michigan ($1,203,971 lead by Lawrence Technological University).
- The Ames, Iowa project aims to develop a solid-state elastocaloric heat pump targeting ethanol distillation at 2 kW scale to reduce energy consumption by a factor of 3 or more and decrease carbon intensity of corn ethanol by 40%–80%.
- The Worcester, Massachusetts project aims to demonstrate process heating electrification using laser technology to enhance energy efficiency by up to 40% in food applications and 20% in paper applications.
- The North Las Vegas, Nevada project aims to develop a high-lift air-source oil-free closed-cycle mechanical vapor compression heat pump using low-global warming potential refrigerants.
- The Alameda, California project aims to develop an advanced heat battery for temperatures >1300°C with 25-40% increased storage capacity and >85% greenhouse gas emissions reduction, scalable up to 1100°C.
- The Southfield, Michigan project aims to validate a laser-based powder coat curing technology for industrial coatings to replace natural gas ovens, targeting lower temperature (<250°C) and higher temperature (>400°C) systems.
- Topic Area 2 (Efficient Energy Use in Industrial Systems) includes projects in University Park, Pennsylvania ($2,491,443 lead by Penn State University); College Park, Maryland ($1,468,298 lead by University of Maryland: College Park); Atlanta, Georgia ($2,126,875 lead by Georgia Tech Research Corporation); Des Plaines, Illinois ($2,280,000 lead by GTI Energy); Amherst, New York ($3,000,000 lead by SUNY at Buffalo); and Austin, Texas ($2,371,167 lead by The University of Texas at Austin).
- The Penn State project aims to develop a ceramic siliconized silicon carbide (SiSiC) heat exchanger for heat recovery at temperatures over 800°C, doubling heat transfer rates using additive manufacturing.
- The College Park, Maryland project aims to develop a polymer-based heat exchanger for low- to medium-temperature waste-heat recovery tailored for data centers, power plants, and HVAC systems.
- The Atlanta, Georgia project aims to develop and scale-up a continuous roll-to-roll fabrication process for reduced graphene oxide (rGO) and rGO-X nanofiltration membrane technology, with applications in pyrolyzed bio-oils, crude biofuels, and waste plastics.
- The Des Plaines, Illinois project aims to demonstrate a high-temperature heat exchanger (RESE) capable of preheating combustion air up to 500°C from exhaust gases up to 900°C.
- The Amherst, New York project aims to scale up carbon-doped titanium oxide (CDTO) membranes for organic solvent separation, demonstrating a prototype system processing 20 kilograms of crude soybean oil per day for >500 hours.
- The Austin, Texas project aims to improve silver ion based facilitated transport membranes (FTMs) for petrochemical waste streams and fruit/vegetable shipping refrigeration.
- Topic Area 3 (Optimizing Organic Wastewater and Wet Organic Waste Treatment) includes projects in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania ($2,497,554 lead by Philadelphia Water Department); New York, New York ($2,494,408 lead by Columbia University); Champaign, Illinois ($2,495,585 lead by University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign); Knoxville, Tennessee ($2,500,000 lead by University of Tennessee, Knoxville); and Baton Rouge, Louisiana ($2,237,383 lead by Louisiana State University).
- The Philadelphia project aims to optimize autothermal pyrolysis for converting wastewater biosolids into biochar and gas, potentially reducing sludge volumes by at least 85%.
- The New York project aims to develop control strategies to minimize N2O emissions from biological nitrogen removal while increasing process efficiency.
- The Champaign project aims to minimize N2O emissions from ammonia removal processes using ion-exchange and electrolysis as alternatives to traditional nitrification/denitrification.
- The Knoxville project aims to use nanobubbles in water resource recovery facility aeration processes to improve oxygen transfer and decrease energy requirements.
- The Baton Rouge project aims to reduce or eliminate N2O emissions from wastewater disinfection by combining TiO2 catalysts with UVC LEDs to replace chlorine.
