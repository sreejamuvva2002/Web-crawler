# South Korean Battery Manufacturers

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-12-09

**Source:** [Trump EV scepticism threatens US$54 billion in South Korean investments - The Business Times](https://www.businesstimes.com.sg/international/trump-ev-scepticism-threatens-us54-billion-south-korean-investments)  ·  `page_050795`

## Facts

- South Korean companies are reconsidering their US$54 billion investment blitz to build electric vehicle (EV) battery plants in the US.
- The reconsideration is driven by concerns that president-elect Donald Trump could undo tax credits for EVs.
- Some Korean companies have slowed or hit the pause button on ongoing construction of some plants due to concerns about reduced EV demand and potential policy changes under Trump's second term.
- Posco Future M, which makes cathodes for General Motors, delayed completion of its plant in Quebec due to “local conditions” per a September filing.
- Kenny Kim, CEO at Seoul-based SNE Research (a firm focusing on Korean battery makers), stated that many companies are anxious about how much Trump would slash government incentives for the EV market.
- Trump has long criticized President Joe Biden’s efforts to subsidise EVs through the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA).
- The incoming administration is looking to slash fuel-efficiency requirements.
- According to a Reuters report last month, the incoming administration could eliminate the key US$7,500 consumer-tax credit.
- Ending hundreds of billions of dollars in subsidies, tax credits, and incentives would threaten tens of thousands of US jobs.
- Ending these incentives would undo years of work shifting the global EV supply chain away from China.
- Cutting incentives could hit the earnings of Korean firms, which are key US partners in reducing reliance on Chinese suppliers.
- Korean firms are already suffering from weaker demand for EVs and falling battery prices.
- Byeonghoon Kim, CEO at Ecopro Materials (a supplier of precursor materials for EV batteries used by Ford Motor and General Motors), said: “We are paying attention to every single word from Trump” about EVs.
- Byeonghoon Kim added: “We have considered the Inflation Reduction Act as a very important issue so far... If there’s any change in the policy, we may have to change our strategy too.”
- The Biden administration last week offered US$7.5 billion to help finance a joint venture between Samsung SDI and Stellantis to create cell manufacturing plants in Indiana.
- The Trump transition team quickly called the US$7.5 billion offer into question.
- Vivek Ramaswamy, nominated co-chair of Trump’s Department of Government Efficiency, said on X that the department would scrutinise the facility.
- South Korea’s three largest battery makers – Samsung SDI, LG Energy Solution, and SK On – have announced 15 battery plants in the US.
- This represents the most aggressive investment drive of any of the world’s three major battery hubs: China, Japan, and South Korea.
- Half of the Korean plants were announced after the IRA was signed in 2022.
- The announced plants promise to create more than 20,000 jobs in total.
- The plants are located largely in the “battery belt”, which stretches from Michigan through Ohio and Kentucky to Georgia.
- Park Tae-sung, vice-chairman at the Korea Battery Industry Association, stated: “South Korea has contributed to creating jobs and investments in the Rust Belt.”
- Park Tae-sung noted that having Korean battery suppliers is beneficial for the US to win competition against China-dominated supply chains for EVs.
- The Korea Battery Industry Association is in talks with US authorities to lobby to keep the credits.
- EV batteries accounted for about half of all foreign direct investment (FDI) and reshoring from overseas in the US between 2021 and the first quarter of 2024, according to Reshoring Initiative data.
- Korean FDI and reshoring in the US created 20,360 jobs in North America in 2023, more than any other country.
- Prices of lithium, a key mineral tied to EV battery selling prices, plunged nearly 90% from their highs in 2022 due to slower-than-expected EV adoption.
- LG Energy Solution, a key partner of GM, booked about one trillion won (S$943 million) of IRA credits in its accounts so far this year but analysts expect a net loss for fiscal year 2023.
- SK On, the partner of Ford, received about 211 billion won in tax credits from the US during the first three quarters, yet still reported an operating loss during the period.
- Korean companies worry Trump might allow Chinese battery companies to enter the US market.
- CATL said it will consider building a US plant if Trump opens the door, according to Reuters.
- The IRA has blocked investments from China by asking carmakers to gradually reduce sourcing critical battery minerals from “foreign entities of concern”.
- Park Chulwan, professor in the car engineering department at Seojeong University, warned: “China’s entry to the US would be a disaster for Korea. Chinese battery firms would offer much lower prices.”
- Some industry players are optimistic Trump will not cut credits given their plants are mostly located in Republican-governed states.
- Kitae Kim, CEO at SungEel Recycling Park Indiana (a battery recycler under construction in Whitestown, Indiana), said: “I don’t think there is a high chance they will reduce the benefits in the Inflation Reduction Act.”
- The state of Georgia is home to four SK On plants.
- Pat Wilson, Georgia Department of Economic Development Commissioner, stated in an email that Georgia will help Korean companies “maximise their ability to efficiently reach consumers across the country”.
- Pat Wilson added: “The US market is still the most important consumer market in the world. Korean companies knew that prior to the Biden Administration, and that fact will not change with the new administration.”
