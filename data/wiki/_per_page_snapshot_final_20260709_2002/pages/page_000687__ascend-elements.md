# Ascend Elements

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2025-06-10

**Source:** [page_000687](https://gizmodo.com/battery-recyclers-dont-know-how-to-respond-to-trumps-clean-energy-reversal-2000613768)  ·  `page_000687`

## Facts

- Workers at a recycling facility in Covington, Georgia grind up dead batteries into a fine, dark powder known as black mass.
- The Covington facility previously shipped black mass overseas to refineries for metal extraction but now processes it on site to produce lithium carbonate.
- Lithium carbonate is a critical ingredient for making new batteries to power electric vehicles and store grid energy.
- The Covington facility is owned by Massachusetts-based Ascend Elements.
- It is the first new lithium carbonate producer in the nation in years and the only source of recycled lithium carbonate in North America.
- The company is finalizing upgrades to its Covington facility that will allow production of up to 3,000 metric tons of lithium carbonate per year beginning later this month.
- The only other domestic source of lithium carbonate is a small mine in Silver Peak, Nevada.
- Since January, President Donald Trump has frozen grants and loans for clean energy efforts, hollowed out key agencies, and stalled renewable energy projects via executive action.
- Trump cited economic and national security reasons to advance domestic production of critical minerals like lithium.
- U.S. battery recyclers face uncertainty due to fast-changing tariff policies, potential repeal of Biden-era tax credits by Congress, and signs that the clean energy manufacturing boom is fading.
- Battery recyclers are in a "limbo moment" and waiting for next steps, according to Beatrice Browning, a recycling expert at Benchmark Mineral Intelligence.
- Recycling end-of-life batteries from EVs, e-bikes, and cell phones can provide metals while reducing destructive mining needs, a process already scaling in China where most lithium-ion battery manufacturing occurs.
- Under Biden, U.S. battery recyclers announced major expansion plans propelled by government financing and incentives.
- The DOE launched R&D initiatives to support battery recycling and awarded hundreds of millions of dollars in funding to expanding firms.
- The DOE Loan Programs Office offered to lend nearly $2.5 billion to two battery recycling companies.
- The industry benefited from tax credits established or enhanced by the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act, particularly the 45X advanced manufacturing production credit which subsidizes domestic production of critical minerals including those from recycled materials.
- Roger Lin, VP of government affairs at Ascend Elements, stated the 45X incentive has a direct bottom-line impact for recyclers.
- The DOE did not respond to requests for comment on Biden-era grants and loans status, but recyclers report at least some federal support is continuing under Trump.
- In 2022, Ascend Elements was awarded a $316 million DOE grant to construct a second battery recycling plant in Hopkinsville, Kentucky.
- The Kentucky grant funds capacity to make battery cathode precursor materials from recycled metals and is still active with minimal impact from the administration change, according to Lin.
- Ascend Elements expects the Kentucky plant to come online in late 2026.
- American Battery Technology Company, a Reno, Nevada-based firm, finalized a $144 million DOE contract in December for its second battery recycling facility to extract and refine battery-grade metals from scrap and end-of-life batteries.
- CEO Ryan Melsert stated the American Battery Technology Company grant remains active with no changes since Trump's inauguration.
- Cirba Solutions learned that a $200 million DOE grant to construct a new battery recycling plant in Columbia, South Carolina is moving forward.
- At full capacity, the Columbia facility will produce enough battery-grade metals to supply half a million EVs a year.
- Cirba Solutions is still spending funds from two earlier DOE grants, including a $75 million grant to expand a battery processing plant in Lancaster, Ohio.
- Danielle Spalding, VP of communications and public affairs at Cirba Solutions, stated the company aligned very much to the administration's priorities.
- Administration priorities include establishing the U.S. as the leading producer and processor of non-fuel minerals and facilitating domestic mineral production, per Trump executive orders in January and March.
- The Trump administration believes America's national security depends on controlling critical mineral supply chains due to their use in military weapons.
- Ajay Kochhar, CEO of Li-Cycle, wrote that resource recovery and recycling companies will continue playing an important role in providing domestic sources of critical minerals.
- Li-Cycle closed a $475 million loan with the DOE Loan Programs Office in November but is now facing possible bankruptcy.
- Trump pursued onshoring goals using tariffs and fast-tracking new mines, while economists criticized his indiscriminate and unpredictable application of tariffs.
- Some battery recyclers are cautiously optimistic they will benefit from increased trade restrictions amid an escalating trade war with China that includes recent limits on exports of various critical minerals to the U.S.
- China is the world's leading producer of most key battery metals.
- Beatrice Browning stated limiting imports from China could strengthen mineral production in other regions, including the U.S.
- Trade restrictions between the U.S. and partners outside China could be more harmful as U.S. recyclers often sell black mass to refiners in South Korea who pay a premium to secure material abroad.
- Trump imposed 25 percent tariffs on Korean imports in April before placing them on a 90-day pause, which could cut off a key revenue stream if South Korea implements retaliatory tariffs.
- Recycling companies noted there are currently no export bans or tariffs affecting their black mass and emphasized plans to build up local refining capacity.
- Danielle Spalding stated the company sees tariffs as an opportunity to focus on domestic manufacturing.
- Battery recyclers' interests diverge from Trump's on energy policy because without a clean energy manufacturing boom in the U.S., there would be far less need for battery recycling.
- Nearly 40 percent of material available to U.S. battery recyclers is production scrap from battery gigafactories, according to Benchmark data.
- Another 15 percent consists of used EV batteries at end-of-life or recalled, while grid storage and micromobility batteries account for 14 percent.
- The remaining third of material available for processing is portable batteries like those in consumer electronics.
- As more EVs reach end-of-life, an even greater fraction of battery scrap will come from the clean energy sector.
- If planned battery and EV manufacturing facilities are canceled due to IRA tax incentive repeal, loss of federal funding, or rising costs, the recycling industry may have to scale back its ambitions.
- A budget bill passed the House in May would undo key IRA provisions, eliminating some clean energy tax credits like the consumer EV tax credit at the end of this year.
- The legislation scheduled the 45X manufacturing credit to end in 2031 rather than 2032, but could face significant changes in the Senate before heading to Trump's desk, possibly by July 4.
- Despite IRA uncertainty, Trump's actions have put a damper on U.S. manufacturing as firms abandoned or delayed plans for $14 billion worth of U.S. clean energy projects since January, according to E2.
- As Ascend Elements ramps up lithium production in Georgia, it has lined up at least one buyer outside the battery supply chain.
- The battery industry accounts for nearly 90 percent of lithium demand globally, but the metal is also used in industrial applications like ceramics and glass making.
- Integrating into the EV battery supply chain remains the ultimate goal for Ascend Elements, but the company is looking at other plans to ensure economic viability continues.
