# Hyundai Motor Group

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-04-01

**Source:** [Editorial: As Hyundai looks abroad, unions at home push it away](https://www.chosun.com/english/opinion-en/2025/04/01/QGOENNILJ5C33FTBXBWUVVOXZ4/)  ·  `page_009208`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group has begun operations at its third U.S. plant in Georgia.
- The Georgia facility broke ground two and a half years prior to operations.
- The plant incorporates artificial intelligence, information systems, and robots to dramatically improve productivity.
- The automation rate at the Georgia plant is 40%, which is four times higher than the average 10% rate at other Hyundai factories.
- Hyundai has concentrated advanced systems and equipment in the Georgia facility, backed by a $8 billion investment.
- The Georgia plant is Hyundai’s largest single-plant project to date.
- More than 950 robots have been deployed across production lines at the facility.
- The production lines are capable of producing around 100,000 vehicles.
- The plant employs roughly 880 production workers, meaning robots outnumber human workers on the line.
- Hyundai employs around 8,500 local workers at the Georgia facility.
- Robots at the plant are primarily deployed for tasks that are physically demanding or difficult to standardize.
- Human workers take on more advanced responsibilities, such as programming and managing digitalized production systems.
- Hyundai and Kia are ramping up new plant construction and announcing major investments in the United States.
- Labor unions in South Korea have voiced concerns over job security due to Hyundai's overseas expansion.
- South Korean labor unions are demanding more domestic investment from Hyundai.
- The only new Hyundai plant under construction in South Korea is an electric vehicle-dedicated facility in Ulsan.
- The Ulsan EV plant is slated for completion next year.
- The Ulsan plant will be Hyundai's first new plant since the Asan plant opened in 1996, nearly three decades ago.
- Hyundai Motor Group plans to invest $8.5 billion to build a Hyundai Steel plant in the United States.
- The Hyundai Steel union is on strike, demanding bonuses equivalent to those at Hyundai Motor.
- Due to a slump in the steel industry, Hyundai Steel’s Incheon plant is set to suspend operations for a month in April.
- The Hyundai Steel union has questioned whether the company has money to build a U.S. steel plant but not to pay out performance bonuses.
- Corporate investments like the U.S. steel plant are funded through means such as corporate bonds or the sale of idle assets, with expectations of future revenue and profit.
- Performance bonuses require immediate profitability, unlike long-term capital investments.
- Union strikes and demands for higher pay are driving companies to shift investment overseas instead of staying in South Korea.
