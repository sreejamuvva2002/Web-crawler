# State of Georgia

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2023-07-25

**Source:** [Powering Ahead: How Georgia Can Become a Global Energy Thought Leader - The Pendleton Group](https://pendletonatlanta.com/2023/07/25/powering-ahead-how-georgia-can-become-a-global-energy-thought-leader/)  ·  `page_015476`

## Facts

- The article was written by Craig Lesser and Jorge Fernandez of The Pendleton Group and appeared in Global Atlanta.
- This white paper builds on a previous edition published by Insider Advantage.
- A new “Georgia Global Energy Policy Center” is proposed to capitalize on the state’s existing assets and cooperate with international partners.
- The proposed Georgia Global Energy Policy Center would be funded by state government, private industry, federal grants, and foundations.
- The Georgia Institute of Technology could serve as the hub for the Center of Energy Supply Research.
- The University of Georgia could form the Center for Rural Energy Research and Policy through its engineering and agricultural schools.
- Georgia Southern University could house the Center of Air and Sea Energy Research and Policy.
- Georgia State University could become the Center of Urban Energy Research and Policy.
- The proposed center would connect global players to address pressing energy issues and form relationships with similar energy think tanks worldwide.
- Private sector companies in Georgia include AGCO, Delta Air Lines, Fieldale Farms, Georgia Power Co., Gulfstream, Hyundai, Kia, Lockheed, Mercedes Benz, Porsche, Southern Co., and dozens of others.
- These companies could utilize the new policy center to build partnerships with university researchers that further their own energy projects.
- They could also cooperate on joint energy work to bring in additional international partners and funding.
- Energy-related organizations like AEE and INPO could cooperate with the new policy center to pool resources and knowledge.
- The existing Center of Innovation for Energy is led by the Georgia Department of Economic Development.
- The Center of Innovation for Energy is working with companies like Stryten Energy, Mitsubishi Turbines, and Siemens Energy and Automation to advance energy technologies for improved generation, storage, and consumption.
- This energy innovation center has worked with other partners for about 10 years to lay the groundwork for the electric vehicle (EV) mobility industry in Georgia.
- Georgia has positioned itself as a hub for electric transportation with 35 EV-related projects investing in the state since 2018.
- Hyundai Motor Group’s fully dedicated EV and battery manufacturing “meta plant” facility near Savannah is expected to ramp up to full production in 2025.
- Rivian’s largest manufacturing facility in the U.S. is slated to start production in Georgia in 2026.
- Georgia is rapidly expanding its full EV supply chain with related manufacturing and solutions.
- NVH Korea has announced a $72 million battery parts factory in Henry County.
- Woory Industrial Co., Ltd. plans to open an EV parts R&D center and manufacturing facility in Dublin, Ga.
- Both NVH Korea and Woory Industrial are coming to support the Hyundai Meta Plant.
- LG Energy Solution Ltd. is partnering on a $4.3 billion battery operation at the Hyundai Meta Plant.
- Anovion Technologies is building an $800 million plant to manufacture synthetic graphite anode materials for lithium-ion batteries in Bainbridge, Ga.
- Georgia’s ambitions to become a hub for clean vehicles underscores its desire to be a clearinghouse for expertise around clean energy in general, forging new paths in EV infrastructure, battery development and commercialization and charging stations.
- Georgia’s existing energy innovation center is focusing on identifying EV charging equipment manufacturers, such as Netherlands-based Heliox.
- The state is especially interested in enticing global players in hydrogen energy technology and storage to invest in Georgia.
- The state could work with Hydrogen Europe on innovation and investment for deploying hydrogen power.
- The Center of Innovation for Energy is hoping to put together demonstration hydrogen projects and potentially the first hydrogen living laboratory.
- This hydrogen initiative would be similar to its work on the net-zero transportation initiative The Ray and the smart city Curiosity Lab.
- Georgia is bidding for funding to be a center for hydrogen expertise via a coalition of regional utilities, including Southern Company.
- Georgia Power is conducting research into hydrogen fuels.
- Related supplier Atlanta Cylinders Inc., whose parent company is in the Czech Republic, is lining up to support Georgia’s hydrogen endeavor.
- Automakers Hyundai and Toyota Motor Corp. are looking to hydrogen as the fuel of the future.
- If successful with hydrogen initiatives, the industry will start paying attention to Georgia, as the EV mobility industry did.
- The state’s previous initiatives to coalesce expertise and business clusters around fintech and global health are evidence of Georgia’s ability to garner local and international support to boost certain industries.
- Existing and future innovation centers in Georgia could contribute important resources to share with a new Georgia Global Energy Policy Center and forge new connections among international and local innovators and between those innovators and public and private sector actors.
- The creation of a Georgia Global Energy Policy Center would encourage public education programs around new energy technology.
- Educational and community programs would engage children in learning about better ways to create and use energy to cultivate students for universities and technical schools.
- With educational programs in place, Georgia can cultivate energy-minded students to feed universities and technical schools, which would develop new programs focused on the energy sector and build leaders who will direct the world’s energy future.
- With a growing community of educated professionals, Georgia can further its reputation as the best place for smart, motivated individuals to come to learn about and put into practice new energy technologies, attracting talented entrepreneurs and innovators from around the world.
- Georgia’s startup ecosystem could benefit from – and contribute to – new energy development.
- The Georgia Research Alliance has been instrumental in accelerating the growth of university research and development in the state.
- By bringing private and public research investment to Georgia, the Georgia Research Alliance supports startup companies and creates jobs.
- Georgia Research Alliance’s efforts, aligned with those of a new energy policy center, could result in even further economic impact throughout the state.
- The Georgia Global Energy Policy Center would be a nonpartisan effort that benefits the entire state.
- Research and development on this scale can only benefit every Georgian, whether by creating new jobs or inventing new efficiencies that impact gas and electric bills.
