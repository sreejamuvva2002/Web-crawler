# Rivian Electric Vehicle Plant at Stanton Springs North

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-10-17

**Source:** [Dirt pollution complaints at controversial Rivian site underline critics’ environmental concerns - SaportaReport](https://saportareport.com/dirt-pollution-complaints-at-controversial-rivian-site-underline-critics-environmental-concerns/columnists/johnruch/)  ·  `page_047015`

## Facts

- The state is investigating reports of dirt blowing and flowing off the site of the Rivian electric vehicle plant in Morgan and Walton counties.
- Critics say the situation confirms their fears about environmental issues associated with the controversial and troubled project.
- Before recent complaints, critics expressed concern that the state and a local development authority hired Plateau Excavation, which is among defendants in a federal lawsuit alleging dirt from a neighboring Facebook data center project polluted a local stream and ponds.
- Site preparation is speeding ahead on a project whose $15 billion bond deal and hundreds of millions of dollars in tax breaks were just shot down in court and still lacks all permits for work near wetlands and waterways.
- JoEllen Artz, leader of Morgan Land, Sky & Water Preservation’s “No to Rivian” campaign, stated she has tremendous concern about land disturbance work being performed at this time.
- Following her group’s legal victory on the bonds and tax breaks, Artz questioned whether Rivian will actually build on the site or leave a 500-acre, muddy, Georgia red clay pad that becomes a source of runoff and silt to streams, ponds, and spring heads.
- The co-developers on Rivian’s behalf are the Georgia Department of Economic Development (GDEcD) and the Joint Development Authority of Jasper, Morgan, Newton and Walton Counties (JDA).
- GDEcD and JDA issued a joint written statement not explaining exactly why they are proceeding with site clearing despite the bond and permitting status, only that they are legally and physically able to do so.
- An Oct. 13 runoff of dirt or clay from the site into the Rawlings Branch stream was self-reported by Plateau.
- JDA and Plateau stated they will continue following all regulations on this project while deploying best practices and taking precautions to minimize environmental impacts.
- Austell-based Plateau did not respond to emails and a phone message seeking comment.
- California-based Rivian also did not respond to questions.
- The Georgia Environmental Protection Division (EPD) was investigating the dirt complaints that included an on-site visit on Oct. 17, according to spokesperson Sara Lips.
- EPD staff were at the Rivian site on Oct. 17 to investigate and determine what corrective actions are necessary.
- The plan for Rivian’s $5 billion, 2,000-acre plant was announced late last year by Gov. Brian Kemp.
- The project generated enthusiasm as a green-economy economic development deal but stirred rare local rebellion over the state’s practice of secretly signed deals involving huge tax breaks and incentives, roughly $1.5 billion in this case.
- A main point of contention is the industrialization of a rural area about 40 miles east of Atlanta at I-20 and U.S. 278 near Rutledge and Social Circle.
- Outrage over the lack of public input intensified when the state took over the project to speed permitting and avoid local zoning and other reviews as Morgan County planners sought to subject the project to standards above and beyond the usual due to its scale.
- The plant became an issue in the Republican gubernatorial primary, with unsuccessful Kemp challenger David Perdue siding with the local opposition.
- Last month, Artz’s opposition group scored a major victory with a court ruling that the deal for tax-exempt bonds was unwarranted for an unproven startup company and that the proposed property tax breaks were unlawful.
- GDEcD and JDA are considering an appeal of the court decision.
- Rivian, which specializes in electric trucks, had an overall rocky year with production struggles and an announcement of more than 800 layoffs.
- Just days before the court decision, GDEcD and JDA began site clearance, following through on an April announcement that such work would begin on an initial 500 acres by avoiding wetlands and waterways while awaiting permitting under the federal Clean Water Act and state statutes.
- Plateau was selected by the JDA in August, about eight weeks after the company was among defendants sued by two residents for alleged Clean Water Act violations and other actions related to a neighboring data center project.
- The Rivian site is part of a sprawling industrial park conceptualized by the JDA called Stanton Springs that already includes facilities for biopharmaceutical company Takeda and Facebook data centers.
- The Rivian site is known internally as Stanton Springs North.
- The billion-dollar data center project within a Newton County section of the industrial park began in 2018 and expanded in 2020.
- Site clearance for the data center project produced local complaints of dust storms of dirt blown off the area and culminated in a federal lawsuit filed in July by Morgan County residents Julie G. Jenkins and James P. Gunn against Facebook parent company Meta Platforms, general contractor Holder Construction Company, Plateau, subcontractors Alif Transport and AM Grading & Hauling, CEO Adnan Mohamed, and local property owner Daniel Bruce.
- The complaint alleges that starting in May or June of 2020, Alif dumped hundreds and perhaps in excess of one thousand dump truck loads of red clay waste taken from the data center’s site onto Bruce’s property without permits or proper erosion controls.
- As a result, red clay flowed off the site into the Rawlings Branch, polluting it and two large ponds owned by Jenkins and Gunn.
- The lawsuit claims negligence, trespass, creation of a nuisance and violations of the Clean Water Act and other statutes, and seeks damages and remediation.
- Meta, Holder, Plateau and Bruce filed answers broadly claiming there is no pollution and that they are not responsible for it if it does exist.
- Alif, AM Grading and Mohamed did not file answers by a deadline, which could open them to a default judgment in the plaintiffs’ favor.
- In early October, Rev. Alan Jenkins circulated a press release about Plateau’s work on the Rivian site while the lawsuit was pending, warning of significant risk that similar erosion, sedimentation, and water pollution problems could occur.
- The Jenkins family sold about 40 acres of land to the JDA for the plant for nearly $2.8 million, though they say they did so only because the authority threatened that the Georgia Department of Transportation would take it by eminent domain anyway.
- Alan Jenkins told SaportaReport that his mother is aware of his press release but is not commenting on it.
- Artz mentioned Plateau’s involvement in her comments to EPD opposing a variation GDEcD and JDA are seeking from a stream buffer rule related to erosion and sedimentation control, stating she did not trust they would perform any better on the Rivian site.
- Artz and Alan Jenkins reported the grading several times to EPD because Plateau was not watering the dirt in order to keep dust and dirt under control, noting water trucks seemed to be used sporadically.
- Artz alleged the site has a dirt dump with no silt fencing and provided an aerial photo illustrating it.
- EPD confirmed receiving those complaints and said they are still under investigation.
- Artz said Rivian runoff on Oct. 13 resulted in the stream teeming with muddy red water.
- Alan Jenkins said the dirt did not reach the ponds, which he believes is due to upstream beaver dams that could be overwhelmed by larger rains.
- GDEcD and JDA stated based on project requirements Plateau provided the strongest proposal at the lowest cost and are a reputable contractor with a strong track record.
- Plateau has committed to considering local subcontractors for work on the project, which GDEcD and JDA said is an important priority.
- GDEcD and JDA stated there is no off-site dumping for Rivian and all materials are contained on the site.
- GDEcD and JDA blamed the runoff on a period of drought-like conditions followed by persistent rainfall, noting rainfall in the preceding 24 hours at Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport was 1.11 inches.
- Officials stated best practices were in place to prevent the runoff but the rainfall was significant enough to overwhelm the double-row silt fence that was installed.
- Officials with Morgan and Walton counties did not respond to questions; Walton County reportedly is in charge of local-level environmental monitoring on the project.
- Rivian touts itself as a green-focused company aiming to build on the rural site atop a local groundwater source.
- GDEcD and JDA have said the project will have minimized but some unavoidable impacts on wetlands and waterways as it seeks a federal Clean Water Act Section 404 permit.
- In an April press release announcing the Section 404 application, GDEcD and JDA noted the plant plan has features such as leaving more than half the site unpaved and collecting rainwater for reuse on-site.
- Officials have said the finished plant would have less runoff in total than the current site due to stormwater infrastructure.
- GDEcD Commissioner Pat Wilson stated Rivian’s corporate ethos prioritizes the natural environment in every aspect of their planning process and that the State and JDA continue to work closely with the company to thoughtfully develop its environmentally conscious manufacturing site at Stanton Springs North.
- Artz expressed doubts about environmental commitments, stating she does not think anybody at the JDA particularly cares about these issues and does not believe the Walton County person in charge of monitoring the site has enough authority to influence what is done because of downward pressure from Walton County management and the JDA.
- Artz said many opponents believe the site grading is probably being done in a snit of vindictiveness and that the project should have been postponed until all permits and funding were received.
