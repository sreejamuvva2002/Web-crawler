# Global E-waste Recycling Sector

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2025-07-13

**Source:** [In rare earth metals power struggle with China, old laptops, phones may get a new life – NBC 6 South Florida](https://www.nbcmiami.com/news/business/money-report/in-rare-earth-metals-power-struggle-with-china-old-laptops-phones-may-get-a-new-life/3656714/)  ·  `page_001275`

## Facts

- The need for rare earth metals is growing and critical in making everything from fighter jets to power tools.
- U.S. manufacturers have typically purchased essential metals and rare earths from foreign producers led by China, but unpredictable tariffs and trade policies, and geopolitics, have made these sources unreliable.
- The U.S. government just announced a landmark equity stake in MP Materials and the country's only working rare earths mine, Mountain Pass.
- A new generation of recyclers see electronic waste like old laptops, phones and TVs as another key to extracting critical elements in short supply globally.
- As the U.S. and China vie for economic, technological and geopolitical supremacy, the critical elements and metals embedded in technology from consumer to industrial and military markets have become a pawn in the wider conflict, with nowhere more so the case than in China's leverage over the rare earth metals supply chain.
- The Department of Defense took a large equity stake in MP Materials, the company running the only rare earths mining operation in the U.S.
- Next-generation recyclers, a mix of legacy companies and startups, are innovating ways to gather and process the ever-growing mountains of electronic waste, or e-waste, which comprises end-of-life and discarded computers, smartphones, servers, TVs, appliances, medical devices, and other electronics and IT equipment.
- Spent EV batteries, wind turbines and solar panels are fostering a burgeoning recycling niche.
- The e-waste recycling opportunity isn't limited to rare earth elements; any electronics that can't be wholly refurbished and resold, or cannibalized for replacement parts, can be recycled to strip out gold, silver, copper, nickel, steel, aluminum, lithium, cobalt and other metals vital to manufacturers in various industries.
- Recyclers are increasingly extracting rare-earth elements, such as neodymium, praseodymium, terbium and dysprosium, which are critical in making everything from fighter jets to power tools.
- Kunal Sinha, global head of recycling at Swiss-based Glencore, stated that recycling of e-waste hasn't been taken too seriously until recently as a meaningful source of supply, and added that a lot of people are still sleeping at the wheel and don't realize how big this can be.
- Traditionally, U.S. manufacturers purchase essential metals and rare earths from domestic and foreign producers—an inordinate number based in China—or through commodities traders, but with those supply chains now disrupted by unpredictable tariffs, trade policies and geopolitics, the market for recycled e-waste is gaining importance as a way to feed the insatiable electrification of everything.
- John Mitchell, president and CEO of the Global Electronics Association, said the United States imports a lot of electronics that come with gold and aluminum and steel, creating a great opportunity for tariffs to act as an impetus for greater recycling in this country for goods bought from other countries.
- Recycling contributes only around $200 million to Glencore's total EBITDA of nearly $14 billion, but the strategic attention and time the business gets from leadership is much more than that percentage.
- Glencore believes that while a lot of mining is necessary to get to all the copper, gold and other metals needed, recycling is going to play a huge role.
- Glencore has operated a huge copper smelter in Quebec, Canada for almost 20 years on a site that's nearly 100-years-old.
- The Quebec facility processes mostly mined copper concentrates, though 15% of its feedstock is recyclable materials, such as e-waste that Glencore's global network of 100-plus suppliers collect and sort.
- The smelter pioneered the process for recovering copper and precious metals from e-waste in the mid 1980s, making it one of the first and largest of its type in the world.
- The smelted copper is refined into fresh slabs that are sold to manufacturers and traders, and the same facility also produces refined gold, silver, platinum and palladium recovered from recycling feeds.
- The importance of copper to OEMs' supply chains was magnified in early July when prices hit an all-time high after President Trump said he would impose a 50% tariff on imports of the metal.
- The U.S. imports just under half of its copper, and the tariff hike is intended to boost domestic production.
- It takes around three decades for a new mine in the U.S. to move from discovery to production, which makes recycled copper look all the more attractive as demand keeps rising.
- According to estimates by energy-data firm Wood Mackenzie, 45% of copper demand will be met with recycled copper by 2050, up from about a third today.
- Foreign recycling companies have begun investing in U.S.-based facilities; in 2022, Germany's Wieland broke ground on a $100-million copper and copper alloy recycling plant in Shelbyville, Kentucky.
- Last year, another German firm, Aurubis, started construction on an $800-million multi-metal recycling facility in Augusta, Georgia.
- Aurubis CEO Toralf Haag stated that as the first major secondary smelter of its kind in the U.S., Aurubis Richmond will allow them to keep strategically important metals in the economy, making U.S. supply chains more independent.
- The proliferation of e-waste can be traced back to the 1990s when the internet gave birth to the digital economy, spawning exponential growth in electronically enabled products.
- This trend has been supercharged by the emergence of renewable energy, e-mobility, artificial intelligence and the build-out of data centers, translating to a constant turnover of devices and massive amounts of e-waste.
- In 2022, a record 62 million metric tons of e-waste were produced globally, up 82% from 2010, according to the most recent estimates from the United Nations' International Telecommunications Union and research arm UNITAR.
- That global e-waste number is projected to reach 82 million metric tons by 2030.
- The U.S. produced just shy of 8 million tons of e-waste in 2022, yet only about 15-20% of it is properly recycled, illustrating the untapped market for e-waste retrievables.
- The e-waste recycling industry generated $28.1 billion in revenue in 2024, according to IBISWorld, with a projected compound annual growth rate of 8%.
- E-waste that stores data, especially smartphones, computers, servers and some medical devices, must be wiped of sensitive information to comply with cybersecurity and environmental regulations through a service referred to as IT asset disposition (ITAD).
- ITAD is offered by conventional waste and recycling companies, including Waste Management, Republic Services and Clean Harbors, as well as specialists such as Sims Lifecycle Services, Electronic Recyclers International, All Green Electronics Recycling and Full Circle Electronics.
- Full Circle Electronics CEO Dave Daily stated they are definitely seeing a bit of an influx of e-waste coming into their warehouses, which he thinks is due to some early refresh cycles.
- This influx references businesses and consumers choosing to get ahead of the customary three-year time frame for purchasing new electronics and discarding old stuff in anticipation of tariff-related price increases.
- Daily is also witnessing increased demand among downstream recyclers for e-waste Full Circle Electronics can't refurbish and sell at wholesale.
- The company dismantles and separates e-waste into 40 or 50 different types of material, from keyboards and mice to circuit boards, wires and cables.
- Recyclers harvest those items for metals and rare earths, which continue to go up in price on commodities markets, before reentering the supply chain as core raw materials.
- Entrepreneurs have been launching e-waste recycling startups and developing technologies to process them for domestic OEMs even before the Trump administration's efforts to revitalize American manufacturing by reworking trade deals and recent changes in tax credits key to the industry.
- Sinha noted that many regions of the world have been kind of lazy about processing e-waste, so a lot of it goes offshore, but there seems to be a trend of nationalizing e-waste because people suddenly realize they have the same metals they've been looking for from overseas sources.
- People have been rethinking the global supply chain, realizing it's too long and needs to be more localized.
- China commands roughly 90% of the global rare earth market in mining, processing and refining.
- In the production of rare-earth magnets used in EVs, drones, consumer electronics, medical devices, wind turbines, military weapons and other products, China dominates the supply chain.
- The lingering U.S.–China trade war has exacerbated the disparity; in April, China restricted exports of seven rare earths and related magnets in retaliation for U.S. tariffs, a move that forced Ford to shut down factories because of magnet shortages.
- In mid-June, China issued temporary six-month licenses to certain major U.S. automaker suppliers and select firms; exports are flowing again but with delays and still well below peak levels.
- Before the Trump administration deal, the Biden administration awarded $45 million in funding to MP Materials and the nation's lone rare earths mine in Mountain Pass, California.
- Back in April, the Interior Department approved development activities at the Colosseum rare earths project located within California's Mojave National Preserve.
- The Colosseum project is owned by Australia's Dateline Resources and will potentially become America's second rare earth mine after Mountain Pass.
- Several recycling startups are extracting rare earths from e-waste; Illumynt has an advanced process for recovering them from decommissioned hard drives procured from data centers.
- In April, hard drive manufacturer Western Digital announced a collaboration with Microsoft, Critical Materials Recycling and PedalPoint Recycling to pull rare earths, as well as copper, gold, aluminum and steel, from end-of-life drives.
- Canadian-based Cyclic Materials invented a process that recovers rare-earths and other metals from EV motors, wind turbines, MRI machines and data-center e-scrap.
- The company is investing more than $20 million to build its first U.S.-based facility in Mesa, Arizona.
- Late last year, Glencore signed a multiyear agreement with Cyclic to provide recycled copper for its smelting and refining operations.
- End-of-life lithium-ion batteries are a hot feedstock for e-waste recyclers, providing not only lithium but also copper, cobalt, nickel, manganese and aluminum.
- Those materials are essential for manufacturing new EV batteries, which the Big Three automakers are heavily invested in.
- Their projects are threatened by possible reductions in the Biden-era 45X production tax credit featured in the new federal spending bill.
- It's too soon to know how that might impact battery recyclers including Ascend Elements, American Battery Technology, Cirba Solutions and Redwood Materials, who themselves qualify for the 45X and other tax credits.
- They might actually be aided by other provisions in the budget bill that benefit a domestic supply chain of critical minerals as a way to undercut China's dominance of the global market.
- Sinha warned that the looming uncertainty should be a warning sign for e-waste recyclers, cautioning them not to build a recycling company on the back of one tax credit because it can be short-lived.
- Investing in recyclers can be precarious; while recycling is getting its due as a meaningful source of supply, Sinha cautions people to be careful when investing in this space because startups may have developed new technologies but lack good enough business fundamentals.
- He advised not to invest on the hype but on the fundamentals.
- Glencore has invested $327.5 million in convertible notes in battery recycler Li-Cycle to provide feedstock for its smelter.
- The Toronto-based startup had broken ground on a new facility in Rochester, New York but ran into financial difficulties and filed for Chapter 15 bankruptcy protection in May.
- This prompted Glencore to submit a stalking horse credit bid of at least $40 million for the stalled project and other assets.
- Sinha stated that the current environment will lead to more startups and investments in e-waste recycling, adding that they are investing themselves.
