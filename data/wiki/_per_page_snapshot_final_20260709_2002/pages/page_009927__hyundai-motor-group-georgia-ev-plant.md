# Hyundai Motor Group Georgia EV Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [475 people detained in Hyundai plant immigration raid in Georgia, Homeland security says | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/us-south-korea-ice-raid-georgia-hyundai-9394482c195664d7cc3db67ae998ac05)  ·  `page_009927`

## Facts

- Immigration authorities detained 475 people during an immigration raid in Georgia on Friday.
- Most of the detained individuals were South Korean nationals.
- The raid targeted Hyundai's sprawling manufacturing site in Ellabell, Ga., where the automaker makes electric vehicles.
- Steven Schrank, lead Georgia agent of Homeland Security Investigations (HSI), said the raid resulted from a monthslong investigation into allegations of illegal hiring at the site.
- Schrank called it the “largest single site enforcement operation” in HSI’s two-decade history.
- The Thursday raid targeted one of Georgia’s largest and most high-profile manufacturing sites.
- Hyundai Motor Group began manufacturing electric vehicles at a $7.6 billion plant there a year ago.
- The site employs about 1,200 people in an area about 25 miles (40 kilometers) west of Savannah.
- Gov. Brian Kemp and other officials have touted the project as the state’s largest economic development project.
- Agents focused their operation on an adjacent plant still under construction where Hyundai partnered with LG Energy Solution to produce EV batteries.
- Court records indicated prosecutors do not know who hired the “hundreds of illegal aliens.”
- The U.S. Attorney’s Office wrote that the identity of the “actual company or contractor hiring the illegal aliens is currently unknown.”
- The South Korean government expressed “concern and regret” over the operation targeting its citizens.
- Only 46 Koreans were deported during the 12-month period ending Sept. 30, 2024, out of more than 270,000 removals for all nationalities, according to ICE.
- South Korean Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lee Jaewoong said in a televised statement from Seoul that “The business activities of our investors and the rights of our nationals must not be unjustly infringed in the process of U.S. law enforcement.”
- Lee said the ministry is dispatching diplomats from its embassy in Washington and consulate in Atlanta to the site, and planning to form an on-site response team.
- Immigration attorney Charles Kuck said two of his detained clients arrived from South Korea under a visa waiver program allowing stays of 90 days or less without a visa.
- One client had been in the U.S. for a couple of weeks, while the other had been there about 45 days.
- The attorney did not provide details on their work but said they planned to go home soon.
- Schrank told reporters that some detained workers illegally crossed the border, while others entered legally with expired visas or visa waivers prohibiting work.
- Some detained individuals worked for the battery manufacturer, while others were employed by contractors and subcontractors at the construction site.
- Schrank said he didn’t know precisely how many of the 475 detained were Korean nationals but that they made up a majority.
- No one has yet been charged with any crimes, and the investigation is ongoing.
- South Korea’s Foreign Minister Cho Hyun said Saturday that more than 300 South Koreans were among the 475 people detained.
- Schrank explained it was not a simple roundup but a multimonth criminal investigation where evidence was developed, interviews conducted, documents gathered, and presented to a court for a judicial search warrant.
- Most detainees were taken to an immigration detention center in Folkston, Georgia, near the Florida state line.
- President Donald Trump’s administration has undertaken sweeping ICE operations as part of a mass deportation agenda.
- Immigration officers have raided farms, construction sites, restaurants, and auto repair shops.
- The Pew Research Center cited preliminary Census Bureau data saying the U.S. labor force lost more than 1.2 million immigrants from January through July.
- The Democratic Party of Georgia condemned the raid on Friday.
- DPAG chair Charlie Bailey called the raids “politically-motivated fear tactics designed to terrorize people who work hard for a living, power our economy and contribute to the communities across Georgia that they have made their homes.”
- Kemp and other Georgia Republican officials issued statements saying all employers in the state were expected to follow the law.
- The Hyundai site sits on 3,000 acres (1,214 hectares) in a largely rural area of Bryan County.
- The site draws workers from several surrounding counties and communities including Savannah.
- Sammie Rentz opened the Viet Huong Supermarket less than 3 miles (4.8 kilometers) from the Hyundai site six months ago.
- The store sells American and Asian products, but Rentz mostly relies on Hyundai workers seeking foods imported from Korea.
- Rentz said “Koreans keep this store running” and worried business might not bounce back after falling off sharply since the raid.
- Ellabell resident Tanya Cox lives less than a mile from the site and had no ill feelings toward Korean nationals or immigrant workers.
- Cox noted few neighbors were employed there and felt more construction jobs at the battery plant should have gone to local residents.
- Cox said “I don’t see how it’s brought a lot of jobs to our community or nearby communities” and noted noise replaced bird chirping when the plant operates at night.
- Hyundai began producing electric vehicles at the site last September.
- Hyundai Motor Group Executive Chairman Euisun Chung credited President Trump with the company’s decision to create more American jobs by building an EV factory in Georgia during a White House appearance.
- Chung said at the March event: “Our decision to invest in Savannah, Georgia, creating more than 8,500 American jobs, was initiated during my meeting with President Trump in Seoul in 2019.”
- The battery plant operated by HL-GA Battery Co., a joint venture by Hyundai and LG Energy Solution, is slated to open next year.
- Search warrant affidavits indicated agents wanted employment records, personnel files, payroll info, bank account info, timecards, video/photos of workers, immigration documents, Social Security cards, visas, passports, birth certificates, and records about ownership/management of construction companies/contractors.
- Documents included names and photos of four people identified as “target persons.”
- LG stated it was “closely monitoring the situation and gathering all relevant details” and couldn’t immediately confirm how many of its employees or Hyundai workers had been detained.
- Plant spokesperson Bianca Johnson said operations at Hyundai’s EV manufacturing plant weren’t interrupted by the raid.
- Hyundai Motor Company stated none of its employees had been detained as far as it knew and would review practices to ensure suppliers/subcontractors follow U.S. employment laws.
- Hyundai’s statement said: “Hyundai has zero tolerance for those who don’t follow the law.”
- HL-GA Battery Co. did not immediately respond to a request for comment but previously stated it’s “cooperating fully with the appropriate authorities.”
- Those arrested who fight deportation may be detained as cases wind through immigration court.
- The number of people in ICE custody topped 60,000 in August, an all-time high.
