# SK Battery

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2021-04-05

**Source:** [SK Battery threatens to abandon Georgia plant if Biden doesn't side with them | FOX 5 Atlanta](https://www.fox5atlanta.com/news/sk-battery-threatens-to-abandon-ga-plant-if-biden-doesnt-side-with-them)  ·  `page_049982`

## Facts

- SK Battery is threatening to stop work on its promised factory in Jackson County and leave the country unless President Joe Biden sides with them.
- President Biden has until April 11 to reverse a decision punishing SK over allegations it stole trade secrets from another Korean competitor to build the plant.
- The Jackson County plant investment is valued at $2.6 billion and is expected to provide an estimated 2,600 green jobs for Georgia.
- State and local leaders provided one of the biggest tax breaks in state history to secure the plant: $300 million in tax abatements and free land.
- The plant was scheduled to start producing batteries for electric vehicles in 2022.
- In February, SK Battery's parent company SK Innovation faced a ruling from the United States International Trade Commission (USITC).
- The USITC ruled that SK tried to destroy evidence it stole 22 trade secrets from rival Korean battery manufacturer LG, now called LG Energy Solutions.
- The punishment includes a 10-year ban on the battery technology SK is accused of stealing, which would essentially shut down the Jackson County operation.
- SK denied wrongdoing and urged the White House to veto the USITC ruling.
- An SK spokesperson told Reuters that if Biden does not side with them, the company is reviewing options to move its US battery production to Europe or China.
- Federal authorities report catching Korean nationals trying to sneak into the country to help build the plant, allegedly taking jobs from American workers.
- Many neighborhoods surrounding the plant have homes occupied by multiple overseas workers shuttled back and forth to the site.
- SK stated that around 250 Americans currently work at the plant, with many more assisting with construction.
- Many Georgia politicians are lobbying on behalf of SK, fearing that punishing the company would force the United States to rely more on Chinese-made batteries for the EV market.
- LG Energy Solutions took out a full-page ad in the Atlanta Journal-Constitution highlighting the possibility of Georgia receiving one of its two new US battery plants.
- Denise Gray, president of LG Energy Solutions Michigan Tech Center, stated her company will not leave Georgia without jobs and is ready to provide batteries SK was supposed to build for the Ford F-150 electric pickup and VW ID.4 SUV.
- The government ruling grants SK a four-year grace period to fulfill the Ford contract and a two-year grace period for the VW contract.
- SK and LG have attempted to negotiate a settlement regarding the trade secret dispute.
- Korean press reports indicate LG is asking for 3 trillion won (approximately $2.3 billion), close to the plant's total value, while SK countered with 1 trillion won (around $888 million).
- Gray stated she cannot confirm the accuracy of the reported settlement numbers but emphasized her company's desire for a settlement to secure jobs in the US and Georgia.
- SK was granted a four-year exemption to build batteries for the new electric Ford F-150.
- Commerce city manager James Wascher emailed FOX 5 expressing hope that the Biden Administration will use its authority to overturn the ITC decision and support job creation commitments.
- Wascher noted the dispute should be settled in federal court, where damages can be awarded if wrongdoing is proven at trial.
- A settlement between SK and LG is currently viewed as the only way to ensure the future of the Jackson County plant.
- Gray agreed that a presidential thumbs-up could speed up a settlement, while a thumbs-down could delay it indefinitely.
