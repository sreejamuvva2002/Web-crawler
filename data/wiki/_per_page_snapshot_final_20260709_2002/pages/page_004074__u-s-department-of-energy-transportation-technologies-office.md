# U.S. Department of Energy Transportation Technologies Office

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [Lightweight Materials for Cars and Trucks | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/vehicles/lightweight-materials-cars-and-trucks)  ·  `page_004074`

## Facts

- Advanced materials are essential for boosting the fuel economy of modern automobiles while maintaining safety and performance.
- A 10% reduction in vehicle weight can result in a 6%-8% fuel economy improvement.
- Replacing cast iron and traditional steel components with lightweight materials such as high-strength steel, magnesium (Mg) alloys, aluminum (Al) alloys, carbon fiber, and polymer composites can directly reduce the weight of a vehicle's body and chassis by up to 50 percent.
- Using lightweight components and high-efficiency engines enabled by advanced materials in one quarter of the U.S. fleet could save more than 5 billion gallons of fuel annually by 2030.
- Lightweight structural materials allow cars to carry additional advanced emission control systems, safety devices, and integrated electronic systems without increasing overall vehicle weight.
- Lightweight materials are especially important for hybrid electric, plug-in hybrid electric, and electric vehicles.
- Using lightweight materials in these vehicles can offset the weight of power systems such as batteries and electric motors, improving efficiency and increasing all-electric range.
- The use of lightweight materials could result in needing a smaller and lower cost battery while keeping the all-electric range of plug-in vehicles constant.
- Research and development into lightweight materials is essential for lowering their cost, increasing their ability to be recycled, enabling their integration into vehicles, and maximizing fuel economy benefits.
- The Transportation Technologies Office (TTO) works to improve these materials in four ways: increasing understanding through modeling and computational materials science; improving properties like strength, stiffness, and ductility; improving manufacturing regarding material cost, production rate, or yield; and developing alloys of advanced materials.
- In the short term, replacing heavy steel components with high-strength steel, aluminum, or glass fiber-reinforced polymer composites can decrease component weight by 10-60 percent.
- Scientists already understand the properties of these short-term materials and associated manufacturing processes, but researchers are working to lower their cost and improve processes for joining, modeling, and recycling.
- In the longer term, advanced materials such as magnesium and carbon fiber reinforced composites could reduce the weight of some components by 50-75 percent.
- The Office is working to increase knowledge of these long-term materials' chemical and physical properties and reduce their cost.
- Further developing advanced materials requires increasing understanding of their composition and morphology, with computational materials science speeding up the process by simulating experiments.
- Computational approaches can bring advanced materials like magnesium into the market much faster than past materials and help create vehicle designs that maximize their potential.
- To improve research tools, TTO works with the Materials Genome Initiative, an interagency effort supporting reduced time to develop advanced materials through integrated computation, experimentation, and data.
- Work supported by TTO has developed computational tools enabling improvements in joining methods, corrosion prevention, and predictive models.
- TTO collaborates with DOE's National Laboratories on lightweight materials research.
- TTO collaborates with CMEI's Advanced Materials and Manufacturing Technologies Office on lightweight materials research.
- TTO works through cross-cutting government/industry partnerships including the U.S. DRIVE Partnership focusing on light-duty vehicles.
- TTO works through cross-cutting government/industry partnerships including the 21st Century Truck Partnership focusing on heavy-duty vehicles.
- TTO works through cross-cutting government/industry partnerships including the US Automotive Materials Partnership (USAMP).
- A key research and development goal is to validate by 2015 the ability to reduce the weight of a passenger vehicle body and chassis system by 50% compared to a 2002 vehicle.
- This 50% weight reduction goal requires cost-effectiveness and recyclability of the materials.
- Results of these R&D activities are described annually at the Annual Merit Review and Peer Evaluation Meeting and in the annual Progress Report.
- These reports serve as a benchmark for current state-of-the-art technologies and technical goals in Light-Duty Vehicles Technical Requirements and Gaps for Lightweight and Propulsion Materials, and Trucks and Heavy-Duty Vehicles Technical Requirements and Gaps for Lightweight and Propulsion Materials.
- Researchers present summaries of VTO-supported lightweight materials projects at the Annual Merit Review and Peer Evaluation.
- Selected presentations from the 2014 Merit Review include Materials Technologies R&D Overview, Multi-Material Lightweight Vehicles, and High-Speed Joining of Dissimilar Alloy Aluminum Tailor Welded Blanks.
- Magnesium alloys can achieve a mass reduction of 30-70%.
- Carbon fiber composites can achieve a mass reduction of 50-70%.
- Aluminum and Al matrix composites can achieve a mass reduction of 30-60%.
- Titanium can achieve a mass reduction of 40-55%.
- Glass fiber composites can achieve a mass reduction of 25-35%.
- Advanced high strength steel can achieve a mass reduction of 15-25%.
- High strength steel can achieve a mass reduction of 10-28%.
