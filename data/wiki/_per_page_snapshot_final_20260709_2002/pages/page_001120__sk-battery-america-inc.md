## Facts

- A Commerce electric vehicle battery manufacturing plant laid off over a third of its workforce.
- 958 employees were let go at the SK Battery America plant.
- Impacted workers will still be paid through May 6.
- Jackson County Commission Chairman Marty Clark called the layoffs difficult news for the community and noted that while Jackson County's economic outlook is stable, the decision affects hardworking families.
- The Korean company opened the $2.6-billion plant in Commerce, Georgia, in January of 2022.
- Georgia U.S. Senators Jon Ossoff (D) and Raphael Warnock (D) issued statements on Friday regarding the layoffs.
- Senator Jon Ossoff stated that battery manufacturing jobs are gone and blamed Trump's war on electric vehicles for hurting Georgia's economy, noting Georgians are losing jobs.
- Senator Raphael Warnock said the Trump Administration is putting politics over people, praised Georgia workers for making the state a leader in advanced manufacturing, criticized the administration for slashing clean energy investments and imposing harmful tariff taxes that make manufacturing in Georgia more expensive, and vowed to fight for Georgia workers and protect investments.
- SK Americas spokesperson Joe Guy Collier stated the workforce reduction was made to align operations to market conditions.
- Collier said SK Battery America remains committed to Georgia and building a robust U.S. supply chain for advanced battery manufacturing, and is pursuing future customers including the Battery Electric Storage System arena.
- The Associated Press provided original reporting on March 6, 2026 @ 4:50 p.m. by Alexa St. John and Jeff Amy.
- SK Battery America Inc. laid off nearly 1,000 workers at its manufacturing plant northeast of Atlanta on Friday amid automakers’ changing electrification plans and uncertain consumer demand for EVs.
- The layoffs mark the last working day for 958 plant employees, about 37% of its workforce, according to a WARN notice filed by human resources chief Chuck Moore.
- The plant will continue to employ about 1,600 workers after the layoffs.
- SK notably supplied the Ford F-150 Lightning electric pickup truck before Ford announced plans in December to cancel the fully electric version of the truck.
- The U.S. electric vehicle market is at a standstill as the Trump administration steers federal support away from electrification toward lax automotive emissions policies and an agenda supporting oil and gas industries.
- The City of Commerce and the Jackson County commission chair did not immediately respond to requests for comment.
- Ford scrapped the fully-electric version of its F-150 pickup in December, opting for an extended-range version, and a Ford spokesperson declined to comment on supplier personnel actions.
- SK and Ford previously invested $11.4 billion in joint battery plants in the U.S., but SK ended the joint venture in December.
- SK is also a supplier to Volkswagen.
- SK has invested significantly in Jackson County, Georgia, as automakers developed EVs and the Biden administration supported a domestic EV supply chain.
- In June 2020, SK announced plans to pour $940 million to expand its battery manufacturing presence in Atlanta, which Gov. Brian Kemp’s office said would create 600 jobs.
- SK and Hyundai are still jointly building a $5 billion battery factory near Cartersville, northwest of Atlanta.
- Georgia has attracted massive EV manufacturing investments, including Rivian’s $5 billion factory and Hyundai’s own $7.6 billion factory complex.
- Few states benefited more than Georgia from Biden’s Inflation Reduction Act, which accelerated a rush of green energy projects.
- According to environmental business group E2, 33 additional EV-related projects were announced nationwide by the end of 2024, with Georgia projects topping $20 billion and pledging more than 25,000 jobs.
- Qcells, a unit of South Korea’s Hanwha Solutions, resumed normal production on Friday after temporarily reducing hours and pay last year due to U.S. customs officials detaining imported components for solar panels.
- EV demand has not met automakers’ ambitious expectations, with EVs accounting for about 8% of new vehicle sales in the U.S. in 2025, matching the prior year.
- Automakers have been reevaluating multibillion-dollar electrification plans as financial losses mount and consumer demand shifts.
- Manufacturers including Ford, General Motors, Stellantis, and others across the EV supply chain have reneged on factory, investment, and product plans, laid off workers, and pivoted to hybrid and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles.
- Hybrids and more efficient gasoline-powered vehicles are becoming more palatable for mainstream buyers concerned about EV driving range and charging infrastructure availability.
- Under President Donald Trump, Congress eliminated tax credits of up to $7,500 for consumers’ purchases of new or used EVs.
- The Trump administration announced plans to weaken fuel economy and greenhouse gas emissions rules for automakers, eliminating federal incentives for cleaner vehicle fleets.
