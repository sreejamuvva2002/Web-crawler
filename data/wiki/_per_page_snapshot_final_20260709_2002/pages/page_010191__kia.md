# Kia

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Which Kia Electric Cars Qualify for the EV Tax Credit – Guide](https://carinterior.alibaba.com/buyingguides/kia-evs-eligible-for-$7,500-tax-credit-(2025–2026))  ·  `page_010191`

## Facts

- The 2025–2026 Kia EV9 and certain trims of the 2025 Kia EV6 stand out for full eligibility for the federal EV tax credit.
- Both the EV9 and qualifying EV6 trims are assembled in West Point, Georgia.
- Other Kia models like the Niro EV generally do not qualify due to overseas manufacturing in South Korea.
- For leasing, even non-qualifying Kia models may offer the full $7,500 incentive under commercial fleet rules.
- Federal tax credit eligibility now hinges on where the car and its battery components are made rather than brand origin.
- The shift in policy opened the door for Korean automakers like Kia to qualify provided they meet domestic production thresholds.
- The current federal tax credit offers up to $7,500 per vehicle: $3,750 for final assembly in North America and another $3,750 if critical minerals and battery components meet sourcing requirements.
- As of early 2025, only the EV9 and specific EV6 trims satisfy both assembly and battery sourcing conditions.
- Hyundai Motor Group’s U.S.-assembled EVs were previously ineligible despite strong specifications.
- In 2025, the Georgia plant began producing the EV9 and adjusted EV6 output to meet federal criteria.
- The EV9 became a top consideration among three-row electric SUVs under the $80,000 MSRP price cap for credit eligibility.
- Kia provides a five-year/60,000-mile warranty on batteries and an eight-year/100,000-mile powertrain coverage.
- All trims of the 2025–2026 EV9 (Light, Wind, Wave, and GT-Line) assembled in West Point, Georgia are eligible for the full $7,500 credit assuming buyer income and MSRP thresholds are met.
- The EV9 features an 800V architecture enabling fast charging, high towing capacity up to 3,500 lbs, and standard advanced safety features.
- The base range of the EV9 is approximately 230–280 miles.
- Only certain 2025 EV6 trims qualify; the Light and Wind AWD models produced in Georgia are eligible while the high-performance GT trim imported from Korea is excluded.
- The EV6 GT trim hits 0–60 mph in 3.4 seconds and features V2L (vehicle-to-load) capability but has limited rear headroom and a smaller trunk than competitors.
- The Niro PHEV qualifies for a partial credit of $3,750 only if battery components meet sourcing rules, which is rarely the case.
- Both the Sorento PHEV and Sportage PHEV are plug-in hybrids assembled outside North America and do not qualify for the full credit, with partial eligibility being uncommon due to battery sourcing.
- At least 50% of battery components must originate in North America to qualify, increasing to 60% in 2026.
- Critical minerals requirements mandate that 80% be sourced from U.S. free-trade partners or extracted/recycled domestically for the second $3,750 portion of the credit.
- The MSRP cap for SUVs and vans is $80,000; the EV9 GT-Line with full options can approach this limit.
- Household income caps apply at up to $300,000 for joint filers and $225,000 for single filers.
- The EV9 starts around $56,000 and reaches $77,000 fully loaded.
- The U.S.-built EV6 starts near $53,000.
- Manufacturer-specific caps on tax credits have largely phased out, so Kia buyers are not limited by volume-based roll-offs.
- High demand for Georgia-built EV9s has led to extended wait times of 3–6 months in high-demand areas.
- The EV9 competes directly with the Ford Explorer EV (assembled in Oakville, Canada), Chevrolet Blazer EV (Hamtramck, MI), and Tesla Model Y (Austin, TX / Fremont, CA).
- The Kia EV6 is assembled in West Point, Georgia and Hwaseong, South Korea.
- Both the EV9 and EV6 earned Top Safety Pick+ from IIHS with standard features including forward collision warning, automatic emergency braking, lane keeping assist, and adaptive cruise control.
- Battery health monitoring is built into the MyKia app for Kia EVs.
- The tax credit is claimed when filing annual returns and requires documentation from the dealer, typically IRS Form 8936.
- Tax credit rules may change in late 2025, requiring buyers to verify eligibility close to the purchase date.
- When leasing, the tax credit typically goes to the leasing company but savings are passed to the buyer via lower monthly payments.
