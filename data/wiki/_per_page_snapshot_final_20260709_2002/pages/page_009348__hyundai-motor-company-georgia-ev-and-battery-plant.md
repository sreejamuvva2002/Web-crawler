# Hyundai Motor Company Georgia EV and Battery Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-08-24

**Source:** [Permit for Hyundai's Georgia wells to get second look from feds](https://bucswire.usatoday.com/story/news/environment/2024/08/24/permit-for-hyundais-georgia-wells-to-get-second-look-from-feds/74925297007/)  ·  `page_009348`

## Facts

- The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers is launching a “reevaluation” of a key environmental permit for four proposed Floridan Aquifer wells.
- The wells are intended to supply up to 6.6 million gallons of water per day to Hyundai Motor Company’s Bryan County manufacturing facility and related development around the nearly 2,500-acre site.
- USACE's move is in response to a threatened legal challenge by a coastal Georgia conservation group regarding regulatory approval for the South Korean automaker’s electric vehicle and battery operation near Savannah.
- State and local development officials omitted the site’s projected water use in their application for a federal permit required by the U.S. Clean Water Act.
- At maximum capacity, the four Bulloch County wells would remove more than 6.6 million gallons per day from the aquifer.
- USACE determined that new information has surfaced regarding the effects the project may have on municipal and private water supplies, warranting a reevaluation of its permit decision.
- The Ogeechee Riverkeeper organization informed USACE in June that it intended to challenge the October 2022 approval of a permit for the $7.6 billion Hyundai project.
- The organization accused USACE of “not completing required steps and overlooking water supply concerns during the permitting process” for the site.
- The Clean Water Act requires USACE to collect pertinent information and analyze permit requests that impact or disturb “waters of the U.S.,” including wetlands.
- The organization challenged USACE’s findings that it would be reasonable to assume that the Bryan County water supply is adequate to support the plant and related growth without requiring water withdrawals or a permit from the EPD.
- Applicants for the federal permit included Savannah Harbor-Interstate 16 Corridor Joint Development Authority Secretary Trip Tollison and Pat Wilson, commissioner of the Georgia Department of Economic Development.
- Tollison and Wilson knew months before the permit was approved that the Hyundai plant and related development would require up to 6.6 million gallons of water per day and the drilling of four new wells.
- This water requirement information was included in a confidential “letter of intent” dated April 25, 2022, addressed to Robert Boehringer, managing partner at consulting firm KPMG International, and signed by Tollison and Wilson.
- The water needs were not mentioned in the federal permit application submitted on June 7, 2022, which was also signed by Tollison and Wilson.
- USACE approved a revised version of the permit on Oct. 4, 2022, including findings that no additional water or wells would be needed.
- A Friday letter from USACE requests the JDA provide an impact assessment of the Megasite’s water supply wells to show they will not impact the Floridan Aquifer, its users, aquatic resources, and environment.
- The required impact assessment must include any groundwater and surface water modeling or data collected by the JDA.
- USACE will assess the submitted report to determine if the permit needs adjustment, and the JDA may be required to compensate for any resulting impacts.
- The Ogeechee Riverkeeper organization welcomed the Corps' decision to revisit the permit.
- Damon Mullis, riverkeeper and executive director of ORK, stated that these considerations should have been taken into account from day one and urged the Corps to independently vet information transparently during the reevaluation.
- A spokeswoman in USACE’s Savannah office did not respond to a request for comment about the reevaluation or how it could affect the Hyundai project.
- Trip Tollison defended the application process, stating that the JDA has been working with local governments and the state on water service since the Georgia Environmental Protection Division is responsible for drinking water and water usage, not the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers.
- Experts and federal agency comments suggest Tollison’s defense missed the point, as the Clean Water Act directs USACE to account for “probable impact including cumulative impacts” on factors including “water supply and conservation.”
- Stan Meiburg, former deputy administrator at the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and now executive director of the Sabin Family Center for Environment and Sustainability at Wake Forest University, explained that while USACE cannot dictate where a project gets its water, it must weigh those factors when reviewing applications for permits under Section 404 of the Clean Water Act.
- The EPA concurred with Meiburg’s assessment via an email from its Washington office, confirming that the Corps and EPA consider potential effects on potable water supplies when reviewing projects for compliance with Clean Water Act Section 404 regulations.
- The Clean Water Act regulates the discharge of dredged or fill material into “waters of the United States,” including wetlands.
- The Clean Water Act applied to the Hyundai project because the site contained more than 300 acres of “jurisdictional wetlands” as defined by the law.
- USACE is charged with weighing the impact on waters around the site, including those potentially affected by the new wells.
- Significant withdrawals from underground aquifers can impact surface waters, which are at the heart of Section 404 permits.
- In its Friday letter, USACE directed Tollison and Wilson to provide an assessment of effects on municipal and private water supplies, including whether the anticipated drawdown of the Floridan aquifer would result in any drainage of aquatic resources.
