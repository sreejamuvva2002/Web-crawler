# Metro Site Fabricators

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2024-11-18

**Source:** [Metro Site wins $31M settlement after lithium-ion battery fire - Construction & Demolition Recycling](https://www.cdrecycler.com/news/metro-site-wins-31m-settlement-after-lithium-ion-battery-fire/)  ·  `page_060596`

## Facts

- Scott Ledford is the owner of Commerce, Georgia-based waste and recycling company Metro Site Fabricators.
- Metro Site Fabricators received a $31 million settlement from SK Battery America Inc. after a 16-month litigation process.
- SK Battery America Inc. is an electric vehicle battery manufacturer that operates a north Georgia factory.
- The settlement was awarded following the improper disposal of batteries that sparked a devastating fire at Metro Site's material recovery facility.
- Ledford shared his story at C&D World in January, and Waste Today reported on the fire in its March issue.
- Metro Site lost its entire facility after the lithium-ion battery fire.
- Investigators believe the blaze started after a drop-off of more than 200 batteries short-circuited upon contact, causing thermal runaway.
- Ledford alleged SK Battery mishandled the batteries because Metro Site is not permitted to handle batteries among its recyclables and did not know it had received them.
- SK Battery America Inc. is a subsidiary of Korean conglomerate SK Group.
- SK Battery did not admit liability as part of the settlement but agreed to pay $20 million in punitive damages and $11 in tort-related claims and damages to Metro Site.
- Before the fire, Metro Site had segregated waste received from SK from the rest of the waste in the facility.
- Ledford stated that his team was able to identify lithium-ion battery pouches as being manufactured from SK's facility, noting this is rare for identifying fire origins in recycling centers.
- As part of the litigation process, Ledford pushed for a non-confidentiality agreement so he could discuss the case and help other operators prepare for fire hazards.
- Ledford advises operators doing business with groups that deliver hazardous materials to hire an attorney to draft agreements covering responsibilities and liabilities.
- He recommends that in the event of a lithium-ion battery fire, operators should secure all evidence, ensure it is handled by proper authorities, and quickly hire an attorney well-versed in this type of litigation.
- Ledford noted he spoke with his attorney seven days a week for 16 months during the case.
- Moving forward, Ledford plans to increase his visibility by speaking at waste industry meetings and conventions and making himself available to others with similar experiences.
