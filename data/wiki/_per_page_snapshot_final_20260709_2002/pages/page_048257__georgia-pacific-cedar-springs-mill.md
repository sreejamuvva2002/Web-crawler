# Georgia-Pacific Cedar Springs Mill

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** 2025-07-29

**Source:** [Economic impact study on Georgia-Pacific Cedar Springs closure paints bleak picture for Early County](https://www.developearly.com/post/economic-impact-study-on-georgia-pacific-cedar-springs-closure-paints-bleak-picture-for-early-county)  ·  `page_048257`

## Facts

- Georgia-Pacific announced on May 14 that most positions at its Cedar Springs mill would be eliminated by Aug. 1, with plans to permanently close the site this year.
- The plant closure will eliminate approximately 535 jobs in Early County.
- The closure is expected to strip away about $6.5 million from tax revenue in Early County, according to an analysis completed by David Bridges.
- David Bridges, former president of Abraham Baldwin Agricultural College and a rural Georgia advocate, hosted the economic outlook town hall for the Early County Development Authority.
- Bridges stated the closure will have devastating ripple effects across Georgia, Alabama, and Florida.
- Early County historically generated sales tax returns of about $5 million annually, compared to an average of $2 million from surrounding counties, largely due to Georgia-Pacific's retail value and economic movement.
- The county faces a property tax shortfall equivalent to replacing two-to-three mills.
- Bridges' analysis utilized historical data from Georgia-Pacific, county finances, and expertise from Georgia State University and the University of Georgia.
- Nearly all areas of Early County will be affected by the closure, including retail, schools, health care, and banking sectors.
- Blakely, the Early County seat, will lose about $200,000 in revenue from Georgia-Pacific's natural gas purchases.
- The local school system will experience revenue loss and likely see enrollment decline as families are forced to relocate.
- An additional 206 jobs are predicted to be lost in the county due to income loss, requiring layoffs by the city of Blakely and the school district.
- Local hospitals and medical centers will lose approximately 535 privately insured patients, significantly impacting their revenue streams.
- The timber industry is experiencing a chain reaction as far north as LaGrange, with loggers forced to transport wood farther distances and facing lower prices due to lost abundant supply.
- A logger from LaGrange reported that the closure has killed his market.
- Georgia-Pacific's Cedar Springs mill closure was in development for five years, accelerated by Hurricane Michael.
- U.S. paper and paperboard capacity declined by 1.6% in 2023 to 79.7 million tons, compared to an average annual decline of 0.9% since 2014, according to the American Forest & Paper Association.
- The plant was largely depreciated and lacked necessary investments for compliance and operation over recent years.
- Retired mill worker William Cleveland noted that workers sensed the closure when repairs stopped on some equipment.
- Bridges stated it is unlikely a new paper producer will move into the mill due to its depreciation.
- Dave Atkins, chairman of the Blakely Downtown Development Authority, emphasized the need to diversify revenue streams and capitalize on local assets like the river.
- Early County Commission Chairman Hank Jester highlighted White Oak Pastures in Bluffton as a model for diversification beyond traditional beef production through regenerative practices, retail products, and agritourism.
- City and county leaders are navigating budget processes to make difficult financial choices following the closure.
- Early County Commissioner Jeffery Haynes is seeking federal aid, citing past recoveries from Hurricane Michael and the COVID-19 pandemic.
- State Rep. Gerald Greene (R-Cuthbert) and Sen. Freddie Powell Sims (D-Dawson) attended the town hall and pledged to seek state help for Early County.
- Sen. Sims encouraged residents to approach the Capitol about the county's needs.
- Bridges urged making a compelling case to the governor for short-term help, emphasizing that the community must move forward rather than give up.
