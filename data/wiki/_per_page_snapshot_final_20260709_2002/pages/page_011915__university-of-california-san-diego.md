# University of California San Diego

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-02

**Source:** [US scientists turn dead EV battery waste into high-performing new battery](https://interestingengineering.com/energy/new-recycling-method-for-old-ev-batteries)  ·  `page_011915`

## Facts

- Researchers at the University of California San Diego developed a new upcycling process that converts retired lithium iron phosphate (LFP) battery cathodes into more powerful lithium manganese iron phosphate (LMFP) materials.
- The upgraded LMFP material can store more energy while maintaining the safety and long life characteristics that made LFP batteries popular.
- This recycling method upgrades existing cathodes into a better material rather than breaking old batteries down into raw materials to make new cathodes.
- The process could cut down on waste, use less energy, and make retired EV batteries more valuable.
- LFP batteries are among the most commonly used in electric vehicles and large energy storage systems because they cost less by avoiding expensive metals like cobalt or nickel.
- LFP batteries currently make up almost half of the world’s lithium-ion battery market.
- Most traditional recycling methods use high heat or strong chemicals, which are not environmentally friendly, use a lot of energy, and create waste and emissions.
- Wei Li, the study’s first author and a postdoctoral researcher in Zheng Chen’s lab at UC San Diego, explained that conventional processes are not environmentally friendly due to high energy use and waste generation.
- Chen’s research group previously developed a method to restore old LFP batteries to fresh LFP material, but the recycled batteries retained the same chemistry.
- The new upcycling process offers a more valuable end use for spent batteries by upgrading them to LMFP instead of just regenerating them to LFP.
- The recycling process begins by opening battery packs and removing the tightly rolled layers inside known as a jelly roll.
- Researchers cut these layers into sheets, soak them in water, and gently shake the material to separate the cathode coating from the aluminum foil.
- The separated aluminum foil can also be recycled separately.
- The remaining cathode material is dried and ground into a fine black powder.
- Researchers add lithium, manganese, and phosphate salts to provide the necessary ingredients for LMFP production.
- A major challenge was that the added salts and original LFP material have incompatible crystal structures, which would cause non-uniform atomic distribution and worse electrochemical performance if mixed directly.
- To solve this, researchers first prepare an intermediate compound called lithium manganese phosphate (LMP) with a crystal structure similar to LFP.
- The powder is finely ground and mixed, then heated to allow LMP to form and mix evenly with the LFP.
- During heating, manganese atoms slowly replace some iron atoms, creating a uniform LMFP structure.
- A thin layer of carbon forms around each particle during the process, which helps conduct electricity and protects the material during many charging cycles.
- The upgraded LMFP material stored more energy than the original LFP while maintaining its durability and safety.
- Researchers tested the method using LFP batteries sourced from different manufacturers.
- The team scaled up the process to kilogram quantities.
- The recycled material performed well in both small lab coin cells and larger pouch cells, which are similar to batteries used in electric vehicles and large energy storage systems.
- The research team plans to make the process more efficient and extract more material from it.
- They also aim to improve the material’s composition and structure to boost performance and prepare the technology for large-scale recycling.
- The research was published in the journal Joule.
