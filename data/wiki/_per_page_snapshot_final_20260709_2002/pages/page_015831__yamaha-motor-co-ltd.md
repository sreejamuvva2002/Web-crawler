# Yamaha Motor Co., Ltd

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Yamaha relocating HQ from California to Georgia](https://moneywise.com/news/yamaha-just-ditched-california-after-50-years-for-georgia-and-its-not-alone-breaking-down-the-corporate-exodus)  ·  `page_015831`

## Facts

- Yamaha Motor Co. is relocating its U.S. headquarters from Cypress, California to Kennesaw, Georgia.
- The relocation is cited as necessary to cut costs and streamline operations amid rising tariff pressures.
- The move begins this year and runs through 2028.
- Yamaha will sell off its California fixed assets outright, including land, offices, and warehouses.
- On Feb. 26, Yamaha stated it is "undertaking structural reforms aimed at improving the profitability of its U.S. operations in response to cost increases resulting from U.S. tariffs and changes in the market environment."
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp welcomed the news on March 10, calling Georgia the "No. 1 state for business" and stating it is a testament to what the state offers job creators.
- Research by the Public Policy Institute of California shows 789 companies moved their headquarters out of California from 2011 to 2021, roughly 1.9% of all headquarters in the state at the start of that period.
- The number of firms moving out of California increased over time while the number moving in declined.
- California lost an estimated 77,600 jobs due to those headquarters moves between 2011 and 2021.
- In 2025, Chevron, John Paul Mitchell Systems, and Realtor.com announced relocations to Texas.
- In-N-Out Burger announced a move to Tennessee, with its CEO citing the difficulty of raising a family and running a business in California.
- Recent California defectors include Oracle, Tesla, SpaceX, Charles Schwab, Hewlett Packard Enterprise, and Palantir.
- U-Haul ranked California dead last among "growth states" for the fifth consecutive year for 2024.
- Atlas Van Lines reported that 60% of its 2024 California shipments were outbound compared to just 40% inbound.
- United Van Lines found similar findings with 58% outbound to 42% inbound in 2024.
- California faces a projected $50 billion to $70 billion deficit in 2025-2026, a near reversal from the $97 billion surplus recorded in 2021-2022.
- California levies the highest state income tax rate in the nation at 13.3% on top earners compared to 5.19% in Georgia.
- California's corporate income tax rate of 8.84% is among the steepest in the country.
- According to the Hoover Institution, businesses leave California due to taxes, regulations, litigation costs, labor costs, energy and utility costs, and employee cost of living.
- California's permitting process is widely regarded as one of the most cumbersome in the U.S., leading to high compliance costs.
- Georgia has been recognized as No. 1 for business by Area Development magazine for 12 consecutive years.
- Gov. Brian Kemp stated Georgia's approach is to "trust job creators and get government out of the way."
- Kemp identified the top three things that kill deals in Georgia as a burdensome tax code, slow permits, and lack of available sites.
- Georgia invested heavily in its Rural Site Development Initiative, partnering with local development authorities to grade land, install infrastructure, and create ready-made sites before companies look at them.
- Through the Georgia Ready for Accelerated Development (GRAD) program, the state does upfront heavy lifting so businesses are not waiting on infrastructure when ready to build.
- Migration data shows a large decline in higher-income adults moving out of California, with 28% fewer leaving in 2024 versus the peak in 2021.
- California's population has grown recently due to international immigration offsetting domestic outflows.
- California retains advantages including the largest U.S. state economy, world-class research universities, deep venture capital networks, and unmatched tech talent concentration.
- Researchers at the Public Policy Institute of California cautioned that headquarter relocations should not be viewed in isolation; far more headquarters launched (17%) or closed (30%) over the same period than relocated.
- California had the highest net outflow of domestic companies across the U.S. from 2015 to 2025.
- Yamaha's decision is a business decision weighing costs against returns rather than a dramatic act of protest.
