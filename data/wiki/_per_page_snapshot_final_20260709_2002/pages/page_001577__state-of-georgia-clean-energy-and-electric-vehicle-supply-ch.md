# State of Georgia Clean Energy and Electric Vehicle Supply Chain

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [The top state for clean energy jobs could lose them all to GOP budget bill cuts | EnergySage](https://www.energysage.com/news/ira-tax-credit-cut-impact-clean-energy-georgia/)  ·  `page_001577`

## Facts

- Georgia is potentially facing the greatest loss of clean energy jobs in the nation if the House GOP’s “big, beautiful bill,” which is now in the Senate, is passed.
- A new report released by Senator Raphael Warnock estimates that as many as 42,000 jobs, created by 51 clean energy projects that represent more than $28 billion in investments statewide, will be in jeopardy if the clean energy tax credits included in the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) are repealed.
- The report highlights Georgia’s position as the top beneficiary of IRA incentives, leading in clean energy job growth with the most announced or advanced projects underway.
- Following the IRA’s passage in 2022, Georgia has seen more than $4.50 in private investment for every $1 of federal funding received.
- Senator Raphael Warnock stated that Georgia businesses and workers agree that investments should be protected because they are spurring good-paying manufacturing jobs across the state, supporting Georgia jobs, manufacturing, and innovation, but those good-paying clean energy jobs are under threat.
- Warnock said we should not be sacrificing Georgia jobs for tax cuts for the well-off and cannot allow political games to derail a generational economic transformation that is already putting people to work and creating economic opportunity.
- Georgia has led the nation in renewable energy growth since former President Joe Biden signed the IRA into law in 2022.
- Sen. Warnock’s analysis details the Peach State’s massive influx of public and private investment in domestic manufacturing since the beginning of 2023.
- Georgia’s $28 billion in investments is second only to New York’s $115 billion.
- While 42,000 new jobs have been created in Georgia—the most of any state—that figure is poised to grow significantly in the next few years as more clean energy projects come online.
- According to the Department of Energy, Georgia’s clean energy workforce is set to be “turbocharged” by an estimated $180 million investment in large-scale clean power generation and storage through 2030.
- The majority of these jobs and investments are primarily in Georgia’s rural areas and counties, where families with incomes below the national median and the lowest rates of higher education levels live.
- Most of those areas also happen to be within Republican-led congressional districts.
- Three of the top ten GOP districts for clean energy job growth are in Georgia: Rep. Buddy Carter’s 1st District, where a Hyundai Metaplant is located in Bryan County.
- Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene’s 14th District is home to a QCells solar factory in Dalton.
- A new Qcells solar plant has been built in Georgia's 11th congressional district represented by Rep. Barry Loudermilk.
- Rep. Mike Collins’ 10th District is where SK Battery is based in Commerce, Georgia.
- More than 85% of funding from the IRA has flowed to red states, according to E2.
- According to Sen. Warnock’s report, government funding for clean energy in Georgia jumped from just over $200 million in 2022 to more than $1.1 billion in 2023, and $2.3 billion in 2024.
- The report calls out the 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production tax credit as a provision that incentivizes domestic production of clean energy components.
- The report calls out the 48C Advanced Energy Project tax credit as a provision that supports investment in facilities that produce these components.
- The report calls out the 30D New Clean Vehicle credit as a provision that provides up to $7,500 to qualified buyers of new clean energy (EV) vehicles that are assembled in North America.
- These tax credits have been instrumental in onshoring the clean energy supply chain and bolstering factory buildout in Georgia, turning it into the EV and domestic manufacturing hub it is today.
- If President Trump gets his way in repealing what he calls the “Green New Scam,” Georgia’s clean manufacturing investment boom could be stopped in its tracks.
- The current budget reconciliation bill would eliminate or accelerate the phaseout of many IRA credits to pay for the $4.5 trillion tax cuts for the wealthy and large corporations that Trump is calling for, as well as introduce a number of stricter eligibility requirements.
- Among the proposed changes to IRA, the Investment tax credit (48 ITC) would be reduced to 6% by 2030 and eliminated by 2032.
- 45X credits would begin to phase out by 25% per year for components sold after December 31, 2029, before expiring after 2031.
- The consumer tax credit for new EVs would phase out by the end of 2026.
- The current legislation would impose new restrictions on project financing, credit transferability, and eligibility for projects with ties to “foreign entities of concern” (FEOCs), such as China, which dominates the supply chain for raw materials and components used in clean energy technologies.
- Clean energy projects must begin construction no later than 60 days after the bill’s passage and be placed in service by the end of 2028—a timeline that could be unworkable for many announced or early-stage projects.
- Earlier this year, battery manufacturers Freyr Battery and Aspen Aerogels scrapped plans to build new factories in Georgia—projects that would have created 1,400 jobs and brought nearly $3 billion in investment to the state.
- These losses constitute just a fraction of the $14 billion in clean energy projects and 10,000 jobs which have been already cancelled since Trump took office in January, according to E2.
- Qcells continues to expand its new solar manufacturing facility in Cartersville, which is bringing approximately 3.3 GW of annual solar capacity and up to 2,000 jobs to the state.
- In Cedartown, SolarCycle plans to invest $344 million in a solar glass manufacturing facility, the first of its kind in the U.S. to make new solar glass out of recycled materials from solar panels.
- The SolarCycle facility is expected to begin operations later this year and create more than 600 jobs.
- Hyundai's new $7.6 billion Metaplant America EV factory near Savannah and Qcells’ Dalton plant expansion are already transforming Georgia's manufacturing landscape and proving the economic power of American-made clean energy.
- IRA rollbacks would impact local solar installers, nonprofits, and families relying on its funding, who will feel the consequences of a repeal much sooner.
- Under the current tax bill, the 25D residential solar tax credit, which provides a credit to U.S. homeowners equal to 30% of their solar installation costs, would be eliminated by the end of the year.
- Clean energy credits for individuals such as 25D and 30D, along with a range of other IRA programs—from subsidies enabling low-income households to install rooftop solar to home energy rebates reducing the cost of electrification—are already making tangible impacts on people’s daily lives.
- Seth Gunning, CEO of Sunpath Solar, a local Georgia-based provider of solar energy solutions, says an IRA repeal would not only hurt his business, but the communities and homeowners he helps on an everyday basis.
- Gunning stated that many projects enabled by churches and organizations of faith around Georgia have been enabled by the Inflation Reduction Act's direct pay or elective pay provision, which allows nonprofits to capitalize on some of the tax incentives that they previously didn't have access to.
- Without credits like 25C, Gunning says those in need will be the ones excluded from the clean energy transition.
- Gunning noted the real impact is going to be to homeowners, farmers, local small business owners, and nonprofit faith communities who are already struggling to make ends meet and to pay increasingly high costs of power, stating that not having a way to access solar energy resources makes life that much more expensive.
- Gunning advised Congress that consistency and certainty are key, calling the immediate elimination of the residential tax credit and the 60-day requirement for commenced construction on the commercial tax credit really abrupt changes that make it hard for homeowners, businesses, nonprofits, and solar installers to effectively plan for the future.
- Gunning suggested providing a roadmap, ideally several years, to sunset those credits is much better for the industry to be able to get its feet fully under itself.
- The House budget bill would have serious consequences for Georgia if it retains these cuts to clean energy tax credits: ending the Inflation Reduction Act's tax incentives would jeopardize billions in investments, kill good-paying jobs, and raise household electric bills by around $143 more per year for the average American household.
