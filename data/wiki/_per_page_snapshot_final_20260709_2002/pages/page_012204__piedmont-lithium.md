# Piedmont Lithium

**Source:** [Push for new US lithium mine leaves some Americans wary - Tuko.co.ke](https://www.tuko.co.ke/business-economy/548315-push-lithium-leaves-americans-wary/)  ·  `page_012204`

## Facts

- Kristal Lee and her husband bought a house in Gaston County, North Carolina two years ago, envisioning it as a 'forever home'.
- A planned lithium mine is bringing Lee sleepless nights.
- The nearby area is earmarked for a $1.2 billion project to produce battery grade lithium for US electric vehicle supply chains.
- This site is one of only a few such sites in the country.
- Kristal Lee expressed anxiety about the Piedmont Lithium project, noting residents do not have an option to move right now due to the economy and inflation.
- President Joe Biden is working to build domestic EV and battery industries.
- Companies are seeking lithium supplies within the United States, which holds major deposits in Nevada, North Carolina, and California.
- Biden's aims include furthering the energy transition and lowering dependence on China, which supplies the bulk of global lithium-ion battery exports.
- Lithium-ion battery demand is projected to grow nearly 30 percent annually from 2022-2030, according to McKinsey & Company.
- Pushback against Piedmont and other mining companies underscores how officials' efforts leave some residents skeptical.
- Aimee Boulanger, executive director at the Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance (IRMA), identified water pollution and reduced water supplies as top mining risks.
- Boulanger stated that moving rock opens up land and risks soil and metals washing into streams, rivers, and drinking water.
- IRMA certifies mines for major companies.
- Lee, who is 41 and has five children, worries about dust and noise from the planned open pit mine where Piedmont intends to blast once or twice daily as needed.
- Piedmont maintains that blasting would not cause structural damage to nearby properties.
- Neighbors fear water contamination or supply disruption.
- Piedmont plans to test and treat water draining into its pits before using or discharging it.
- The company's backup water plans involve drilling new wells, providing municipal water access, or supplying bottled water, though these measures have failed to quell resident jitters.
- Boulanger emphasized the need to ensure mines do not cause harm that outweighs or outlasts their benefits.
- Resident Jim McMahan cited the mine's expected decade-long lifespan as a concern.
- McMahan, a 65-year-old retiree and farmer, noted that while the project will provide temporary jobs, they will eventually disappear along with local farms.
- Locke Bell, a 73-year-old retired county district attorney, acknowledged reducing emissions is a legitimate argument for new industries but warned against reckless destruction of earth and waters for minimal lithium production.
- Piedmont Lithium received a North Carolina state mining permit this month but still requires further local permissions.
- Chad Brown, chair of the Gaston County Board of Commissioners, stated officials must ensure the project is done correctly and assured residents before proceeding.
- Brown indicated a local decision could be reached by November.
- Lee learned about the mine a year after moving in when Piedmont mailed her family coffee and a note.
- The Piedmont Lithium project is expected to produce 30,000 metric tons of lithium hydroxide annually, significantly boosting US capacity.
- World's top lithium producer Albemarle is working to reopen a dormant mine in Kings Mountain, North Carolina.
- Albemarle stated it benefited from policies like the Inflation Reduction Act and received nearly $150 million via a Department of Energy grant to support costs.
- President Biden has struggled to win over Americans with his economic policies despite triggering a surge in clean energy investments.
- Bell noted the situation leaves a 'negative' view of the government's clean energy push in Gaston County.
- Lee supports clean energy but is less convinced of EVs due to a lack of charging infrastructure and high costs even with tax rebates.
- McMahan described the lithium mining process as 'invasive'.
- McMahan stated he does not feel the push for electric vehicles is being done in the way it should be.
