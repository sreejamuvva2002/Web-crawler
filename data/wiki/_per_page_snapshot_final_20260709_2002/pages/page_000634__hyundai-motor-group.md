# Hyundai Motor Group

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-04-30

**Source:** [Industry Spotlight: Spurring Increased Investment - Georgia Trend Magazine](https://www.georgiatrend.com/2023/04/30/spurring-increased-investment/)  ·  `page_000634`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group has tied a significant share of Georgia’s economy to its fate and decisions for almost 17 years.
- In 2006, Hyundai decided to build a $1 billion Kia automobile assembly plant in West Point.
- In 2022, Hyundai decided to build an electric vehicle (EV) assembly plant in Bryan County.
- Kia Georgia’s investment in the 2,200-acre West Point site has grown to $1.8 billion since its first car rolled off the line in 2009.
- The West Point plant produces 340,000 K5, Sportage, Sorento, and Telluride vehicles a year.
- Kia Georgia employs nearly 3,000 people working in three shifts around the clock.
- Kia claims to have spurred 14,000 plant and supplier jobs in the region, well above original projections.
- Troup and Harris counties are historically preferred locations for suppliers, especially for Kia’s 39 Tier 1 suppliers.
- Kia’s Tier 1 suppliers provide primary components for the production process.
- About 70% of Kia’s Tier 1 suppliers are subsidiaries of South Korean companies.
- Kia’s first suppliers included those that already served Hyundai’s then-new Montgomery, Ala., plant.
- In 2013, the Alabama Department of Commerce listed 106 Kia suppliers and vendors on its website, concentrated along the I-85 corridor linking West Point and Montgomery.
- West Point hosts Kia suppliers including Hyundai Mobis, Hyundai TRANSYS Georgia Powertrain, Hyundai Transys Georgia Seating System, Hamco America Inc., Daehan Solution Georgia, Daesol Material Georgia, Adient, and JinTech.
- LaGrange in Troup County hosts suppliers including Sejong Georgia, GLOVIS Georgia, Sewon America, Seoyon E-Hwa Interior Systems, and Daeha-America Corp.
- The COVID pandemic disrupted global supply chains and challenged Kia’s management of its suppliers.
- Many materials and parts for Kia are imported from Canada, Mexico, and abroad due to different regional restrictions.
- Kia Georgia currently makes only gas-powered internal combustion engines (ICE) but will soon change.
- In April 2023, Kia announced the West Point plant will begin assembling the EV9 in 2024.
- The EV9 will be the first Kia EV assembled locally in North America.
- Kia has not yet stated how the workforce will change, how suppliers will be affected, or what the new investment will total for the EV9 transition.
- Stuart Countess, president and CEO of Kia Georgia, stated the EV launch will create unique processes requiring changes to existing tooling and equipment.
- An EV does not require a fuel tank or circulation system, so specific process area changes will be necessary.
- Kia will adopt new testing and validation processes to maintain high-quality standards for the EV9.
- Thomas Kurfess, professor and executive director of the Georgia Tech Manufacturing Institute, noted that suppliers failing to pivot toward EV components will lose out to those who do.
- Hyundai’s $5.54 billion EV manufacturing “Metaplant” in Bryan County will produce EVs for its Hyundai, Genesis, and Kia brands.
- The Bryan County Metaplant will employ 8,100 workers.
- Joon Georgia announced a $317 million investment near Statesboro in Bulloch County to create 630 jobs as part of the first wave of EV suppliers.
- Ecoplastic Corporation announced plans for a $205 million facility in Bulloch County to make automotive interior and exterior plastic parts and hire 456 workers.
- Hyundai Mobis is building a $926 million Bryan County plant generating 1,500 jobs to make EV power systems and integrated charging-control units.
- Seoyon E-Hwa will create 740 jobs at a $76 million plant in Chatham County.
- Sewon America is investing $300 million in an Effingham County facility expected to generate 740 jobs.
- Chatham County won a $67 million, 400-worker investment from South Korea’s PHA, a global auto parts manufacturer.
- Hyundai’s EV battery plant will be located in Bartow County rather than on-site at the Bryan County Metaplant.
- The Bartow County battery facility is a $4 billion to $5 billion joint venture with SK On, a subsidiary of SK Innovation.
- SK On’s existing SK Battery America plant in Commerce was Georgia’s first EV battery plant.
- The new Bartow County plant is expected to create 3,500 jobs and begin operating in 2025.
- In January, Hyundai senior executives held a town-hall meeting with staff in Korea explaining a strategy transition to electrification and software-centered systems (software-defined vehicles).
- Hyundai set a goal by 2040 of offering only zero-carbon EVs in major markets and producing “flawless” software-defined vehicles.
- Hyundai-Kia announced that as of 2025, it will apply over-the-air software update functionality to all its EV models.
- A company spokesperson in Korea stated they are currently focused on building world-class electric vehicles when asked if the zero-carbon goals apply to the Bryan County Metaplant.
- Thomas Kurfess noted that future EV costs will increasingly be in software and recalls may become software updates.
- Stuart Countess identified charging station availability and charging time as the main challenges to EV acceptance, requiring changes in consumer lifestyle and behaviors.
- The Biden administration plans to install 500,000 EV charging stations across the U.S. by 2030.
- The federal government is setting aside $5 billion in formula funding for states and $2.5 billion in competitive grants to help cities, tribes, and states build charging infrastructure.
- Federal officials released new domestic manufacturing standards for federally funded EV chargers.
- The Georgia Department of Transportation will deploy federal funds to build charging stations filling gaps along interstates per its EV Infrastructure Deployment Plan.
- Georgia attracted the North American headquarters of Heliox, a Netherlands-based company that makes e-mobility charging systems.
- The U.S. Department of Transportation requires final assembly in the U.S. for new charging stations to be eligible for federal funds.
- Georgia Power owns about 3% of the 1,700 charging stations in Georgia.
- Bentina Terry, senior vice president of customer strategy and solutions for Georgia Power, stated the company builds public charging infrastructure in low-income or underserved areas where necessary.
- Georgia Power’s primary role is encouraging EV use through incentives, rebates, and guidance, with a motto to “enable, empower and educate.”
- Kristi Brigman, deputy commissioner of Global Commerce at the Georgia Department of Economic Development, stated that Georgia consistently targets entire industry ecosystems and one investment often spurs further investment directly or indirectly.
