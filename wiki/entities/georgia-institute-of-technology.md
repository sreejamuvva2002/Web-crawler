# Georgia Institute of Technology

**Type:** Research Center  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[battery-materials|Battery Materials]]  ·  **Location:** Georgia, United States

## Overview

Georgia Tech researchers, led by Associate Professor Hailong Chen, have developed a low-cost iron chloride (FeCl₃) cathode that could dramatically reduce lithium-ion battery costs and accelerate electric-vehicle adoption and large-scale energy storage.

## EV supply-chain relevance

The FeCl₃ cathode could slash the cost of EV batteries, which currently represent about 50% of a vehicle’s price, and enable cheaper, more sustainable electric vehicles and grid storage.

## Timeline

- **2019** — Georgia Tech lab began attempts to make solid-state batteries using chloride-based electrolytes with traditional oxide cathodes.
- **Early 1990s** — Sony first commercialized lithium-ion batteries, sparking the modern battery industry.
- **23 September 2024** — Publication of the Nature Sustainability paper describing the low-cost iron trichloride cathode.
- **1990s** — Commercialization of lithium-ion batteries, which later became the foundation for EV battery packs.
- **2024-09-24** — Georgia Tech announced the development of an iron-chloride cathode for solid-state batteries.

## Key facts

- The new iron chloride (FeCl₃) cathode costs only 1-2% of typical cathode materials while storing the same amount of electricity.
- Batteries currently account for about 50% of an EV’s total cost; the FeCl₃ cathode could significantly reduce this expense.
- When combined with a solid electrolyte and lithium-metal anode, the whole battery system cost could be reduced to 30-40% of current LIBs.
- All-solid-state batteries using the FeCl₃ cathode are safer, more efficient, and capable of higher energy density than conventional liquid-electrolyte LIBs.
- The cathode uses abundant elements iron and chlorine, avoiding expensive and toxic nickel and cobalt.
- Researchers estimate commercial viability for EV applications within five years.
- "For a long time, people have been looking for a lower-cost, more sustainable alternative to existing cathode materials. I think we’ve got one," said Hailong Chen.
- "This could not only make EVs much cheaper than internal combustion cars, but it provides a new and promising form of large-scale energy storage, enhancing the resilience of the electrical grid," Chen added.
- Batteries account for about 50% of an electric vehicle's total cost due to reliance on expensive nickel and cobalt.
- The FeCl₃ cathode uses only 1-2% of traditional cathode materials like cobalt and nickel while offering comparable energy storage capacity.
- Early tests indicate the FeCl₃ cathode could reduce total battery system costs by 30-40%.
- The cathode maintains 83% capacity after 1000 cycles with 99.95% Coulombic efficiency.
- Lead researcher Hailong Chen says the technology could be ready for commercial EV use in less than five years.
- The FeCl3 cathode uses abundant materials iron (Fe) and chlorine (Cl), avoiding nickel and cobalt.
- Initial tests showed FeCl3 performed as well or better than other more expensive cathodes, with a higher operational voltage compared to LFP batteries now flooding the market.
- Researchers estimate the technology may need less than five years before reaching commercial viability in electric vehicles.
- The team aims to perfect the materials in the lab and understand underlying mechanisms, while remaining open to scaling up for commercial applications.
- The cathode is potentially significant for both electric vehicles and large-scale energy storage systems.
- Rubber electrolyte can be stretched up to almost 300% of its original state, providing superior mechanical stability for lithium-metal batteries.
- The rubber-based lithium metal batteries demonstrated energy density exceeding 410 Wh/kg and could enable electric vehicles to travel over 490 miles on a single charge.
- Rubber electrolytes are flame-retardant, reducing fire risk compared with conventional liquid electrolytes.
- The rubber electrolyte can be produced at low temperatures and is compatible with existing manufacturing processes.
- Higher ionic conductivity of the rubber electrolyte allows faster ion transport at room temperature than traditional solid polymer electrolytes.
