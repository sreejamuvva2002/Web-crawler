# Advanced Air Mobility (AAM)

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-05-10

**Source:** [Electric flying vehicles about to become reality in Georgia - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2024/05/10/electric-flying-vehicles-about-to-become-reality-in-georgia/)  ·  `page_001639`

## Facts

- At least 20 states, including Georgia, are starting to explore Advanced Air Mobility (AAM) technology.
- Current investment in AAM technology is more than $8 billion.
- AAM technology investment is expected to grow to $45 billion by 2030.
- Clement Solomon serves as the director of the Georgia Department of Transportation’s Intermodal Division.
- The Federal Aviation Administration is expected to certify the first electric vertical take-off and landing vehicles (eVTOLs) late this year or early in 2025.
- Georgia Commissioner of Transportation Russell McMurry noted that while the technology is futuristic, there has already been activity in Georgia with these types of AAMs.
- California-based Archer Aviation is building a manufacturing plant adjacent to the Covington Municipal Airport in Newton County.
- The Archer Aviation manufacturing plant will be 350,000 square feet.
- The Archer Aviation manufacturing plant will produce up to 650 eVTOLs each year.
- Augusta-based private air charter operator Bravo Air is working with Japan’s SkyDrive to begin flying passengers on short trips aboard eVTOLs.
- SkyDrive has begun building the eVTOLs through a joint venture with Suzuki Motor Corp.
- AAM technology focuses on transporting passengers and cargo on short trips at low altitudes.
- eVTOLs fly at altitudes of 1,500 feet to 4,500 feet.
- eVTOLs are capable of transporting four to six passengers up to 250 miles.
- The current operational concept for AAM is described as an "Air Uber".
- Freight potential for AAM technology includes carrying low-volume, high-value cargo such as computer chips, medical equipment, or human organs needed for transplant.
- Because they are electric, eVTOLs are quieter and more environmentally friendly than traditional combustion engine aircraft.
- State Transportation Board member Cathy Williams, representing Georgia’s 2nd Congressional District, noted that traditional airport operations in Columbus create a lot of noise and eVTOLs would not exacerbate that problem.
- The Georgia DOT formed a working group last year to assess the potential for AAM technology in Georgia.
- The Georgia DOT working group included representatives of airports, municipal planning organizations, and the academic and business communities.
- A study by the working group found that nearly all of Georgia’s airports are compatible for eVTOLs and have the capacity to support AAM.
- A network of eVTOL chargers needs to be built at a cost of $500,000 to $750,000 each.
- Currently, the only eVTOL chargers in Georgia are located in Augusta and Valdosta.
- The goals of the Georgia DOT study were to set the stage for rolling out AAM technology in the next three to five years.
- eVTOLs are expected to begin commercial operations between 2026 and 2030.
- Archer Aviation announced an agreement with the Abu Dhabi Investment Office last month to begin air taxi operations using its four-passenger Midnight eVTOL model in the United Arab Emirates as soon as next year.
- Adam Goldstein is the founder and CEO of Archer Aviation.
- SkyDrive’s agreement with Bravo Air calls for developing infrastructure to support a network of eVTOL air-taxi services that will include Augusta Regional Airport.
- Bravo Air is buying five three-passenger eVTOL models from SkyDrive.
- Bravo Air aims to compete for certification to begin operations during the Osaka World Expo next year.
- Dann Standard serves as the vice president of operations at Bravo Air.
