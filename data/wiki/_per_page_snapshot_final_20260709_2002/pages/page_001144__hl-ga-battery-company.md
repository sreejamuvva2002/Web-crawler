# HL-GA Battery Company

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [fDi Intelligence – Your source for foreign direct investment information - fDiIntelligence.com](https://www.fdiintelligence.com/content/879432b1-9194-435e-84c1-fa338cdb7d8f)  ·  `page_001144`

## Facts

- The US immigration raid targeted a $4.3bn battery plant being developed by South Korean companies.
- On September 4, 317 South Koreans were detained at the Hyundai and LG Energy Solution site in Georgia.
- The detained individuals were engineers and technicians setting up the sprawling plant, which will power up 300,000 electric vehicles each year.
- The joint venture is called HL-GA Battery Company.
- President Donald Trump called on foreign investors to transfer knowledge to support more US jobs following the raid.
- FDI experts argue it would be impractical for locals to perform the temporary work at the Bryan County site due to a lack of institutional knowledge of how the specialist machinery works together.
- Hyung-Gul Kim, associate professor of international economic law at TLBU Graduate School of Law in South Korea, states that more than 90 per cent of the equipment is custom-made, designed and manufactured in Korea before being brought into the US for installation, testing, and refinement.
- Kim notes it is very difficult for US engineering companies to perform this work.
- Within large FDI projects, brick-and-mortar construction and manufacturing operations can often be performed by local labour with the right training.
- Bringing sophisticated plants online requires knowledge, skills, and coordination honed over years by investors and sometimes their partner companies.
- South Korea has fostered a 'mother factory' strategy where firms fly in businesses and workers from their supplier network to replicate the ecosystem at new plants abroad rather than relying on local engineers and technicians.
- Of the 317 detained Koreans, 47 were reportedly employees of LGES while the rest were hired by the JV’s partner companies and subcontractors.
- US authorities claim the detainees breached their stay permit terms, which are understood to have been B1 visas, ESTAs (allowing certain types of business), and B2 visas (predominantly for tourism).
- Kim compares plant setup to a conveyor system, noting Korean firms prefer doing the whole process with partnering companies they have worked with for more than 30 years.
- Kim's observations are based on research he co-led in 2023 involving site visits to incentives-chasing mega projects in the US by Samsung, LG, and Hyundai, plus Hyundai’s biggest production plant and R&D centre in Ulsan, South Korea.
- Didi Caldwell, president and CEO of site selector Global Location Strategies, says it is almost unheard of for foreign investors in advanced technologies to not fly in workers who have established the same operations elsewhere.
- In 2023, Taiwanese chipmaker TSMC announced delaying the commissioning of a fab in Arizona due to a local shortage of specialised expertise to handle and install its advanced equipment.
- Experts warn that training local workers in these processes is a formidable task.
- Kim notes equipment installation is a one-off job for a single company’s new plant, making little sense to have a team of local specialists dedicated to such sporadic tasks.
- An LGES spokesperson states that support from engineers with specialised knowledge and experience is essential, but the firm will hire more local personnel to operate production lines.
- Jae Kim, president of the Southeast US Korea Chamber of Commerce, says hiring Korean specialists is also a business decision aimed at earning the best financial returns.
- To build a US battery plant on the fastest timescale, working with Korean suppliers and workers with know-how from other battery plants will work better than finding and training new local companies.
- Both Jae Kim and Hyung-Gul Kim note that language and cultural barriers are additional factors in hiring decisions.
- Hyung-Gul Kim notes Korean workers are very hard working and having them work as a team around the world makes a big difference in timing and scheduling.
- He adds that while some processes can be conducted by US local companies, it may cause problems in teamwork.
- The immigration raid shocked other investors in the US who rely on foreign specialists.
- Caldwell says the raid has sent shivers through the FDI community.
- Data from fDi Markets suggests South Korean, Chinese, Japanese and Taiwanese investors have pledged to invest some $276bn into US high-technology mega projects since the start of 2020.
