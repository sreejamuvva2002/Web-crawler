# Cherokee County, Georgia

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** 2011-02-01

**Source:** [Cherokee County: Optimism And Resolve - Georgia Trend Magazine](https://www.georgiatrend.com/2011/02/01/cherokee-county-optimism-and-resolve/)  ·  `page_048404`

## Facts

- Cherokee County, near the top of the Metro Atlanta region, has been hard hit by the recession but prepared itself with investments to enhance employment and correct a 70-plus percent commuter imbalance.
- Leaders hope residential subdivisions filling again will keep residents working and shopping closer to home.
- Gene Hobgood, mayor of Cherokee’s county seat Canton, is optimistic about the long term despite having enough homes to last 100 years at current rates.
- The county doubled in population from 2000 to 2009, reaching 210,000 residents.
- Revitalizing downtown Canton is a priority for Mayor Hobgood to stimulate commercial activity.
- Canton’s downtown has a new 21,000-square-foot office/retail building, the first new non-governmental building there in 35 years.
- Highridge Partners, consisting of locals Stan Rogers and Chuck Ware, invested in the downtown building and already have two attorney offices.
- Cherokee County is finishing a Liveable Centers Initiative project funded by the Atlanta Regional Commission to acquire right-of-way for sidewalks and drainage projects from downtown to south Canton.
- The LCI grant area extends into Holly Springs, south of Canton.
- Holly Springs Mayor Tim Downing aims to create a three-sided town square with City Hall mixed with commercial and residential uses, similar to Smyrna and Cumming.
- Implementation funding for the Holly Springs town square was delayed due to the economy and tight lending.
- Transportation needs are serious throughout Cherokee County; GDOT recently finished widening Highway 92 through part of the county.
- Mayor Downing hopes for more funding from a T-SPLOST referendum in 2012 to improve Highway 20, which runs through Holly Springs and could redirect trucks and commuters from I-575 and I-75.
- Improving Georgia 20 and potentially Georgia Highway 140 could significantly relieve congestion in Metro Atlanta by creating an alternative east-west corridor.
- GDOT has released contracts to widen a portion of Highway 20 through Gwinnett.
- Cherokee County abandoned the Northern Loop outer perimeter concept previously floated by former Gov. Roy Barnes.
- Woodstock Mayor Donnie Henriques says two blocks were torn up in downtown to make it more pedestrian friendly, requiring people to cross Highway 5.
- A new interchange is going in at Ridgewalk by Rope Mill Road with additional retail space.
- Woodstock partnered with the county on the Elm Street Cultural Arts Village, initiated by grassroots nonprofit Elm Street Alliance.
- Four acres were purchased downtown for the arts village, including a 100-year-old farmhouse to be renovated.
- The arts village is integrated with the Greenprints trail system, another city-county partnership.
- Cherokee voters approved a parks bond in 2008, leading to strategic parks development within budget.
- Woodstock hopes its eco-cultural destination status will bolster chances of relocating the Georgia Music Hall Of Fame from Macon.
- West of Woodstock, widening Highway 92 from I-75 has boosted industrial development.
- Cherokee County purchased 100 acres fronting I-75 for industrial growth.
- Growth is occurring at Cherokee Commerce Center, a private industrial property owned by Majestic Realty.
- Consumer Products Services moved into 50,000 square feet at the center to repair damaged products.
- Curt Manufacturing, which distributes trailer hitches, occupies 34,000 square feet of space at the center.
- The Cherokee Commerce Center sits right on Interstate 75 with lots of frontage and is performing well.
- Misti Martin, executive director of the Cherokee Office of Economic Development, states the county is well positioned for industrial growth.
- The district was designated as an Opportunity Zone by the state Department of Community Affairs.
- Commission Chairman L.B. “Buzz” Ahrens, Jr. notes that widening Highway 92 dramatically improved logistics and the Opportunity Zone adds a major fiscal incentive for employers.
- A key ingredient in Cherokee’s industrial growth strategy is the 400-student Chattahoochee Technical College campus, which opened last year in Ball Ground.
- The $12-million tech school campus backs up to the Bluffs of Technology Park, an almost full industrial park expected to grow significantly.
- Enrollment at Reinhardt University in Waleska is currently 1,275 and continues to rise.
- Kennesaw State University in Cobb County, located just south of Cherokee, is another fast-growing campus part of the Cherokee strategy.
- The county is marketing heavy investments in the Cherokee County Airport as a magnet for future growth.
- An airport area master plan was developed over the last year to ensure quality business corporate development.
- The runway extension from 3,414 to 5,000 feet is almost complete.
- The county built a 10,000-square-foot passenger terminal that opened in 2010.
- Pam Carnes, Cherokee County Chamber of Commerce president and CEO, believes the Wilbanks-Sosebee Terminal will be a flagship for visitors and corporate traffic.
- Plans are underway to build a connector road between Bluffs of Technology Park and the airport via Highway 5.
- A white-collar recruitment strategy began 18 months ago to address commuter imbalance by matching resident skill sets and pay ranges with local opportunities.
- An employer survey indicates Cherokee can accommodate five to 10 employers with highly skilled employees, leveraging training at Chattahoochee Tech, Reinhardt, and KSU.
- Chart Industries, a biomedical company, is building the county’s first LEED-Certified manufacturing facility.
- The LEED certification was acknowledged as part of a Going Green effort.
- Chart makes gas production and storage equipment and is expanding its presence with a new facility in the Interstate 575/Airport Industrial Park.
- Chart purchased the last lot in the industrial park after working on the deal for a long time.
- Chart will hire more than 80 people and invest $6 million, creating white-collar jobs following their relocation of labs and regional offices from Cobb a few years ago.
- Northside Hospital-Cherokee announced in 2008 it would spend $8 million to expand its emergency room and offer other services.
- The hospital expansion will create some 40 jobs and offer robotic-assisted surgery for general, gynecological, and urology procedures.
- Mayor Hobgood notes the hospital expansion will create off-campus office jobs beneficial for tax collections.
- Piolax, a universal alloy corporation, has expanded in Cherokee County.
- Belnick Inc., an office furniture distributor, moved into the vacant Herman Miller facility, which is 327,000 square feet and one of the largest industrial buildings in the county.
- Parks are being developed along the Etowah River to link cities’ LCI districts with trails.
- Canton is building a new city park on 65 acres on Brown Industrial Parkway along the Etowah with multiple fields and walking areas.
- A new trail on the northern end of Heritage Park crosses over the river to continue along it to Boling Park.
- Canton is upgrading zoning, subdivision, and development regulations to manage growth quality.
- Woodstock’s Greenprints Project will add 60 miles of trails connecting city spaces and public places in southwest Cherokee.
- The Greenprints Alliance holds a Streetfest in March to raise funds and awareness for trail projects.
- Cherokee County purchased 541 acres on Yellow Creek Road near the Etowah River, increasing green space inventory to 1,500 acres.
- This purchase links Boling Park in Canton to Shoal Creek near the Georgia National Cemetery in Sutallee.
- Sales tax collections dropped from $250,000 a month four years ago to $175,000 a month due to revenue downturns limiting capital projects.
- Cherokee County extended its Special Purpose Local Option Sales Tax in 2010, but it is mainly for debt service.
- Canton is backing the Hickory Log Creek Reservoir, a drought-contingency reservoir north of the city.
- The city has $60 million in debt, mostly bonded, with a big chunk for the 300-acre reservoir owned by the city and Cobb County-Marietta Water Authority.
- Only 25 percent of the reservoir debt is Canton’s responsibility, amounting to more than $22 million.
- Top five industries in Cherokee County include Pilgrim’s Pride, Universal Alloy Corporation, Meyn America, Chart Industries, and Piolax Corporation.
