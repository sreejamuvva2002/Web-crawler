# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-06

**Source:** [Hyundai Plant Detentions Linked to Illegal Hiring - Postindustrial](https://postindustrial.com/stories/federal-operation-at-savannah-area-hyundai-plant-nets-475-detentions/)  ·  `page_001147`

## Facts

- The raid on Hyundai’s Savannah-area electric vehicle plant was part of a lengthy investigation into the unlawful hiring of illegal aliens at the industrial site that Georgia officials had promised would create thousands of jobs for locals, federal officials said.
- Operation Low Voltage resulted in more than 475 people detained — more than 300 of whom were Koreans — for alleged immigration violations.
- Federal agents executed a criminal search warrant to gather evidence at the battery plant jointly owned by Hyundai Motor Group and LG, two of South Korea’s largest companies.
- According to the search warrant, federal agents were seeking employment records from the lithium battery factory that could reveal illegal immigration and hiring practices at HL-GA Battery Company LLC and five other companies identified by federal officials as subcontractors.
- Federal officials had identified four people with Hispanic names as part of the basis for the warrant signed by a magistrate judge in the Southern District of Georgia.
- Steven Schrank, the special agent in charge of Homeland Security Investigations for Georgia and Alabama, said the raid at Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America was the largest single site enforcement operation ever carried out by the Department of Homeland Security’s enforcement division.
- Schrank told reporters that while some of the detained workers illegally crossed the U.S. border, others had entered the country legally but had expired visas or had entered on a visa waiver that prohibited them from working at the $7.6 billion site where Hyundai Motor Group and an interconnected group of suppliers are building vehicles.
- Hyundai broke ground at the site two years ago after Georgia promised billions of dollars in tax credits and tax abatements if the world’s largest carmaker met its goals of creating more than 8,000 jobs at an average salary of approximately $58,000.
- As of March, Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America reported that it had 1,200 people currently employed, not counting Koreans, and was operating one shift at the plant.
- At that time, the battery facility was not operational and remains under construction.
- No criminal charges had been filed.
- Federal officials said the majority of workers detained this week have been sent to Folkston, the Immigration and Customs Enforcement agency’s detention center in Charlton County.
- The governor’s office responded to the raid by saying that state agencies remain committed to the rule of law.
- Spokesman Carter Chapman stated that all companies operating within the state must follow the laws of Georgia and our nation.
- There was no immediate comment from Georgia Attorney General Chris Carr.
- The South Korean government expressed “concern and regret” over the operation.
- South Korean Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lee Jaewoong said in a televised statement from Seoul that the business activities of investors and the rights of nationals must not be unjustly infringed in the process of U.S. law enforcement.
- Trump stated that the people arrested were immigrants who entered the country illegally, adding, “We had as I understand it a lot of illegal aliens... Some not the best of people. But we had a lot of illegal aliens working there.”
- South Korean officials told The Wall Street Journal that many of the Koreans detained worked for LG Energy and were at the metaplant on a business trip with visas that allowed them to train employees in Georgia.
- In addition to Koreans, foreign nationals from Guatemala, Colombia, Chile, Venezuela, and Mexico had been detained, according to Migrant Equity Southeast, a Coastal Georgia migrant workers’ organization.
- The operation involved 10 federal agencies, including the Federal Bureau of Investigation and the Internal Revenue Service’s criminal investigation unit, according to Margaret Heap, the U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of Georgia.
- Schrank noted that the operation was more complex than other ICE enforcement actions since Trump took office earlier this year, describing it as a multi-month criminal investigation where evidence was developed, interviews conducted, documents gathered, and presented to a court to obtain a judicial search warrant.
- The Current has previously reported on a pattern of workplace deaths and injuries affecting both American and foreign workers at the metaplant, including the death of a Korean technical worker in March.
- There is also a reported pattern by key Hyundai suppliers of not reporting those incidents to the federal agency responsible for workplace safety.
- HMGMA negotiated nearly $2 billion in state tax abatements and credits with Georgia and has previously stated that it was not responsible for the actions of its supplier companies on the site.
- In response to the ongoing federal investigation, Hyundai Motor Group announced that North America Chief Manufacturing Officer Chris Susock would assume governance of the entire EV site in Bryan County.
- The company stated it will conduct an investigation to ensure all suppliers and subcontractors comply with all laws and regulations.
- Hyundai is reviewing its processes to ensure all parties working on its projects maintain high standards of legal compliance, including thorough vetting of employment practices by contractors and subcontractors.
