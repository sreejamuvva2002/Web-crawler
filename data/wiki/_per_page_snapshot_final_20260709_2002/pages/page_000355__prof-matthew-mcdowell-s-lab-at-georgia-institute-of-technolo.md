# Prof. Matthew McDowell's Lab at Georgia Institute of Technology

**Source:** [page_000355](https://mtmcdowell.gatech.edu/)  ·  `page_000355`

## Facts

- The lab's research goal is to understand how materials behave and transform in real-life environments within energy storage devices to engineer improved materials for better performance.
- In energy storage devices, mass/electron transport, high temperatures, and reactive environments are the norm, causing materials to change, transform, or degrade in response to operating conditions.
- The group focuses on using in situ experimental techniques to probe materials transformations under realistic conditions to understand how changes influence properties.
- Fundamental scientific advances from these studies guide the engineering of materials for breakthrough new devices.
- Students and other researchers in the group synthesize materials, analyze their properties, and utilize a variety of in situ characterization methods to understand dynamic structure and chemistry.
- Current Project 1 investigates transformation and degradation mechanisms in next-generation battery materials that require higher charge storage capacity or entirely new materials beyond lithium-ion, such as sodium-ion or multivalent batteries.
- The reaction and phase transformation mechanisms of new materials determine how much charge they can store and how long they last within batteries.
- It is critical to understand these reaction mechanisms across multiple length scales from the atomic scale to the mesoscale within battery electrodes.
- The group uses powerful in situ experimental techniques including X-ray tomographic imaging and electron microscopy to reveal structural, chemical, and morphological transformations in real time.
- Current Project 2 focuses on understanding interfaces in solid-state batteries, which could be safer and have higher energy density than Li-ion batteries but face performance limits from chemical and structural instabilities at interfaces between solid-state electrolytes and electrode materials.
- The group is working on understanding the evolution of interfaces between electrodes and solid electrolyte materials in solid-state batteries.
- With this knowledge, the group is designing new materials for solid-state batteries with improved performance.
- Current Project 3 investigates and develops sustainable materials for battery systems that are more sustainable, lower cost, and have improved energy storage properties.
- The group is investigating the use of these sustainable materials in lithium-ion batteries, sodium-ion batteries, and grid-scale energy storage flow batteries.
- Current Project 4 focuses on understanding and developing high-capacity electrode materials for use at low temperatures beyond that which is possible with conventional Li-ion batteries.
- This low-temperature battery work could be important for spaceflight, electrified aircraft, and other applications involving extreme environments.
