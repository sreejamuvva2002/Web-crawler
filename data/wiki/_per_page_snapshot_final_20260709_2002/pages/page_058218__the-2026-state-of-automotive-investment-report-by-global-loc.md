# The 2026 State of Automotive Investment Report by Global Location Strategies (GLS)

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-02-27

**Source:** [Billion-Dollar Automotive Mega-Projects Account for Nearly Half of Global Capital Expenditures | Manufacturing.net](https://www.manufacturing.net/automotive/news/22961639/billiondollar-automotive-megaprojects-account-for-nearly-half-of-global-capital-expenditures)  ·  `page_058218`

## Facts

- The automotive industry contributes more than $1.2 trillion annually to the U.S. economy, representing up to 5% of GDP.
- The number of announced automotive projects has declined since the pandemic, but capital expenditures remain consistent.
- The size and complexity of each automotive investment have surged, concentrating billions of dollars in fewer, mission-critical commitments.
- $1B-plus automotive projects now account for 43% of all automotive capital spending, up from just 18% a decade ago.
- The average capital expenditure per project increased 24% (inflation-adjusted) between 2015 and 2024.
- Investments are becoming fewer but significantly larger, increasing balance-sheet exposure per decision.
- Employment per project is rising more slowly than capital, signaling capital deepening and advanced manufacturing rather than labor expansion.
- Supplier investment has grown more selective, often waiting for clear OEM execution before committing capital.
- Investment is increasingly concentrated in next-generation vehicle platforms, including battery-electric vehicles, hybrid systems, advanced battery manufacturing, and large-scale retooling of existing internal-combustion-engine plants.
- Next-generation facilities require far more to be built out on day one compared to traditional auto plants.
- These facilities must consider their ability to adjust strategies based on changing market demands and policy considerations.
- Next-generation facilities require significantly more power and water, specialized space for high-tech operations such as battery production, and room for suppliers to operate nearby.
- Bringing next-generation facilities online requires more coordination to staff, permit, and bring online, which increases the risk of delays.
- These projects are multi-billion-dollar, ground-up commitments that must work at scale from the start.
- Traditional cost advantages alone, such as incentives, no longer offset execution risk for automakers.
- Automakers increasingly screen locations based on their ability to execute reliably rather than just incentive packages.
- Large automotive facilities now compete directly with data centers, semiconductor fabrication plants, and other energy-intensive users for power and utilities.
- Interconnection delays and grid constraints are increasingly stalling automotive projects nationwide.
- Regions may show adequate labor totals on paper but lack the specialized commissioning and technical talent required during construction and ramp-up, the period when capital exposure is highest.
- Multi-agency approvals, environmental reviews, and utility coordination frequently disrupt construction timelines, extending ramp-up and delaying returns.
- The U.S. faces decentralized infrastructure, localized labor markets, and layered permitting that place more delivery risk on individual projects.
- The European Union sees smaller, phased investments that limit exposure per project but face regulatory complexity and slower demand growth.
- China utilizes coordinated infrastructure and clustered ecosystems to absorb execution risk at the system level.
- The U.S. remains attractive for large, platform-defining investments only where delivery certainty can be demonstrated early.
- Automotive leaders should now focus on excluding non-viable locations earlier in the site selection process.
- Validating power and utility delivery before shortlisting sites is now a critical step.
- Stress-testing workforce ramp assumptions is recommended for new automotive manufacturing playbooks.
- Mapping permitting dependencies end-to-end is necessary to avoid delays.
- Phasing mega-site infrastructure is advised to preserve flexibility.
- Structuring incentives as risk-sharing tools rather than upfront discounts is the new standard.
- Global Location Strategies President and CEO Didi Caldwell stated that automotive site selection has shifted from cost optimization and incentives to execution certainty, noting that locations must deliver power, workforce, and permitting on schedule for projects to move forward.
