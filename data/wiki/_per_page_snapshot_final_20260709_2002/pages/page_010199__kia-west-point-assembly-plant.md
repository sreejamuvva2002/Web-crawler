# Kia West Point Assembly Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-09-30

**Source:** [Kia plant CEO tells state lawmakers affordable housing shortage hurting worker recruitment | Georgia Public Broadcasting](https://www.gpb.org/news/2022/09/30/kia-plant-ceo-tells-state-lawmakers-affordable-housing-shortage-hurting-worker)  ·  `page_010199`

## Facts

- Kia’s assembly plant in West Point opened in 2008 with more than enough workers available to produce the first cars.
- It has become much harder to fill the 500 new jobs needed to get a new compact SUV off the assembly line.
- Jobs at the plant pay $17-$23 per hour.
- Kia Georgia CEO Stuart Countess told a committee of Georgia legislators that an affordable housing shortage is the biggest obstacle to filling these positions.
- Many interested applicants for the $17-$23 per hour jobs are having trouble finding affordable housing near the west Georgia plant.
- Kia has been in close communication with Troup County and other government officials about housing development plans.
- Countess urged other large employers to collaborate with government and community leaders to prepare for the 50,000 manufacturing jobs expected to be created in Georgia in the next decade.
- Kia Georgia talked to a developer about the possibility of building workforce housing as a creative solution.
- The housing shortage worsened during the pandemic when home development stalled due to supply chain issues, making it harder to buy or rent a house.
- Countess stated that the housing market creates additional complications for families considering relocation for employment, noting high demand and affordable homes lasting only hours before asking prices are offered to buyers.
- The meeting where these points were raised was held at the Georgia State Capitol.
- Before the new legislative session begins in January, the Georgia House Study Committee on Regulation, Affordability and Access to Housing will have met multiple times to identify solutions for the lack of affordable housing in the state.
- Macon Republican state Rep. Dale Washburn offered support for workforce housing, noting it will take a multilayer approach primarily below $300,000.
- Washburn recalled a Macon neighborhood where cotton mill employees once lived in company-owned housing and said investing in housing could be a good investment for big industries.
- Barbara Holmes, president of the Albany Area Chamber of Commerce, stated that south Georgia is working to identify what constitutes affordable housing in the region.
- There was a 6% drop in home ownership in Albany and surrounding Dougherty County from 2009 to 2020.
- Home prices have risen by 17% over the past couple of years.
- Holmes said it is hard to find nice housing between $100,000 and $150,000 in the Albany area.
- Holmes stated that bidding wars in Dougherty County’s real estate market are squeezing out truly affordable homeownership, leaving only an old stock of homes with nothing for recent college graduates or first-time home buyers.
- Tax credits are being used in south Georgia to build a multifamily workforce housing complex six miles from a Hyundai manufacturing plant planned near Savannah.
- Due to the loss of federal funding, the Department of Community Affairs (DCA) has been unable to provide as many loans and down payments for homeowners who earn 120% or less than their area’s median household income.
- DCA officials expect a surge in interest in the loan program when the house price limit increases to $297,000 for most of the state and even higher in Atlanta and Savannah.
- The committee discussed how building codes and government design standards drive up developer costs.
- Chatsworth Republican state Rep. Jason Ridley asked DCA Commissioner Christopher Nunn if any cost-benefit analyses had been conducted on new regulations.
- Dallas Republican state Rep. Martin Momtahan said an international code standard created a major burden for builders in Paulding County and throughout the state.
- The DCA is involved in a statewide advisory group composed of representatives of general contracting, code enforcement, city and county government leaders, and trade associations.
- Nunn stated that Georgia does not take a blanket approach to adopting all building codes as is.
