# Woodbridge Foam Corporation

## Facts

- Dan Dubblestyne serves as the director of health, safety, and environment at Woodbridge Foam Corporation.
- Woodbridge Foam Corporation is a private Canadian company that operates 50 to 60 facilities across approximately 12 countries.
- The company's facilities largely serve the automotive market and manufacture seating and interior parts for buses, motorcycles, and golf carts.
- Woodbridge Foam's core business is manufacturing polyurethane foam products, primarily producing car seats, bolsters, headliners, armrests, and headrests.
- During the COVID-19 pandemic, Woodbridge pivoted its production lines to manufacture N95 face masks made from polyurethane foam.
- The company collaborated with INOAC, a Japanese partner in one of its global joint ventures, to utilize an existing mask design for production.
- Transitioning to PPE required splitting operations across multiple plants, with one facility producing the foam and another cutting and assembling it into final products.
- Retooling did not require significant capital investment because foam cutting was performed on programmable CNC machines requiring only minor design modifications.
- Woodbridge called back several idled teammates to return to work during the PPE manufacturing transition.
- The N95 masks had to meet strict standards for sterilization, liquid penetration resistance, and particle filtration, requiring testing with federal and provincial agencies and laboratories.
- Federal and provincial governments actively assisted Woodbridge in overcoming regulatory hurdles to transition mask production.
- Plant workers received extra training through online programs focused on quality inspections and safe, consistent manufacturing processes for the new product.
- The company implemented a Pandemic Response Toolbox and a Safe Work Playbook detailing protocols for temperature screening, facility access, PPE use, personal hygiene, case tracking, and containment.
- An executive-level pandemic response team met daily to oversee operations and ensure all plants adhered to safety protocols.
- Physical barriers were installed in facilities where employees had to work shoulder-to-shoulder to maintain safety standards.
- Among approximately 7,500 global teammates, Woodbridge reported fewer COVID-19 cases than expected due to these safety measures.
- Most manufacturing facilities resumed operations in late April or early May, though production volumes remained reduced as customers across the supply chain faced disruptions.
- Pandemic response protocols implemented during the transition were expected to carry over well into 2021.
