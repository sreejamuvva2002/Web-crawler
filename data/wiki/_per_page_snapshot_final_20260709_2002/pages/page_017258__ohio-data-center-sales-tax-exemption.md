# Ohio Data Center Sales-Tax Exemption

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-01-07

**Source:** [Indefensible tax breaks for data centers will cost Ohio - Policy Matters Ohio](https://policymattersohio.org/research/indefensible-tax-breaks-for-data-centers-will-cost-ohio/)  ·  `page_017258`

## Facts

- Ohio has implemented a generous sales-tax exemption for companies operating data centers.
- Investments announced by Amazon, Google, and Microsoft over the past two years could cause the state and localities to lose almost $1.6 billion in sales-tax revenue with only modest job creation.
- Data centers demand enormous amounts of electricity due to artificial intelligence demands, which could increase electric rates for Ohioans.
- Other states are questioning the wisdom of tax giveaways to wealthy companies for data center expansion.
- Ohio’s sales-tax exemption was first created in 2013 and covers computer data center equipment as well as materials used in constructing the centers.
- The Ohio Tax Credit Authority, a five-member board headed by the director of the state development department, approves initial exemptions.
- To qualify for an exemption, a company must make at least $100 million in capital investment over three years and pay annual employee compensation of at least $1.5 million.
- On Oct. 28, the Tax Credit Authority approved a 15-year sales tax exemption estimated to be worth $72.5 million for Microsoft covering three new data centers in Licking County.
- Microsoft’s $1 billion investment is projected to create a minimum of just 20 new jobs.
- Exemptions originally granted to Meta and Google required the creation of at least 50 and 30 jobs, respectively.
- Each of those original exemptions was estimated to cost more than $1 million per job.
- Ohio’s tax expenditure report pegged the total annual cost of this tax break at $127.4 million for the fiscal year.
- As of late September, 13 agreements had been approved covering nearly $5.1 billion in investment but generating just 356 jobs and $31.6 million in annual payroll.
- This results in a revenue loss for the state alone of $281.9 million.
- Including local sales-tax revenue losses, total losses exceed $343 million, averaging nearly $1 million per job.
- These estimates exclude recently announced centers like those by Amazon and Google.
- Amazon’s latest $10 billion investment, announced December 16, could result in an exemption of more than half a billion dollars between now and 2030.
- If Cologix receives a 75% sales-tax exemption on its planned $7 billion investment for a campus of eight data centers in Johnstown, it would cost the state more than $300 million in sales-tax revenue.
- These exemptions reduce sales taxes flowing to counties and transit agencies.
- Tax credit agreements require companies to annually report their sales-tax exemption amounts but some allow companies to reject public disclosure.
- Policy Matters Ohio has requested copies of these reports from the state and companies without success.
- Major data-center operators do not need new approval for tax breaks when expanding, as original approvals grant open-ended ability to avoid paying taxes if investment thresholds are met.
- Based on existing and announced investments, Amazon will qualify for the exemption through 2055 under a 2014 agreement initially estimated to cost $77 million but potentially costing 20 times that amount.
- Bo Williams, executive responsible for Microsoft’s data centers in North America, stated he could not think of a site selection decision decided on tax incentives.
- American Electric Power is seeking guarantees from the Public Utilities Commission of Ohio that operators will cover the enormous capacity required for data center expansion.
- Intervenors including Walmart and the Ohio Consumers’ Counsel (OCC) are concerned that data centers could boost electricity costs for businesses or individuals.
- The OCC tried to prevent AES Energy (Dayton Power & Light) from shifting costs to consumers if investments to serve an Amazon data center in Fayette County do not pay off.
- Massive subsidies to wealthy corporations could result in higher costs for Ohioans and Ohio businesses.
- Electricity demand growth from data centers could threaten progress in cutting greenhouse gas emissions.
- In Georgia, the legislature approved a two-year pause on new data center sales-tax exemptions while examining effects on the energy grid, but the measure was vetoed by the governor.
- An analysis in Georgia found that the state’s sales tax exemption had a negative fiscal impact because increased tax collection from construction and operation was not high enough to offset forgone state tax revenue.
- In late 2023, more than two dozen nonprofits, homeowners’ groups, and residents formed a Virginia Data Center Reform Coalition seeking regulatory and rate-making reforms.
- A recently issued report by the Joint Legislative Audit and Review Commission in Virginia has raised the possibility of new regulation.
- Legislators from Connecticut to South Carolina have been addressing the data center issue.
- Many Ohio data centers also receive local subsidies through property tax abatements.
- Ohio does not tax tangible property like data-center equipment, providing large invisible subsidies compared to states that do levy such taxes.
- In Virginia, Loudoun County received more than $600 million in property taxes from data centers in 2023, mostly from taxes on equipment Ohio does not levy.
- Amazon was receiving a Job Creation Tax Credit prior to its recent $10 billion announcement, allowing it to be credited with much of the state income tax paid by its data-center employees.
- In Virginia, data centers and tenants reported saving $928.6 million in sales taxes from that state’s exemption in Fiscal Year 2023.
- Recommendations include requiring all reports to be made public, including investment amounts, exemption values, job creation, payroll, and energy/water use.
- Other recommendations include setting a cap for the total tax break allowed per job or each year, with Good Jobs First recommending a cap of $50,000 per job in 2016.
- Additional recommendations include ending open-ended extension of the tax break without specific consideration by the Tax Credit Authority, requiring 60-day notice prior to exemption decisions, guaranteeing data center growth will not increase electric rates for other customers, and conducting regular independent audits of electricity use and grid effects.
