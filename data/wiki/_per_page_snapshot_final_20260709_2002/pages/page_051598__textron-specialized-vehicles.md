# Textron Specialized Vehicles

**Type:** workforce_training  ·  **Role:** workforce_training  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Driven to Excel: Textron’s high school outreach initiative - GCMOnline.com](https://gcmonline.com/course/environment/news/driven-to-excel)  ·  `page_051598`

## Facts

- RPM stands for Reaching Potential Through Manufacturing and is a public-private partnership between Textron Specialized Vehicles and the Richmond County School System based in Augusta, Ga.
- The program combines an instructional day with a $10 an hour four-hour shift in manufacturing at a facility that is half schoolhouse and half manufacturing plant.
- The facility includes classrooms and educational space to allow students to make progress toward their high school degree while earning valuable paid work experience.
- Students produce components and subassemblies for products manufactured by Textron, which is Richmond County’s largest industrial employer with a workforce exceeding 1,700 in Augusta.
- The company manufactures E-Z-GO golf cars and Cushman utility vehicles at its Augusta facilities.
- Students apply for admission to RPM and are selected for participation by Richmond County School System officials based on factors including lack of progress toward a high school diploma, absenteeism, and personal situations such as poverty, homelessness, or being teen parents.
- More than 300 students have graduated from the RPM program.
- More than 100 graduates have gone on to full-time employment at Textron Specialized Vehicles.
- About one-third of RPM graduates go on to work for Textron.
- Derek Wynns, director of GSE operations for Textron Specialized Vehicles, oversaw the RPM program until a recent internal job change within the company.
- Textron and the Richmond County School System announced the creation of the RPM program in January 2016.
- Jason Moore was hired as the RPM principal and given seven months to get the program up and running before school started in August.
- The RPM model was inspired by Southwire’s "12 For Life" program, which Southwire (a leading manufacturer of wire and cable based in Carrollton, Ga.) started in 2007 in collaboration with Carroll County schools.
- Southwire's initiative helped raise the total district graduation rate in Carroll County to over 91% and now also incorporates schools in Florence, Ala.
- RPM debuted in the summer of 2016 with teachers in place and an initial cohort of 75 students who started running machines on day two.
- Parts built by RPM students are incorporated into E-Z-GO and Cushman vehicles that are used all over the world.
- Adrianne Bogans, Ph.D., serves as the RPM assistant principal.
- Janiya McKinnie dropped out of T.W. Josey High School in Augusta during her junior year due to the COVID-19 pandemic but was enrolled in RPM by her mother and later helped turn tassels for the class that pushed RPM above 300 graduates.
- Phillip Bowman, director of assembly operations at Textron Specialized Vehicles, oversees assembly operations and highlights the program's impact on student growth and accountability.
