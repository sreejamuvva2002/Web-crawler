# Hyundai Motor Group Savannah Manufacturing Facility

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-09-30

**Source:** [475 people were detained during immigration raid in Georgia: Homeland security official](https://nypost.com/2025/09/05/us-news/475-people-were-detained-during-immigration-raid-in-georgia-homeland-security-official/)  ·  `page_009960`

## Facts

- Immigration authorities detained 475 people, most of them South Korean nationals, during a raid on the sprawling manufacturing site in Georgia where Hyundai makes electric vehicles.
- Steven Schrank, lead Georgia agent of Homeland Security Investigations, said the raid resulted from a monthslong investigation into allegations of illegal hiring at the site.
- Schrank described the operation as the “largest single site enforcement operation” in the agency’s two-decade history.
- The Thursday raid targeted one of Georgia’s largest and most high-profile manufacturing sites, where Hyundai began manufacturing electric vehicles about a year ago at a $7.6 billion plant.
- The site employs about 1,200 people and is located roughly 25 miles (40 kilometers) west of Savannah in Bryan County.
- The facility sits on 3,000 acres (1,214 hectares) in a largely rural area, drawing workers from several surrounding counties and communities including Savannah.
- Gov. Brian Kemp and other officials have touted the Hyundai site as Georgia’s largest economic development project.
- Agents focused their operation on an adjacent plant still under construction where Hyundai has partnered with LG Energy Solution to produce batteries that power EVs.
- The battery plant is operated by HL-GA Battery Co., a joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution, and is slated to open next year.
- Court records filed this week indicated that prosecutors do not know who hired the “hundreds of illegal aliens” at the site.
- The U.S. Attorney’s Office wrote in a court filing that the identity of the actual company or contractor hiring the illegal aliens is currently unknown.
- The South Korean government expressed “concern and regret” over the operation targeting its citizens.
- South Korean Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lee Jaewoong stated that the business activities of investors and the rights of nationals must not be unjustly infringed in U.S. law enforcement.
- The South Korean Foreign Ministry is dispatching diplomats from its embassy in Washington and consulate in Atlanta to the site and planning to form an on-site response team.
- Immigration attorney Charles Kuck said two detained clients arrived under a visa waiver program that allows stays of 90 days or less without a visa for tourism or business.
- One client had been in the U.S. for a couple of weeks, while the other had been there for about 45 days.
- Schrank said some detained workers illegally crossed the U.S. border, while others had entered legally but had expired visas or entered on a visa waiver that prohibited them from working.
- Some of those detained worked for the battery manufacturer, while others were employed by contractors and subcontractors at the construction site.
- Schrank did not know precisely how many of the 475 detained were Korean nationals but said they made up a majority.
- No one has yet been charged with any crimes, and the investigation remains ongoing.
- Most of the detainees were taken to an immigration detention center in Folkston, Georgia, near the Florida state line.
- Hyundai began producing electric vehicles at the site last September.
- A few months after production began, Hyundai Motor Group Executive Chairman Euisun Chung credited President Trump with the company’s decision to create more American jobs by building an EV factory in Georgia during a White House appearance.
- Chung stated that the investment decision was initiated during his meeting with President Trump in Seoul in 2019 and would create more than 8,500 American jobs.
- Agents sought employment records, personnel files, payroll information, bank account information, timecards, video and photos of workers, immigration documents, social security cards, visas, passports, birth certificates, and ownership/management records of multiple construction companies and contractors.
- The search warrant materials included the names and photos of four people identified as “target persons” to be searched.
- LG stated it was closely monitoring the situation and gathering all relevant details but could not immediately confirm how many of its employees or Hyundai workers had been detained.
- Plant spokesperson Bianca Johnson said operations at Hyundai’s EV manufacturing plant were not interrupted by the raid.
- Hyundai Motor Company stated it is working to understand the specific circumstances of the raid and understands that none of those detained are directly employed by Hyundai Motor Company.
- HL-GA Battery Co. stated it is cooperating fully with the appropriate authorities.
- Gov. Brian Kemp and other Georgia Republican officials issued statements saying all employers in the state were expected to follow the law following the raid.
- The Democratic Party of Georgia condemned the raid, with chair Charlie Bailey calling it “politically-motivated fear tactics designed to terrorize people who work hard for a living, power our economy and contribute to the communities across Georgia that they have made their homes.”
