# Section 179 Tax Deduction Provision

## Facts

- Section 179 is a provision of the US tax code that allows businesses to deduct (write-off) the full purchase price of qualifying vehicles and equipment in the year of purchase, rather than depreciating them over several years.
- For taxable years beginning in 2026, the Section 179 maximum deduction is $2,560,000.
- The deduction begins to phase out dollar-for-dollar when total qualifying purchases exceed $4,090,000, and is fully phased out at $6,650,000.
- Section 179 is generally limited to taxable income from the active conduct of your trade or business.
- Amounts elected but unable to be used due to the business-income limit may be carried forward to future years.
- Vehicles must be used more than 50% for business to qualify for the deduction.
- Business use includes traveling between job sites, transporting goods and equipment, and business-related travel away from the regular work location.
- Commuting and personal use are not deductible, even if some business activity occurs in the vehicle during those times.
- A separate 'heavy SUV' cap limits expensing to up to $32,000 for certain heavy SUVs (generally >6,000 and ≤14,000 lbs GVWR) primarily designed to carry passengers in 2026.
- Some vehicles are excluded from the heavy SUV cap.
- Bonus depreciation allows businesses to claim an additional first-year depreciation deduction on eligible property, including some vehicles.
- For 2026, the bonus depreciation rate is 100% for qualified property acquired after Jan. 19, 2025.
- Heavy SUVs are subject to a separate Section 179 SUV limitation, and passenger automobiles (generally vehicles at or below 6,000 lbs GVWR) are subject to annual depreciation limits under Section 280F.
- The IRS uses Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR), not curb weight, to determine the 6,000-lb threshold for eligibility.
- GVWR is the maximum operating weight specified by the manufacturer and can typically be found inside the driver’s side door jamb.
- Eligible vehicles include heavy SUVs, pickups, and vans over 6,000 lbs GVWR with more than 50% business use.
- The list of qualifying vehicles includes models from Audi, Bentley, BMW, Buick, Cadillac, Chevrolet, Chrysler, Dodge, Ford, GMC, Honda, Infiniti, Jeep, Land Rover, Lexus, Lincoln, Mercedes-Benz, Nissan, Porsche, Rivian, Rolls-Royce, Tesla, and Toyota.
- Rivian R1T and R1S have a GVWR of 8,532 lbs for model years 2022–2026.
- Tesla Model X Long Range has a GVWR of 6,130 lbs (model years 2021–2026) and the Plaid trim has a GVWR of 6,561 lbs (model years 2021–2026).
- Vehicle weights listed are estimates for standard configurations and can vary by model year, trim package, engine size, towing package, and other factors.
- Actual GVWR should be verified on the driver's side door label as multiple weight classifications may apply.
- Used vehicles can qualify for Section 179 provided they are 'new to you', meet general requirements like >50% business use, and are acquired by purchase for trade or business use.
- Vehicles acquired by gift or inheritance do not qualify, and property acquired from a related person generally does not qualify either.
- If bonus depreciation is claimed on a used vehicle, additional eligibility rules apply.
- Taxpayers who claim Section 179 or bonus depreciation on a vehicle generally cannot use the standard mileage rate method for that same vehicle.
- To calculate business-use percentage, divide total miles driven for business by total miles driven (including personal/commuting) for the year.
- Detailed records of vehicle usage, including mileage logs, receipts, invoices, and related expenses, must be kept for at least three years after filing.
- Depending on your state, state-specific rules and regulations may apply to Section 179 deductions; businesses should contact their Department of Revenue or a tax professional.
