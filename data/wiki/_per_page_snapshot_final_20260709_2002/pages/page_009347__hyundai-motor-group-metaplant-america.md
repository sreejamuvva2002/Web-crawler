# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-08-27

**Source:** [US agency to reexamine permit for Hyundai's $7.6 billion electric vehicle plant in Georgia | Georgia Public Broadcasting](https://www.gpb.org/news/2024/08/27/us-agency-reexamine-permit-for-hyundais-76-billion-electric-vehicle-plant-in)  ·  `page_009347`

## Facts

- A federal agency plans to reassess its environmental permit for Hyundai's $7.6 billion electric vehicle plant in Georgia.
- The reassessment follows a conservation group complaint that regulators failed to properly examine the factory's potential impacts on the area's water supply.
- The Army Corps of Engineers stated in a Friday letter that state and local economic development agencies applied for the project's 2022 permit without mentioning Hyundai's plan to withdraw up to 6.6 million gallons (25 million liters) per day from an underground aquifer.
- This underground aquifer is a major regional source of drinking water.
- Details about the plant's water needs emerged earlier this year as the Georgia Environmental Protection Division considered a proposal for four new wells to supply the auto factory.
- As a result, the Army Corps will revisit its prior finding that the project would have "negligible impacts."
- The Army Corps sent a similar letter to the Ogeechee Riverkeeper conservation group.
- The conservation group gave notice in June that it planned to sue if the agency refused to revisit the permit issued for the Hyundai project in October 2022.
- Ben Kirsch, legal director of the riverkeeper group, stated that the concentration of pumping will have local impacts on domestic and agricultural wells.
- The conservation group is concerned about potential impacts on natural springs, wetlands, tributaries, and streams in the area.
- Hyundai is pushing to start production before the end of the year at its 2,900-acre (1,170-hectare) plant in Bryan County west of Savannah.
- The site will produce EVs and the batteries that power them.
- The South Korean automaker plans to employ 8,000 workers at the plant.
- This project is described as the largest economic development project Georgia has ever tackled.
- The Army Corps ordered no delays or disruptions to construction at the plant site as a result of its permit reassessment.
- Cheri Dragos-Pritchard, spokesperson for the Army Corps' Savannah District, stated via email that the permit is still valid and they have not requested that the permittee stop work.
- It was unknown how long the additional review might take.
- Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America stated it will assist as needed to ensure the Army Corps gets the information it needs.
- The company stated in a Monday statement that it has worked tirelessly with relevant authorities to be good neighbors and ensure operations do not negatively impact the community's water resources.
- Sara Lips, spokesperson for Georgia's Environmental Protection Division, said the extra federal scrutiny is "unlikely to impact or delay" a final decision by state regulators on permitting the wells.
- The Army Corps permit obtained by state and local economic developers authorized the filling or dredging of 221 acres (89 hectares) of wetlands at the plant site.
- This authorization occurred just a few months after Hyundai announced plans to build its EV factory in May 2022.
- The Army Corps initially concluded the project would have "negligible impacts on municipal and private water supplies."
- The agency's Friday letter stated it relied on information provided by economic developers for this conclusion.
- Trip Tollison, president and CEO of the Savannah Area Economic Development Authority, stated they never purposefully withheld anything.
- Tollison expected the updated information requested by the Army Corps to be submitted within 10 days.
- He noted the federal agency would typically complete its review within a month.
- Tollison expressed confidence that the reevaluation won't hold up the project.
- Tollison stated, "There's enough water for everyone," and added, "We feel really good about where we are."
- Georgia's environmental agency issued draft permits in July for the four wells to supply Hyundai.
- The state is now evaluating public comments before reaching a final decision on the wells.
- The wells would be drilled in neighboring Bulloch County.
- Some farmers and rural residents in Bulloch County have expressed worry that the auto plant will siphon water away from their crops and homes.
- State regulators concluded that water withdrawals by the Hyundai plant would lower aquifer levels up to 19 feet (5.8 meters) for private wells within 5 miles (8 kilometers).
- Regulators stated most wells won't see impacts because they reach deeper into the ground.
- The state agency also said nearby rivers and streams won't be affected because dense layers of rock seal off the aquifer from surface water.
- Ben Kirsch with Ogeechee Riverkeeper hopes the Army Corps' second look will provide more details on how state regulators reached their conclusions.
- Kirsch stated, "We definitely want to see the Corps independently evaluate this."
- He added, "This should have been all considered before the wetlands were filled and buildings went up."
