# Inflation Reduction Act Private Clean Energy and EV Manufacturing Investment Wave

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-11-04

**Source:** [A wave of private energy spending is coming — but may be too late to help Democrats - POLITICO](https://www.politico.com/news/2024/11/04/private-energy-spending-democrats-election-00185344)  ·  `page_052388`

## Facts

- Democrats’ climate law launched a huge surge of manufacturing initiatives across critical swing states including Georgia.
- Companies say market realities, questions about President Joe Biden’s policies or worries about a second Trump administration have clouded some of the heady expectations raised by the August 2022 passage of the Inflation Reduction Act.
- Two-thirds of the announced investment won’t be completed until next year or later.
- Seven swing states — Arizona, Georgia, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Nevada, North Carolina and Wisconsin — account for 44 percent of all clean energy investments announced since Biden signed the IRA.
- Vice President Kamala Harris has ramped up her pitch to voters on the administration’s efforts to foster a U.S. manufacturing renaissance, while warning that a Trump victory would leave hundreds of planned projects in limbo.
- Atlas Public Policy tracks company announcements in clean energy and technology manufacturing, reporting 555 facilities announced with at least $153.4 billion in planned investments as of Oct. 24.
- The 555 announced facilities promise to create roughly 160,000 jobs.
- Roughly 92 percent of those dollars were pledged for projects that have yet to come online.
- About 230 of those facilities announced they would begin operations by at least the end of this year.
- Of those, fewer than half are up and running.
- An additional 11 have canceled altogether.
- Roughly 30 percent of 75 facilities that responded to questions from POLITICO said they had either delayed their projects, named potential delays they are monitoring, or canceled altogether.
- The Inflation Reduction Act created an estimated $527 billion in tax incentives for the deployment and manufacturing of low-carbon energy technologies, in an attempt to reclaim U.S. control of supply chains now dominated by competitors such as China.
- Vice President Kamala Harris stood before a crowd of factory workers in Dalton, Georgia, to celebrate the thousands of jobs that solar power was bringing to a deep-red corner of the state.
- Qcells, a South Korean solar manufacturer, owns a plant in Dalton, Georgia, which Harris highlighted as symbolic for bringing clean energy manufacturing jobs to the carpet capital of the world.
- Qcells said it would spend $2.5 billion to expand its Dalton facility and build a new factory outside Atlanta.
- Biden administration rules gave automakers a two-year grace period to continue buying graphite from China while still receiving the IRA’s tax credits for electric vehicles.
- North American graphite producers and lawmakers had argued against the move, saying it would undermine the business case for domestic producers at a crucial time for the industry.
- In Georgia, Anovion Technologies said it was delaying plans for a battery manufacturing facility that had initially promised to bring more than 400 jobs to the southwest corner of the state by 2025.
- As of August, Anovion Technologies had hired seven people for its Georgia project, according to chief commercial officer Patrick Donnelly.
- Anovion Technologies postponed its Georgia battery facility project because of Biden administration rules that gave automakers a two-year grace period to continue buying graphite from China while still receiving the IRA’s tax credits for electric vehicles.
- The White House estimates that businesses have announced a total of $694 billion in manufacturing and clean energy investments since the IRA and CHIPS and Science Act were enacted.
- One new tax credit under the IRA rewards companies for the manufacture of clean energy components in the U.S., while others push companies to use domestically made products.
- The provisions spurred a wave of factory announcements across the U.S. — with the majority of the projects planned for Republican districts.
- More than half of the sites that responded to POLITICO’s inquiry said their facilities remain on target.
- Of the roughly 450 announced factories that have selected locations, nearly two-thirds are poised to be in GOP-held congressional districts.
- Those projects represent nearly $115 billion in potential investments, or three-quarters of the total.
- Over a quarter of the 555 announced facilities tracked by Atlas are poised to be in the seven presidential battleground states.
- Most of those facilities and their expansions are still in the planning stage and have not yet begun construction.
- Fewer than 25 percent are operating.
- In a separate analysis, the Rhodium Group and Massachusetts Institute of Technology estimated that nearly $90 billion in clean energy manufacturing investments have been spent between the quarter the IRA passed and June of this year.
- Analysis commissioned by the BlueGreen Alliance estimated that the IRA will create more than 9 million jobs over the next decade.
- Schneider Electric announced $440 million in U.S. manufacturing investments since 2020, including plans to expand facilities in Tennessee and South Carolina since the IRA passed.
- A recent national survey of nearly 930 businesses found that more than half said they would lose business or revenue if Congress repeals the IRA.
- Twenty-seven percent said they would lose projects or contracts, and nearly 9 percent of companies said they would have to relocate to another country should the law be repealed.
