# Battery Belt Manufacturing Expansion Initiative

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-07

**Source:** [New ‘Battery Belt’ Opens Organizing Front in the South | Labor Notes](https://www.labornotes.org/2024/07/new-battery-belt-changing-south)  ·  `page_049983`

## Facts

- Construction is humming at the Toyota Battery mega-site in Liberty, North Carolina as of early June.
- Production at the Toyota Battery site is slated to begin in 2025.
- By 2030, the 7 million-square-foot Toyota Battery complex will be fully operational with 14 production lines (10 for EVs and plug-in hybrids, 4 for hybrid electric vehicles).
- The Toyota Battery site will operate 5,100 workers.
- The total population of Liberty, North Carolina is 2,655.
- The Toyota Battery mega-site carries a $13.9 billion price tag.
- In Kings Mountain, North Carolina, two companies backed by a $90 million Department of Defense grant plan to drain a lake that was once an open-pit lithium mine and resume mining.
- The Kings Mountain lithium project is expected to yield enough lithium for 1.2 million electric vehicles per year.
- The mined lithium will be refined at a soon-to-open $1.3 billion facility in Chester County, South Carolina.
- Much of the EV supply chain expansion is driven by the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) passed in 2022.
- The IRA provided grants and tax credits to encourage cleaner energy production.
- State governments are adding record-breaking incentive deals including tax breaks, infrastructure development, and workforce development funding.
- Georgia will give Hyundai up to $2.1 billion in tax breaks and other state assistance for its $7.9 billion capital investment in an EV and battery production complex outside Savannah.
- The Georgia Hyundai EV and battery complex is forecast to employ up to 8,500 workers.
- The IRA aims to compete with China, which currently produces the most EVs at the lowest prices.
- Stephen Roach, former chair of Morgan Stanley Asia, told Axios in July: “We want to contain China, even if it comes at the expense of addressing the world’s climate change crisis.”
- The Biden administration recently imposed massive tariffs on imports from China related to various aspects of EV production.
- Since the IRA passage, companies have announced $110 billion in capital investments in new EV manufacturing.
- Roughly half of the $110 billion investment and corresponding jobs are destined for the U.S. South according to the EV Jobs Hub.
- Eighty-five percent of the EV investments are concentrated in right-to-work states.
- S&P Global Market Intelligence estimates that as many as two-thirds of EV jobs may eventually be located in the South.
- Michigan tops the list in EV capital investments, followed closely by Georgia ($23 billion), North Carolina ($20 billion), and Tennessee ($17 billion).
- A joint venture between Ford and SK On is investing $5.6 billion to develop BlueOval City outside of Memphis, Tennessee.
- BlueOval City is a six-square-mile complex where 6,000 workers will produce EVs and batteries.
- The company boasts BlueOval City will be “among the largest auto manufacturing facilities in U.S. history.”
- The BlueOval City site will receive batteries from BlueOval SK Battery Park in Kentucky, a $5.8 billion project that will employ another 5,000 people.
- Scout Motors, owned and operated by Volkswagen, recently broke ground on a $2 billion investment site outside Columbia, South Carolina.
- Once operational in 2026, the Scout Motors site will employ 4,000 workers and produce 200,000 vehicles a year.
- South Carolina is the country’s number one exporter of tires and home to BMW’s massive 11,000-worker plant in Spartanburg.
- The BMW Spartanburg plant will soon begin receiving batteries from a nearby facility operated by AESC.
- In April, AESC announced an expansion adding a second battery production facility for a total investment of $3.1 billion.
- Auto and manufacturing industries are drawn to the South due to a pro-business climate with the lowest union density, lowest wages, and most stringent anti-labor laws in the country.
- Georgia Economic Development Commissioner Pat Wilson told the Financial Times last October that massive labor stoppages in traditional union states will benefit Southern states as companies look to grow.
- In March, Georgia passed a law forbidding companies that receive state incentives from recognizing a union by any method other than secret ballot elections.
- South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster pledged in his State of the State address to “fight [unions] to the gates of hell.”
- McMaster and five other Southern governors issued a joint statement claiming the UAW drive at Chattanooga Volkswagen “threatens the values we live by” and would “certainly put our states’ jobs in jeopardy.”
- Nearly every new foreign-owned auto plant that has opened in the U.S. since the 1990s has chosen the South for its base of operations.
- The Midwest’s share of automotive jobs fell from around 60 percent to around 45 percent, while the South’s share grew from 15 percent in 1990 to 30 percent today.
- The South is home to 60 percent of Black workers along with a growing population of immigrant workers.
- Nearly two-thirds of plant construction investments in Southern states are for manufacturing beyond EVs.
- The UAW committed $40 million over the next two years to organize 13 non-union automakers largely concentrated in the South.
- Workers at Ultium Cells in Lordstown, Ohio, which manufactures batteries for General Motors, recently won a strong agreement providing the UAW’s initial step into the EV industry.
- Chattanooga Volkswagen workers won their union vote overwhelmingly in April.
- Daimler Truck North America workers in North Carolina, Georgia, and Tennessee won a record contract several weeks after the VW vote.
- Workers at Mercedes in Alabama came up short in their May vote on unionizing but continue organizing through worker committees and shop floor militancy.
- Last year, workers in Georgia at Blue Bird, the country’s second-largest bus manufacturer which produces electric buses, won a victory to join the Steel Workers (USW).
- Workers at electrified bus manufacturer New Flyer in Alabama voted yes this year for the Industrial Division of the Communications Workers (IUE-CWA).
- The Southern Workers Assembly, a cross-sector rank-and-file network, recently launched a program aimed at getting workers jobs in strategic sectors to organize.
