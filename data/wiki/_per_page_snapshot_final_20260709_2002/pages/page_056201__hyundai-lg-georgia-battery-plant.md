# Hyundai-LG Georgia Battery Plant

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-04

**Source:** [Korea’s major US investment projects halted as detained LG Energy workers set for release - KED Global](https://www.kedglobal.com/business-politics/newsView/ked202509080002)  ·  `page_056201`

## Facts

- The South Korean government has secured the release of about 300 nationals detained following a raid by US authorities on an electric vehicle battery joint venture between Hyundai Motor Co. and LG Energy Solution Ltd. in Georgia.
- Kang Hoon-sik, Seoul’s presidential chief of staff, told senior ruling party officials on Monday that negotiations with the US government on their release have concluded.
- Kang stated, “Our government, business associations and relevant companies worked as one to secure their release.”
- The detained Korean workers will return home on a chartered plane under a “voluntary departure” process, avoiding deportation records and future entry bans.
- Korea’s Foreign Minister Cho Hyun is scheduled to travel to Washington later on Monday to finalize administrative procedures with US Secretary of State Marco Rubio, clearing the way for repatriation flights as early as Wednesday.
- Nearly 500 employees, including 300 Koreans (mostly LG Energy staff, subcontractors and engineers), were taken into custody last Thursday by agents from ICE, HSI, DEA, and Georgia state troopers.
- The detained employees are alleged to have worked on the site without proper work permits, having entered the US under ESTA or short-term B1 business visas.
- The $4.3 billion joint venture battery facility is among the largest projects in Georgia and was expected to create 8,500 jobs.
- The facility was scheduled for completion later this year to supply battery cells to Hyundai’s nearby EV factory, Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America LLC (HMGMA).
- Construction work on the site was suspended following the raid.
- The incident has thrown Korea’s flagship investment projects in the US into disarray.
- At least 22 other factory sites involving Korean business groups in autos, shipbuilding, steel, and electrical equipment have been nearly halted.
- Samsung Electronics Co. and SK Hynix Inc. are heavily investing in US chip facilities.
- Samsung SDI Co. and SK On Co. are building battery plants with their partners GM and Ford, respectively.
- Several of these Korean-invested sites rely on rotating teams of Korean engineers.
- There are growing concerns that major Korean investment projects in the US could be affected, including the MASGA (Make American Shipbuilding Great Again) program, a joint US-Korea shipbuilding project.
- Hyundai Steel Co. is constructing a US steel mill.
- Power equipment manufacturers HD Hyundai Electric Co., Hyosung Heavy Industries Co., and LS Electric Co. are expanding local factories.
- These projects require the deployment of hundreds of employees from headquarters and partner companies.
- Korean companies with US interests have frozen travel plans and recalled staff already in the US over fears of further raids.
- A company executive in Seoul stated, “Korean workers are being treated like criminals for building factories that Washington itself lobbied for,” adding, “If this continues, investment in the US could be reconsidered.”
- The incident underscores a structural weakness in Korea Inc.’s overseas expansion model.
- With H-1B and L-1 work visas in short supply, Korean firms have routinely rotated engineers through 90-day ESTA entries or short-term B-1 visas to meet tight construction schedules.
- Industry officials in Seoul warn that unless visa arrangements for skilled Korean employees are settled through bilateral talks, investment timetables worth more than 140 trillion won ($101 billion) across multiple US states will face serious delays and cost overruns.
- The ICE raid risks inflaming tensions between Washington and Seoul, a key ally and major investor in the US.
- Political pundits said ICE adopted aggressive enforcement quotas under President Donald Trump’s pledge to deport 1 million undocumented migrants annually.
- According to DHS, the US government deported nearly 200,000 people during the first seven months of the Trump administration, which is the highest number in a decade but short of Trump's campaign goal.
- The New York Times reported that collateral arrests not originally the goal are on the rise.
- Analysts said the Georgia raid reflects partisan fault lines in Washington.
- The Hyundai-LG joint venture project was announced during former President Joe Biden’s 2022 visit to Seoul and championed by him as a pillar of his clean-energy agenda.
- Trump has sought to roll back EV subsidies and has been skeptical of such plants, according to analysts.
- A Korean industry analyst stated, “There is an unavoidable political dimension here,” noting that the project was once celebrated as a symbol of the alliance but has now become collateral damage.
