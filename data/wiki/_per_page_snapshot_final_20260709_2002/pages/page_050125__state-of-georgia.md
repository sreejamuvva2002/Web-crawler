# State of Georgia

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Georgia Poised to Become EV Nexus – MEAG Power](https://www.meagpower.org/georgia-poised-to-become-ev-nexus/)  ·  `page_050125`

## Facts

- Rivian chose a site near Covington for its new $5 billion assembly plant and battery factory.
- Rivian plans to employ 7,500 workers at its factory.
- State officials have said Rivian's workforce could grow to 10,000.
- Gov. Brian Kemp called the Rivian project “the largest single economic development project ever in this state’s history.”
- Hyundai announced in May an investment of $5.5 billion for its own EV and battery plant in Bryan County, near Savannah.
- Hyundai's project will create 8,100 new jobs.
- Numerous EV-related projects and initiatives have been announced over the past couple years positioning Georgia as a nexus of the growing EV industry.
- Related infrastructure companies and suppliers are investing billions of dollars more in the state.
- Participant communities are benefiting from direct and secondary investment from EV-related plant construction, tax-base growth, and ancillary economic benefits.
- A boost in electricity sales is a likely benefit for MEAG Power Participants as EVs begin to predominate.
- SK Battery America is building two plants on a massive site along I-85 in Commerce.
- The $2.6 billion SK Battery America project was announced in 2018.
- SK Battery America's plants will produce batteries for the Ford F-150 Lightning pickup truck and the Volkswagen ID.4.
- Plant one at the SK Battery America site was slated to begin commercial operation early this year.
- Plant two at the SK Battery America site is set to begin production in the first quarter of 2023.
- Approximately 2,600 jobs will be created at the SK Battery America plants by the end of 2023.
- Rivian's new plant near Covington is only its second production plant.
- Rivian plans to build a carbon-conscious campus with the plant eventually employing more than 7,500 workers.
- Once ramped up, the Rivian facility will be capable of producing up to 400,000 vehicles per year.
- Construction on the Rivian facility is expected to begin in the summer of this year.
- Start of production at the Rivian facility is slated for 2024.
- Rivian's site considerations included logistics, environmental impact, renewable energy production, availability and quality of talent, and a good fit with company culture.
- Kia chose West Point for its first and only U.S. production plant 15 years ago.
- Kia's investment delivered 2,700 jobs from Kia and thousands more from suppliers that also located in the state.
- Kia is part of the Hyundai Motor Group.
- Kia says it will have 11 new electric vehicles by 2026.
- Kia hopes that by 2030, EVs, hybrids, and plug-in hybrids will comprise 40 percent of all its sales.
- The West Point plant was touted to be in the running to build an Apple EV before that plan was scuttled last year.
- Rivian’s new plant will produce pickups and SUVs, as well as Amazon delivery vans.
- Blue Bird manufactures school buses in Fort Valley.
- Blue Bird expects to scale up eBus production capacity to 4,000 vehicles a year by 2024 to meet demand.
- Blue Bird expects eBus demand to increase in part based on the Bipartisan Infrastructure Bill.
- The Bipartisan Infrastructure Bill allocates $5 billion in funding for electric and low-emission school buses.
- The University of Georgia has the largest fleet of electric buses on any college campus in the country.
- The UGA fleet consists of 33 Proterra E2 buses made in Greenville, S.C.
- The UGA electric bus fleet is part of the university's strategic plan to advance sustainability at its Athens campus.
- In July, Georgia launched a statewide initiative to strengthen its position as a national leader in the electric mobility industry.
- The Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance (EMIA) is led by the Georgia Department of Economic Development (GDEcD).
- EMIA includes partners from the private and public sectors ranging from electric utilities, government, industry, education, nonprofits, and other stakeholders.
- EMIA is focused on advancing Georgia’s role in developing the electric mobility industry and its supply chain.
- ECG’s Daryl Ingram, senior vice president & chief external officer, is an advisor to the EMIA board.
- GDEcD launched a new website for the initiative.
- The state legislature passed the Joint Study Committee on the Electrification of Transportation.
- The committee plan covers the rollout of EV charging stations.
- The study committee plan should be signed by Gov. Kemp soon.
- The U.S. Departments of Transportation and Energy announced the availability of nearly $5 billion under the new National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure (NEVI) formula program on Feb. 10.
- NEVI was established by the infrastructure law to build a national charging network.
- NEVI will distribute the money over five years to help states create a network of EV charging stations, especially along the Interstate Highway System.
- The American Public Power Association (APPA), the National Rural Electric Cooperative Association, the Joint Office, and the Edison Electric Institute (EEI) are working together to support EV charging plan development efforts.
- Each organization has designated individuals to be electric power industry liaisons to the Joint Office to help ensure utility engagement and facilitate appropriate connections.
- APPA's designated contact for EV charging liaison duties is Patricia Taylor, Senior Manager, Regulatory Policy and Business Programs.
- APPA has produced a presentation called Navigating the Electric Vehicle Market.
- In late 2021, the National Electric Highway Coalition (NEHC), comprising more than 50 power companies mostly investor-owned, announced plans to install EV fast-charging infrastructure along major U.S. highways by the end of 2023.
- The EEI is leading the NEHC effort.
- According to the U.S. Department of Energy’s Vehicle Technologies Office, sales of light-duty EVs in the United States rose 85% last year.
- Sales of plug-in hybrid vehicles grew 138%.
- The EEI estimates that about 22 million EVs will be on U.S. roads in 2030.
- Meeting the projected EV growth will require more than 100,000 fast chargers.
