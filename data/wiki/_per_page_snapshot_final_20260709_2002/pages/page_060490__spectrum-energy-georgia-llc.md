# Spectrum Energy Georgia, LLC

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** 2022-05-02

**Source:** [Spectrum Energy wood pellet plant a health hazard far beyond Adel –WWALS to GA-EPD 2022-05-02 | WWALS Watershed Coalition (WWALS) is Suwannee RIVERKEEPER®](https://wwals.net/2022/05/02/spectrum-energy-wood-pellet-plant-a-health-hazard-far-beyond-adel-wwals-to-ga-epd-2022-05-02/)  ·  `page_060490`

## Facts

- WWALS submitted public comments on Air Permit Application No. 28143 for Spectrum Energy Georgia, LLC to the GA-EPD Stationary Source Permitting Program on May 2, 2022.
- The draft permit for Spectrum's wood pellet plant in Adel, Georgia, follows EPD's approval of a similar Renewable Biomass Group (RBG) plant in Adel, which WWALS argues set a problematic precedent.
- WWALS states that the draft permit does not adequately address comments submitted on November 19, 2021.
- The pellet plant has Environmental Justice implications for Adel, a plurality minority community where EPD has already approved one polluting pellet plant and is considering approving another.
- Hazardous emissions from these pellet plants are considered statewide health risks for Georgia and Florida, not confined to Adel.
- Historical evidence cited shows that smoke from trees burning around the Okefenokee Swamp in 2007 traveled up to 80 miles west to Quitman, causing hospitalizations among the elderly, as well as reaching Atlanta and Miami.
- Both RBG and Spectrum Energy, along with Enviva Pellets Waycross, LLC (Enviva), will likely draw wood from a 75-mile radius around Adel, extending from Tallahassee, Florida, to the Okefenokee Swamp.
- Most of the population in this 75-mile wood sourcing area is economically disadvantaged and minority.
- RBG CEO Craig Whitlock told the Adel City Council in September 2020 that RBG's plant would not burn whole trees, but RBG's EPD air quality application stated it could.
- WWALS notes a historical trend where woody biomass plants initially claiming to use only tops and stems end up burning or pelletizing whole trees.
- The pellet plants threaten flooding and topsoil loss through deforestation across south Georgia and north Florida.
- Deforestation is linked by WWALS to the record "700-year" floods of 2009 and 2013, which caused record sewage spills from Valdosta and elsewhere.
- Deforestation promotes runoff of cattle manure into waterways, a major focus of WWALS's volunteer water quality testing program.
- WWALS testing and data from Valdosta, Lowndes County, and downstream Florida agencies show E. coli contamination in the Withlacoochee River travels to the Gulf of Mexico via the Suwannee River.
- E. coli contamination is strongly suspected to enter water wells near the rivers during these incidents, though further testing is needed to confirm.
- River contamination poses health risks to people swimming, boating, and fishing in the rivers.
- Increased truck traffic around the Adel plant will produce diesel fuel combustion emissions and increase traffic risk, adding to Environmental Justice concerns.
- The pellet plant contributes to adverse climate change effects through greenhouse gas emissions from burning wood pellets, even if burned in Europe.
- WWALS cites regional climate impacts including Georgia peach crop failures due to insufficient winter cold, Florida citrus industry struggles from heat-induced diseases, and pine beetle outbreaks during droughts in both states.
- WWALS argues poor rural south Georgia and north Florida do not need more greenhouse gas sources driving faster temperature increases.
- Director Dunn's narrative for Spectrum acknowledges comments from EIP and WWALS questioning emission factors used in the application, such as high control efficiency without detailed information and using after-control tested data as uncontrolled emission factors.
- EPD determined that limited credible information exists for pellet mill emission factors and will require performance testing to validate all emission factors per Section 6 of the permit.
- WWALS points out that Enviva operates similar plants in six U.S. states, including the Waycross plant, suggesting massive amounts of emission data should be available.
- After acquiring the Waycross plant, Enviva submitted EPD Application No. 610193, which resulted in significant increases in many Enviva emission estimates.
- WWALS recommends EPD require Spectrum to use the emission estimation methods employed by Enviva in its re-evaluation of the Enviva pellet plant.
- WWALS questions why EPD accepted Enviva's updated numbers for Waycross but did not require such updates before the acquisition, or if Section 6 testing was required in the original Waycross permit.
- WWALS concludes that if credible estimation or measurement methods do not exist for these plants, EPD should deny the Spectrum air quality permit application.
