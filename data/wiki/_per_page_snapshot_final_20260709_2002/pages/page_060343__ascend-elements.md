# Ascend Elements

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2023-04-22

**Source:** [One of the biggest battery recycling plants in the US is up and running | Grist](https://grist.org/solutions/one-of-the-biggest-battery-recycling-plants-in-the-us-is-up-and-running/)  ·  `page_060343`

## Facts

- Ascend Elements is a Massachusetts-based battery recycling startup that opened a plant in Covington, Georgia in late March.
- The Covington facility is claimed to be the largest electric-vehicle battery recycling facility in North America.
- The plant can process 30,000 metric tons of input each year.
- This capacity equates to breaking down the battery packs from 70,000 electric vehicles annually, according to Ascend CEO Mike O’Kronley.
- The facility breaks down old batteries and preps the most valuable materials inside to be processed and turned into new batteries.
- Currently, 80 to 90 percent of what goes into the Covington facility is scrap materials from battery factories.
- SK Battery America’s plant in Commerce, Georgia supplies scrap materials to the Covington facility.
- Ascend also accepts used consumer electronics from battery-collection programs such as Call2Recycle.
- The company pays other companies for their old batteries to keep them from going into landfills.
- These deals are mostly with EV or battery makers that have high volumes of spent batteries to get rid of.
- Covington sits in the emerging “Battery Belt,” a swath of new battery factories and electric-vehicle plants opening up across the Midwest, Carolinas, Georgia, Tennessee, and Kentucky.
- The location was influenced by nearby battery factories sending their scrap down the road for pre-processing.
- Scrap and used batteries go through mechanical shredding and sieving to produce “black mass.”
- Ascend extracts lithium carbonate from the black mass, while the remaining mass contains materials such as graphite, nickel, cobalt, and manganese.
- Ascend currently sells most of these extracted substances to the market.
- The company also converts some black mass into cathode precursor and cathode active materials at its Massachusetts R&D center.
- Ascend is building a second commercial-scale facility in Hopkinsville, Kentucky.
- The Kentucky plant will take outputs from Covington and convert them into cathode precursor and cathode active material for new battery manufacturing.
- The Hopkinsville plant is valued at $1 billion and received $480 million in grant funding from the Department of Energy as part of the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law’s investment in domestic supply for critical materials.
- The Kentucky location will introduce a technique dubbed “hydro to cathode” for efficiently extracting cathode materials from black mass.
- CEO Mike O’Kronley stated that recycling plants will need to be within about an hour’s drive of every new battery gigafactory because EV batteries are classified as Class 9 hazardous materials and should not be long-distance shipped.
- Ascend is raising money to build more plants due to the surging popularity of EVs producing more worn-out batteries than current recyclers can process.
- Redwood Materials, another battery recycling startup founded by Tesla co-founder JB Straubel, has a Nevada facility processing 40,000 metric tons of input annually, equivalent to around 100,000 battery packs.
- Redwood Materials is building a forthcoming $3.5 billion recycling facility in South Carolina.
- Redwood Materials won a $2 billion conditional loan from the DOE for its Nevada plant to make new batteries from recycled materials.
- Canada-based Li-Cycle received a $375 million conditional DOE loan for its own facility to process lithium carbonate from a network of recycling plants.
- Cirba Solutions is expanding a battery-recycling plant in Ohio.
- Investment in lithium-ion recycling soared in the last few years to match spiking demand for battery materials, as developing new mines in the U.S. remains extremely hard.
- The federal government is lavishing funds on new battery recycling plants, and revamped EV tax credits call for increasing shares of domestically sourced batteries and battery materials.
- These facilities tie into the Biden administration’s goal to make the U.S. more capable of supplying itself with batteries pivotal to electrifying transportation and decarbonizing the grid.
