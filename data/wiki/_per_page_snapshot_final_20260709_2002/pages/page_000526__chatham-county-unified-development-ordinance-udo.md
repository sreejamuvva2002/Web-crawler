# Chatham County Unified Development Ordinance (UDO)

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2024-10-02

**Source:** [Unpacking the challenges of Chatham County's Unified Development Ordinance - Chatham Journal Newspaper](https://chathamjournal.com/2024/10/02/unpacking-the-challenges-of-chatham-countys-unified-development-ordinance/)  ·  `page_000526`

## Facts

- The Chatham County Unified Development Ordinance (UDO) is a comprehensive set of regulations designed to guide the county’s growth and development while balancing environmental concerns, economic development, and community needs.
- The UDO aims to ensure that new developments are compatible with the rural character of Chatham County while promoting sustainability.
- Chapter 4 of the UDO imposes strict limits on building expansions for businesses in Chatham County.
- Under the new regulations, any business that expands its physical footprint by more than 10% must bring its entire building up to current code.
- Planning board member Eric Andrews states that a 10% expansion threshold is too restrictive compared to the national norm of 25 to 50%.
- Andrews warns the 10% rule could discourage local businesses from expanding or upgrading facilities, potentially driving them to neighboring counties with more flexible regulations.
- Businesses expanding under the new rules may face expensive upgrades for parking, landscaping, lighting, and energy efficiency.
- The UDO mandates that large commercial buildings of 100,000 square feet or more install solar energy infrastructure.
- Eric Andrews questions making solar installations mandatory for smaller developers who may lack the financial resources of large corporations.
- Andrews advocates for strong recommendations for solar power instead of hard-and-fast rules to allow businesses flexibility.
- The UDO mandates significant landscaping investments, including the installation of 10-foot trees and irrigation systems.
- Andrews notes that requiring large trees adds hundreds of thousands of dollars to project costs, which gets passed on to consumers.
- Andrews argues that research shows smaller plants have a higher survivability rate than the large trees required by the UDO.
- The ordinance requires businesses to surround parking lots with hedges and other vegetation.
- Andrews raises security concerns about vegetation in parking lots providing hiding spots for criminals.
- The UDO includes a requirement for electric vehicle (EV) charging stations in new developments.
- Eric Andrews cautions that demand for EV charging stations may not yet be high enough to justify the mandate.
- Andrews argues the free market should dictate whether apartment complexes and other developments include EV charging stations to attract tenants.
- Forcing developers to include EV charging stations now could result in unnecessary expenses passed on to consumers as higher rent or purchase prices.
- Chapter 6 of the UDO redefines open space, stating only 10% of riparian buffers can be counted as open space in new developments.
- Andrews cites American Planning Association guidelines, noting that 50 to 100% of riparian buffers are typically counted as open space across most of the country.
- Limiting riparian buffer counting to 10% will force developers to set aside more prime land for open space, potentially driving up costs and limiting development land.
- Andrews warns the new open space rules will have a huge impact on affordable housing by forcing developers to raise prices.
- Local leaders face challenges managing development to preserve rural character while promoting economic growth and sustainability.
- Andrews emphasizes the need to strike a better balance between promoting sustainability and allowing businesses/developers flexibility.
- Andrews advocates for a more flexible, market-driven approach that considers the needs of local businesses, developers, and residents.
- Andrews urges the county to listen to feedback from the business community and developers to ensure development makes sense for everyone.
