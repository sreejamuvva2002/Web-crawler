# Wabash National Corporation

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** 2026-02-04

**Source:** [Wabash National Corporation Q4 2025 Earnings Call Summary](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/wabash-national-corporation-q4-2025-213235434.html?fr=sycsrp_catchall)  ·  `page_014478`

## Facts

- Management attributed the fourth quarter's underperformance to prolonged softness in freight, construction, and industrial demand, which led customers to defer capital spending decisions.
- The company is proactively idling manufacturing facilities in Little Falls and Goshen to align production capacity with current demand.
- Idling the Little Falls and Goshen facilities will reduce fixed manufacturing overhead by approximately $10 million annually.
- The Parts and Service segment grew 33% year-over-year, serving as a structural stabilizer that generates higher-margin, more predictable revenue compared to cyclical equipment sales.
- Early signs of freight market stabilization are appearing but have not yet reached the magnitude required to drive a sustained recovery in product orders.
- The company is monitoring domestic trailer industry antidumping and countervailing duty petitions, which could potentially lead to penalties on international competitors by late 2026.
- Strategic focus remains on maintaining a strong balance sheet and liquidity to provide flexibility for a powerful response once the freight market recovery threshold is met.
- First quarter 2026 is expected to be the weakest period of the year for both revenue and operating margins as deferred capital spending persists.
- Management anticipates full-year 2026 revenue and operating margins will likely exceed 2025 levels, despite limited visibility into the exact timing of a demand rebound.
- The company expects to recognize $4 million to $5 million in additional charges in 2026 related to facility idling, with $1 million to $2 million resulting in cash expenditures.
- Guidance for Q1 assumes revenue between $310 million and $330 million, with adjusted EPS ranging from negative $0.95 to negative $1.05.
- Management believes the industry is building pent-up demand due to multiple years of replacement cycles falling below normalized levels, positioning for a stronger 2027.
- The idling of Little Falls and Goshen facilities resulted in $16 million in non-cash charges during the fourth quarter.
- A $30 million legal settlement was paid in the fourth quarter, impacting full-year free cash flow.
- The Upfit business volume more than doubled in 2025 compared to 2023, with expectations to exceed 2,500 units in 2026 as new centers in Atlanta and Phoenix scale.
- Management has paused near-term investments in the Trailers as a Service (TaaS) initiative after establishing the foundational assets in 2025.
- Management clarified they are not exiting the refrigerated market; rather, they are repositioning the product for a better cost structure ahead of an expected 2027 market upswing.
- The Goshen closure is specifically aimed at overhead optimization by leveraging structural changes made to the truck body manufacturing network over the last several years.
- Revenue growth in the Parts and Service segment is expected to continue into 2026, though Q1 margins will likely be the weakest of the year due to startup costs for new upfit locations.
- Management expects margins to bounce back after Q1 as upfit operations normalize and new locations like Phoenix begin contributing to the revenue profile.
- Wabash expects an initial determination on February 6 regarding potential percentage penalties for international competitors related to antidumping petitions.
- While physical collection of duties would occur later in the year, management believes the industry will understand the competitive impact much sooner.
- The primary use of available cash will be to pay down the $45 million pulled on the ABL in 2025.
- Secondary capital allocation priorities include funding internal CapEx and dividends, followed by potential share repurchases or addressing high-yield bonds due in 2028.
