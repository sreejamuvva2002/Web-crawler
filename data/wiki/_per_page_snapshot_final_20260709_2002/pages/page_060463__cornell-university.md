# Cornell University

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-10

**Source:** [Spent lithium EV batteries get 95% power back with new chemical bath](https://interestingengineering.com/energy/cornells-electrochemical-bath-spent-batteries-power)  ·  `page_060463`

## Facts

- Cornell University researchers developed a battery recycling method called Direct Electrode-to-Electrode Regeneration (DEER) to recover critical minerals in lithium EV batteries.
- The DEER electrochemical bath process restores spent lithium EV batteries to 95 percent of their original power capacity.
- The method can cut recycling manufacturing costs by 56 percent compared to traditional approaches.
- Vibha Kalra, the Fred H. Rhodes Professor of Chemical Engineering at Cornell's Duffield College of Engineering, stated that the process repairs batteries as-is without shredding or powdering them and puts them back into new batteries.
- The dissolution in the chemical solution helps the battery recover its capacity, showing 95 percent recovery and shortening the circularity loop.
- Standard industry recycling typically destroys dead cells using extreme-heat furnaces or grinds them into "black mass" drenched in harsh, corrosive acids.
- When lithium-ion batteries die, a thick, crusty layer of solid electrolyte interphase builds up inside the cell, blocking energy flow even though the materials remain intact.
- Cornell's DEER process involves opening the battery casing, extracting intact electrodes, and submerging them in 1,3-dimethyl-2-imidazolidinone to dissolve the insulating buildup while preserving internal structures.
- The DEER method cuts down air pollution and slashes industrial water consumption compared to conventional recycling.
- The United States currently possesses very few domestic reserves of critical minerals required for modern batteries and depends mostly on complex, foreign supply chains.
- The US lacks the massive infrastructure needed to refine raw materials or rebuild crushed battery powder from scratch, causing domestic recycling to lag.
- By keeping battery components intact, the DEER method eliminates the need for expensive overseas refabrication and allows the entire recycling process to happen locally, cheaply, and quickly.
- The technique currently successfully treats batteries at a 70-80 percent state of health, which represents the typical retirement threshold for electric vehicles.
- Researchers plan to test the DEER method on larger, industrial-scale batteries and adapt the process to combat other forms of wear, such as permanent lithium loss.
- The study findings were published in the journal Energy and Environmental Science on June 9.
