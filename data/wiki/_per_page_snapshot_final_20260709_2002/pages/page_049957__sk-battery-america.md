# SK Battery America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2020-08-27

**Source:** [SK Battery announces major change as homeowners complain some workers have become troublesome neighbors | FOX 5 Atlanta](https://www.fox5atlanta.com/news/sk-battery-announces-major-change-as-homeowners-complain-some-workers-have-become-troublesome-neighbors)  ·  `page_049957`

## Facts

- A $2.6 billion battery plant is under construction in Commerce, Ga.
- The facility is located in Jackson County.
- SK Battery America expects the plant to eventually create more than 2000 jobs building batteries for electric cars.
- State and local governments agreed to provide $300 million in tax breaks and free land for the project.
- Contractors for the plant have been flying in overseas workers rather than using Americans for basic construction tasks.
- An SK Battery America contractor recruited workers from Micronesia, which is more than 7800 miles from Commerce, GA.
- Residents on Glenn Fuller Circle in Commerce complained that construction workers are sleeping in single-family dwellings, with neighbors counting up to 14 men at one house.
- Jackson County ordinances limit the number of unrelated people living in a home to four.
- One five-bedroom, three-bath home on Glenn Fuller Circle was reported to have housed 14 men, though workers claimed only five lived there and others were just dropping by for lunch.
- Workers from the Federated States of Micronesia signed six-month work contracts starting in July and perform labor duties at the site.
- Residents of the Federated States of Micronesia do not need a visa to work in the United States.
- SK Battery America stated it has already created more than 1000 American jobs during the construction phase.
- The company told its contractors from the start to place a priority on hiring local American workers and believes they are appropriately doing so.
- Neighbors have called the sheriff’s office multiple times due to loud drunken parties, loud music, and work vans driving across private lawns.
- A renewed effort is underway to get Immigration and Customs Enforcement to launch a comprehensive investigation into the worker housing situation.
- The FOX 5 I-Team observed that most construction workers at the site appeared to be Korean, with some sharing rental homes in neighborhoods around the plant.
- Five Korean workers were found living in a Braselton home; they did not speak English and moved out the day after the news team's visit.
- Congressman Doug Collins asked ICE and Customs and Border Protection to investigate SK Battery and its contractors, citing more than 30 houses rented to support the workforce.
- Rep. Collins highlighted a separate situation in neighboring Banks County involving an abandoned chicken farm five miles from the construction site.
- Homeowner Chris Phagan reported noticing a steady stream of work trucks heading to the abandoned farm starting in July.
- Workers at the Banks County site told Phagan they were conducting secret welding training.
- Banks County code enforcement officer Paul Ruark filed an incident report stating a manager said testing sites were set up to qualify welders.
- Rep. Collins described the Banks County operation as an effort to replace American workers with Korean foreign nationals.
- SK Battery stated it has found no information that such an off-site operation existed and clarified that any off-site workshops perform welding for the site, not training programs.
- Work at the Banks County site ceased after local authorities checked on it.
- Hart County resident and Plumbers, Pipefitters & HVACR Technicians Local Union 72 representative David Cagle insisted there are plenty of local union and non-union workers available for the construction jobs.
- Cagle stated that contractors appear to be set up to work Koreans and emphasized that Americans can man the job.
- In May, Customs and Border Protection stopped 33 Koreans at Hartsfield-Jackson airport for allegedly trying to use fake employment letters to get construction jobs at the plant.
- The contractor involved in the May CBP incident was not named.
- SK Battery announced it will now require all workers to provide daily documentation proving they are lawfully allowed to work in the country, with a policy of 'no documentation, no work'.
- The company stated that no illegal workers are permitted on site and that contractors in violation will face severe sanctions, including possible termination.
- SK Battery plans to conduct ongoing inspections to ensure contractor compliance with applicable laws.
- Rep. Collins asked Customs to suspend visas for any SK employee traveling to Georgia from anywhere in the world until the illegal worker issue is fully investigated.
- Neither CBP nor ICE commented on Rep. Collins' requests.
- The mega-plant is connected to an International Trade Commission probe and is scheduled to be up and running by 2022.
