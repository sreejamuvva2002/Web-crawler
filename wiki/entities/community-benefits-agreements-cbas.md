# Community Benefits Agreements (CBAs)

**Type:** Policy / Incentive  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[government-support|Government Support]]  ·  **Location:** Georgia, United States

## Overview

Community Benefits Agreements are private, legally enforceable contracts between developers and labor-community coalitions that secure wages, union rights, hiring standards, affordable housing, child care, environmental protections and other benefits for workers and surrounding communities.

## EV supply-chain relevance

CBAs are highlighted as a mechanism to ensure that new electric-vehicle (EV) and battery manufacturing projects in the South deliver good jobs, union standards and community benefits.

## Timeline

- **2005** — Atlanta passed an ordinance specifying worker and community benefits for the Beltline redevelopment.
- **June 2024** — Developers shelved plans for a $1.3 billion data center in Indiana after local opposition over environmental concerns (cited as CBA-related example).
- **2025** — Tennessee Republicans passed legislation prohibiting any company that enters into a CBA from receiving state economic development funds.
- **Q3 2023** — More than $125 billion worth of clean-energy manufacturing investments were announced across Georgia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Kentucky and Texas.

## Workforce & partners

- Project Labor Agreements (PLAs) — Labor agreement type used in construction phases of projects
- Community Workforce Agreements (CWAs) — PLA variant that includes community-oriented hiring and training commitments
- Union Neutrality / Card Check Agreements — Labor agreement ensuring employer neutrality during union organizing
- Labor Peace Agreements — Agreement where unions refrain from work stoppages in exchange for employer commitments

## Key facts

- Community Benefits Agreements are private contracts that do not require government action but can shape outcomes of major projects in hostile political environments.
- CBAs can secure living-wage jobs, union rights, local hiring, affordable housing, child care, environmental standards and community investments for new manufacturing facilities.
- Southern states have historically used low taxes, lax regulations and corporate subsidies to attract manufacturers, resulting in lower wages and weaker worker protections.
- Unionization rates in Southern manufacturing are the lowest in the nation and have declined sharply since the 1980s.
- Recent federal incentives (IRA, IIJA, CHIPS) have driven a surge of clean-energy and EV battery manufacturing projects in the South, creating thousands of jobs.
- Strong CBAs can mitigate community opposition, reduce legal costs for developers, and provide leverage to secure better labor standards.
- Labor-community coalitions that negotiate CBAs also build lasting organizing capacity that benefits future campaigns beyond a single project.
