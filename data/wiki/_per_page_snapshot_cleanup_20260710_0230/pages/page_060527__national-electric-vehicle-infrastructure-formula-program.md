# National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure Formula Program

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Use of Synchrophasors to Transform NEVI EV Charging Stations and Energy Storage into Distribution Grid Nodes](https://www.energycentral.com/energy-biz/post/use-synchrophasors-transform-nevi-ev-charging-stations-and-energy-storage-F502NxnizLMtgwN)  ·  `page_060527`

## Facts

- US National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure (NEVI) program starts in 2023 and runs for five years.
- NEVI will fund $5B of 600kW DC fast charging stations.
- Each station includes four 150kW chargers located 50 miles apart on primary highway corridors.
- Integrating synchrophasor grid measurements via a micro phasor measurement unit (μPMU) and microgrid controller can transform distribution grid problems into assets.
- NEVI charging stations can be turned into microgrid distribution nodes to provide grid services for asset owners.
- Co-located energy storage and renewables can enhance grid resiliency and stability via microgrid control and ultrafast μPMUs.
- Phasor measurement units (PMUs) process synchrophasors, which are precise measurements of voltage, current, and frequency phase angles at transmission/distribution grid nodes.
- Synchrophasors are time-stamped and geo-located via GPS, enabling instantaneous wide-grid views including system changes and stresses.
- PMU measurements occur at 60 samples per second, two orders of magnitude faster than SCADA systems which measure once every four seconds.
- The complete synchrophasor system includes PMUs, GPS-synchronized satellite clocks, phasor data concentrators (PDC), communications, and software.
- Utilities use PMUs at transmission grid nodes for visualization, operations, and forensics.
- Distribution grids require more accurate monitoring due to smaller power angles compared to transmission lines.
- Micro-PMUs (μPMUs) are increasingly used at distribution grid nodes to monitor and control distributed energy resources (DERs) like renewables, energy storage, and microgrids.
- Using μPMUs with microgrid control is relevant for DERs and a ubiquitous network of high-power EV charging stations sponsored by USDOT's NEVI program.
- Thousands of NEVI-class EV charging stations enhanced by energy storage and renewable generation could establish resources to provide resiliency, stability, and other grid services.
- USDOT will work through state DOTs to administer the $5B NEVI program over five years (fiscal year 2022 to 2026), primarily for light-duty EVs.
- Program technical requirements mandate high-power DC fast charging stations located no more than 50 miles apart along major state highway corridors.
- Each station requires a minimum of four 150kW DC fast chargers for a total minimum power output of 600kW.
- The program alone could fund 5,000 to 10,000 NEVI-class EV fast charging stations across the US.
- High load for local distribution grids with weak capacity is a challenge for these stations.
- High demand charges from electric utilities pose an issue for EV service providers (EVSPs).
- A network of relatively high-power nodes can provide grid services, stability, and resiliency to local and wide distribution grids.
- Many potential locations along highway corridors are in rural areas with low distribution grid capacity, requiring major upgrades for a new 600kW load.
- Even with sufficient grid capacity, utilities impose demand charges (e.g., $5/kW), challenging the EVSP business model.
- These challenges often drive the installation of energy storage and local renewable power.
- Configuring charging stations, energy storage, and renewables into a microgrid can transform them from grid loads into assets that enhance resiliency and stability.
- Integrating a μPMU with the microgrid controller provides robust operation and value to grid operators.
- A corridor segment of NEVI-class EV charging stations could be aggregated into a larger system of grid assets, with μPMU data combined and coordinated by grid operators for control.
- Synchrophasor data from NEVI EV charging station μPMUs provides situational awareness, remote monitoring, and wide-area grid visualization.
- Distribution control systems must handle intermittency from rapid EV charging adoption and new residential/commercial solar incentives.
- Combined resource variability requires a new generation of distribution grid controllers.
- Disturbances and loss of inertia from spinning generators weaken the grid, causing oscillations that can collapse it without active control.
- Under-frequency and under-voltage load shedding systems have been installed in distribution grids for years.
- Grid controllers must estimate and control distribution grid inertia using distributed energy resource management systems (DERMS) operating at high speed.
- Controllers will send real-time signals to controllable devices, with microgridded EV charging systems acting as one of those devices.
- Oscillations in the grid have been an active concern since the 1970s.
- More powerful DERMS controllers will send control signals primarily to microgrids rather than individual DER devices.
- Having all DER devices inside a microgrid allows fast, accurate control of real and reactive power flow at the point of interconnection (POI).
- Clusters of NEVI charging station microgrids located every 50 miles can implement DERMS controllers in a hierarchy to control grid voltage and frequency bottom-up.
- EV chargers can provide reactive power injection, frequency regulation, and other grid services.
- Energy storage can provide additional grid services for EVSPs, significantly improving their business model.
- FERC Order 2222 requires new DER markets to be developed for all US regions.
- ERCOT's Fast Responding Regulation Service for frequency regulation requires active control intervals of 200 mS or less and a response within 2 cycles.
- This control speed is much faster than traditional EMS SCADA systems can provide.
- EV charging stations with battery and PV can access ancillary services and DER markets for additional income.
- Microgrids meeting IEEE 2030.7 Standard for Microgrids and tested per IEEE 2030.8 are ideal for this application.
- A modular 250kW/650kWh microgrid system can be installed in a single parking stall under a PV canopy.
- Grid control and services require microgrids to directly control power flow into or out of the grid, as dispatching DERs behind the meter doesn't allow controlled power flow due to other loads.
- Microgrids supervising behind-the-meter loads enable control of both real and reactive power, giving utilities necessary distribution system control authority.
- An EVSP in central Oregon requested a near-term 300kW electrical service for 2023 and potential additional 300kW for 2024, totaling 600kW.
- The utility for the central Oregon site could provide 500kW in 2023 but no additional power until 2025.
- An EVSP in eastern Oregon requested a near-term 1MW electrical service, but the utility said it would need to upgrade a substation.
- Modeling of eight EVs simultaneously charging showed that three of four separate distribution grids in Nevada (at 24.9kV) could handle the worst-case NEVI load without difficulty.
- The lowest voltage grid (13.2kV) with the weakest amperage capacity might struggle with the simultaneous charging load.
- Logical candidates for owner/operators are EVSPs like EVgo, Electrify America, EVCS, and Tesla.
- EVSPs have experience developing site host agreements, procuring/installing infrastructure, working with utilities, and owning/operating multi-state networks of high-powered chargers.
- EVSPs face challenging O&M economics for DC fast charging stations and seek revenue sources to improve them.
- Additional capital costs for components and energy storage to convert stations into hybrid μPMU-enhanced microgrids could be offset by upside revenue potential, turning break-even operations profitable.
- Utilities could also be owner/operators but typically have only regional service territories, little multi-state DC fast charging network experience, and dissimilar customer profiles.
- Installing large energy storage to charge at a lower electrical service (e.g., 200kW) but discharge at 600kW peak can mitigate utility demand charges if the storage recharges during valleys or uses solar panels.
- Higher projected charger utilization requires larger energy storage devices to avoid running out of discharge power.
- Energy storage mitigating EV charger utility demand charges has a positive rate of return only in territories with extremely high demand charges (e.g., $10/kW).
- Factoring in transformer upgrades, switchgear, grid services, and utility benefits can give energy storage an immediate positive return on investment.
- Adding local PV generation to the microgrid could further improve economics.
- Electricity networks balance generation with loads through tightly orchestrated control of frequency and voltage.
- For over a hundred years, generation relied on central plants with spinning reserves for frequency regulation.
- Networks increasingly rely on distributed generation from wind, solar, and renewables rather than central coal/nuclear plants.
- This shift reduces spinning reserves due to fossil plant decommissioning.
- Energy storage smooths variability, helps with dispatchability of renewables, and provides grid stability.
- Energy storage can react within milliseconds for grid frequency regulation, much faster than the 15-minute period typical of natural gas peaker turbines.
- A network of distributed 600kW (or greater) microgrids located every 50 miles could provide dramatic stability, resiliency, and other grid services.
- As of February 2022, California had 113 NEVI-class EV charging stations and planned to add another 143 via NEVI funding.
- State funding in California will bring the total to approximately 500 potential microgrid sites.
- USDOT has approved NEVI plans for all other states and some territories, potentially providing thousands of distributed microgrids nationwide.
- Federal and state funding is available for medium- and heavy-duty EV charging infrastructure beyond light-duty EVs.
- Charging stations along US highway corridors for class 8 heavy-duty trucks could be 5 to 10MW, requiring a greater role for energy storage.
- Overnight charging depots for EV buses and heavy-duty port drayage vehicles could exceed 10MW.
- Adding massive charging stations to NEVI-class networks would provide excellent assets for resiliency and grid reliability at distribution or transmission levels.
- High-power EV stations often require transformer upgrades and could lead to expensive substation upgrades.
- A network of EV charging stations configured with energy storage and high-speed control via μPMU/microgrid controllers could act as pseudo-generation, potentially delaying substation upgrade needs.
- IEEE 2030.5 is the communications protocol adopted by California for energy storage, solar inverters, and EV charging.
- IEEE 2030.5 has been extensively tested by NIST for cybersecurity as a client-server messaging system for secure internet communications.
- One of the largest field deployments of this protocol is in Western Australia, including remote control of entire towns over the internet.
- The PMU communications standard is IEEE C37.118.
- New streaming telemetry transport protocols are being introduced by IEEE to enable faster PMU data transmission over the internet (IEEE 2664), using TLS security and very short messages for distribution area control systems.
- μPMUs can be used to detect and diagnose potential cyber and physical attacks on DER systems.
- Cyber-attacks and physical attacks are growing problems leading to increasing DER cybersecurity requirements.
- NEVI-class EV charging stations with micro-grid control and μPMU synchronization can provide distribution grid visualization and control at nodes located 50 miles or less apart.
- Extending PMUs to the distribution grid level via expansive μPMU use brings synchrophasor advantages to DER assets.
- These systems can detect grid events like outages and assist with resiliency, especially when co-located with energy storage.
- They can provide high-value grid services including VAR compensation, frequency regulation, and demand charge mitigation.
- They enable installation of large loads and grid assets in regions with relatively weak distribution grids.
- Co-located energy storage and PV can economically enable high-power EV charging even in weak grid zones.
- They can defer costly distribution and potential transmission grid upgrades for utilities.
- High-speed μPMU synchrophasor measurements can quickly detect cyber-attacks, while IEEE security protocols can mitigate/defend against them.
