## Facts

- Hyundai announced it will continue expanding its facility in southeast Georgia with a $2.7 billion investment over the next three years.
- The expansion will create 3,000 new jobs.
- The company's adjacent battery plant construction site was raided this month by federal immigration agents.
- The raid led to the detention and deportation of more than 300 South Koreans.
- Hyundai's announcement indicates the raid will not deter their plans to invest in the Bryan County site.
- The opening of the battery plant has been delayed due to the federal raid.
- Didi Caldwell is president and CEO of Global Location Strategies and one of the world's most authoritative site selection and incentive negotiations experts.
- Gov. Brian Kemp congratulated Hyundai on the announcement, writing on social media that it reinforces Hyundai's commitment to this project and the state.
- Savannah Mayor Van Johnson wrote on social media that the project will bring new opportunities to the workforce, bolster infrastructure, and strengthen Savannah's position as a hub for advanced manufacturing.
- The investment announcement comes weeks after a mass immigration raid at the site that rounded up hundreds of workers.
- The raid caused diplomatic tension between the U.S. and South Korea.
- The raid delayed completion of a Hyundai LG Energy Solutions battery factory.
- South Korea's president said companies from his country might be "more hesitant" to invest in the U.S. if they fear difficulty related to immigration.
- Georgia has key economic ties to Korean firms including Hyundai, SK Battery, and Qcells.
- Didi Caldwell stated that the raid undermines trust in the U.S. administration having Korean interests at heart, which is critical for investment decisions.
- Overseas firms sometimes send workers to the U.S. under various short-term visas, often resulting in overstays and an improperly documented workforce.
- There is a need for specialized technicians and engineers experienced in these operations to transfer institutional knowledge to the U.S., as companies aim to replicate successful models exactly.
- Georgia is a right-to-work state, which tends to foster more friendly labor management relations.
- Georgia has a very strong technical college system and a state incentive program called Quick Start to help companies identify, recruit, and train workers.
- About 40% of construction workers in the United States are immigrants with various levels of documentation.
- Fear of ICE detention is exacerbating labor shortages, causing documented and undocumented workers to miss work or flee due to raid rumors.
- Companies investing in the U.S. prioritize certainty, as uncertainty related to immigration, tariffs, and monetary/fiscal policy acts as an enemy of investment.
- Scenario planning for investments has grown by an order of magnitude compared to the past.
- Gov. Brian Kemp stated he does not expect the Hyundai raid to deter companies from locating in Georgia.
- Kemp spoke to reporters on Tuesday at the groundbreaking for the Rivian Electric Vehicle Plant east of Atlanta.
- Kemp reported having good conversations with current and prospective companies in Georgia, as well as with the White House regarding visa issues.
- Former President Donald Trump campaigned on cutting red tape and improving efficiency regarding visa processing.
