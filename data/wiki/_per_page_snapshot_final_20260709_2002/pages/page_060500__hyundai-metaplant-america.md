# Hyundai Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-12-16

**Source:** [Water Council meeting draws citizens angry at 4-well plan for Hyundai plant - Statesboro Herald](https://www.statesboroherald.com/local/water-council-meeting-draws-citizens-angry-4-well-plan-hyundai-plant/)  ·  `page_060500`

## Facts

- The Thursday evening, Dec. 14 meeting of the Coastal Georgia Regional Water Planning Council addressed citizen concerns regarding Bulloch and Bryan counties’ permit applications for four large wells to supply the Hyundai Motor Group manufacturing complex.
- More than 100 people attended the meeting at the River Room at Ogeechee Technical College, with more than a dozen speaking during the public comment period.
- EPD Water Supply Program Manager Wei Zeng, Ph.D., stated that citizen concerns will be considered to guide the agency's assessment before issuing well permits.
- Zeng reported hearing concerns over potential impacts, local impacts, saltwater intrusion, and calls for surface water alternatives.
- The EPD will complete a draft of groundwater withdrawal permits followed by a 30-day comment period before the EPD director signs them.
- EPD is required to hold its own public meeting on the permits, which Zeng said might occur even before draft documents are released due to the volume of concerns, though no timeline has been set.
- Engineers from Thomas & Hutton and Hofstadter & Associates, a consultant from TPR Consulting Services, and Bryan County’s engineering director presented the plan to meet projected growth needs and deliver water to Hyundai Metaplant America.
- Hyundai Metaplant America is being built at a site provided by the four-county Joint Development Authority in northern Bryan County.
- The electric vehicle and battery manufacturing complex is targeted to begin production in 2025.
- The facility is projected to employ 8,500 people after a hire-up period lasting several years.
- The deep Floridan aquifer supplies groundwater for much of southern Georgia and coastal South Carolina and has shown signs of saltwater intrusion along the coast for more than 50 years.
- Georgia has assigned Bryan and Liberty counties to a “yellow zone” with restrictions on EPD permitting of new wells for over a decade, while Chatham County and Effingham County are in a “red zone” with active reductions in existing permits.
- Bulloch County and surrounding areas are in a “green zone” with no such restrictions.
- County officials proposed for Bryan County to own two wells and Bulloch County to own two wells, but all four wells will be drilled within Bulloch County near the county line.
- Bulloch County’s application seeks a permit for two wells that together could withdraw up to 3.15 million gallons per day.
- Bryan County applied for a permit for two wells that together could withdraw up to 3.5 million gallons per day.
- The four wells in total could withdraw up to 6.65 million gallons per day.
- Bulloch County officials plan to request later that their two wells’ combined limit be increased to 3.5 million gallons per day, making the total for the four wells 7 million gallons per day, according to engineer Trent Thompson.
- Citizen Charles Moore noted that South Effingham and Chatham counties are in the red zone and “begging for water,” while Liberty and Bryan counties are in the yellow zone and “about to run out of water because they mismanaged their water supply.”
- Moore questioned who gave Bulloch County the authority to furnish water to these areas and the Hyundai plant, noting no formal agreement or memorandum of understanding was provided.
- Citizens noted counties cited a figure of almost 10 million gallons per day in paperwork submitted to EPD, but engineer Trent Thompson clarified this is a prediction of future demand, not an amount sought in the current application.
- Thompson stated no permits have been applied for that future projected amount and that decisions will depend on future land use planning.
- Thompson discussed using surface water from the Savannah River basin as part of a long-term strategy to meet regional water demand.
- Developing a surface water system to reach the Hyundai plant could take 25 years, according to Thompson.
- The city of Savannah operates an industrial and domestic water treatment plant on Abercorn Creek with a reported capacity of 75 million gallons per day.
- Thompson said the 25-year estimate depends on funding, expanding the surface water plant, obtaining additional capacities from Abercorn Creek, working with EPD, and funding water mains to Bryan County.
- Another consultant noted that the surface water solution would be much more expensive than drilling the four wells and installing nearly 12 miles of pipeline to connect the farthest well to the Hyundai plant.
- Citizen Randy Proctor suggested obtaining water from the Savannah River instead of opposing a surface water solution outright.
- The Coastal Georgia Regional Water Planning Council encompasses nine counties, has about 25 board members, and its chairman is Development Authority of Bulloch County CEO Benjy Thompson.
- Shayne Wood of CDM Smith provided an overview of the council's regional planning work, including a 156-page updated Coastal Georgia Regional Water Plan completed in June.
