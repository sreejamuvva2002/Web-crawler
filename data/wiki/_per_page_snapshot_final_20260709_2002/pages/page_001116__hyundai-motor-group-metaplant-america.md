# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-09-20

**Source:** [Hyundai’s water use: What does it take to build an EV? - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2024/09/20/hyundais-water-use-what-does-it-take-to-build-an-ev/)  ·  `page_001116`

## Facts

- Bryan and Bulloch counties are seeking permits to withdraw 6.625 million gallons of water a day from the Floridan aquifer to support the Hyundai EV manufacturing plant.
- Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America is the legal entity contracted with the state of Georgia to receive $2.1 billion in tax deferments, subsidized construction costs, and other perks.
- Consultants hired to secure permits described Hyundai's water usage data as a commercial secret for six months.
- Thomas & Hutton Vice President Trent Thompson stated they are not in possession of the breakdown of needs for Hyundai’s various uses including fire protection, EV production, bathrooms, cafeteria, battery assembly, and landscaping.
- HMGMA communications head Joe LaMuraglia stated that water use data is proprietary and impossible to calculate exactly by dividing usage by vehicles produced.
- Local residents in Bryan and Bulloch County are mobilizing to oppose the water permits due to fears of adverse effects on farm wells.
- The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers has announced it is reevaluating the impact of the water permits.
- Retired metallurgical engineer Ken Copi expressed concern about knowing what the water will be used for and requested mitigation or reduction methods.
- An informational meeting regarding the water permits was held by the Georgia Environmental Protection Division at Southeastern Bulloch High School.
- Construction of the Hyundai EV plant began in January 2023 on almost 3,000 acres of clear-cut land just south of I-16.
- Hyundai officials aim to bring their first electric vehicle off the assembly line by the end of 2024 to qualify for Biden Administration’s 2024 Inflation Reduction Act buyer incentives.
- Hyundai initially requested 4 million gallons a day for the Ellabell site in August 2023, which surfaced in public documents submitted to EPD.
- Officials at the Georgia Department of Economic Development and the Savannah Harbor Interstate 16 Corridor Joint Development Authority (JDA) knew of the water request before finalizing the agreement in July 2022 but did not push back.
- Savannah Economic Development Authority president and CEO Trip Tollison described his organization's role as a middleman between Bryan County and EPD regarding water volumes.
- In June, Thomas & Hutton revised the justification of need for water usage, citing car manufacturer needs as "industry trade secrets."
- The justification document submitted to EPD fails to connect requested water volume to Hyundai’s manufacturing processes at the Ellabell site.
- The document states that without the wells, Bryan County would not meet commitments to provide water capacity for upcoming manufacturing facilities and regional growth would stall.
- Four wells will be drilled in Bulloch County, just over the county line from Bryan County.
- Bulloch County was chosen because it is the only one of four counties whose development authorities make up the JDA that is not under state-imposed water withdrawal restrictions to protect the aquifer and avoid a lawsuit with South Carolina regarding saltwater intrusion in Hilton Head wells.
- Development authority officials explained that water needs were for the manufacturing plant plus anticipated industrial, commercial, and residential projects near the site.
- Surface water from the Savannah River was rejected as not cost-effective compared to Bulloch well water.
- Drilling wells in Effingham County was also rejected due to cost effectiveness.
- Trip Tollison stated in July that a surface water alternative would have cost $362 million more than Bulloch well water and taken 10 years longer to implement.
- Consultant Trent Thompson told the JDA in August that the cost for local counties for a surface water option would be upwards of $1 billion.
- Tollison expressed confidence that Bulloch water wells would not negatively impact area residents or farmers, stating they would never pursue a project where groundwater or drinking water would be sacrificed.
- Hyundai has not been required to explain why it needs the right to use the equivalent of six Olympic swimming pools full of water a day in public documents.
- The nine-page justification document originally submitted in August 2023 and revised in June 2024 lists "N/A" for unit loadings for the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America/Battery JV.
- Hyundai listed various water uses including concrete during construction, office domestic water, cafeteria operations, utility systems, boilers, chillers, HVAC systems, car body leak testing, coolant, paint shop equipment, fire suppression, plant cleaning, and general manufacturing operational use.
- Hyundai's 2023 sustainability report disclosed an average water use of 714 gallons per vehicle across its global manufacturing lines.
- Tesla reports using 655 gallons per vehicle for EV assembly.
- Georgia Tech Professor Bert Bras stated that electric vehicle assembly generally uses less water than gas-powered cars due to less part cleaning required for engines and gearboxes.
- Hyundai plans to produce 300,000 vehicles a year at the Ellabell site.
- Based on Hyundai's global average, producing 300,000 cars would equate to approximately 0.587 million gallons per day.
- The EPA’s Lean Water Toolkit estimates industrial workers use 10–25 gallons of water per shift, with higher estimates needed for facilities with toilets, showers, and kitchens.
- The Hyundai site in Bryan County is projected to hire 8,500 workers.
- Using the EPA's higher-end metric, the workforce alone would require an additional 0.213 million gallons a day.
- LG Energy Solution will be making batteries for the Hyundai cars assembled in Ellabell but has not disclosed its water needs despite multiple requests.
- LG Energy Solution stated they are cooperating with the county regarding water usage at their joint venture with Hyundai Motor Group.
- LG's 2023 sustainability report states it evaluates water risks before establishing facilities and avoids using groundwater, obtaining processed water from approved suppliers instead.
- All water expected to be piped to the Ellabell plant will come from groundwater in the Floridan aquifer.
- Researchers estimate mining EV battery materials takes 500,000 to 2 million gallons of water to extract a ton of lithium.
- The LG battery plant is projected to have an annual production capacity of 30 gigawatt hours, equal to about 450,000 vehicle batteries.
- An SK Innovation battery site in Stanton, Tenn., is predicted to require 1.2 million gallons of water per day for a 45 GWh annual capacity.
- Scaled to LG's Ellabell production, the SK Innovation rate would mean a 0.8 million gallon per day requirement at the Ellabell site.
- SK Battery America in Commerce requested 3.6 million gallons per day for its EV battery factory with a 22 GWh capacity.
- Scaling SK Battery's usage to LG's Ellabell production equates to 4.9 million gallons a day, which is more than Hyundai has requested for the whole site.
- Irrigation at the megasite will require a portion of the 4 million gallons a day until the wastewater treatment plant is operational.
- Trent Thompson told the JDA board in August that once operational, reclaimed water will be sent back to Hyundai for use.
