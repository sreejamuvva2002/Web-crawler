# Hyundai-LG Battery Plant Joint Venture

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-04

**Source:** [Immigration Raid at Hyundai Plant: Fallout and Policy Changes - LegalClarity](https://legalclarity.org/immigration-raid-at-hyundai-plant-fallout-and-policy-changes/)  ·  `page_009959`

## Facts

- On September 4, 2025, hundreds of federal agents detained 475 workers at Hyundai Motor Group’s electric vehicle complex in Ellabell, Bryan County, Georgia.
- The detention was one of the largest worksite immigration raids in recent U.S. history.
- The operation was carried out by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and the investigative arm of the Department of Homeland Security (DHS).
- The raid targeted employees at a battery plant being built as a joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution.
- Most detained workers were South Korean nationals working on temporary visas that did not authorize employment.
- 23 Mexican nationals were also among those detained.
- LG Energy Solution reported that 47 of its direct employees and roughly 250 contractor workers at the joint venture factory were swept up in the operation.
- Detainees were transported to the Folkston ICE Processing Center, located more than 100 miles from the plant.
- Steven Schrank, special agent in charge of Homeland Security Investigations for Georgia and Alabama, stated no criminal charges were filed at the time and investigators were sorting out employment details.
- Schrank noted some workers had been hired by subcontractors rather than directly by Hyundai or LG.
- Schrank characterized the workers as “either illegally present in the U.S. or working unlawfully.”
- Many South Korean workers had entered the U.S. on short-term business visas or through the visa waiver program, neither of which authorizes paid employment.
- Internal ICE arrest records reviewed by the New York Times showed that among a sample of 11 detained workers, six had entered on B-1 or B-1/B-2 business visas and four had used the visa waiver program.
- The visa waiver program permits stays of up to 90 days for travel purposes.
- Ten of the eleven sampled workers were recorded as “working unlawfully,” and one worker deemed not to have violated his visa was still forced to leave the country.
- South Korean companies had long relied on a “grey zone” of loose interpretation around visa categories, sending technicians to install specialized manufacturing equipment under the guise of business visits or supervisory roles.
- One equipment technician told Reuters he had previously obtained a B-1 visa by describing himself as a “supervisor” rather than an “equipment specialist.”
- Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick stated that Hyundai had “skirted the rules” by using tourist visas and called on the company to “change it.”
- Hyundai responded that it has “zero tolerance” for those who do not follow the law and said it was investigating to ensure subcontractors complied with immigration requirements.
- Workers reported having their phones confiscated during the raid, leaving them unable to contact their families.
- Images showed detainees shackled at the wrists, waist, and ankles.
- Workers described overcrowded and unsanitary cells, mouldy beds, and foul-smelling water.
- Some detainees alleged that ICE agents failed to inform them of their rights and mocked their nationality.
- After a week of tense negotiations between Seoul and Washington, the two governments reached a deal to release the workers and fly them home rather than subject them to formal deportation proceedings.
- A chartered Korean Air flight departed Atlanta on September 11, 2025, carrying 316 South Korean nationals along with 10 Chinese nationals, 3 Japanese nationals, and 1 Indonesian.
- One South Korean worker chose to remain in the U.S. because he had family in the country.
- South Korea’s foreign ministry called on the U.S. Congress to support a new visa category for Korean firms.
- South Korean President Lee Jae Myung called the raid “bewildering” and warned that if the U.S. did not ease visa requirements, South Korean businesses would “hesitate to build new factories” in America.
- President Lee emphasized that the detained individuals were not permanent employees but technicians helping install equipment.
- South Korean Foreign Minister Cho Hyun spoke directly with U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio about the workers’ status.
- South Korean lawmakers held emergency parliamentary hearings, with ruling party members declaring that “if the United States truly wants to attract investment from Korean companies, this kind of incident should not happen.”
- Construction and operations were halted at the battery facility and at least 22 other Korean factory sites across the automotive, shipbuilding, steel, and electrical equipment sectors as companies paused to verify their employees’ visa statuses.
- Seoul launched a formal investigation into whether its citizens had suffered human rights violations during detention, examining visa status, conditions of confinement, and access to counsel and interpreters.
- As of mid-2026, no final conclusions from that investigation have been published.
- DHS Secretary Kristi Noem defended the raid, arguing it would not deter investment because it created regulatory certainty.
- White House border czar Tom Homan signaled on September 7 that additional worksite raids were planned.
- President Trump posted on Truth Social on September 15 that skilled foreign workers are “welcome” in the U.S. when brought in to train the domestic workforce, adding that he did not want to “frighten off or disincentivize Investment into America by outside Countries or Companies.”
- President Trump later told reporters he was “very much opposed” to the raid, citing an understanding that foreign experts were needed to set up specialized facilities.
- Deputy Secretary of State Christopher Landau expressed “deep regret” over the incident during a meeting with South Korean counterparts on September 14 and assured those affected would face no disadvantages in reentering the United States.
- In November 2025, Hyundai CEO José Muñoz said publicly that the White House had phoned him to apologize for the raid, calling it an “accident.”
- Muñoz said he received “apologies from the president of the United States.”
- The White House declined to confirm or deny the apology, issuing a statement that foreign workers “must enter the United States legally and with proper work authorisations.”
- On September 30, 2025, the U.S. and South Korea reached an agreement clarifying that South Korean workers could install, service, and repair equipment at investment sites using existing B-1 temporary visas and the ESTA visa waiver program.
- U.S. officials acknowledged “practical legislative constraints” that prevented creating a new visa category or increasing quotas.
- The U.S. Embassy in Seoul established a new section dedicated to visas for South Korean businesses and opened a communication channel with South Korean missions to improve coordination.
- Industry representatives described these measures as a “short-term fix” that did not resolve the underlying uncertainty for workers, who could still be denied entry at U.S. ports.
- A formal U.S.-South Korea Business Travel and Visa Working Group met at least three times, including a January 2026 session at which the U.S. highlighted a newly published fact sheet on allowable uses of business visas.
- Representatives Young Kim of California and Sydney Kamlager-Dove introduced the “Partner with Korea Act” (H.R. 4687) in July 2025, proposing the creation of 15,000 E-4 highly skilled work visas specifically for Korean nationals with specialized expertise.
- As of mid-2026, the bill remains in committee.
- Governor Brian Kemp’s office had celebrated the Hyundai Metaplant as a generational economic opportunity for Georgia, and the state had committed roughly $2 billion in taxpayer-backed incentives to the project.
- Kemp was not given advance notice of the federal operation and scrambled to contact Trump aides for details.
- Kemp’s communications team agonized over the initial statement, at one point removing the word “excited” from a draft to avoid appearing too supportive of Hyundai amid the fallout.
- Publicly, Kemp maintained that companies must follow the law while defending the need for specialized foreign workers to install equipment that requires expertise unavailable in the domestic workforce.
- Kemp noted that Hyundai had been “following state laws and state rules.”
- In October 2025, Kemp traveled to South Korea to shore up the relationship, meeting with Korean industry executives and attending a ceremony marking 40 years of Georgia’s economic presence in Seoul.
- Kemp acknowledged that “we certainly had to do some explaining about what happened” but said he had seen no signs that the raid had deterred investment.
- At least a half-dozen businesses in the area surrounding the plant reported double-digit percentage drops in revenue.
- The Viêt Huong Supermarket in Ellabell, which had purchased a former Piggly Wiggly grocery store to serve the Korean workforce, saw its customer base drop to what its owners described as a “trickle.”
- A Korean-American restaurant in nearby Pooler reported an 18% drop in weekly income.
- Real estate agent Sean Kim, who had built a business housing Korean workers, saw his livelihood evaporate and moved back to Atlanta by the end of September.
- Local business owners reported that Hispanic construction workers and their families also stopped frequenting stores, apparently frightened by the heightened immigration enforcement in the area.
- Construction at the 2,900-acre site was suspended in the days following the raid.
- On September 6, protesters gathered outside the plant to demonstrate against what they called government overreach.
- Hyundai Engineering America served as the general contractor and hired a network of subcontractors, some of which faced allegations of mistreating workers well before the September operation.
- In August 2024, two workers named Martin Garcia and Jonathon Rincon filed a federal lawsuit against subcontractor SYS-CON and related entities H&B USA and PPE Trading Co., alleging they had been denied owed raises and overtime pay.
- That case was settled in October 2025 for $43,000, paid by PPE Trading Co., after a federal judge initially rejected an earlier settlement proposal.
- In April 2023, a worker named Victor Javier Cajija Gamboa, employed by Louisiana-based steel contractor Eastern Constructors Inc., died in a 60-foot fall at the site.
- The contractor was subsequently labeled a “severe violator” by federal safety regulators.
- Local contractors publicly complained about being shut out of work at the megasite in favor of Korean-affiliated subcontractors, some of which operated from residential addresses with minimal local footprints.
- Hyundai CEO José Muñoz said shortly after the raid that the disruption would cause a “minimum two to three months delay” for the battery plant, which had been scheduled to open by the end of 2025.
- The company said it would source batteries from other plants in the region while waiting for the Georgia facility to come online.
- Replacing the specialized South Korean labor proved difficult.
- Hyundai held a job fair at Savannah Technical College in October 2025 that attracted more than 350 applicants, but internal reports indicated high turnover among new hires.
- As of late 2025, the main vehicle plant was operating at only one shift per day.
- In September 2025, Hyundai announced a $2.7 billion expansion to increase vehicle production capacity to 500,000 units per year, bringing total investment in the auto plant to $10.3 billion, separate from the $4.3 billion joint venture with LG for the battery factory.
- Muñoz said the company was “not discouraged” and that its commitment to the site had not changed.
- Hyundai had separately pledged to increase its total U.S. investment to $26 billion through 2028.
- By April 2026, Muñoz confirmed at the Semafor World Economy conference that the battery plant was “fully operational” and calibrating for production, with an opening scheduled for later that month.
- The facility is designed to supply battery cells for electric vehicles assembled at the adjacent Hyundai Metaplant.
- Under its agreement with the state and local authorities, Hyundai is contractually obligated to invest $5.545 billion and employ at least 8,500 workers at an average annual salary of $58,105 by 2031, with the jobs retained through 2048.
- As of September 2025, the site employed 3,129 workers.
