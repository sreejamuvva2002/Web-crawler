# Dublin-Laurens County Development Authority

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Incentives | Dublin-Laurens County Development Authority](https://dlcda.com/business-advantages/incentives)  ·  `page_056128`

## Facts

- The Dublin-Laurens County Development Authority is the primary conduit for business incentives offered to qualified industrial prospects, allowing relocating or expanding companies to offset transition costs and risks associated with investing and creating jobs in Dublin-Laurens County.
- Incentives offered through the state of Georgia and local community require signed performance agreements mandating companies meet certain job and investment expectations.
- Manufacturers, distributors, and certain qualifying businesses may be offered exemptions from local property taxes for a negotiated time period based on commitments to new jobs and capital investment.
- Development Authority staff assists companies in structuring proposed tax incentives to comply with local, state, and federal law.
- The Dublin-Laurens County Development Authority can serve as a conduit for low-interest Industrial Revenue Bonds (IRBs) for qualifying projects that obtain external letters of credit for collateral.
- Local leadership commits to assist with expedited permitting and plan review for certain projects, to the extent allowed by local and state law.
- Qualifying projects are eligible for Laurens County Closing Fund (LCCF) grant funds usable for real property improvements and other expenditures allowed under Georgia law.
- Qualifying companies may be eligible to receive donations of land located in established industrial parks based on proposed jobs and investment projections.
- Companies generating a significant economic impact may be eligible for assistance with the preparation of a building pad.
- In 2005, Georgia became the first state in the Southeast to adopt a Single Factor Gross Receipts apportionment formula for corporate income tax.
- This formula treats a company's gross receipts or sales in Georgia as the only relevant factor for determining the portion of income subject to Georgia's six percent corporate income tax.
- Georgia is one of only 13 states currently using Single Factor Apportionment, which significantly reduces the effective rate of Georgia income taxation for companies with substantial sales to customers outside Georgia.
- Georgia does not use the Throw Back Rule, which many states use to tax income from sales of goods or services to out-of-state customers if the customer's state lacks that tax income.
- Companies and headquarters engaged in strategic industries such as manufacturing, warehousing, distribution, processing, telecommunications, broadcasting, tourism, and research and development may qualify for Georgia's Job Tax Credit Program.
- Depending on the community's tier, companies must create between five and 25 net new full-time jobs in the first year to qualify for the Job Tax Credit.
- Credits may also be accrued for additional jobs created in years 2-5, but jobs created outside of year five may not be claimed unless a new threshold for job creation is met.
- Qualified companies can claim tax credits with a value of $750-$3,500 per job, per year, beginning with the first taxable year and continuing for following years the job is maintained.
- An additional $500 credit is offered in counties that participate in a multi-county Join Development Authority (JDA).
- Increased tax credits, equal to Tier 1 credits, are allowed for companies creating jobs in Less Developed Census Tracts (LDCT), Opportunity Zones (OZ), Military Zones, or Georgia's 40 least developed counties.
- Credits may offset 100 percent of state corporate income tax liability in Tier 1 and 2 counties, or 50 percent in Tier 3 and 4 counties.
- Unused Job Tax Credits may be carried forward for 10 years from the close of the taxable year in which qualified jobs were established.
- In Tier 1 counties, excess credits may be credited to Georgia payroll withholding taxes with a limitation of $3,500 per job, per year.
- The Port Tax Credit Bonus is available to taxpayers who increase imports or exports through a Georgia port by 10 percent over the previous or base year.
- Base year port traffic must be at least 75 net tons, five containers, or 10 TEUs; if not met, the percentage increase will be calculated using these figures as the base.
- The Port Tax Credit Bonus can be used with either the Job or Investment Tax Credit program provided requirements are met, and may offset up to 50 percent of corporate income tax liability.
- Unused Port Tax Credits may be carried forward for 10 years provided the increase in port traffic remains above the minimum level and job/investment requirements continue to be met.
- The Port Tax Credit Bonus cannot be utilized with the Quality Jobs Tax Credit and is limited to Opportunity Zones, Military Zones, and Less Developed Census Tracts by existing large distribution centers.
- An additional $1,250 per job credit is added to the Job Tax Credit for taxpayers with qualified increases in shipments through a Georgia port.
- Companies creating at least 50 jobs in a 12-month period where each job pays wages at least 110 percent of the county average are eligible for the Quality Jobs Tax Credit.
- The Quality Jobs Tax Credit provides $2,500-$5,000 per job, per year, for up to five years, and new quality jobs created within seven years can qualify.
- Quality Jobs Tax Credits may offset payroll withholding once all other tax liability is exhausted and may be carried forward for 10 years.
- Georgia offers a research and development incentive providing a 10 percent tax credit of increased RD expenses subject to a base amount calculation.
- The R&D base amount equals Current Year Georgia Gross Receipts multiplied by the average ratio of qualified Georgia research expenses to gross receipts for the preceding three taxable years, or 0.300, whichever is less.
- For new Georgia companies or those with no prior R&D expenditures in Georgia, the base amount is 30 percent of the current year's Georgia gross receipts.
- The R&D credit is applied to 50 percent of net Georgia income tax liability after all other credits have been applied.
- In the first five years of a newly formed business entity in Georgia, any excess R&D credit can be applied to state payroll withholding.
- Companies employing at least 1,800 new employees and either investing a minimum of $450 million or having a minimum annual payroll of $150 million may claim the Mega Project Tax Credit.
- The Mega Project Tax Credit provides $5,250 per job, per year for the first five years of each net new job position, with excess credits eligible for use against payroll withholding and carried forward for 10 years.
- Employers purchasing or building qualified child care facilities are eligible for Georgia income tax credits equal to 100 percent of construction costs, spread over 10 years at ten percent each year.
- Unused child care construction credits can be carried forward three years, and the facility must be licensed by the state.
- Employers providing or sponsoring child care for employees are eligible for a credit against Georgia income tax equal to 75 percent of direct costs, with operating cost credits carried forward five years.
- All child care credits can be used against 50 percent of the taxpayer's income tax liability in a given year.
- The Georgia Department of Labor coordinates the federal Work Opportunity Tax Credit Program (WOTC) for private-sector businesses hiring individuals from nine target groups facing employment barriers.
- Target groups include certain TANF and food stamp recipients, and residents of Empowerment Zones or Rural Renewal Counties.
- Participating companies can reduce federal income tax liability with a WOTC between $1,200 to $9,000 per qualified employee depending on the target group.
- The most frequently certified WOTC is $2,400 for each adult new hire, requiring GDOL certification before claiming on a federal return.
- Qualified equipment purchases or leases are exempt from sales tax when used in the manufacturing process.
- Under certain conditions, primary material handling equipment in warehouses and distribution centers, computer equipment, and Class 100 (or less) clean room machinery, equipment, and materials can also be exempted from sales tax.
- Effective January 1, 2011, business inventory is exempt from state property taxes at 0.25 mills.
- Many Georgia counties exempt up to 100 percent of qualified raw material, work-in-process, and finished goods inventory under the local-option Freeport law.
- In most counties, distribution center and warehouse inventories are exempt if destined to be shipped out of state.
- Georgia is home to multiple Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ) sites and facilitates the program to streamline importing/exporting and lower costs for qualified companies.
- The FTZ program allows qualified companies to defer, decrease, or eliminate duties on materials imported from overseas that are used in products assembled in Georgia.
- GDOL assists company recruitment by posting job notices, collecting and screening applications/resumes, providing interview space, scheduling interviews, and hosting job fairs.
- GDOL works with private employment agencies that list jobs with the state to assist in hiring.
- Georgia's Quick Start program provides customized training for skill-based jobs at no cost to qualifying companies, providing training space, instructors, and materials, potentially saving millions in training costs.
- A company's direct investment in training can be claimed as a retraining tax credit of up to $500 per full-time employee per approved program, with a total maximum of $1,250 per employee per year.
- Retraining programs must be approved by the Technical College System of Georgia and focus on quality/productivity enhancements and certain software technologies.
- The retraining tax credit offsets up to 50 percent of a company's state corporate income tax liability, can be carried forward 10 years, and can be combined with other tax credits.
- The HOPE Scholarship provides tuition assistance at one of Georgia's 35 public colleges or universities for graduating high school seniors with a B or better average.
- The HOPE Grant allows all Georgians to receive degree or certificate programs at low cost through Georgia's technical colleges and schools, advantageous for relocating families and company training.
- Worksource Heart of Georgia covers 17 counties including Appling, Bleckley, Candler, Dodge, Emanuel, Evans, Jeff Davis, Johnson, Laurens, Montgomery, Tattnall, Telfair, Toombs, Treutlen, Wayne, Wheeler, and Wilcox.
- This area receives Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) funds for On-The-Job training, Youth Services, and Individual Training Accounts.
