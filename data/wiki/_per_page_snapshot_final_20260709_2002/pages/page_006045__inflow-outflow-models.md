# Inflow-Outflow Models

## Facts

- The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and the Georgia Center of Innovation for Energy Technology teamed up with DemocracyLab Volunteers to provide collaborative tools for visualizing environmental and economic impacts in local communities, states, and countries.
- The visualization tools utilize 24 indicators including job growth, value-added, air quality, energy use, water use, land use, biodiversity, local conservation projects, and sustainable development goals.
- Supply chain analysis features show which industries provide inputs and receive outputs from specific sectors.
- State commodity and job totals are tracked using Pymrio and MARIO Input-Output Analysis.
- The collaborative tools visualize economic inflow-outflow using economy-wide input-output models of industries, households, and local environments.
- Available reporting tools and directories include Georgia Farmers Markets, Georgia Recycling Directories, Georgia Supply Chain Inflow-Outflow, Georgia County Selection, Georgia Industry Annual Payrolls, and Georgia Land Use Inflow-Outflow.
- The platform supports creating economic development pages to weigh outcomes during community decision-making regarding environmental aspects, quality job creation, land and water use, health impacts, and value-added contributions to the state and county tax base.
- Users can evaluate prospective industries and their potential direct and indirect job creation alongside their potential environmental performance.
- The tools help determine how technological improvements in local industries, utilities, or consumption patterns could transform local outcomes toward cleaner air, water, land conservation, and biodiversity.
- Community carbon cycling resources explore how manufacturers and municipalities are reusing material to capture carbon and generate clean energy.
- Lifecycle impact visualization tools explore topics including biodiversity, energy innovation, farm fresh produce, waste-to-energy, bioproducts, and local product footprints.
- Collaborating community participants include local government, educational institutions, development authorities, regional commissions, religious organizations, local industry groups, and local non-profits.
- Teaching tools and student research frameworks allow users to use and create applications to learn about the connections between industries, households, and the environment in local communities.
- Economist Mariana Mazzucato discusses entrepreneurial ecosystems in relation to the innovation economy.
