# Georgia Electric Vehicle (EV) Industry

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-06-05

**Source:** [If Congress Cuts EV Credits, Could Georgia Pick Up the Slack?](https://www.govtech.com/policy/if-congress-cuts-ev-credits-could-georgia-pick-up-the-slack)  ·  `page_000009`

## Facts

- Georgia lawmakers revoked a $5,000 tax credit for no-emission vehicles in 2015.
- In 2015, Georgia legislators imposed an over $200 annual registration fee for EV owners.
- In 2023, Republican state lawmakers implemented a tax on public EV charging stations expected to take effect in 2027.
- Congressional Republicans passed President Donald Trump’s “big, beautiful” taxation and budget bill in May that would end federal subsidies bolstering Georgia’s EV manufacturing industry.
- If the Senate passes the bill and Trump signs it, the measure might slow EV sales while making owning a battery-powered vehicle more costly.
- Republican-controlled Georgia General Assembly legislators are not eager to replace proposed federal rollbacks with state incentives.
- State Sen. Steve Gooch (R-Dahlonega) stated that consumers should buy the vehicle of their choice without government subsidies.
- State Sen. Frank Ginn, vice chairman of the Senate Economic Development and Tourism Committee, said the Legislature's plans depend on the state’s needs at the time but does not believe ending federal incentives will slow EV manufacturing production in Georgia.
- Industry observers disagree with Ginn, saying removal of Biden-era incentives could hurt manufacturing projects across the state.
- Ginn noted tax credits make sense to jump-start a new industry but companies must eventually navigate the market independently.
- Supporters of EV subsidies fear that without new state incentive programs, Georgia could lose jobs and fall behind other states as an EV manufacturing hub.
- State Sen. Emanuel Jones (D-Decatur), a car dealer, said it appears government is stepping away when it should be pushing the industry forward.
- The reconciliation bill targets provisions from the Inflation Reduction Act signed by President Joe Biden in 2022.
- Proposed rollbacks include sunsetting a $7,500 tax break for new U.S.-manufactured electric vehicles by the end of 2026.
- The bill phases out production subsidies for manufacturers of batteries and other clean energy inputs.
- The measure ends a tax credit for EV charging stations.
- The bill imposes an annual $250 fee to compensate for taxes not paid by EV owners at the gas pump.
- Georgia’s rise as an EV manufacturing hub began before the late 2022 passage of the Inflation Reduction Act.
- Rivian announced plans in 2021 to build a factory 40 miles east of Atlanta.
- Hyundai announced plans for its “Metaplant” outside of Savannah around the same time as Rivian's announcement.
- Federal incentives have aided Georgia’s plan to become a clean technology hub, with the industry employing 82,000 Georgians as of 2024.
- GOP state lawmakers and Gov. Brian Kemp have not expressed enthusiasm for government tax breaks despite federal subsidies accelerating EV and clean tech in Georgia.
- Gov. Kemp aimed to make Georgia a leader in producing electric vehicles and parts but criticized the IRA as a “reckless tax and spending spree.”
- Kemp stated the IRA disproportionately favors particular manufacturers by “picking winners and losers.”
- If passed, Georgians would pay more when purchasing some new EVs.
- Owners of battery-powered vehicles in Georgia would pay more than $450 annually between state and federal fees if the tax bill passes.
- Longtime EV advocate Don Francis, who works at an e-mobility nonprofit, is conflicted about the congressional bill.
- Francis acknowledged the EV tax credit should end eventually but argued the proposed annual fee is not comparable to gas pump taxes paid over a year.
- Francis said the fees make consumers want to buy gas guzzlers to save money and are shortsighted as more people shift away from gas-powered vehicles.
- The more than 1,000-page bill could change significantly in the Senate where Republicans have limited margin for lost votes.
- Congressional Republicans hope to pass the legislation by July 4.
