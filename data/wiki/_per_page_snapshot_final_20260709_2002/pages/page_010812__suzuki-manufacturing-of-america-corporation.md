# Suzuki Manufacturing of America Corporation

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Explore the ATV manufacturing process with an exclusive factory tour.](https://mytour.vn/en/blog/lifestyle/explore-the-atv-manufacturing-process-with-an-exclusive-factory-tour-mytour.html)  ·  `page_010812`

## Facts

- The Suzuki Manufacturing of America Corporation operates an ATV factory in Rome, Georgia.
- An ATV's frame starts as straight pipes that hydraulic presses bend into correct shapes while robotic plasma cutters trim them to appropriate lengths.
- Each frame piece takes about six hours to become part of the final ATV.
- Pipes of various shapes and sizes form the left, right, top, and bottom of the ATV frame, while smaller components known as 'kiddie parts' combine to create the 'mother part'.
- Human welders are still required for tasks in extremely tight spaces that robot arms cannot reach.
- Welders use specialized masks with light-sensitive windows that automatically darken when welding begins to shield eyes from sparks and intense light.
- After frames are completely assembled and welded, an automated system cleans, paints, and bakes them.
- Workers pre-assemble components by attaching handlebars and other necessary parts before sending them to the assembly line.
- The Rome facility manufactures red and black fenders using an injection-molding machine.
- The injection-molding machine draws small resin granules through a hose, melts them, and molds them into the required shape.
- The injection-molding machine applies 3,300 tons of pressure to form the molten resin.
- Some black fenders receive a camouflage coating at a different location.
- After leaving the conveyor belt, workers trim fenders and apply a plastic coating to protect them as they cure.
- Stickers and logos are applied to fenders at another station before they are sent to the assembly line.
- Different fender designs require specific molds that are incredibly heavy.
- Moving the heavy fender molds requires a 25-ton crane.
- Workers use pneumatic and battery-powered tools on the assembly line to install the engine, shock absorbers, fenders, and tires.
- During the visit, an ATV took 102 seconds to pass through each station, resulting in one ATV completed every 102 seconds.
- As of July 2006, the time per station was reduced to 89 seconds.
- The Rome plant produces around 310 ATVs daily, or roughly 80,000 per year.
- The final two assembly line stations are for inspection and packaging.
- A seesaw-like mechanism lifts each ATV off the assembly pedestal and sets it on its wheels.
- Workers fill the gas tank and test the ATV's performance on a drum tester where drums rotate beneath the wheels to allow high speeds without moving forward.
- Employees drain the gasoline after the performance test.
- The factory uses between 200 and 300 gallons of gasoline each week for these tests.
- Each completed ATV is placed on a wooden pallet, covered with a box, and secured with industrial staple guns before a forklift transports it directly to a shipping truck.
- The Rome plant does not store completed vehicles.
- Over 250 people work at the Rome factory.
- The entire manufacturing process, including engine and part production at other facilities, involves more than 300 individuals.
- When adding staff who design ATVs, plan manufacturing processes, and provide support, it takes over 350 people to produce a single ATV.
- Alan Horne and the team at Suzuki Manufacturing of America Corporation provided support for the article.
