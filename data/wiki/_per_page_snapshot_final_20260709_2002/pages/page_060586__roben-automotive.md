## Facts

- Autonomous vehicles are expected to hit the road by 2025.
- Autonomous vehicles will function more as public transport environments rather than privately owned machines.
- The business model for autonomous vehicles will shift significantly toward predictive maintenance compared to traditional cars and trucks.
- OEMs may redefine themselves as manufacturers of the vehicle environment, with separate entities owning the operational environment.
- Tier-1, tier-2, and tier-3 suppliers will need to deliver more data for prognostics but will maintain their existing positions in the supply chain relative to the vehicle maker.
- A major challenge for predictive maintenance is processing the massive volume of generated data without enough engineers to review it manually.
- Smart data processing environments are required to filter out 90% of raw data and isolate component health metrics for targeted maintenance triggers.
- Maintenance will be triggered only when a component risks breaking down, performance drops, or fuel efficiency decreases.
- ISO and SAE have stepped up standardization efforts for connectivity and system architecture, moving away from isolated regional initiatives toward international committee collaboration.
- The industry is on the right track for standardization after years of misunderstandings between OEMs and suppliers.
- OBD served as the first diagnostic system in vehicles, establishing foundational methodologies for handling large-scale Type I and Type II errors (false PASS and false FAIL results).
- Modern diagnostic systems are adopting lessons learned from OBD regarding statistical application and data communication to end users.
- Safety diagnostics and security diagnosis face similar underlying problems and rely on methods comparable to prognostic predictive environments.
