# Dawson County, Georgia

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2006-02-01

**Source:** [Dawsonville/Dawson County: Racing Toward Prosperity - Georgia Trend Magazine](https://www.georgiatrend.com/2006/02/01/dawsonville-dawson-county-racing-toward-prosperity/)  ·  `page_057352`

## Facts

- Dawson County population has more than doubled since 1990, reaching about 20,000.
- The county has appeared on the list of Georgia’s fastest growing counties for the past decade or so.
- Through September 2005, Dawson County issued 508 building permits for houses compared to 428 in the same period in 2004.
- Residential development is far outpacing commercial development, supporting a skewed 80-20 residential to commercial tax base.
- The county finished the first step in its long-range land use plan, which includes higher growth densities concentrated mainly along the Georgia 400 corridor with greenbelt buffers.
- County officials are considering impact fees on new development to alleviate costly growing pains and soften the burden on residents.
- Mike Berg, chairman of the county commission and retired Georgia Power executive, discussed creating a 400-corridor tax allocation district (TAD) to afford infrastructure without taxing citizens who do not live in that area.
- Dawson County voters have approved the Special Purpose Local Option Sales Tax (SPLOST) four times, with the most recent approval for $25.5 million.
- Most SPLOST revenue is generated at the North Georgia Premium Outlets, which had more than 5 million visitors last year and historically provided almost 90 percent of the county’s sales tax.
- Tom French, general manager of North Georgia Premium Outlets, reported double-digit increases in traffic for the year.
- A Wal-Mart Supercenter is planned at a development across the street from the outlets near a Home Depot.
- Voters approved a $29 million ELOST to build two new schools for a system that adds 200 students a year on average.
- Lanier Technical College opened a satellite campus in downtown Dawsonville in 2005, located in an old middle school.
- Southern Catholic College opened its doors in 2005 with 72 freshmen on a 100-acre parcel adjoining Golf Creek Golf Club, after founders purchased and later sold a 300-acre site due to a sour economy and fundraising difficulties.
- Southern Catholic College has spent about $16 million getting to its current campus location.
- Jerry Ashcroft, president of Southern Catholic College and former president of East Georgia College, stated students were searching for an institution where they could get a good education within a spiritual environment.
- Dawson County Development Authority chairman Lake Gibson stated the authority is looking to hire a full-time director of economic development within the next few months and find a permanent home for the authority.
- The authority aims to bring more doctors and medical facilities to the area because there is a real shortage.
- Impulse Manufacturing, a sheet metal fabrication company, planned a 50,000-square-foot, $2 million addition to its Dawsonville building with $6 million in additional equipment, expanding its employee base from 120 to more than 150.
- Gold Creek Foods, part of Agora Foods founded by Mark Sosebee, occupies a renovated 50,000-square-foot former textile mill and ships about a million pounds of food to grocery stores and schools every week.
- Agora Foods currently employs 200 people, with plans to employ over 1,000 within the next five years, including new or expanded facilities and freezers.
- Ernie Elliott, owner and operator of Ernie Elliott, Inc. in Dawsonville, would like to see Dawson County position itself for growth in the motorsports, automobile, or aerospace sectors but noted there is not a lot of interest or understanding of the economic impact from courting this type of industry.
- The Georgia Mountains Regional Development Center assisted the city with zoning guidelines that will foster the growth of a mini-city with townhomes, shops, and greenspace.
- A local partnership called Phoenix One purchased the former ThunderRoad USA museum property for $5.5 million and possesses 30-plus contiguous acres planned to be transformed into a development.
- The Georgia Racing Hall of Fame will continue to be housed in the former ThunderRoad building, which also serves as Dawsonville City Hall on a parcel of more than 35 acres.
