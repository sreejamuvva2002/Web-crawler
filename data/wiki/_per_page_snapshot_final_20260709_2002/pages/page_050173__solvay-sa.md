# Solvay SA

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Solvay wants US protection for Georgia EV plastics materials plant > Notice | 우진플라임](https://woojinplaimm.com/bbs/board.php?bo_table=notice_en&wr_id=144)  ·  `page_050173`

## Facts

- Solvay SA is seeking U.S. government protection and import limits for its new polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) plastics materials plant in Georgia.
- The U.S. International Trade Commission (USITC) announced on Feb. 14 that it is investigating a patent infringement complaint brought by Solvay against several Chinese competitors for allegedly exporting PVDF resin to the U.S. in violation of Solvay patents.
- Several Chinese firms, including Sinochem Lantian Co. Ltd., have disputed Solvay's patent infringement allegations.
- Solvay and its spinoff company Syensqo argued in a petition to the U.S. ITC that import restrictions are needed to protect a PVDF thermoplastic resin plant being built in Augusta, Ga., which is using a grant of nearly $180 million from the U.S. Department of Energy.
- Solvay stated that with the DOE's help, Syensqo has invested substantial resources for the domestic production and distribution of its patent-protected, battery-grade PVDF called Solef.
- Solvay noted these significant investments in Solef's development and the domestic industry were made with the understanding that the patent protecting the technology would prevent competitors from unfairly importing infringing products.
- Solvay said the planned PVDF resin plant in Alpharetta is part of $5 billion in U.S. government grants to develop the battery supply chain and electrical grid under the U.S. Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, signed by President Joe Biden in 2021.
- Solvay stated that protecting the complainant's investment in the Georgia plant will promote the substantial domestic production planned by the complainants and their federal and local partners.
- The USITC investigation is a Section 337 review examining whether the Chinese firms violated a Solvay patent with their materials and seeks to limit imports of the allegedly infringing material in the U.S.
- Besides Sinochem Lantian in Hangzhou, the U.S. ITC complaint lists Inner Mongolia 3F Wanhao Fluorochemical Industry Co. Ltd. in Fengzhen; Zhejiang Juhua Co. Ltd. in Quzhou; Zhejiang Fluorine Chemical New Material Co. Ltd. in Shaoxing; and China Hubei Fluorine New Materials Co. Ltd. in Qianjiang.
- The U.S. ITC accepted Solvay's complaint but stated it has not made any rulings on the merits of the case.
- Sinochem Lantian argued that Solvay was venue shopping its intellectual property complaint after related patent infringement claims by Solvay and its business units in China were not going well.
- Sinochem wrote in a U.S. ITC filing that the Chinese litigations are struggling and ending badly for the complainants.
- Sinochem noted that in recognition of the struggling Chinese cases, complainants have started shopping their claims to the ITC with patent infringement claims on a related patent having the same claim limitations as the one unsuccessfully litigated in China.
- Solvay stated that the patents at issue in the U.S. and China are different.
- Sinochem argued that Solvay is trying to protect a non-existent domestic U.S. industry, since the Georgia plant is not yet open and may not finish before 2028.
- Zhejiang Juhua told the trade commission that restricting PVDF imports would harm U.S. consumers by exacerbating shortages and raising prices.
- Juhua stated there had been a persistent shortage of PVDF in the U.S. in recent years, which is projected to continue in the future.
- Juhua warned that the requested remedial orders would distort prices and competition, negatively impacting U.S. consumers.
- Juhua stated that limits on PVDF resin would make other U.S. industries less competitive, including electric car manufacturing.
- Juhua noted that higher battery prices would drive up EV costs, further deceasing the competitiveness of EVs manufactured in the U.S.
- Juhua added that a continued shortage or persistently high cost of PVDF would drive up costs for medical devices and water purification plants, negatively impacting U.S. consumers.
- Solvay countered that PVDF shortages are not a real concern because there are other large makers of PVDF in the U.S. and globally who can supply materials to EV manufacturers and other companies.
- Solvay warned that if the U.S. government does not act, its Chinese rivals could gain an unfair advantage in the U.S. market.
- Solvay stated it is expected that the Chinese firms, if allowed to continue their infringement, could gain an unfair competitive advantage in the domestic market in the near term.
- The original complaint was brought to the U.S. ITC by Solvay Specialty Polymers, USA LLC in Alpharetta, Syensqo SA of Brussels, and Solvay Specialty Polymers Italy SpA.
- Executives held a groundbreaking for the Georgia plant making PVDF resins in 2024.
