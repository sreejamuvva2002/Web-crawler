# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-04

**Source:** [2025 Georgia Hyundai plant immigration raid - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Georgia_Hyundai_plant_immigration_raid)  ·  `page_000306`

## Facts

- On September 4, 2025, American law enforcement conducted an immigration raid at the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America, an electric vehicle (EV) production site in Ellabell, Bryan County, Georgia, detaining approximately 475 workers.
- The raid was described as the largest immigration enforcement operation carried out at a single location by the United States Department of Homeland Security.
- The raid led to a diplomatic dispute between the United States and South Korea, with over 300 Koreans detained, and increased concerns about foreign companies investing in the United States.
- In 2023, Hyundai Motor and LG Energy Solution announced a joint venture in Georgia to produce EV battery cells as a $4.3 billion investment, with both companies holding a 50% stake.
- The plant cost $7.6 billion and is located on a 3,000-acre site that holds a Hyundai electric vehicle manufacturing site and an EV battery joint venture plant, together employing about 1,200 people at the time of the raid.
- Georgia state leaders had promised the facility would bring in 8,500 jobs and transform the rural area's economy.
- In July 2025, South Korea agreed to purchase $100 billion in US energy and make a $350 billion investment in the US in return for the lowering of tariff rates by the US.
- The raid involved hundreds of federal and state officers, including members of ICE, Georgia State Patrol, the FBI, DEA, ATF, and others, targeting the battery plant which was still under construction.
- Homeland Security Investigations special agent Steven Schrank indicated the raid culminated a months-long investigation that included a search warrant filed on September 2 identifying four people to be searched.
- During the raid, officials alleged several individuals attempted to flee into a sewage pond on the property, with one person allegedly attempting to swim under an arrest boat and flip it.
- An anonymous eyewitness reported company bosses ordered all operations shut down via a deluge of phone calls during the raid.
- The majority of those arrested were identified as South Korean by Schrank, though no further nationality breakdown was provided; twenty-three Mexican nationals were also detained.
- On September 9, 2025, Japanese Foreign Minister Takeshi Iwaya reported the detention of three Japanese nationals during the raid.
- On September 10, 2025, it was reported that 10 Chinese nationals and one Indonesian national were detained.
- Republican candidate for Georgia's 12th congressional district, Tori Branum, announced she played a part in causing the raid by investigating rumors of safety concerns and undocumented immigrants using a site contact, recording conversations, and submitting evidence through the ICE website.
- At least some of the detained workers were in the US on visa waivers intended for tourists and business trips up to 90 days.
- A leaked document reported on September 10 showed one detained worker had a valid B1/B2 working visa and had not violated it, despite initial DHS statements claiming unauthorized work; a voluntary departure option was ordered by the Atlanta Field Office Director.
- On September 12, more than 300 Korean workers returned to South Korea on a chartered Korean Air jet after President Trump delayed their departure by a day, during which only one worker chose to stay.
- On September 6, dozens of protesters gathered outside the Hyundai megasite protesting against government overreach.
- At least 22 other factory sites in America involving other Korean business groups were nearly halted following the raid.
- Hyundai CEO José Muñoz stated the raid would delay the opening of the plant by a minimum of 2–3 months.
- About two weeks after the raid, Hyundai announced the site was scheduled to receive an about $2.7 billion investment to expand production rates and create more jobs.
- On September 15, South Korea announced an investigation into the raid for potential human rights violations and reviewed if demands were properly addressed, triggered by workers reporting they were not read their rights and mocked by ICE agents about North Korea and "rocket man".
- Savannah Economic Development Authority President and CEO Trip Tollison told reporters on September 17 that discussions were underway to bring back detained South Korean nationals, stating it was imperative they return as they were the only ones who could install and teach future employees how to use factory technology.
- A Hyundai spokesperson stated they did not believe anyone arrested was a direct employee of Hyundai Motor Company and emphasized full commitment to complying with all laws and regulations.
- On September 7, 2025, Donald Trump posted on Truth Social calling on foreign companies investing in the US to respect immigration laws, encouraging them to legally bring technical talent while hiring and training American workers.
- US Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem told reporters she did not think the mass detention of South Korean nationals would deter investment in the US, as it demonstrated policy certainty under the Trump administration.
- On September 15, after backlash, Trump posted on Truth Social stressing foreign companies are welcome to bring skilled employees to train the domestic workforce but are expected to return home, adding he did not want to frighten off or disincentivize investment into America.
- The Wall Street Journal's editorial board issued an opinion on September 12 criticizing the Trump administration, writing that raids like the one in Georgia are a deterrent to the foreign investment Donald Trump says he wants.
- In a September 11, 2025 news conference, South Korean President Lee Jae Myung stated the raid could significantly impact future direct investment in the US and make South Korean companies very hesitant about investing there, calling the raid and its images bewildering.
- The South Korean government expressed concern and regret over the detention of its citizens, leading to broader concerns among South Korean politicians about foreign investment in the United States.
- South Korea's labour minister Kim Younghoon told the Financial Times that the treatment of detained workers felt worse than prisoners of war, causing shock among many.
- The conservative South Korean daily Chosun Ilbo urged officials to convey the seriousness of the situation to Washington, questioning what the US alliance really means given the development.
- Trade minister Yeo Han-koo told reporters after arriving in Washington, D.C. to finalize a bilateral tariff deal that many US officials felt the raid was excessive.
