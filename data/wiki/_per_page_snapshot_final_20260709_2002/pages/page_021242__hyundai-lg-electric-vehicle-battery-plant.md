# Hyundai-LG Electric Vehicle Battery Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [What we know about the South Korean workers arrested in the Hyundai raid in Georgia](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/south-korea-nationals-return-delayed-immigration-raid-hyundai-rcna230149)  ·  `page_021242`

## Facts

- More than 300 South Koreans — 307 men and 10 women — were detained in the Sept. 4 raid on a Hyundai plant in Georgia.
- One detainee decided to remain in the U.S. rather than agree to “voluntary departure.”
- The 316 others will be on the flight to Seoul, along with 14 employees of South Korean companies from other Asian countries: 10 from China, three from Japan and one from Indonesia.
- Workers were released from the Folkston ICE Processing Center in rural Georgia at 2:18 a.m. ET.
- They were being driven several hours to Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport where a plane sent by the South Korean government is waiting for them.
- The Korean Air plane is scheduled to leave Atlanta at noon ET and arrive in Seoul on Friday around 4 p.m. local time (3 a.m. ET).
- South Korean officials originally hoped the plane would leave Atlanta as early as Wednesday afternoon local time, shortly after it arrived from Seoul.
- The foreign ministry said its departure was likely to be delayed “due to circumstances on the U.S. side,” and an airport spokesperson later said the charter flight had been canceled.
- At a meeting with Secretary of State Marco Rubio in Washington on Wednesday, South Korean Foreign Minister Cho Hyun learned that Trump had ordered the suspension of the repatriation process.
- The suspension was to discuss whether detained nationals — all skilled workers helping set up an electric vehicle battery plant — should remain in the U.S. to continue their work rather than be sent home.
- Cho told Rubio that it would be better for them to return home first and then re-enter the U.S. “since these individuals were already in shock and are exhausted,” a South Korean government official said.
- The U.S. agreed to repatriate the South Koreans and Trump instructed they be transported to the airport without physical restraints such as handcuffs, departing from standard U.S. practice.
- Images of South Koreans being shackled at the wrists and ankles have caused outrage in South Korea, a key U.S. ally that has pledged hundreds of billions in U.S. investment as part of tariff negotiations.
- The raid came weeks after South Korean President Lee Jae Myung reinforced that commitment at a meeting with Trump at the White House.
- A South Korean government official stated that Trump’s consideration stems from deep bonds formed between leaders at the successful South Korea-U.S. summit and their consistent position on the detention issue.
- Lee said all but one of the 317 detained South Koreans had elected to fly home.
- Lee noted at a news conference in Seoul marking his 100th day in office that each person was allowed to choose whether to go or stay.
- The raid has raised concerns about potential impact on U.S.-South Korea relations, though Lee said his government had “not considered it to that depth yet.”
- Lee warned the incident could make South Korean companies more hesitant to invest in the U.S., where they have long sent employees temporarily for training or assistance where U.S. expertise is lacking.
- Lee stated that if restrictions continue, companies will worry about proceeding with setting up factories in the U.S. due to endless difficulties.
- The South Korean nationals were among the 475 people federal and immigration agents arrested in the raid on a construction site in the town of Ellabell.
- Hyundai and LG Energy Solution are jointly building an electric vehicle battery plant at the construction site.
- U.S. officials described the raid as the largest single-site enforcement operation in the history of Homeland Security Investigations, a division of Immigration and Customs Enforcement.
- No criminal charges have been filed against the detainees.
- Of the detained South Koreans, 47 were employed by LG Energy Solution and the rest were hired by subcontractors.
- Hyundai, South Korea’s largest automaker, said none of its direct employees were detained.
- Most detained South Koreans were engineers or worked in after-sales services and installation, according to Atlanta-based immigration attorney Charles Kuck, who is representing at least seven of them.
- The workers were in the U.S. under various visa programs that foreign nationals have long used to do business amid U.S. bureaucratic delays and murky regulations.
- Sarah Park, president of the Korean American Coalition, said detained workers should not be blamed for challenges companies face securing proper U.S. visas for key facility staff.
- James S. Kim, chairman of the American Chamber of Commerce in Korea, urged Americans to be sympathetic and work with South Korea to mitigate the incident and move forward.
- Federal authorities executed a judicial search warrant at the Hyundai-LG plant regarding a criminal investigation into allegations of unlawful employment practices.
- The warrant authorized agents to seize employment records, immigration documents, and ownership/management records related to the construction site.
- Authorities were looking for four specific individuals, but the reasons for their interest remain under seal.
- On Sunday, U.S. and South Korean government officials reached a deal to release workers from immigration custody and return them home.
- Consulate officials met with detainees at the Folkston ICE Processing Center over the weekend, according to Meredyth Yoon, litigation director at Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta.
- Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta has been assisting detainees and their families seeking legal help.
- As part of the deal, most South Korean nationals agreed to “voluntary departure,” avoiding formal deportation orders.
- About 175 remaining detainees from Guatemala, Colombia, Chile, Mexico, Ecuador and Venezuela remain in immigration detention.
