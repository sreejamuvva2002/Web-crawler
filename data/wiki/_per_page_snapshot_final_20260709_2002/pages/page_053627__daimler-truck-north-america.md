# Daimler Truck North America

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Daimler Truck North America Pursues Efficiency, Zero Emissions - Oregon Business & Industry](https://oregonbusinessindustry.com/daimler-truck-north-america-pursues-efficiency-zero-emissions/)  ·  `page_053627`

## Facts

- Daimler Truck North America has produced big rigs in Portland since the 1940s and is garnering attention for work on trucks that run on alternative fuels and operate autonomously.
- In the 1930s, Leland James, president of Consolidated Freightways, asked manufacturers to use aluminum rather than steel to make trucks lighter for mountainous Pacific Northwest routes; when they refused, he established the company that became Freightliner.
- The Freightliner manufacturing facility in Portland opened in 1947.
- In 1981, Freightliner was bought by Daimler-Benz AG, followed by acquisitions of Thomas Built Buses and Western Star Trucks.
- By 1992, Freightliner had become the nation’s leading producer of heavy-duty trucks.
- In 2004, Freightliner opened a full-scale wind tunnel in Portland, the first in North America designed to test full-size commercial vehicles.
- In 2008, Freightliner assumed the name Daimler Truck North America, which was renamed again in 2021 following a spin-off from German industrial conglomerate Daimler AG.
- Daimler Truck North America continues to produce more medium- and heavy-duty vehicles in North America than any other company, with headquarters, testing, and key manufacturing functions remaining in Oregon.
- Most of the company’s Oregon activity happens on Portland’s Swan Island, including a LEED Platinum-certified headquarters on the banks of the Willamette River.
- The Western Star facility is unique in that it also manufactures Daimler Truck’s electric vehicles alongside its specialized heavy-haul and off-highway trucks.
- Production of the heavy-duty Freightliner eCascadia began last year.
- Production of the company’s electric box truck, the eM2, will begin by the end of this year, according to spokesman Fred Ligouri.
- Demand for battery-powered big rigs is currently constrained by limited recharging infrastructure.
- Battery-powered trucks are well-suited to specific applications such as local pickup and delivery as well as drayage, which involves the delivery of container trucks from ports to nearby repackaging centers.
- Battery power works well for school buses because they have a very defined duty cycle that includes a mid-day pause perfect for recharging.
- About 400 electric buses manufactured by Daimler Truck’s South Carolina-based Thomas Built Bus line are now on the road.
- Daimler Truck opened “Electric Island” in 2021, a charging station for battery-powered commercial vehicles located across the street from its headquarters.
- The Electric Island facility operates as a partnership with Portland General Electric and is open to the public.
- All of the company’s reliability and durability testing takes place at its High Desert Proving Grounds, an 87-acre facility located about 120 miles southeast of headquarters in Madras.
- The facility features a 3.5-mile test track that allows the company to simulate a vehicle’s full life cycle in only six months.
- Daimler Truck uses the proving grounds to test new technology away from busy roads and has tested autonomous trucks there, which the company hopes to produce at scale by the end of the decade.
- Daimler Truck acquired Virginia-based Torc Robotics in 2019 to advance its autonomous technology capabilities.
- The company partnered with Alphabet subsidiary Waymo, a leading developer of automated driver technology, in 2020.
- The company employs about 3,000 people in Oregon and southwest Washington.
- Design and development work orchestrated from and tested in Oregon continues the pursuit of efficiency that began back in the 1940s.
- The company’s Cascadia on-highway truck has become 35% more efficient since its introduction in 2007.
- Daimler Truck aims to produce only vehicles that are CO2-neutral in driving operation for its core markets by 2039.
