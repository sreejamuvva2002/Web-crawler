# Bojangles

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** 2026-05-23

**Source:** [EV Charging Meets Chicken Rippers At Bojangles](https://cleantechnica.com/2026/05/23/ev-charging-chicken-rippers-bo-jangles/)  ·  `page_000462`

## Facts

- On May 20, Bojangles debuted its first ever on-site EV charging station.
- The initial charging station is located at a single store in Savannah, Georgia.
- Bojangles positions itself as an ultra-enthusiastic booster of the "charge-and-dine" model among the nation's leading quick service restaurant chains.
- Cracker Barrel, Waffle House, and almost every other leading QSR chain have already launched new EV charging initiatives.
- In a press statement, Bojangles explained that the new charging hub signals its evolution beyond traditional quick-service dining and positions the brand as a forward-thinking player at the intersection of mobility and hospitality.
- The company declared it is stepping forward as a category leader by transforming charging time into an opportunity for comfort, convenience, and craveable Southern flavor.
- Bojangles Chief Information Officer Richard Del Valle stated that with EV charging, time becomes an asset and they are turning the stop into a chance to relax, refuel, and enjoy a true Bojangles experience.
- Del Valle emphasized that the initiative is about more than charging vehicles and is redefining the stop along the way.
- Bojangles declared that the war on EVs is over by integrating EV infrastructure into its footprint.
- The company asserted it is supporting broader adoption of electric vehicles and reshaping expectations for what a quick-service restaurant can be.
- Bojangles elaborated that as consumers prioritize efficiency and sustainability, the brand is leaning into hospitality and quality to meet those needs in new ways.
- The Southeastern US has proven itself to be EV-friendly territory despite its red-state political environment.
- EV sales took a steep downward turn in the Southeast and elsewhere after President Donald Trump and his Republican allies in Congress terminated the $7,500 EV tax credit last September.
- The Southeast Alliance for Clean Energy (SACE) anticipated that EV sales in the region would recover despite the sales drop.
- SACE noted in its March 2026 update that while sales fell significantly in Q4, data from December already showed early signs of a shift upward toward higher sales figures.
- Car-friendly businesses continue to bank on the ability of on-site charging to attract more traffic to their stores.
- New-bought EVs will not vanish simply due to a change in federal tax policy, and the widespread availability of used EVs could factor into continued demand for convenient, public EV charging stations.
- EV-only startups Slate Auto and Rivian are moving forward on plans for launching new EVs into the market later this year into 2027.
- Last month, Rivian nailed down a $4.5 billion loan guarantee from the Trump administration earmarked for constructing its new factory in Georgia.
- Bojangles chose a store in Savannah, Georgia to highlight its new EV charging campaign rather than tapping a store in its home town of Charlotte, North Carolina.
- In the May 20 announcement, Bojangles affirmed that the Savannah charging station is just the first of a series.
- The company currently counts 870 owned and franchised sites on its roster covering 23 states, including 351 stores in North Carolina.
- Bojangles' EV charging management partner is XLR8 America, a California-based startup.
- In June of 2025, XLR8 announced a new branch of operations consisting of an EV charging infrastructure management service for commercial real estate owners, portfolio managers, municipalities, and others.
- XLR8 explained that this service directly addresses the significant operational burdens, unexpected costs, and often low returns experienced by owners managing these assets in-house.
- XLR8 currently counts Lincoln Electric, Autel Energy, Atom Power, DG Matrix, EVoke Systems, and Kennedy Power Systems on its partnership roster.
- XLR8 CEO Frank O’Connor indicated that the initial Bojangles installation in Savannah represents the tip of the EV charging iceberg.
- O’Connor enthused that when a driver pulls in for a Bo-Berry Biscuit and their battery tops off while they dine, it is the charge-and-dine experience made real.
- He added that together they are building a national network that makes EV charging effortless and every stop genuinely worth making.
- The charging equipment itself comes from the Florida firm Energy and Environmental Design Services (EEDS), which specializes in commercial properties.
- EEDS has its eye on new opportunities to install EV chargers at convenience stores, with a particular focus on independent stores through its new “C-Store United” initiative.
- The C-Store United Program has been developed to provide support to the independent C-Store owner looking to enter the EV charging market while maintaining their branding, customer data, and a flexible ownership stake.
- EEDS notes that it is on a mission to help C-store clients “go on the offensive.”
