## Facts

- At least one Korean worker detained in a huge ICE immigration raid on a Hyundai Motor factory site in Georgia last week was living and working legally in the US with a valid visa.
- ICE officials mandated that the legal worker agree to be removed from the US despite not violating his visa.
- Immigration officials were aware that someone with a valid visa was among those arrested during the raid at the Hyundai factory and taken to ICE detention for removal proceedings.
- The arrested detainees remained in ICE detention on Tuesday before expected deportation flights back to South Korea.
- A leaked internal ICE agent document reports on the detained worker's case, obtained exclusively by the Guardian.
- The detained man arrived in the US in June and was redacted from the document because he could not be reached directly and his legal representation status is unclear.
- Immigration agents from Atlanta determined the man entered with a valid B1/B2 visa and was employed at HL-GA Battery Company LLC as a contractor from the South Korean company SFA.
- Law enforcement database queries confirmed the man had not violated his visa, but the Atlanta Field Office Director mandated he be presented for Voluntary Departure.
- The man accepted voluntary departure despite not violating B1/B2 visa requirements.
- Georgia-based immigration attorney Charles Kuck, representing several arrested individuals, called detaining a valid visa holder illegal and described the situation as an 'actual crime' committed by the government.
- Kuck called the detention 'outrageous.'
- The leaked document contradicts ICE claims that all 475 people arrested during the raid were working illegally or violating their visas.
- Attorneys representing detainees claimed immigrants with valid working status were swept up alongside those allegedly working unlawfully and placed in removal proceedings.
- An anonymous agency official backed up the claim that legal immigrants were swept up, calling the arrest 'illegal.'
- The government maintains the detainee acted unlawfully by admitting to unauthorized work on a B1/B2 visa.
- It remains unclear whether other people with valid visas were detained or how many were actually alleged to be working illegally at the factory.
- Approximately 300 arrested people could be put on flights back to South Korea as early as Wednesday, while non-Koreans arrested are expected to remain in ICE detention.
- Last Thursday, ICE led a raid at the Hyundai battery factory under construction in Ellabell, near Savannah, Georgia, arresting nearly 500 people, the majority from South Korea.
- The arrested workers were part of a $12.6bn investment program in Georgia by Hyundai.
- Construction on the plant was halted due to the raid.
- The plant is designed to supply batteries for electric vehicles.
- A DHS spokesperson stated the individual admitted to unauthorized work and accepted voluntary departure, contradicting the leaked document's account.
- DHS reiterated its statement when pressed for clarification, while ICE did not respond to requests for comment about legal workers being arrested.
- The man entered the US with a valid B1/B2 visa, which allows for some business-related activities and tourism, and worked at the factory as a contractor for SFA.
- Kuck noted that 'voluntarily' in immigration means leaving under ICE custody with consequences including loss of visa and possible inability to return to the US.
- After the raid, additional ICE officers and agents were sent to the detention facility to process the sheer number of people arrested.
- Officials say the arrested people violated immigration laws, but many with valid legal status were offered voluntary departure, with no clear legal mechanism to remove those who refuse if they are not in violation.
- There is no suggestion that the Korean man in question has a criminal record in the US.

- Samantha Hamilton, a litigation attorney with Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta, stated the workers were put in incredibly vulnerable positions simply by taking work opportunities on an ambitious international project.
