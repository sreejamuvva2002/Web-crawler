# HL-GA Battery Company, LLC

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-05-26

**Source:** [ICE warrant for Hyundai megasite sought four "target persons"](https://www.savannahnow.com/story/news/2025/09/10/ice-warrant-for-hyundai-megasitein-ellabell-georgia-sought-four-target-persons/86043831007/)  ·  `page_001129`

## Facts

- Construction at the HL-GA Battery Company joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution has been paused.
- Homeland Security Investigations targeting of four individuals led to the detainment of about 475 people on Thursday, Sept. 4.
- On Sept. 5, a U.S. Department of Justice press release referred to the HSI and ICE raid at HL-GA Battery Company, LLC at 9730 US Highway 280 in Ellabell, Ga, as Operation Low Voltage.
- The warrant for the Sept. 4 raid named Andreina Fuentes-Tovar, Kevin Zavaleta-Ramirez, David Zavaleta-Ramirez and Julio Gonzalez Alvarado as "Target Persons."
- Approximately 400 officers across multiple federal and Georgia agencies assisted with the operation, according to ICE public affairs officer Lindsay Williams.
- The battery plant was first announced on Friday, May 26, 2023, as part of a state economic agreement with Hyundai to build an EV manufacturing plant and affiliate facilities in Bryan County.
- Production of EV batteries was slated to begin later this year once construction had been completed on what eventually came to be known as HL-GA Battery Company.
- LG was responsible for the governance of the HL-GA facility, including during its construction, until the past week, according to HMGMA Communication and Public Relations Manager Bianca Johnson.
- LG officials announced they are cooperating fully with authorities and have paused construction on the site.
- The warrant identifies the HL-GA plant as a 35-acre site within the larger Hyundai megasite campus in Ellabell, Georgia.
- The Hyundai megasite also houses or will house five Hyundai affiliates: Hyundai Mobis, Hyundai Glovis, Hyundai Transys, Hyundai Steel, and Hyundai Material.
- HMGMA officials have requested that media outlets refer to the location as "the Bryan County megasite," "the Hyundai megasite," or "the Hyundai vehicle manufacturing site in Bryan County" because HMGMA is just one independent operation onsite.
- Hyundai's economic agreement with Georgia requires all companies on the megasite to create and sustain a combined 6,800 to 8,500 permanent jobs by Dec. 31, 2031.
- The agreement mandates that the megasite jobs' salaries must average $58,105.
- The battery plant initially committed to fulfilling 1,600 of the required jobs and later committed to 400 more for a total of 2,000.
- HMGMA officials have stated it aims to hire around 2,600 workers.
- Roughly 300 of the detained individuals were reported to be Korean nationals.
- LG Communications and Government Affairs Manager Phil Lienert confirmed that 47 of the detained individuals are LG employees, with 46 being Korean and one Indonesian.
- About 250 detainees are affiliated with HL-GA's partner companies, most of whom are Korean according to Lienert.
- Individuals detained in the Sept. 4 operation were immediately taken to Folkston Detention Center in South Georgia.
- In June, Charlton County approved a $50 million contract with ICE to expand Folkston Detention Center, which would make it the largest detention center in the country.
- Some detained workers may have relied on the U.S. Visa Waiver Program or B-1 business visas to enter the United States for stays of 90 days or less without a traditional visa.
- Travelers under the Visa Waiver Program must have a valid Electronic System for Travel Authorization (ESTA).
- Atlanta-based immigration lawyer Charles Kuck affirmed that many Korean detainees from the HL-GA plant were in the country on B-1 visas, which may be used for business consulting, contract negotiation, or short-term training.
- As of publication, no evidence confirmed that any detained person used these authorizations to work at or contract for work at the HL-GA plant.
- The warrant for Operation Low Voltage was filed on Tuesday, Sept. 2, and commanded execution on or before Sept. 14.
- U.S. Magistrate Judge Christopher Ray of the Southern District of Georgia signed the warrant.
- Assistant United States Attorney Tania Groover, on behalf of U.S. Attorney Meg Heap, filed a request to unseal the warrant on Thursday, Sept. 4.
- Groover, along with Ryan Bondura, Kelsey Scanlon, and E. Greg Gilluly, Jr., are investigating the case.
- The warrant stated officials were seeking to seize documents related to ownership and management of HL-GA and contractors TK LLC, Autorica LLC, SBY America, Beyond Iron Construction LLC, and Steel Brothers Development Inc.
- Potential documentation sought included time cards, payroll information, photographs of employees, Visas, permanent resident cards, employment authorization cards, passports, and birth certificates.
- Beyond Iron Construction LLC's site address is listed as 1 LG Road, Ellabell, Georgia, 31308, with a mailing address at 3229 Weyhill Trail, Buford, Georgia, 30519.
- Whether the construction pause will impact EV production at HMGMA remains unknown.
- HMGMA had asserted it will produce 500,000 units annually once fully operational.
- Hyundai Motor Company President and CEO José Muñoz stated in March at the metaplant's grand opening that production would include hybrid vehicles at what was initially touted as an all-EV plant.
- The HSI and ICE operation followed other recent challenges to the EV industry, including lower than expected consumer demand over the past year.
- In February, the Trump Administration froze $5 billion for EV charger infrastructure for America's highways, which had been appropriated by Congress under the Biden Administration.
- The funding freeze lasted about six months until June when a federal judge ruled in favor of several states that sued the federal government over withholding the funds.
- In February, Aspen Aerogels Inc. pulled out of its contract to construct a $325-million plant in Statesboro.
- The Trump Administration removed federal tax credits for EVs, meaning after Sept. 30, credits worth $4,000 for used EVs or $7,500 for new ones will no longer be available to consumers.
