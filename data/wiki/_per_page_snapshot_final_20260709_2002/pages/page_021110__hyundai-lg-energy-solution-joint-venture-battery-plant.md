# Hyundai-LG Energy Solution Joint Venture Battery Plant

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-09

**Source:** [South Korea readies plane to bring home workers detained in Hyundai immigration raid](https://www.nbcnews.com/world/asia/south-korea-hyundai-immigration-raid-plane-us-return-rcna230011)  ·  `page_021110`

## Facts

- A chartered plane is set to leave South Korea on Wednesday to retrieve hundreds of South Korean nationals detained in an immigration raid on a Hyundai facility in Georgia.
- About 300 South Korean nationals were among the 475 people detained Thursday in a raid by Homeland Security Investigations, Immigration and Customs Enforcement, and other federal officials on a construction site in Ellabell, Georgia.
- The South Korean companies Hyundai and LG Energy Solution are jointly building a battery plant at the raided construction site.
- The incident has strained relations between the U.S. and South Korea.
- South Korea has pledged hundreds of billions in U.S. investment as part of tariff negotiations.
- Three Japanese nationals were also among those detained, according to the government of Japan.
- U.S. officials detained workers from Guatemala, Colombia, Chile, Mexico, Ecuador, and Venezuela at the site, according to Migrant Equity Southeast.
- South Korean President Lee Jae Myung met with President Trump at the White House just weeks before the raid and expressed a profound sense of responsibility for the safety of South Koreans.
- President Lee stated his government will move quickly through close consultations with the U.S. to advance reasonable institutional improvements so similar cases do not occur again.
- A Korean Air spokesperson confirmed a chartered plane would leave for Atlanta on Wednesday to facilitate the return of detained South Korean nationals.
- Seoul and Washington are conducting detailed conversations to arrange for the detained South Korean nationals to voluntarily return home as soon as possible.
- South Korean Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lee Jae-woong stated the government is making its utmost effort to enable detainees to leave the U.S. by Wednesday local time.
- Because of changes in time zone, the chartered plane will land in the U.S. the same day it leaves South Korea.
- The South Korean foreign ministry said it was continuing to work with U.S. officials to address visa difficulties faced by Korean businesspeople.
- Before the raid, the South Korean foreign ministry held dozens of meetings with members of the U.S. House and Senate, congressional staff, and officials from the U.S. Embassy in Seoul, the federal government, and academia regarding visa issues.
- The South Korean foreign ministry emphasized that visas are essential for the short-term stay of Korean professionals needed for the initial operation of factories and for training local staff during U.S. expansion.
- Sarah Park, president of the Korean American Coalition, stated that the Hyundai-LG plant required highly specialized subcontractors and technicians to move the project forward, but visas were not granted at the scale required.
- Park noted that technicians entered the U.S. on short-term visitor visas to meet urgent needs, highlighting a failure to align immigrant policy with economic promises.
- U.S. officials stated the detainees were working unlawfully and the raid resulted from a monthslong criminal investigation.
- Hyundai, South Korea’s biggest automaker, stated that none of the detained individuals were its direct employees.
- LG Energy Solution reported that 47 of its employees and about 250 workers from partner companies were detained at the site.
- LG Energy Solution instructed its employees to suspend most U.S. business trips following the detention.
- LG Energy Solution stated it is making active efforts to resolve visa issues to ensure smooth overseas assignments for its employees and partner companies.
- LG Energy Solution clarified that its workers were at the factory temporarily to offer training and other support for construction, and that the site has been shut down since their detention.
- Atlanta-based attorney Charles Kuck stated he is representing two detained South Korean nationals who entered the U.S. legally under the Visa Waiver Program, which allows stays of up to 90 days.
- Kuck noted that agreeing to a voluntary departure would suggest a violation of the law and wants ICE to release the detainees with no charges to preserve their future visa eligibility.
- Kuck is also representing three other detained individuals on B-1 visas who work in after-sales and service installation, stating they were doing exactly what the B-1 visa authorizes.
- Kuck criticized Homeland Security Investigations for not doing proper homework before arresting everyone at the site.
