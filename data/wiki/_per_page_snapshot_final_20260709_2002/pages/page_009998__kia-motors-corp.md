# Kia Motors Corp.

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2006-03-13

**Source:** [Georgia Finally Lands the Big One — Site Selection Online](https://siteselection.com/ssinsider/bbdeal/bd060316.htm)  ·  `page_009998`

## Facts

- Kia Motors Corp. and State of Georgia officials in Seoul announced Kia's $1.2-billion investment in its first North American manufacturing complex a few days after DaimlerChrysler's March 8 announcement.
- The plant will be located in West Point, Ga., hard by the Alabama state line.
- The plant will have a capacity of 300,000 vehicles a year when it opens in 2009.
- Euisun Chung, president and CEO of Kia Motors Corp., stated at the signing ceremony that Kia has entered an aggressive growth phase in the U.S. and the decision to build in West Point, Troup County, Georgia, is the latest example of the company's commitment to the marketplace.
- Georgia Gov. Sonny Perdue accompanied Euisun Chung at the Seoul signing ceremony.
- Kia's first priority in selecting a site was to maximize synergy with Hyundai Motor's Alabama plant near Montgomery.
- Other site selection considerations included availability of qualified workforce, logistics and transportation infrastructure, risk of natural disasters, surrounding infrastructure, and incentive package.
- The Southern region is the most strategically important market for Kia's U.S. sales strategy.
- West Point was considered the most favorable site among all options.
- Runners-up for the site included multiple locations in Mississippi: one near Kewanee on Alabama's western state line and one in Lowndes County near Columbus.
- Mississippi offered an incentive package worth some $200 million plus Gulf Opportunity Zone federal incentives enabling faster depreciation on materials and machinery investments.
- Another 2,000 jobs are expected from five suppliers that the state and company agreed would locate in Georgia.
- Hyundai Mobis is the only one of the five suppliers officially confirmed to be on or near the chosen Kia site.
- The West Point site covers 3,300 acres (1,336 hectares) shaped like a warm front moving in on I-85.
- The plant complex itself will occupy 2,200 acres (890 hectares), with the rest given over to infrastructure and transportation improvements.
- A total of 37 parcels belonging to 30 different owners were put under option by the state in approximately two months, concluding in February.
- The state expects all options to be exercised within the next month in preparation for a late April groundbreaking ceremony.
- Landowner sentiment shifted from negative when selling was first broached in late December to nearly universal support that selling is in the best interest of the community's future and leaves a legacy for future generations.
- Groundwork for the project was laid in October 2003 when Gov. Perdue first visited South Korea.
- Georgia projects from Korean firms LG Electronics and Samsung have come to fruition since that time.
- A Kia consultant previously looked at the state's mega-site in Pooler near Savannah, a site in Morgan County, and several in middle Georgia under Project Pine Tree.
- The site selection process for the West Point plant took place largely in the States rather than Korea.
- Bert Brantley, communications manager for the Georgia Dept. of Economic Development, described the competitive battle with Mississippi as largely hooey.
- Hyundai-Kia's negotiating style was described as very proper and respectful, illustrated by Mr. Ahn only calling Georgia project manager Chris Clark by his first name after the agreement was final.
- West Point may become the third point of a bi-state opportunity zone within a zone alongside Auburn-Opelika, Ala., and Columbus, Ga.
- AFLAC announced a $100-million, 2,000-employee expansion in Columbus, exclamation point on a year of multiple projects and BRAC news bringing ~10,000 military personnel to nearby Fort Benning.
- Hyundai supplier Mando America invested $65 million in two separate projects in Opelika during 2004-2005 and relocated its small headquarters there from Detroit.
- The Atlanta-Birmingham-Montgomery triangle formed by Interstates 85, 20, and 65 has a longest arm of only 160 miles (257 km).
- Local organizations involved in cementing the Kia deal included the Development Authority of LaGrange, LaGrange-Troup County Chamber of Commerce, West Point Development Authority, cities of LaGrange and West Point, and Troup County.
- Troup County's population was 58,779 in 2000.
- Milliken and Co. remains the area's largest employer with around 2,000 employees despite a weakening textile base over 30 years.
- Recent corporate investors in the area include Wal-Mart, Caterpillar, and German automotive supplier ae group, which located there in 2005.
- Gulfstream Aerospace announced $300 million over seven years investment in Savannah operations a week before the Kia announcement, boosting employment by 1,100 to 5,400.
- Toyota committed same day to building 100,000 Camrys a year at the Subaru plant in Lafayette, Indiana, transferring production from Japan.
- Kia is taking Toyota's multifaceted facility development strategy as its model for North America, choosing to site production, design and headquarters where its target market resides.
- Construction is under way on Kia's $87-million U.S. corporate campus in Irvine, Calif., expected to open in December 2006.
- The Irvine campus will include a 65,000-sq.-ft. design center.
- Kia Motors has invested more than $300 million in the United States over the last four years.
- Kia's investments include a state-of-the-art research and development center in Ann Arbor, Mich., and a custom-built vehicle proving grounds in Mojave, Calif.
- Hyundai-Kia's Ann Arbor center expanded beyond original plans, boosting capital investment there to $162 million.
- The company has a plan to sell some 800,000 vehicles a year in North America by 2010.
- For the two months ending in February, Kia's total global export sales were up by more than 18 percent vs. the same period in 2005, at 181,659.
- Kia has enjoyed 12 years of consecutive U.S. sales records since coming to market, including 275,871 vehicles in 2005.
- The deal coincides with U.S. and Korea embarking on free trade agreement (FTA) negotiations.
- U.S. Sen. George V. Voinovich (R-OH) and Sen. Carl Levin (D-MI) wrote a February letter fearing the FTA will significantly benefit Korean automakers without giving U.S. automakers additional access to Korea's historically closed market.
- One study indicates Korean exports to the U.S. would increase by as much as 17 percent with an FTA, boosting Korea's GDP by 2 percent.
- Frank Vargo, vice president of international economic affairs for the National Association of Manufacturers, stated he would be surprised if trade negotiations affected the timing of Kia or Samsung Austin investments.
- Assistant U.S. Trade Representative Wendy Cutler said Korea is currently the U.S.'s seventh largest trading partner and export market, and sixth largest agricultural market.
- Two-way trade between the U.S. and Korea is valued at $73 billion and expected to grow under an FTA.
- The Korea Institute for International Economic Policy estimates two-way trade will increase by almost $20 billion as a result of the agreement.
