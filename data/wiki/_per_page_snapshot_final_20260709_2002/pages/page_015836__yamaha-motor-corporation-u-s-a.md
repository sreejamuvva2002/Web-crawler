# Yamaha Motor Corporation, U.S.A.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-03

**Source:** [Yamaha to relocate U.S. headquarters to Georgia](https://powersportsbusiness.com/top-stories/2026/03/03/yamaha-to-relocate-u-s-headquarters-to-georgia-sell-california-campus/)  ·  `page_015836`

## Facts

- Yamaha Motor Co., Ltd. has announced plans to relocate its U.S. subsidiary, Yamaha Motor Corporation, U.S.A. (YMUS), from Cypress, California, to Kennesaw, Georgia.
- The multi-year transition will run from late 2026 through the end of 2028.
- In conjunction with the move, Yamaha will sell all fixed assets at its 25.1-acre Cypress campus, including land, offices, and warehouse facilities.
- The company plans to utilize a sale-and-leaseback arrangement for a period of time to maintain business continuity during the transition.
- Details regarding the sale price, buyer, and timing are still under review.
- The Cypress site has served as Yamaha’s U.S. headquarters since 1979, after the company acquired the land in 1978.
- Yamaha’s Marine Business Unit relocated to Kennesaw in 1999.
- Yamaha’s Motorsports business unit relocated to Kennesaw in 2019.
- Today, the Cypress facility primarily houses corporate administrative functions and Yamaha’s Financial Services operations.
- The consolidation places Yamaha’s core U.S. operations — including marine and motorsports — under a unified Georgia base.
- The move potentially streamlines decision-making, logistics coordination, and cross-division strategy for dealers and the company.
- Yamaha positioned the relocation as part of broader structural reforms aimed at improving asset efficiency and enhancing profitability in the United States.
- Rising costs associated with U.S. tariffs and shifts in the market environment are cited as factors driving the restructuring.
- Yamaha is working to build a profit structure “not solely dependent on top-line growth,” to create a more resilient and adaptable organization over the medium to long term.
- The announcement reflects a wider trend among OEMs reassessing real estate footprints, overhead costs, and regional operating efficiencies as the post-pandemic market normalizes.
- Yamaha first entered the American market in 1960, establishing Yamaha International Corporation in Los Angeles after determining that reliance on third-party trading companies limited growth and profitability.
- Under then-president Genichi Kawakami, the company shifted to direct wholesale distribution and aggressively developed a nationwide dealer network.
- Nearly 50 years after establishing its Southern California headquarters, Yamaha is consolidating around Georgia, where its marine and motorsports divisions have already operated for years.
