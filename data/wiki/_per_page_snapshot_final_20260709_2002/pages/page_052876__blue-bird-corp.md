# Blue Bird Corp.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-06

**Source:** [Workers at Georgia school bus maker Blue Bird begin voting on whether to unionize - The Christian Index](https://christianindex.org/stories/workers-at-georgia-school-bus-maker-blue-bird-begin-voting-on-whether-to-unionize,51857)  ·  `page_052876`

## Facts

- Georgia workers at Blue Bird Corp., one of the nation's largest school bus manufacturers, began voting Thursday on whether to be represented by a labor union.
- More than 1,400 employees at Blue Bird Corp.'s two factories and warehouse in Fort Valley will vote through Friday on unionization under the United Steelworkers.
- The United Steelworkers represents more than 850,000 workers nationwide across various industries.
- Blue Bird workers are seeking higher pay, a more regular schedule, and better vacation and sick time benefits from the publicly held Macon-based company, which is long the largest employer in nearby Peach County.
- Blue Bird has been meeting with employees to urge them to reject unionization, arguing it is undesirable to interject a union as an intermediary between the company and workers.
- Julianne Barclay, a spokesperson for Blue Bird, wrote in an email that the company believes most employees do not desire a union at its facilities.
- This marks the first serious attempt to form a union at Blue Bird in decades.
- The United Auto Workers previously attempted to organize Blue Bird in 2001 but withdrew its petition due to insufficient support, following a pro-union rally disrupted by anti-union workers.
- Maria Somma, organizing director for the United Steelworkers, stated that a majority of workers signed a petition seeking an election this time.
- Barclay noted that only after the employees' secret ballot vote will it be known if a majority want a union.
- The share of unionized workers nationwide has been falling for decades, dipping to 10.1% last year according to the federal Bureau of Labor Statistics.
- Private sector workers are much less likely to be union members, with only 6% paying dues.
- Only 4.4% of Georgia workers are union members, representing the eighth lowest union rate in the country.
- This low unionization rate is part of a broader trend across southern and western states where workers and employers have historically resisted unionization.
- Blue Bird hires general laborers at $16 an hour and states its average starting wage is $17.69 an hour.
- Workers argue that pay has not kept up with inflation, noting that Blue Bird received $40 million in federal aid to build electric school buses.
- Workers claim the company forces them to work overtime, making it difficult to plan their own schedules, while penalizing workers who have unplanned absences.
- Barclay explained that absences can be excused when workers call in at least an hour in advance, and sometimes later.
- She added that employees not present for scheduled work create unnecessary hardship for co-workers and costly rescheduling to meet customer demands.
- The company and pro-union workers are arguing over the amount of vacation and sick time workers receive, presenting sharply different accounts of the benefits level.
