# SK Battery America

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-07-14

**Source:** [Destroyed recycling center settles with SK Battery for $31M, then blasts the company | FOX 5 Atlanta](https://www.fox5atlanta.com/news/destroyed-recycling-center-settles-sk-battery-31-million-blasts-company)  ·  `page_050062`

## Facts

- SK Battery is a North Georgia EV battery maker.
- SK Battery paid $31 million to Metro Site, Inc., a nearby recycling business that went up in flames last year.
- The fire at Metro Site was allegedly caused because the factory illegally dumped hazardous scrap batteries there.
- SK Battery admitted no liability in the settlement of the civil lawsuit filed by Metro Site.
- The settlement had no confidentiality clause.
- Scott Ledford is the owner of Metro Site, an automated industrial recycling center near Commerce, Ga., which he spent five years building.
- The fire at Metro Site started July 14, 2023.
- The fire took four days and three million gallons of water to finally extinguish.
- Court records show SK Battery America accepted a settlement offer from Metro Site last month.
- Ledford’s legal team made the $31 million sum public in a press release that publicly accused the company of destroying evidence in the case.
- A press release stated that during the litigation, SK Battery destroyed critical evidence that it had been on notice to preserve.
- State and local governments wooed SK Battery to Commerce at the end of the last decade with $300 million in tax abatements, grants and land.
- SK Battery's $2.6 billion factory employs more than 3,000 workers.
- The factory has made batteries for the Ford F-150 Lightning and the Volkswagen ID.4.
- Four years ago, SK Battery faced allegations of deleting emails in a trade secrets dispute with a rival battery maker.
- The U.S. International Trade Commission ruled in the other company’s favor in that dispute, with SK settling for $1.8 billion.
- SK Battery denied destroying evidence in that case in a statement to FOX 5.
- In the Metro Site case, Ledford’s attorneys asked for sanctions, with SK Battery pushing back saying everything requested was provided.
- The judge never made a ruling on the allegations regarding evidence destruction.
- Allyn Stockton is a Clayton attorney and part of Scott Ledford's legal team.
- Stockton told the FOX 5 I-Team that SK Battery destroyed videos from inside and outside the plant.
- An affidavit filed in the case was signed by Mike Ramirez, a former safety manager at the plant.
- The affidavit elaborated on accusations that surveillance cameras should have captured scrap batteries being moved from within the plant to the outside.
- Ramirez left behind logbooks when his employment ended, which did not come back in discovery along with the videos.
- Stockton said those videos might have shown the batteries going into the roll-off dumpster bound for Metro Site.
- Ledford’s attorneys described other troubling things learned during the case about the company’s scrap batteries, as well as more fires.
- One record filed in the case shows 62 internal fires at SK.
- The factory has its own internal firefighting team with their own fire truck, confirmed by the company.
- The Ramirez affidavit alleges fires started because the plant ramped up production to unsafe levels, creating high volumes of unusable batteries called NGs or "no good" that often combusted.
- The affidavit stated fires were occurring inside the plant routinely and hundreds upon hundreds of NG pallets were stored in four warehouses in Jefferson, Georgia.
- A fire occurred in Jefferson and was caused by NG cells.
- Ledford and his attorneys said there would be no confidentiality clause so people need to know what happened.
- Stockton described this as part of the culture of that corporation.
- Some of Ledford’s attorneys are involved in a separate ongoing lawsuit over the fire representing Banks County government.
- The lawsuit accused SK Battery of illegally endangering government property and employees, including the fire station next door.
- SK declined to talk on camera about the Metro Site case but said by email through a spokesman that they settled because both parties were ready to move on.
- SK added they are not sure why Metro Site and its attorneys continue to argue the case.
- On the 62 fires, SK said they occurred over three years, with many of the events triggered only by a smoke alarm without any fire, and none resulting in significant damage or burn injuries to employees.
- SK's written statement described stringent procedures for disposing of production material.
- A photo from a 2020 news release shows SK's EV battery cells.
- SK Battery America stated it values its partnership with the Georgia community and is committed to having a positive impact on the area.
- SK added that more than a year ago, they added an extra layer of oversight to further strengthen procedures before any waste material leaves their site.
- The Georgia Environmental Protection Division fined the company $33,000 over the fire.
- The agreement ending the recycling center’s lawsuit provides a generous settlement for Metro Site to rebuild and relaunch its business, according to SK.
- Ledford built his business from the ground up only to watch it burn to the ground in July 2023.
- Ledford said the company had sent him lithium-ion batteries about a half dozen times before, mixed in with recycling materials.
- Fires had broken out previously but nothing severe.
- Ledford said SK acknowledged they were not licensed nor permitted to handle lithium-ion battery packs or any type of hazardous waste.
- Every time Ledford met with them, they made promises that they had their problem fixed on their end.
- A pallet full of batteries was put in a dumpster illegally and shipped over to Metro Site.
- When hundreds were put together, there was a chain reaction that lit everything in the remote vicinity to it on fire.
- SK Battery also faces a lawsuit from Banks County over the July 2023 fire at Metro Site, which is next door to a fire station.
- The fire took four days and three million gallons of water to put out.
- Ledford alleged that found among the embers were more than 600 charred batteries made by SK.
- He filed his lawsuit a few weeks later.
- Ledford said despite how it looks, the settlement did not make him rich.
- He said the $31 million would be sucked up by debt associated with the facility, rebuild costs of the facility, and equipment that was lost, leaving him to about break even.
- One of Ledford’s attorneys is Bo Hatchett, who is also a Republican state senator from Cornelia.
- Asked if tax incentives for SK Battery in retrospect were a mistake, Hatchett said the company brought a lot of jobs to the area.
- Hatchett believed that since the fire occurred, SK has made some significant changes with their safety.
- He noted it was a small business run by a local family that burned to the ground.
