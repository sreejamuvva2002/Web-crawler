## Facts

- Georgia is one of the states where companies are keen to build data centers, thanks to generous incentives and a strong fiber infrastructure, especially around Atlanta.
- Many data centers are popping up in Atlanta suburbs, especially majority-Black ones on the south side.
- Fayetteville, Georgia, located about 40 minutes south of downtown Atlanta, features large parcels of greenery and suburban homes suitable for development.
- Diana Dietz has lived in Fayetteville since the '90s and now tracks data center growth in the area.
- A construction site in Fayetteville has had miles of trees cleared for new facilities.
- QTS is building a 600-acre data center campus in Fayetteville, Georgia.
- Kecia Scott lives in Planters Ridge, a predominantly Black middle-class subdivision, and her house is now right across the street from the new data center.
- Residents like Kecia Scott did not know about the data center project until after moving into their homes.
- Residents worry about environmental impacts and the large amount of water and electricity needed to run data centers.
- Georgia has at least a hundred data centers in operation, most located south of Atlanta.
- Data centers are concentrated south of Atlanta because there is still more rural land available than in other parts of metro Atlanta.
- Lynn McKee teaches real estate at Georgia State University and notes the land availability factor for data center placement.
- Georgia Tech professor Ahmed Saeed states that Georgia is making it very attractive to build data centers here.
- Lucrative tax incentives to the data center industry are supported by Georgia's Republican governor, Brian Kemp.
- Governor Brian Kemp vetoed a bill last year that would have paused the tax exemptions for data centers.
- Residents on the south side are starting to speak out about the impact of data centers in Black neighborhoods.
- Some Republicans want more protections for communities, like state Senator Chuck Hufstetler.
- State Senator Chuck Hufstetler stated: "You know, there are some people that want to give away the farm and others that don't want any growth. And I just want us to be smart about it."
- State Senator Chuck Hufstetler's efforts to implement more community protections have stalled in the legislature.
- Georgia Power reversed its efforts to close two coal-fired power plants, saying it now needs them to serve customers with large energy demands.
- Diana Dietz noted regarding construction vehicles: "You can't go out without seeing several of those huge construction vehicles on the roads."
- Kecia Scott stated about her neighborhood's transformation: "I have seen all of the what used to be cozy, quaint, wooded kind of area completely change."
- Lynn McKee stated regarding data center placement: "People don't want these big data centers near their houses. But you go out and you go to South Atlanta or into rural Georgia, there's just land, land, land."
