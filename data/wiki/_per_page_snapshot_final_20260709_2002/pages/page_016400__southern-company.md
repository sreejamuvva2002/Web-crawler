# Southern Company

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-02-25

**Source:** [Feds Announce $26.5 Billion Loan for Electric Power Expansion in Georgia and Alabama](https://www.usnews.com/news/business/articles/2026-02-25/feds-give-record-27b-in-loans-for-utility-expansion-in-georgia-and-alabama)  ·  `page_016400`

## Facts

- Federal energy officials announced a record $26.5 billion loan to electric utilities in Georgia and Alabama.
- A total of $22.4 billion will go to Georgia Power and $4.1 billion to Alabama Power.
- Both Georgia Power and Alabama Power are subsidiaries of Atlanta-based Southern Company, one of the nation's largest utilities.
- The companies plan to use the loan cash to build new natural-gas fueled power plants, build new transmission lines, and upgrade existing power plants.
- The utility expansion is driven by demand from computer data centers.
- Energy Secretary Chris Wright said the loan will result in more than $7 billion in savings over decades from a lower, federally subsidized interest rate.
- Chris Wright stated that federal officials are focused on driving down costs and the loan would help ensure Southern customers have access to affordable, reliable and secure energy for decades to come.
- Wright and President Donald Trump have frequently advocated for fossil fuel-friendly policies, including orders over the past nine months to keep some coal-fired plants open past planned retirement dates, citing grid reliability.
- Wright claims these orders have saved utility customers millions of dollars and helped keep lights on during last month’s winter storm.
- Critics argue the orders are unnecessary and have raised electric bills as utilities operate older, more expensive plants.
- Chris Womack, Southern’s chairman, president and CEO, said the loans will help lower the cost of grid investments that enhance reliability and resilience for customers.
- The loan announcement comes amid scrutiny over rising utility bills, with electricity prices increasing faster than inflation in many states.
- There is widespread opposition to new artificial intelligence data centers.
- President Trump announced a “ratepayer protection pledge” against higher utility bills tied to AI during his State of the Union address on Tuesday.
- Trump claimed tech companies will provide their own power for data centers and that prices will go down, but did not provide details.
- It remains unclear if any tech companies have signed pledges to build their own power plants.
- Wright stated on a Wednesday reporter call that every major company developing a data center has been in dialogue with federal officials.
- Wright cited cooperation from Microsoft, Google, and Meta regarding data center power but did not specify written agreements.
- Federal officials have previously provided utility loans, including $12 billion guaranteed by the first Trump administration and President Barack Obama’s administration for two nuclear reactors at Georgia’s Plant Vogtle, partially owned by Georgia Power.
- Trump’s tax and budget bill last year reshaped the loan program to focus on increasing electricity generation and transmission capacity.
- Loan guarantees under President Joe Biden previously focused on green energy goals.
- Gregory Beard, director of the newly renamed Office of Energy Dominance Financing, said cutting interest rates and discarding Biden's policy will restore affordability.
- The loan office will review individual projects for financial viability before approving them.
- Beard stated officials will not build plants or deploy capital until sure it is right for the local community and ratepayer.
- Requirements regarding project viability do not appear to be laid out in loan agreements released by Southern on Wednesday.
- Jennifer Whitfield, an attorney for the Southern Environmental Law Center who represented Georgia Power expansion opponents, questioned the wisdom of the loans despite acknowledging they will save money for Georgians.
- Whitfield stated that from a taxpayer perspective, the loan is a bailout paid for by every taxpaying citizen of the United States.
- Any customer savings from the loan must be approved by the elected Public Service Commissions in Alabama and Georgia.
- Commissioners last July approved a three-year rate freeze requested by Georgia Power, while Alabama commissioners approved a two-year rate freeze in December.
- Company officials highlight these rate freezes amid nationwide utility requests for record increases, but opponents complain regulators locked in high prices and profits.
- Voters removed two Republican incumbents from the Georgia commission in November due to complaints about rising bills.
- Commissioner Peter Hubbard, one of two new Democrats, unsuccessfully tried to roll back approval for Georgia Power's expansion in recent weeks.
- Hubbard stated that declining costs of solar, wind, and battery power could make new natural gas plants uneconomic over time.
- Hubbard criticized the federal loan as locking the state into a costlier option that is not meeting the moment of affordability.
