# U.S. Department of Energy Fiscal Year 2025 Technology Commercialization Fund Core Laboratory Infrastructure for Market Readiness Lab Call

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [Fiscal Year 2025 CLIMR Projects: Commercializing Energy Technologies | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/technologycommercialization/fiscal-year-2025-climr-projects-commercializing-energy-technologies)  ·  `page_028706`

## Facts

- The U.S. Department of Energy’s Office of Technology Commercialization announced an investment of more than $35 million in federal funds towards 42 projects through the Technology Commercialization Fund Core Laboratory Infrastructure for Market Readiness Lab Call.
- The selected projects will leverage over $21 million in cost-share from private and public partners, bringing total funding to more than $57.5 million.
- Oak Ridge National Laboratory, Brookhaven National Laboratory, Georgia Institute of Technology, and Pioneer Energy are partnering to advance and validate a cost-effective, environmentally friendly catalytic technology for the direct production of methanol from stranded methane streams.
- The methanol synthesis project integrates two innovations recently developed at Oak Ridge and Brookhaven National Laboratories with a highly selective and scalable sulfur tolerant catalyst that yields high purity methanol.
- Artificial intelligence and machine learning will be used in the methanol synthesis project to predict and optimize conditions for accelerating scaled, commercially viable performance.
- The methanol technology will be validated on a customized fixed bed reactor for a continuous scaled process intensification pathway.
- Technoeconomic and life cycle analysis for the methanol project will be performed by Pioneer Energy and Georgia Institute of Technology to guide market deployment.
- Argonne National Laboratory and PulseForge are collaborating to commercialize a rapid and energy-efficient photonic processing technology for ceramic lithium battery electrolyte material manufacturing.
- The photonic processing technology for lithium battery electrolytes has demonstrated a tenfold reduction in energy consumption and a 200-fold increase in manufacturing speed compared to conventional ceramic sintering technologies.
- Argonne National Laboratory and PulseForge will further increase the technology readiness level and adoption readiness level for the lithium battery electrolyte manufacturing system through intensive customer engagement and prototype manufacturing equipment construction.
- Oak Ridge National Laboratory is partnering with zinc ion battery manufacturers and a university to improve aqueous zinc ion battery cycling performances at the pouch cell level based on developed aqueous electrolytes.
- The zinc ion battery project will assemble and test 1Ah multi-layer zinc ion pouch cells with different energy density levels, identifying failure mechanisms through non-destructive, real-time electrochemical analysis approaches.
- Performance of the aqueous zinc ion pouch cells will be improved by optimizing design factors such as electrode porosity/mass loading, anode thickness, and electrolyte amount, as well as operation conditions like external pressures and fixture designs.
- Ames National Laboratory is developing an injection molding-based technology for the in-situ fabrication of non-rare earth MnBi bonded magnets during the motor assembly process.
- The magnet fabrication project converts fine MnBi powder with a particle size of less than 10 μm into anisotropic granules with a particle size of 300±100 μm for handling in air.
- The injection molding approach for non-rare earth magnets aims to enable cost-effective, large-scale production of high-performance rare-earth free industrial motors.
- Oak Ridge National Laboratory is leveraging patented technology to manufacture novel, lower energy and cost feedstock composite materials incorporating domestically sourced carbon fiber from existing waste material streams with more than 75% reduced embodied energy and cost.
- Recycled carbon fiber recovered from automotive, aerospace, and energy generation scrap will be used as a lower-cost, lower-energy filler for newly developed thermoplastic composites to overcome fiber length degradation caused by conventional re-manufacturing.
- An industrial demonstration at the end of the recycled carbon fiber project will prove that the newly produced feedstock materials meet all mechanical and thermomechanical performance of virgin materials while reducing overall cost and embodied energy.
