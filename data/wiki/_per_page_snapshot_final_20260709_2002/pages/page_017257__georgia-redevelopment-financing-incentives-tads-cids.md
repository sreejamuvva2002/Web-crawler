# Georgia Redevelopment Financing Incentives (TADs & CIDs)

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Redevelopment Financing Incentives in Georgia | Tax Lawyers | Alston & Bird](https://www.alston.com/en/services/practices/tax/state--local-tax/government--economic-incentives/redevelopment-financing-incentives-in-georgia)  ·  `page_017257`

## Facts

- TADs are areas specifically designated by local governments for TIF projects where real estate property taxes gathered above a particular threshold for a set period of time must be used to fund specified improvements.
- Local governments consider geographic areas that have potential for development or redevelopment but suffer from blight or other distressed conditions when establishing TADs.
- As improvements and redevelopment occur in TADs, the taxable value of property increases, resulting in additional tax revenues held by local governments in special funds to pay debt service on tax allocation bonds (TABs).
- The life of a TAD is typically 20-25 years, or sufficient time to repay the TABs.
- In Georgia, tax incentive financing for redevelopment is generally referred to as "TAD financing."
- Local governments in Georgia are authorized to use TAD financing under the state's Redevelopment Powers Law (O.C.G.A. § 36-44-1 et seq.), adopted by the General Assembly in 1985.
- Creating a TAD requires the Redevelopment Law to be made applicable to a county or city via local law passed by the Georgia General Assembly and approved by special election.
- A city or county must prepare a redevelopment plan for the proposed TAD and submit it for consent to the political subdivision or board of education required to consent.
- The local legislative body must adopt a resolution approving the redevelopment plan, describing the TAD territory, creating the district on December 31 (or a subsequent year), assigning a name, specifying the estimated tax allocation increment base, specifying ad valorem property taxes for computing increments, specifying property pledged for TABs, and finding that the area would not be redeveloped without the plan or contains unpreserved assets.
- A certified copy of the authorizing resolution must be submitted to the local legislative body before including ad valorem property taxes or general funds in the tax allocation increment calculation.
- Once designated, TABs may be issued by the city or county to pay redevelopment costs for approved projects within the TAD.
- TABs are secured by incremental tax revenue increases from redevelopment and may qualify to pay interest exempt from state and federal income taxes.
- Over the term of a TAB, the tax increment is set aside to pay debt service; after retirement, the increment goes to general funds at the taxing jurisdiction's discretion.
- TABs are not considered general obligations of the issuer.
- TAD financing can be structured as front-end sales of TABs for initial infrastructure or land acquisition costs, paid back with tax increment revenues as collected, or on a "pay-as-you-go" basis reimbursed as increments generate.
- TAD financing provides funding for redevelopment projects that may otherwise not have been feasible and makes challenging redevelopment sites attractive to developers.
- TAD financing allows local governments to leverage tax increments for new public infrastructure and private investment without spending current tax revenues.
- TAD financing grants access to redevelopment powers such as eminent domain.
- TADs have the potential to create new jobs, eliminate blight, reduce crime, increase property values and tax revenues, decrease poverty, create economic opportunities, and redevelop underperforming neighborhoods and underused commercial parcels.
- Community Improvement Districts (CIDs) are authorized by Article IX, Section VII of the Georgia Constitution for any county, city, or combination thereof.
- A CID is a unit of government with authority to provide governmental facilities and services like street/road construction, parks, water systems, and other services.
- Property owners within CIDs agree to a self-imposed tax used for improving the district.
- CIDs can be public bodies or not-for-profit corporations that collect money from property taxes, business licenses, sales taxes, or user fees to fund district services.
- A CID's administrative body may incur debt without a voter referendum, backed by the full faith, credit, and taxing power of the CID but not an obligation of the State or other local governments.
- Creating a CID requires a resolution consenting to its creation by the governing authority of the county (if unincorporated), municipality (if incorporated), or both.
- Creation also requires written consent from a majority of real property owners subject to taxes/fees/assessments and owners constituting at least 75% (by value) of all real property within the CID subject to such levies.
- The County Tax Commissioner certifies that required approvals and percentages are satisfied before the County Board of Commissioners and/or City Council pass a resolution consenting to creation.
- CIDs are governmental entities administered by a board of directors whose composition is set by local legislation, consisting of elected and appointed members with terms also set by legislation.
- For example, Cobb County and Gwinnett County CIDs are governed by boards of 6 members elected by property owners and 1 member appointed by the County.
- CID boards raise funds by setting ad valorem millage rates on real property, excluding agricultural, residential, or forestry uses.
- The millage rate is constitutionally capped at 2.5% (25 mills) of assessed value, though local laws may limit it further, such as Cobb County's cap of 5 mills.
- CID tax revenues are collected by the Tax Commissioner and sent to the CID Board for expenditure strictly on governmental services and facilities within the district.
- CIDs create special taxing districts to pay for infrastructure and services including street construction/maintenance, parks/recreation, storm water/sewage collection, terminal/dock facilities, parking, and public transportation.
- Numerous CIDs exist in Georgia, with a majority located in and around the Atlanta area.
- Examples of existing CIDs include Cumberland (Cobb County), Buckhead (Atlanta), Downtown (Atlanta), Midtown (Atlanta), Town Center (Cobb County), Gwinnett Place (Gwinnett County), Lilburn (Gwinnett County), and Town of Braselton (Braselton).
- TIF has been used in DeKalb County, Georgia, and the cities of Atlanta, Macon, College Park, East Point, Marietta, Acworth, and Smyrna.
- In 2001, the City of Atlanta issued $67,505,000 in TABs to finance a portion of infrastructure work for the Atlantic Station project, which had a total infrastructure development cost budget of $187,000,000.
- Bond proceeds for the Atlantic Station project were used for clearing and grading, utilities, streets and sidewalks, environmental remediation, parking, costs of issuance, and related items.
- The Atlanta Atlantic Station TAD contained approximately 119 areas.
- For the Atlantic Station project, the incremental tax revenue increase for 2006 over the 2001 base year was estimated at $8,347,772.
- In 2003, the Smyrna TAD was established to finance infrastructure for the redevelopment of two obsolete shopping centers along Atlanta Road in a $27,000,000 project.
