# North American EV & Hybrid Market Analysis

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-01

**Source:** [North American EV demand slows as hybrids gain momentum and automotive OEM strategies reset  - Fastmarkets](https://www.fastmarkets.com/insights/north-american-ev-demand-slows-as-hybrids-gain-momentum-and-automotive-oem-strategies-reset/)  ·  `page_014323`

## Facts

- Hybrid vehicles now hold 14.5% market share, surpassing BEVs at 5.1%, reflecting consumer preference for fuel economy without full EV commitment.
- OEMs are recalibrating investments, with over $234 billion spent since 2020 but recent trends favoring hybrids and ICE platforms.
- Automakers are expanding battery use beyond vehicles into energy storage markets, leveraging growing US electricity demand and aiming to optimize battery capacity amid slower EV growth.
- Changes to EV incentives, infrastructure funding and regulatory frameworks represent a near complete reversal in direction for full battery EV adoption momentum.
- Hybrid vehicles now account for 14.5% of US market share, while BEVs have fallen to 5.1% after peaking at 7.8% in 2024.
- Electrified vehicles as a whole, including hybrids, plug-in hybrids and BEVs, represent around 20% of the market, leaving internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles with an 80% share.
- Consumers are looking for better fuel economy without having to commit to EV charging.
- Hybrid is the only propulsion type that has gained sales momentum compared to last year, while all other propulsion types are down in sales.
- Vehicles priced below $60,000 are more likely to substitute steel for aluminium for fenders and hoods due in part to advances in high-strength steels.
- US light vehicle sales reached around 16 million units in 2025, with forecasts for 2026 converging at approximately 15.7 million units, a modest decline of 300,000 vehicles.
- Industry experts are continuing to lower full battery EV adoption forecasts, with the current projection of 16% market share by 2030 potentially overstated.
- Since 2020, automakers have announced more than $234 billion in investments across North America, with 77% directed toward the United States, 13% to Canada and 9% to Mexico.
- Honda halted its North American EV expansion, stating that EV business viability is not currently assured.
- Honda cancelled the launch and development of several EV models planned for the region while pivoting toward hybrids and prioritizing stable earnings from the ICE business.
- The US Department of Commerce slapped Honda with a 13.07% weighted-average dumping margin on hot-rolled coil in April.
- Uncertainty surrounding the renegotiation of the USMCA is adding complexity, with market participants expecting a more fragmented trade environment where tariffs play a larger role.
- Free trade is expected to be replaced by tariffs, according to Oscar del Cueto, president of American Chamber of Commerce Mexico and Canadian Pacific Kansas City Mexico.
- Local production remains a meaningful initiative from the perspective of tariff measures, according to Honda’s leadership.
- Ford launched a dedicated energy storage business to repurpose excess battery capacity and diversify revenue streams amid slowing EV demand growth.
- US electricity demand is rising due to data centers, industrial activity and renewable energy expansion, accelerating the need for battery storage solutions.
- Batteries are increasingly viewed not as vehicle-specific components but as flexible assets within a broader energy ecosystem.
- Strategic priorities for OEMs include balancing powertrains, managing demand uncertainty, optimizing battery utilization through energy storage, and navigating trade dynamics with tariffs and localization requirements.
