# Ascend Elements

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Ascend Elements' bid to Launch US Lithium Recovery Line | EV Magazine](https://evmagazine.com/charging-and-infrastructure/ascend-elements-us-lithium-recovery-line-launch)  ·  `page_001469`

## Facts

- Ascend Elements has announced plans to launch a new lithium recovery line at its Covington, Georgia, facility in 2025.
- The new line will produce over 99% pure lithium carbonate (Li2CO3) recovered from used lithium-ion batteries.
- With a planned output of up to 3,000 metric tons annually, it will be the first large-scale commercial production of recycled Li2CO3 in the US.
- Ascend Elements was founded in 2015 with headquarters in Massachusetts.
- The company utilizes Hydro-to-Cathode direct precursor synthesis technology to efficiently produce new cathode materials from spent lithium-ion cells.
- Eric Gratz, co-founder and Chief Technology Officer of Ascend Elements, states that this is the first new, domestic source of lithium carbonate in the 21st century.
- Eric Gratz adds that this new domestic supply will help US industries meet growing demand while avoiding the possibility of tariffs on imported materials.
- Li2CO3 is a key ingredient in advanced batteries powering EVs, grid-scale energy storage systems, boats, and aircraft.
- Currently, the only other US source of Li2CO3 is a mining operation in Nevada.
- Ascend Elements' lithium recovery process produces just 2.27 kilograms of CO2 emissions per kilogram of Li2CO3.
- This method is significantly less carbon-intensive than conventional techniques like spodumene mining (16.7 kg CO2/kg Li2CO3) or Chilean brine extraction (3.6 kg CO2/kg Li2CO3).
- Eric Gratz highlights that their current process of lithium extraction from used lithium-ion batteries is about 86% less carbon-intensive compared to spodumene mining and 37% less carbon-intensive compared to Chilean brine extraction.
- The Covington plant has been operational since August 2022.
- The Covington facility is one of North America's largest lithium-ion battery recycling facilities.
- The Covington plant has the capacity to recycle up to 30,000 metric tons of lithium-ion battery materials annually.
- The Covington plant can process approximately 70,000 EV battery packs annually.
- Eric Gratz states that this new capability aligns with their vision of creating a circular economy for battery materials and calls it a win for the industry, the environment, and the nation's energy future.
