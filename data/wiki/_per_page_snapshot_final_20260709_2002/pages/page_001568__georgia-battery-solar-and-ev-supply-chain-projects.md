# Georgia Battery, Solar, and EV Supply Chain Projects

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-06-30

**Source:** [A Georgia town that solidly backed Trump could fall victim to his tax bill's green energy cuts - AOL](https://www.aol.com/georgia-town-solidly-backed-trump-161226881.html)  ·  `page_001568`

## Facts

- Two South Korean companies announced a multibillion-dollar investment to build solar panel and electric battery factories in northwest Georgia.
- Federal subsidies helped close a deal to diversify the local economy of Cartersville.
- The factories promised thousands of new jobs, transforming the manufacturing base in Cartersville.
- Cartersville was once a cotton mill town before an Anheuser-Busch brewery arrived in the 1990s and a tire plant in 2006.
- Republicans in Congress want to gut subsidies for projects across the country in a tax cut bill likely days from final passage.
- President Donald Trump's signature legislation could harm Cartersville despite it being in overwhelmingly Republican Bartow County, which backed Trump with 75% of the vote all three times he appeared on the ballot.
- Both companies say they're continuing their buildout plans despite the legislative threat.
- Steve Taylor, a Republican who is Bartow County's lone elected commissioner, says ending the tax credits would be "a little concerning."
- Taylor said: "Those companies came and it gave us a completely different type of industry and manufacturing for our community."
- No state may have more to lose than Georgia from cuts in Trump's "Big Beautiful Bill."
- Top Georgia Republicans have been mostly silent on the issue.
- Georgia's two Democratic U.S. senators are staunchly opposed to the bill.
- U.S. Sen. Jon Ossoff told The Associated Press: "A vote for this bill is a vote against Georgia's economy and a vote that will put so much of what we've worked so hard to achieve at risk."
- Cartersville is the Bartow County seat about 35 miles (55 kilometers) northwest of Atlanta.
- Bartow County is transforming from rural to suburban.
- Leaders foresee an economic boost from the $5 billion battery factory that Hyundai Motor Group and SK On are building.
- Leaders foresee an economic boost from the $2.3 billion solar panel plant belonging to Qcells, a unit of Hanwha Solutions.
- Both plants pledge to pay workers an average of $53,000 a year.
- Clean energy projects in Georgia had already taken off before 2022, when then-President Joe Biden signed the Inflation Reduction Act.
- The rush of clean energy projects accelerated after the IRA was passed.
- 33 additional clean energy projects were announced by the end of 2024, the most nationwide according to E2, an environmental business group.
- Projects in Georgia top $20 billion, pledging more than 25,000 jobs, though exact figures differ.
- Buyers of Qcells solar panels get a 40% federal tax credit, including a 10% bonus for domestic content.
- The 40% tax credit and 10% domestic content bonus would go away under the proposed bill.
- Qcells itself would still get production tax credits for panels it started producing last year in Cartersville.
- The bill would also tax companies that buy panels or components from some foreign countries including China.
- Taxing Chinese imports could help Qcells, but wouldn't aid domestic producers as much as the domestic content bonus.
- When complete, the 1,900-job plant will take refined polysilicon, cast it into ingots, and thinly slice ingots into wafers that become solar cells.
- Qcells says controlling its own supply chain will let it work more efficiently.
- Those additional steps would earn the company additional tax credits.
- Scott Moskowitz is vice president of market strategy and industry affairs for Qcells.
- Moskowitz said the company built its first American factory up the road in Dalton during the first Trump administration in response to Trump's protectionist trade policy.
- Moskowitz argues that a quick curtailment of federal subsidies undercuts Trump's goal of bolstering domestic manufacturing, pushing buyers back to Chinese-controlled producers.
- 16 GOP state legislators sent a June 17 letter imploring Congress to preserve tax breaks for solar panels.
- Georgia lawmakers, led by Republican state Rep. Matthew Gambill of Cartersville, wrote: "We urge you not to weaken the tax credits, as doing so would only harm the manufacturing renaissance in Georgia while creating opportunities for Chinese companies to take over the solar industry."
- Some argue it's unfair for Congress to pull the rug out after companies relied on the promise of federal support to invest huge sums.
- Cartersville Mayor Matt Santini said: "I would like to think that from a business perspective that when you have agreements in place that you carry those out to fulfillment."
- Clean energy projects have overwhelmingly located in Republican-held congressional districts.
- A report by Atlas Public Policy found GOP districts host 77% of planned spending on these projects.
- Republican U.S. Rep. Barry Loudermilk, who lives in Bartow County, addressed the local impact in a Monday statement to the AP.
- Loudermilk said the cuts will push Qcells and Hyundai-SK On to become "independent of government subsidies."
- Loudermilk said: "It is important to keep industry as free from government control as possible, and not dependent on government."
- Loudermilk labeled the Biden incentives as "the Democrats' Green New scam" in May.
- Republican Gov. Brian Kemp says he's staying out of the debate.
- Kemp spokesperson Garrison Douglas said: "Our position is that Congress needs to be the one to decide the future of the IRA."
- Kemp loves green energy investments and jobs, and declared his goal is to make Georgia the "electric mobility capital of America."
- Kemp and Ossoff clash over who should get credit for Georgia's green energy boom.
- Kemp sharply disputes that the Biden-era incentives spurred the flood of investment, saying industries were already coming to Georgia before the Inflation Reduction Act was passed.
- Kemp vociferously opposed some domestic content requirements that made it hard for Hyundai to access the same tax credits as unionized U.S.-based automakers.
- Douglas said: "Just generally speaking, the Inflation Reduction Act picked winners and losers, and we saw that negatively impact our partners."
- All nine of Georgia's Republican House members voted to support the bill.
- U.S. Rep Buddy Carter earlier signed a letter supporting green energy subsidies.
- Carter is seeking the GOP nomination to oppose Ossoff for Senate in 2026.
- Carter represents a coastal district that includes a $7.6 billion Hyundai plant in Ellabell that started production last year.
- Hyundai wants to make batteries at what would be a 3,500-employee plant near Cartersville.
- The battery plant is intended so Hyundai and Kia buyers can fully take advantage of the $7,500 tax credit for electric vehicles.
- Those EV tax credits would end six months after the bill is enacted under the current version.
- Hyundai is publicly sidestepping the current legislative fight.
- With American demand for electric vehicles slow to take off, Hyundai now says it will also build gas-electric hybrid vehicles in Ellabell.
- The Ellabell plant was projected to make only electric vehicles before this shift.
- Hyundai spokesperson Michael Stewart said: "We remain focused on electrification because we believe it represents a significant long-term opportunity."
- Stewart added: "At the same time, our business is driven by consumer demand, which is why we continue to offer a full range of powertrains."
- Bartow County leaders say it's in everyone's interest to keep the projects on solid footing and that jobs should outweigh politics.
- Santini said: "I don't know that people are lining up along party lines over this topic."
- Ossoff says partisanship is motivating many Georgia Republicans to turn their backs on the state's economic interests.
- Ossoff said: "For national Republicans right now, loyalty to Trump is more important than anything else, and this is what Trump says he wants."
