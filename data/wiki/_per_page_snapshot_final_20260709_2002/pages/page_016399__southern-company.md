# Southern Company

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-02

**Source:** [Feds announce $26.5 billion loan for electric power expansion in Georgia and Alabama - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2026/02/feds-give-record-27b-in-loans-for-utility-expansion-in-georgia-and-alabama/)  ·  `page_016399`

## Facts

- Federal energy officials on Wednesday announced a record $26.5 billion loan to electric utilities in Georgia and Alabama.
- The loan aims to save customers money as companies undertake expansion driven by demand from computer data centers.
- $22.4 billion of the total loan will go to Georgia Power.
- $4.1 billion of the total loan will go to Alabama Power.
- Georgia Power and Alabama Power are subsidiaries of Atlanta-based Southern Company, one of the nation’s largest utilities.
- The companies plan to use the funds to build new natural-gas fueled power plants, build new transmission lines, and upgrade existing power plants.
- Energy Secretary Chris Wright said the loan will result in more than $7 billion in savings over decades from a lower, federally subsidized interest rate.
- Chris Wright stated that Southern is focused on driving down costs and ensuring customers have access to affordable, reliable, and secure energy for decades to come.
- Wright and President Donald Trump have frequently advocated for fossil fuel-friendly policies, including orders over the past nine months to keep some coal-fired plants open past planned retirement dates to ensure grid reliability.
- Wright claims these orders have saved utility customers millions of dollars and helped keep lights on during last month’s winter storm.
- Critics argue the orders are unnecessary and have raised electric bills as utilities operate older, more expensive plants.
- Chris Womack, Southern’s chairman, president and CEO, said the loans will help lower the cost of grid investments to enhance reliability and resilience for customers.
- The loan announcement comes amid scrutiny over rising utility bills, with electricity prices increasing faster than inflation in many states.
- There is widespread opposition to new data centers for artificial intelligence.
- Trump announced a “ratepayer protection pledge” against higher utility bills tied to AI during his State of the Union address on Tuesday.
- Trump stated tech companies will provide their own power as they build data centers, though he provided no details and claimed prices will go down.
- It is unclear whether any tech companies have signed pledges to build their own power plants.
- Wright said on a call with reporters that “every name you know that’s developing a data center has been in dialogue with us.”
- Wright cited “cooperation” from tech giants Microsoft, Google, and Meta, but did not specify any written agreements.
- Federal officials have long provided utility loans, including $12 billion guaranteed by the first Trump administration and President Barack Obama’s administration for two costly nuclear reactors at Georgia’s Plant Vogtle, partially owned by Georgia Power.
- Trump’s tax and budget bill last year reshaped the loan program to focus on increasing capacity to generate and transmit electricity.
- Loan guarantees under President Joe Biden focused on green energy goals.
- Gregory Beard, director of the newly renamed Office of Energy Dominance Financing, said cutting interest rates and discarding Biden’s policy will restore affordability.
- The loan office will review individual projects to ensure financial viability.
- Beard stated they will not build a plant or deploy capital until sure it is right for the local community and ratepayer.
- These requirements do not appear to be laid out in loan agreements that Southern released on Wednesday.
- Jennifer Whitfield, an attorney for the Southern Environmental Law Center who represented Georgia Power expansion opponents, said the loans will save money for Georgians but questioned their wisdom.
- Whitfield called it a “bailout paid for by every taxpaying citizen of the United States.”
- Any savings for customers must be approved by elected Public Service Commissions in Alabama and Georgia.
- Commissioners last July approved a three-year rate freeze requested by Georgia Power.
- Commissioners in Alabama approved a two-year rate freeze in December.
- Company officials tout the freezes when utilities nationwide seek record increases, but opponents complain company-friendly regulators locked in high prices and profits.
- Voters removed two Republican incumbents from the Georgia commission in November amid complaints about rising bills.
- Commissioner Peter Hubbard, one of two new Democrats, unsuccessfully tried to roll back approval for Georgia Power’s expansion in recent weeks.
- Hubbard stated that declining costs of solar, wind, and battery power could make new natural gas plants uneconomic over time.
- Hubbard said the federal loan is “locking us into a costlier option” and is not meeting the moment of affordability.
