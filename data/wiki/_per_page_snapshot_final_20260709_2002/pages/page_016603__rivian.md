# Rivian

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-06-13

**Source:** [China's automotive path to the EU through Spain](https://automotive-risk-digest.elmanalytics.com/p/automotive-supply-chain-risk-digest-480-20260501)  ·  `page_016603`

## Facts

- Rivian is increasing initial production capacity at its Georgia manufacturing plant to 300k vehicles annually, a 50% jump from the original 200k estimate, to support its new midsize R2 platform.
- Vertical construction for Rivian's Georgia plant is expected to begin this spring, with vehicle production starting in late 2028.
- Rivian announced a partnership with Uber to build up to 50,000 robotaxis at the Georgia plant.
- Chinese supplier Yanfeng Automotive Interiors will lay off 153 workers at its Chattanooga, Tennessee, plant on June 29, ending a relationship dating to 2017 when it began supplying dashboards and door panels for VW’s Atlas SUV.
- Although the Yanfeng Chattanooga plant was expected to employ about 325 people at full production after a $55M investment, volume never reached that level.
- VW spokesperson Michael Lowder noted that the automaker periodically changes suppliers for specific components.
- VW spokesperson Michael Lowder confirmed that seating supplier Adient will stop supplying VW around July, resulting in the closure of Adient’s Athens, Tennessee, plant and 210 additional layoffs.
- VW recently ended ID.4 production in Chattanooga to focus on the gas-powered Atlas family.
- General Motors is investing roughly $1.4B across three US plants and one Canadian plant that produce gas engines, transmissions, and metal castings, reinforcing its combustion-engine capacity as EV demand softens.
- The GM investment includes $300M at Romulus, Michigan, for transmission production.
- The GM investment includes $150M at a Saginaw, Michigan, casting plant for engine parts.
- The GM investment includes $40M at a Toledo, Ohio, transmission plant.
- The GM investment includes $505M at an Ontario facility for the next-generation V-8 engine.
- Several major automakers booked roughly $2.3B in expected refunds from US tariff payments in Q1 following a February Supreme Court ruling that struck down some Trump administration tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA).
- Ford recorded $1.3B in tariff refunds; GM, $500M; Stellantis, $467M; and Mercedes-Benz, an unspecified amount.
- These refund bookings were made as accounting estimates, not cash received.
- Up to $166B in reimbursements is due to importers who paid IEEPA tariffs, with companies acknowledging uncertainty over timing.
- President Trump warned he would “remember” companies that opt for refunds.
- GM said tariffs will still cut $2.5B to $3.5B from its 2026 profits, while Ford put its net tariff cost at $1B.
- Foreign automakers have warned the Trump administration that they could pull their cheapest car models from the US market if USMCA is not renewed or is significantly weakened.
- The companies told Trump’s economic advisers that they may not be able to build and sell affordable cars for the US market without meaningfully reduced tariffs on cars and parts made in North America.
- The auto industry has been pressing for an extension of USMCA, calling it crucial to American auto production.
- Trump’s 25% Section 232 tariff on Mexican and Canadian automotive exports replaced the duty-free trade under the agreement.
- The three nations face a July 1 deadline for the USMCA trade deal review.
- The Atlantic Council laid out three scenarios for USMCA’s July review: clean renewal with targeted modifications, no consensus leading to bilateral deals, or no consensus and no replacement.
- Each USMCA scenario carries distinct supply chain consequences: the bilateral scenario forces manufacturers to operate under dual compliance regimes, while the no-deal scenario reverts trade to pre-NAFTA tariff levels.
- The Atlantic Council analysis recommends supply chain teams treat the period before July as a planning window: rethink production footprints, strengthen US hubs, shift from just-in-time to inventory buffers, invest in dual sourcing, monitor supplier solvency, and run bill-of-materials audits to identify components that could lose preferred status.
- Companies with integrated North American platforms face the greatest exposure, particularly if Canadian upstream metals and subassemblies lose preferential access.
- Chinese automakers SAIC Motor and Hongqi are exploring production in Spain to bypass EU tariffs, with SAIC planning an EV factory for its MG brand and Hongqi negotiating to build vehicles at Stellantis’ Zaragoza plant through Leapmotor.
- Volkswagen CEO Oliver Blume told analysts that VW is exploring whether to bring its China-developed cars to Europe and to share its underused European factories with Chinese partners including SAIC, FAW, JAC, and Xpeng.
- Toyota Industries President Koichi Ito said Japanese suppliers are increasingly receiving two weeks’ notice that components will not be available, as the Iran conflict ripples through the auto industry’s supply base.
- Toyota supplier Denso projected $3.1B in operating profit for the fiscal year through March 2027, well below the $4B analyst estimate, and has factored in roughly $282M in profit hit from supply chain uncertainty.
- Toyoda Gosei warned that raw material disruptions could emerge as early as June, particularly thinners used in automotive paint, and has assumed 200k fewer vehicles in its production planning.
- Toyota Boshoku is now seeking short-term supply assurances from individual suppliers, as long-term commitments have become difficult to secure.
- Toyota Boshoku executives note that everything from door trims to seat urethane traces back to naphtha-derived resins.
