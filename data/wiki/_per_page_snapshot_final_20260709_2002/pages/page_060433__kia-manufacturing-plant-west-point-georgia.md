# Kia Manufacturing Plant (West Point, Georgia)

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2011-05-01

**Source:** [LaGrange/Troup County: Good Fortune - Georgia Trend Magazine](https://www.georgiatrend.com/2011/05/01/lagrange-troup-county-good-fortune/)  ·  `page_060433`

## Facts

- LaGrange and Troup County sit at the northern end of a 45-mile corridor anticipated to welcome up to 60,000 new residents, along with a wave of new industries and retail stores.
- Elected officials asked constituents to raise taxes on themselves to meet anticipated infrastructure and service demands during a recession.
- In the mid-2000s, local leaders commissioned Georgia Tech to produce a plan for enhancing services and infrastructure to sustain managed growth into the 21st century.
- The completed Georgia Tech plan was presented in summer 2009 and contains 10 goals and 50 strategies.
- Kay Durand serves as executive director of the Troup County Center for Strategic Planning (TCCSP), a nonprofit created to coordinate stakeholders across government, healthcare, education, commerce, and development authorities.
- The TCCSP board has approximately 12 members who meet monthly to coordinate efforts per the Georgia Tech study goals.
- Troup Works is an online program connecting the unemployed with job opportunities throughout the community and is growing monthly, according to Ricky Wolfe, chairman of the Troup County Board of Commissioners.
- Funding for the TCCSP project comes from its member organizations.
- In 2005, the Pentagon announced the expansion of Columbus's U.S. Army Post Fort Benning mission, bringing 30,000 troops, contractors, vendors, and families to the region (a 40-minute drive down I-185 from Troup County).
- In 2006, Kia announced a $1-billion manufacturing plant in West Point, Troup County, promising 30,000 jobs connected to vehicle production, suppliers, retailers, and attendant businesses.
- Jeff Lukken, mayor of LaGrange, noted that developers from Florida, Chicago, Maryland, the D.C. area, and across the Eastern U.S. arrived within an 18-month period around 2004.
- Local officials approved zoning permits for 11,500 new housing units at a time when LaGrange only had 11,200 houses.
- Developers cited LaGrange's location on the shores of West Point Lake, its position as one of only nine metro areas with an intersection of I-85 and I-185, and having more Fortune 500 companies per capita than any other city in the country.
- LaGrange and Troup County sit almost halfway between Atlanta and Columbus, near Macon, Birmingham, and Montgomery, which are among the region's five fastest-growing cities.
- Everything within Troup County lines is designated as a Tax Allocation District (TAD), which can cut months or years off project launches and ease financing by directing retail sales tax dollars to public coffers.
- In July 2010, Troup County voters approved the TAD issue.
- A developer secured TAD amenities for a 370-acre site along exits 13 and 14 on I-85 for a mixed-use development named LaGrange Station.
- LaGrange Station plans include 1.2 million square feet of retail space predicted to generate $400 million in annual sales when fully built out, potentially opening up to 1,200 new jobs and 550 part-time jobs.
- The TAD will use about $15 million in initial funds for road networks, infrastructure, and parks; a developer will float a bond paid off by increased property taxes over approximately 15 years, after which local governments retain all property taxes.
- Page Estes, president of the LaGrange-Troup County Chamber of Commerce, stated that residents were leaving the county to shop in Newnan, Columbus, and Auburn/Opelika, prompting chamber interest in the TAD to keep money local.
- After Kia's plant opened in 2009, focus groups with Kia executives revealed that new employees struggled to sell their previous homes to take jobs in Troup County.
- The rental market in West Central Georgia and East Alabama has been strong as commuters rent locally while maintaining mortgages on their former homes.
- Although Kia came online last year and Fort Benning hiring is growing, neither site has yet reached its full hiring stride.
- County unemployment remained at 11.5 percent in December 2010, above the statewide figure of 10.2 percent.
- Ricky Wolfe attributed the high unemployment rate to the loss of traditional jobs that Kia could not absorb, crediting Kia with preventing staggering unemployment.
- High-tech workforce skill requirements complicate employment efforts, and steady losses in the local textile industry have left many unskilled workers needing higher training levels.
- Wes Cochran reported there are 70-some-odd Andy Warhol works in LaGrange, claiming more per capita than any town in the country.
- The Callaway Foundation, founded by LaGrange native Fuller Callaway in 1943, is a $200-plus million nonprofit and key partner in downtown development.
- H. Speer Burdette III, president and general manager of the Callaway Foundation, stated the foundation invests approximately $9 million to $10 million annually and has spent over $25 million on downtown infrastructure and promotion.
- In 2000, the Callaway Foundation commissioned a study that led to the development of a 10-screen Carmike Theater downtown with a cupola resembling the old courthouse.
- The foundation purchased an old downtown department store, spent $5 million renovating it for a health club, restaurant, and gift shop, and transferred it to the Downtown Development Authority to generate rent revenue.
- Plans are underway to introduce loft apartments or condos in the downtown area.
- Top employers in Troup County include the Troup County School System, Kia Motors, West Georgia Health Systems, InterfaceFLOR, and the Walmart Distribution Center.
- Population data from 2008 shows Troup County at 64,233, LaGrange at 28,401, Hogansville at 2,912, and West Point at 2,644.
- Per capita income in 2007 was $28,320 for the county compared to $33,499 statewide.
