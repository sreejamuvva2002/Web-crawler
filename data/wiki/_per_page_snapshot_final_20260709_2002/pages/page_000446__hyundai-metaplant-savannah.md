# Hyundai Metaplant Savannah

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-08-02

**Source:** [Engineers needed: Hyundai searching for tech muscle for Savannah EV 'smart factory' - AOL](https://www.aol.com/finance/engineers-needed-hyundai-searching-tech-090100680.html)  ·  `page_000446`

## Facts

- Hyundai's electric vehicle and EV battery plant in Savannah does not open until early 2025.
- Hyundai is recruiting competitively for experienced engineers and leaning on young talent from colleges and universities to operate the region's first smart factory.
- Brent Stubbs, senior manager of learning and development for the Hyundai Metaplant, stated that operating the plant will require computer scientists and software engineers, necessitating relationship building for automation talent.
- Of the 8,100 employees Hyundai is looking to hire overall, the EV manufacturing plant will hire 2,600.
- Approximately 15% of the plant's hires will be professional engineers, according to Hyundai spokesperson Bianca Johnson.
- According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, job growth for industrial engineers (manufacturing engineers) will grow 10% between 2021 and 2031, and job growth in computing and information research will grow 21% for the same period.
- Savannah is home to few advanced manufacturing facilities, and talent is already in demand at existing manufacturers such as Gulfstream and JCB.
- Daniel Park, chief administration officer for the Hyundai Metaplant, noted there was no automotive industry in the area previously, prompting Hyundai to create opportunities and develop programs with technical colleges.
- Recruiting engineers from outside the region is more expensive due to relocation and other incentives, making a homegrown workforce a priority.
- Park stated Hyundai's priority is hiring from Savannah, Bulloch, Bryan, and Effingham counties, prioritizing good local candidates even if they must eventually hire from outside.
- Savannah-based engineering firm Thomas and Hutton has found success recruiting recent engineering school graduates to fill openings.
- Georgia Southern University is partnering with Hyundai to supply a pipeline of talented engineers and business professionals.
- Talks between Georgia Southern and Hyundai began in October, followed by an information session that same month to familiarize students with Hyundai's brand and introduce job opportunities.
- As of May, Hyundai has made nearly a dozen offers to Georgia Southern students.
- Glenn Gibney, associate vice president for career readiness at Georgia Southern, stated the goal is to make Hyundai's job easy.
- Georgia Southern recently announced two new engineering doctorates: a Ph.D. in applied computing and a Ph.D. in engineering.
- The proposed engineering Ph.D. will have concentrations in civil, electrical, advanced manufacturing, and mechanical engineering.
- The Ph.D. in applied computing will enable students to solve advanced technical problems in computing foundations, cybersecurity, and machine learning.
- Mohammad Davoud, a professor at Georgia Southern's Allen E. Paulson College, said faculty and staff planned these new Ph.D. programs for a decade anticipating the region's economic development needs.
- Hyundai is recruiting from other local universities including Mercer, Savannah State, University of Georgia, Georgia Tech, Georgia State, and Kennesaw State.
- Hyundai is also looking across the Savannah River at talent from Clemson and the University of South Carolina.
- Savannah-Chatham School Board Member Michael Johnson emphasized that workforce engagement needs to begin as early as pre-k to teach robotics and art deeply.
- Angie Lewis, executive director of the Office of College and Career Readiness for the Savannah school district, noted existing career pathways are preparing the workforce for high-level engineering jobs.
- Lewis stated computer science is huge because the cars being built are computerized, and the district offers computer science programs at each high school and middle school.
- In 2019, the Georgia General Assembly passed a law mandating a gradual increase in computer science offerings in middle and high schools.
- By the 2024-2025 school year, all high schools must offer a computer science course; middle and elementary schools are directed to offer instruction.
- The legislation aims to increase the number of computer science graduates across Georgia.
- In 2019, the Georgia Department of Education stated there were close to 20,000 open computing jobs in the state, but only 1,200 students graduated from Georgia colleges each year with computer science degrees.
- Savannah high schools offer different concentrations in computer science, including general computer science, cybersecurity, programming and networking, as well as gaming.
- At elementary schools, skills are taught at a much simpler level using an approach called "unplugged coding" that teaches basic coding concepts through games and puzzles.
- Stubbs noted the plant has a responsibility to partner with K-12 to plant seeds and build relationships, giving students opportunities to see and tour the facility.
- As programs become available at universities and colleges, that will impact recruiting at high schools and what young people aspire to.
