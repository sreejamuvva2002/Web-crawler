# State of Georgia

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-05-15

**Source:** [Georgia EV Rebates, Incentives & Tax Credits](https://qmerit.com/locations/georgia/)  ·  `page_000319`

## Facts

- Georgia no longer offers a tax credit for EV purchases or leases; it was ended by the legislature in 2016.
- Carroll Electric Membership Cooperative offers a Drive Free for a Year program providing a $30 monthly energy credit during the first year of EV ownership for purchasing a new battery electric vehicle, totaling $360 in savings.
- Snapping Shoals EMC offers a similar EV incentive for new purchases and leases with a $30 monthly credit intended to cover charging costs during the first year.
- Tri-County EMC residential customers qualify for the EV365 program, which provides a $30 monthly bill credit during the first year of EV ownership.
- EV owners in Georgia can use high occupancy vehicle (HOV) lanes on highways regardless of passenger count.
- With a PeachPass, EV owners can access high occupancy toll (HOT) lanes at no additional cost.
- Commercial entities registered to do business in Georgia can claim a state tax credit of up to $2,500 (up to 10%) to cover a portion of EV charger installation costs for Level 2 and DC fast charging stations.
- Businesses may qualify for the federal 30C commercial charging tax credit worth up to $100,000 per installed EV charging port, available until June 30, 2026.
- Georgia residents may qualify for the federal Alternative Fuel Vehicle Refueling Property Credit worth up to $1,000 to offset home EV charger installation costs.
- Twelve utilities in Georgia offer rebates on EV chargers to reduce installation costs by offsetting equipment and labor costs.
- Georgia Power Company (GPC) serves 2.5 million customers and offers residential customers up to $150 toward a Level 2 charger via website purchase or proof of purchase submission, with no enrollment in the GPC EV charging rate required for the rebate.
- Georgia Power commercial customers can claim rebates for installing EV charging stations worth up to $30,000 per project and up to $60,000 per year.
- Carroll EMC offers a $250 rebate for Level 2 EV charger installation, requiring enrollment in the Drive Free for a Year program for a total savings of $610.
- Cobb EMC residential customers qualify for a $250 rebate on new Energy Star-certified Level 2 chargers with connected features, which includes automatic enrollment in the utility's EV charging program.
- Cobb EMC offers a non-residential EV charger rebate program with grants ranging from $500 to $5,000 for publicly available charging projects.
- Coweta-Fayetteville EMC offers a $100 rebate toward a Level 2 charger for at-home charging.
- Diverse Power offers a $50 rebate per NIMA 14-30 or 14-50 outlet to help build EV-ready homes during new residential construction.
- GreyStone Power Corporation offers a $250 Georgia EV charger rebate for installing a Level 2 charger for at-home charging.
- Jackson EMC provides a $250 rebate toward a residential Level 2 charger upon completion of the utility's application form.
- Sawnee EMC residential customers receive a $200 rebate for Level 2 chargers, requiring sign-up for the plug-in vehicle rate for at least one year.
- Snapping Shoals EMC offers a $250 rebate on Level 2 chargers.
- Sumter EMC customers qualify for a $250 Level 2 charger rebate for home charging.
- Sumter EMC commercial customers qualify for a $500 rebate per charger valid for Level 2 and DC Fast charging.
- Tri-County EMC offers a $250 rebate on Level 2 EV chargers.
- The city of LaGrange is the only MEAG public power community offering a Georgia EV incentive, providing a $250 rebate toward a Level 2 charger for residential use.
- Nine Georgia utilities offer time-of-use programs that provide lower rates for shifting energy consumption to off-peak hours to save on EV charging.
- Georgia Power residential TOU plan charges $0.137 per kWh during on-peak hours (2 to 7 PM weekdays) and $0.0146 per kWh off-peak, with a demand charge of up to $11.70 per kWh.
- Georgia Power's Overnight Advantage plan waves demand charges in exchange for higher per-kWh rates: $0.286 on-peak, $0.098 off-peak, and $0.021 super-off-peak (11 PM to 7 AM).
- Georgia Power offers commercial time-of-use plans based on business size.
- Georgia Power medium business rates are $0.237 per kWh on-peak and $0.059 per kWh off-peak.
- Georgia Power small business rates are $0.236 per kWh on-peak and $0.1118 per kWh off-peak, dropping to $0.042 per kWh from October through May for consumption over 1,500 kWh.
- Coastal Electric Cooperative offers a TOU program with rates ranging from $0.20 per kWh on-peak to $0.05 per kWh super off-peak (10 PM to 6 AM).
- Cobb EMC residential NiteFlex program provides up to 400 kWh of free energy monthly between midnight and 6 AM, followed by $0.05 per kWh, plus an off-peak rate of $0.075 between 6 AM to 1 PM and 9 PM to midnight.
- Cobb EMC commercial customers can sign up for a pilot program for EV charging that reduces costs by around 33% through off-peak shifting.
- Coweta-Fayetteville EMC offers a TOU program with a super-off-peak rate as low as $0.065 per kWh, $0.095 per kWh off-peak, and $0.30 per kWh on-peak.
- Coweta-Fayetteville EMC uses a tiered system for commercial electric rates where higher energy usage lowers the per-kWh rate.
- Flint Energies EV rate includes a $35 monthly basic charge, super-off-peak rate of $0.05 per kWh (midnight to 6 AM), off-peak rate of $0.09 per kWh, a $25 first-year bill credit, and potential additional $8 monthly night charging rewards for eligible models.
- GreyStone Power Corporation offers an EV rate of $0.04 per kWh during super-off-peak hours (midnight to 7 AM), which is 80% cheaper than on-peak charging (2 to 8 PM summer).
- Jackson EMC offers residential EV rates on a case-by-case basis: $30 monthly flat service, $0.34 per kWh on-peak, $0.07 per kWh off-peak, and $0.06 per kWh super-off-peak.
- Jackson EMC commercial TOU program charges $49 monthly for single-phase or $77 for three-phase power, with rates of $0.34 per kWh on-peak and $0.15 per kWh off-peak.
- Sawnee EMC offers a standard TOU program with $0.0445 per kWh off-peak and $0.335 per kWh on-peak, plus a critical peak pricing program with a $0.95 per kWh critical peak rate.
- Sawnee EMC EV charging program requires a second meter and charges $0.04 per kWh for off-peak and $0.28 per kWh for on-peak charging.
- As of October 2025, Georgia has more than 2,200 public EV charging stations including over 1,800 Level 2 and 484 DC fast stations.
- Georgia has 76 Tesla Supercharger stations and 1,005 Tesla Supercharger ports.
- Public EV charging pricing varies by charging level and operator, generally costing more than at-home charging.
- Level 3/DC Fast charging at the Atlantic Station Parking Deck in Downtown Atlanta costs $0.30 per minute.
- Level 2 charging at the Honda dealership on Morrisson Parkway in Alpharetta costs $0.50 per hour for the first two hours and $3 per hour thereafter.
- Free EV charging stations are available at public facilities like municipal libraries, including a free Level 2 station deployed by the City of Blakely on Main Street.
- EV charging stations are located along Alternative Fuel Corridors such as I-85 and I-75, and in major cities like Atlanta, Savannah, and Athens.
- Tesla's Supercharger network locations are approximately 150 miles apart along I-75 and I-85.
- Georgia is offering home energy rebates funded through the Inflation Reduction Act, allocating $109.8 million for home efficiency rebates and $109.2 million for home electrification.
- Home electrification rebates cover appliance rebates, insulation and air sealing, heat pumps for space and water heating, and smart electrical panel upgrades.
