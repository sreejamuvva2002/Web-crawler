# Solvay Specialty Polymers USA

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Environmental Justice Problems and Solutions in Augusta – Science for Georgia](https://scienceforgeorgia.org/environmental-justice-problems-and-solutions-in-augusta/)  ·  `page_029414`

## Facts

- Solvay Specialty Polymers USA plans to shift operations to Augusta, GA, to produce PVDF, a plastic component used in lithium-ion batteries.
- Synesqo, the new parent company of Solvay Specialty Polymers, received a $178M grant from the U.S. Department of Energy for the facility.
- The proposed expansion will be located in Augusta's 30906 zip code, identified as one of the most vulnerable areas in the city.
- Solvay is currently one of the largest emitters of pollution in Augusta and ranks among the city’s top 5 polluters to air, water, and land by RSEI Hazard score.
- The facility discharges approximately 65,000lb of Chlorobenzene into the air every year, a chemical on the EPA’s substance priority list for human health threats.
- EPA reports indicate Solvay increased carbon emissions at its Augusta site by over 70% in the last decade.
- The planned expansion includes construction of a fossil-fuel powered boiler that could produce enough heat for about 13,410 homes per year, potentially generating around 50,000 tons of CO2.
- In June 2023, Solvay agreed to pay $393 million to the state of New Jersey to settle allegations of contaminating water sources with PFAS chemicals.
- Prior to 2010, Solvay discharged over 100,000 pounds of PFAS (PFNA) into New Jersey’s air and waterways.
- In 2013, the Delaware River was found to have a higher concentration of PFNA than anywhere else in the world due to Solvay's operations.
- Solvay has a documented history of withholding chemical toxicity data from regulators and the public, often citing trade secrets or proprietary information.
- The company discovered in 2006 that its PFOA chemical was toxic in animal studies but did not report findings to the EPA until 2012.
- Solvay found PFAS chemicals accumulating in workers' blood in 2011 but delayed reporting this data until 2019.
- In 2021, the Environmental Working Group petitioned the EPA, alleging Solvay violated federal law by withholding crucial health hazard information.
- The expansion aims to support the global transition to renewable energy and EV batteries, but local communities fear worsening environmental and health impacts in an already disadvantaged area.
- Experts like Martin Scheringer, a professor and renowned PFAS expert at ETH Zurich, argue that industries relying on PFAS require rigorous oversight due to their track record of long-term harm.
- The page recommends that before Solvay operates in Georgia, the city should establish independent third-party testing, increased public transparency for all findings, and enhanced regulatory enforcement placing the burden of proof on Solvay.
- Richmond County's Sci4Ga Burden Score is ranked as the 3rd worst, with 80% of its census tracts having stroke rates higher than the national average.
- Citizens in Augusta's highest-burden and lowest-income census tracts live on average 1-4 years less than an average Georgian.
- Augusta's environmental burdens are historically rooted in racial segregation, industrialization (textiles, paper manufacturing, chemical production), and urban planning decisions.
