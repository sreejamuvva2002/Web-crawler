# Freyr Battery

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-11-11

**Source:** [Norway’s Freyr to Build $2.6B Battery Plant in Newnan  - Global Atlanta](https://www.globalatlanta.com/norways-freyr-to-build-2-6b-battery-plant-in-newnan/)  ·  `page_049995`

## Facts

- Norway’s Freyr is a publicly traded company with Norwegian roots.
- Freyr is set to build a nearly $2.6 billion battery plant in Newnan, Georgia.
- The plant aims to meet an anticipated spike in demand for energy-storage systems in North America.
- The company plans to spend $1.7 billion on the first phase of its “Giga America” factory.
- Later expansions could include solar parks with storage modules and materials processing, bringing the total investment to $2.57 billion.
- The factory will focus first on battery energy storage solutions, which are seen as key to the growth of wind and solar energy in the United States.
- Executives say Freyr is receiving consistent inquiries from electric vehicle manufacturers.
- Freyr has committed to hiring 723 people in Coweta County.
- The announcement follows the Hyundai EV and battery plant groundbreaking near Savannah, SK Battery’s success in Commerce, and a planned Rivian truck and SUV facility in Covington.
- The company touted “robust incentives” from the state and Coweta County without outlining details in its news release.
- Freyr CEO Tom Jensen stated at a Friday launch event that people would be the company’s most vital resource.
- Jensen invited local firms to partner with Freyr as it moves into the engineering phase for the plant.
- Mr. Jensen said in a livestream: “We are a partnership-based company,” and emphasized strong, local connections, enabling the community, hiring locals and contractors, and strengthening the company’s core.
- John Daviston, chair of the Coweta County Industrial Development Authority, called Freyr a “perfect example on the leading edge of technology, innovation and caring about their people.”
- Freyr picked Coweta County from five finalist sites in the U.S. after weeding out contenders from an initial pool of more than 150 locations.
- The company waited to announce the location until the passage of the Inflation Reduction Act to qualify for production tax credits under the federal package.
- Freyr will apply for U.S. Department of Energy funds including grants or loan assistance, perhaps under the bipartisan infrastructure bill.
- A 368-acre site in Newnan’s Bridgeport Industrial Park has been purchased for the 34 gigawatt-hour plant.
- The Georgia facility is one node in a broader global plan to build 200 gigawatt hours of capacity by 2030.
- Freyr seeks to fulfill its mission of “accelerating the decarbonization of global transportation and energy storage systems.”
- U.S. plans will be carried out through a $70 million joint venture with Koch Strategic Platforms.
- The Koch conglomerate’s venture arm has invested $50 million, with Freyr contributing $20 million.
- Freyr was founded in 2019 after Swedish battery company Northvolt proved that building massive factories close to clean energy sources could be commercially viable.
- Mr. Jensen noted that the company tapped into Norway’s deep pool of energy-industry expertise and recruited top minds from 30 countries.
- Freyr is technically domiciled in Luxembourg but keeps most intellectual capital in Norway, where it is building its initial Giga Arctic factory in Mo i Rana with strong government support.
- A single test production line called the Customer Qualification Plant will go into operation just after Christmas 2022.
- Processes refined at the test line will be replicated on eight production lines planned for Giga Arctic, which will serve as a model for the Georgia facility with similar size and design.
- Freyr is not yet generating sales, posting only a paper gain of $4.7 million in Q2 after a loss of more than $33 million in Q1 of 2022.
- The company has $488 million of cash on hand and a $1.6 billion market capitalization after going public via a SPAC merger with Alussa Energy in 2021.
- By 2028, with both planned gigafactories built, Freyr projects generating $1.6 billion in free cash flow annually on $5.4 billion in revenues.
- Freyr has secured agreements with massive global companies to secure financing, raw materials, and eventual purchase orders.
- Taiwan-based Aleees is planning to build a lithium-iron phosphate plant in the Nordic region, from which Freyr will purchase supplies and license technology.
- Freyr’s operating premise is that conventional lithium-ion batteries and renewable energy technologies don’t do enough or aren’t building quickly enough to solve the climate crisis.
- Mr. Jensen explained that if batteries are charged or produced with non-renewable energy, it just moves the point of emission rather than solving it.
- Without ample storage, intermittent solar and wind power production can waste energy and create mismatches between supply and demand.
- Mr. Jensen stated that to stay on track with climate commitments by 2030, including electrifying transportation, the U.S. and Europe need a 34-fold increase in energy storage capacity.
- He said the opportunity is enormous for coming to the United States as it advances its energy transition.
- Freyr uses what it calls “the Norway advantage,” leveraging nearly all clean, cheap hydro power and huge offshore wind investments to make battery cells cleanly at an economically viable scale.
- The company established a technology office in Boston where it is licensing and scaling 24M semi-solid battery tech developed at MIT.
- Tom Rosseland, honorary consul for Sweden and Norway in Atlanta, praised Norway’s strides in electric mobility and energy storage markets.
- Mr. Rosseland stated that Norway is a leader in electrifying passenger transportation infrastructure and expanding that knowhow to port operations and other sectors.
- He expressed looking forward to seeing Georgia and Freyr expand the ESS and EV markets for Georgia and North America.
- Mr. Jensen noted the Inflation Reduction Act should make energy cheaper by running on wind and sun.
- Freyr is named after a Norse god representing prosperity, sunshine, peace and virility.
- Mr. Jensen remarked during his speech: “Here we are giving birth to our first gigafactory in the United States.”
