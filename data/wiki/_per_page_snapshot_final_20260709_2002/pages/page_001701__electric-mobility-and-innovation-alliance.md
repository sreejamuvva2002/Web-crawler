# Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2021-07-26

**Source:** [Archives |](https://capitol-beat.org/2021/07/page/2/)  ·  `page_001701`

## Facts

- Kia Motors America dropped “Motors” from its name and is now known simply as “Kia America.”
- The Korean automaker has a $25 billion plan to transition to electric vehicles, which prompted the rebranding.
- Kia operates an assembly plant in Troup County producing 340,000 vehicles a year.
- Gov. Brian Kemp announced July 20 a new statewide initiative aimed at strengthening Georgia’s status as a leader in the electric mobility industry.
- The Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance (EMIA) will bring industry executives together with state leaders in government, education, utilities and nonprofits to build up the industry.
- Gov. Brian Kemp stated that Georgia has a proven track record of investing early in the resources and infrastructure needed to connect it to the world and develop jobs of the future.
- Kemp said the Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance will ensure the state is positioned to continue leading the nation in the rapidly growing electric mobility industry.
- Georgia already has two SK Innovation electric vehicle battery manufacturing facilities in Jackson County, representing a nearly $2.6 billion investment by the South Korean company.
- Dutch electric-vehicle charging company Heliox decided to base its North American headquarters in Atlanta.
- Turkish electric-vehicle parts manufacturer TEKLAS plans to open its first North American plant in Gordon County.
- German-owned GEDIA, a manufacturer of lightweight auto-body parts, plans to open a plant in Whitfield County.
- South Korea-based Duckyang, an electric mobility parts supplier, will build two plants near Braselton.
- Chris Clark, president and CEO of the Georgia Chamber of Commerce, cited projections showing electric vehicles in Georgia will balloon from just 7% of the market in 2025 to 30% by 2030, 58% by 2040 and 96% by 2050.
- Clark stated that Kia, SK and all these companies have to build out these vehicles and emphasized the desire for Georgia to do it.
- Pat Wilson, commissioner of the state Department of Economic Development, said 55,000 jobs in Georgia are connected in some form to the auto industry.
- Many of those 55,000 auto-connected jobs involve manufacturing vehicles with traditional internal-combustion engines in an industry that is rapidly moving to electric vehicles.
- Wilson stated that the state will probably see more change in the next 20 years in the auto industry than in the past 100 years and is focused on public policy to help drive that transition.
- Participants in the EMIA initiative will be divided into five committees that will set objectives in supply chain, infrastructure, workforce development, innovation and policy.
- The Carl Vinson Institute at the University of Georgia will host committee meetings for the initiative.
- The auto industry will be represented in the initiative by executives from Kia, Cox Automotive, Porsche and Sandy Springs-based Mercedes-Benz USA.
- Porsche has its North American headquarters in Hapeville.
- The initiative will also involve executives from Georgia Power and the state’s electric membership cooperatives (EMCs).
- The public sector will be represented by the state Department of Transportation and the Technical College System of Georgia, which will take part in discussions on workforce development.
- Wilson described the initiative as a true public-private partnership.
- What emerges from the initiative could come in the form of legislation for the General Assembly to consider, new policy initiatives or a combination of the two.
- Lawmakers previously approved a tax credit for purchasers of electric vehicles back in the 1990s when EVs were scarce, but eliminated it in 2015 as EV sales soared.
- Georgia Rep. Chuck Martin, R-Alpharetta, the 2015 bill’s chief sponsor, said the tax credit was mainly generating sales for the Nissan Leaf electric vehicle and wasn't helping drive innovation.
- Martin stated that the old tax credit was just subsidizing a purchase rather than driving innovation.
- Clark agreed a tax credit to stimulate sales of EVs is no longer needed but expects some form of tax credit proposal could come out of the new initiative.
- The expected new proposal will neither incentivize EV sales nor offer the jobs tax credit Georgia traditionally has provided industries the state hopes to attract.
- Clark stated that the old jobs tax credit is not going to move the needle for this new industry and economy, and the state will have to listen to the industry.
- Clark envisions new tax breaks that would incentivize gasoline stations to install charging stations for EVs or encourage homebuilders to put chargers in new houses.
- Martin said state government needs to be careful not to get ahead of the industry by pushing too aggressively for a transition to all electric vehicles.
- Martin stated that innovation will get the state there, but in some places there won’t be the electric grid or charging stations for that to happen.
- Wilson said it’s uncertain what solutions the committees will hit upon and emphasized wanting to go into this with a blank slate and really listen to the professionals.
- The EMIA committees will meet this fall with a goal of making recommendations in time for the 2022 General Assembly session starting in January.
