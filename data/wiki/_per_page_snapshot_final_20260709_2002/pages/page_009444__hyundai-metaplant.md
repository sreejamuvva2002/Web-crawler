# Hyundai Metaplant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-05

**Source:** [Immigration raid at Hyundai megaplant in Georgia leads to 475 arrests | CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2025/09/05/us/georgia-plant-ice-raid-hundreds-arrested-hnk)  ·  `page_009444`

## Facts

- Hundreds of federal officers raided the Hyundai Metaplant in Ellabell, Georgia, arresting 475 people.
- The site is located about 25 miles west of Savannah, Georgia.
- The majority of the arrested individuals are Korean nationals.
- All 475 people taken into custody were suspected of living and working illegally in the US.
- Some arrestees entered the US illegally, some had visa waivers prohibiting work, and some overstayed their visas.
- Several people tried to flee during the raid, including some who ran into a sewage pond on the premises.
- Agents used a boat to retrieve individuals from the water, and one individual swam under the boat trying to flip it over.
- Some of the arrested workers may have been contractors or subcontractors rather than direct employees.
- A Hyundai spokesperson stated he does not believe anyone arrested was a direct employee of Hyundai Motor Company.
- Hyundai is committed to full compliance with all laws and regulations, including employment verification requirements and immigration laws.
- Hyundai Motor North America Chief Manufacturing Officer Chris Susock will manage the megasite following the raid.
- An investigation will be conducted to verify that suppliers and subcontractors comply with laws.
- Hyundai is reviewing its processes to ensure contractors and subcontractors maintain high standards of legal compliance, including thorough vetting of employment practices.
- Hyundai has zero tolerance for those who do not follow the law.
- The sprawling 2,900-acre Hyundai Metaplant consists of two parts: a Hyundai electric vehicle manufacturing site and an EV battery plant that is a joint venture between Hyundai and LG.
- The raid halted construction of the EV battery plant.
- LG stated it is closely monitoring the situation, prioritizing the safety and well-being of employees and partners, and will fully cooperate with relevant authorities.
- The operation was part of a multi-month criminal investigation involving evidence development, interviews, document gathering, and judicial search warrants.
- ICE and Homeland Security Investigations executed the search warrant accompanied by the Georgia State Patrol, FBI, DEA, ATF, and other agencies.
- A search warrant sought records related to violations of conspiracy to conceal, harbor or shield people in the US illegally, including employment/recruitment records, correspondence with federal officials, and identification/immigration documents.
- The warrant identified four people specifically to be searched.
- No charges have been filed yet, meaning no wrongdoing is being accused at this time.
- A construction worker described the raid as a 'war zone' and said workers were lined up against a wall for about an hour before being processed.
- Agents asked each worker for their Social Security number and date of birth, and cleared workers received a paper stating 'clear to depart' to show at the gate when leaving.
- Another worker hid in an air duct to evade capture due to the heat.
- Republican Gov. Brian Kemp has touted the Hyundai Metaplant as a boon for the Georgia economy.
- In 2022, Hyundai announced an agreement with the state of Georgia to build its 'first dedicated fully electrified vehicle and battery manufacturing facilities in the United States' in Bryan County.
- The Metaplant was expected to create 8,500 jobs.
- Gov. Kemp noted that with the first 500 employees trained, it is a major milestone toward the full opening of the Hyundai Metaplant.
- Kemp's office stated Georgia will always enforce state and federal immigration laws and that the Department of Public Safety coordinated with ICE to provide support for the operation.
- A spokesperson for South Korea's Foreign Ministry said many Korean nationals were detained and urged special attention to ensure their legitimate rights and interests are not violated.
- Seoul conveyed concerns and regret through the US Embassy, urging protection of citizens' rights during US law enforcement actions.
- Georgia immigration attorney Charles Kuck stated two of his clients were detained after arriving from South Korea under a visa waiver program allowing travel for tourism or business for up to 90 days.
- One client arrived last week and the other several weeks ago; both were authorized to work under the visa waiver pursuing activities consistent with it.
- The detained clients are engineers who came to advise briefly on work as part of their assigned duties abroad and planned to return to South Korea shortly.
- James Rim, president of the Korean American Association of Southeast Georgia, said many Koreans working at the site are skilled construction workers or technicians.
