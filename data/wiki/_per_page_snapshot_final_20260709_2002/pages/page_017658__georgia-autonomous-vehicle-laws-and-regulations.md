## Facts

- Georgia has taken steps to enable the testing and deployment of autonomous vehicles (AVs) while balancing safety, liability, and public policy.
- In Georgia, an autonomous vehicle refers to a motor vehicle equipped with automated driving systems capable of performing some or all driving tasks with limited or no human intervention.
- The state distinguishes between vehicles that require a licensed driver in the operator’s seat and those that can operate with a remote or onboard autonomous system.
- For regulatory purposes, AVs are classed based on their level of autonomy, the presence of a safety operator, and their ability to handle driving tasks under varied conditions.
- Georgia has established channels to regulate the testing and deployment of AVs to ensure safety and accountability.
- Key regulatory elements typically include a formal testing program, mandatory reporting, and adherence to performance standards.
- Operators may need to obtain permission from relevant state agencies, such as the Department of Public Safety (DPS) or the Department of Transportation, before testing on public roads.
- The approval process often involves submitting safety plans, a description of testing routes, vehicle specifications, and a description of the remote or onboard control system.
- For deployment beyond testing, a broader authorization or waiver may be required to operate on public roadways without a traditional driver present in the vehicle.
- Georgia law generally requires AV operators to demonstrate financial responsibility to cover potential damages or injuries arising from autonomous driving operations.
- Operators typically must maintain appropriate automobile liability insurance coverage that extends to AV use and ensure that the vehicle and its operator meet registration requirements.
- Operators should verify the exact coverage limits, naming entities responsible for the autonomous system, and any self-insurance provisions that may apply for fleets or research programs.
- Clear documentation helps allocate liability between the vehicle owner, the operator, and the manufacturer in the event of an incident.
- Liability for AV operations in Georgia often hinges on causation and fault in the event of a crash or safety incident.
- The state emphasizes safety standards for automated systems, including robust testing data, safety case documentation, and post-incident reporting.
- Operators are expected to implement cybersecurity measures to protect vehicle software, sensors, and data streams from tampering.
- Safety evaluations typically cover perception, decision-making, and control modules, and may require ongoing monitoring for performance degradation.
- AV operations generate substantial data, including sensor feeds, mapping, localization, and vehicle health information.
- Georgia’s framework encourages responsible data handling, including privacy protections for individuals and transparent data-sharing practices with regulators and partners.
- Cybersecurity requirements are emphasized to safeguard systems against intrusion, spoofing, or manipulation of autonomous controls.
- Operators should implement encryption, secure software update channels, and routine security audits to maintain integrity and protect consumer trust.
- In addition to state-level rules, Georgia cities and counties may impose local ordinances governing AV operations, curbside usage, or rights-of-way access.
- Operators should consult municipal codes to determine permitted testing times, required signage, and any restrictions near schools, hospitals, or dense pedestrian zones.
- Coordinated efforts between state agencies and local governments promote smoother deployment and clearer expectations for operators.
- Georgia typically expects AV programs to meet higher technical standards, including redundancy in critical sensors, fail-safe mechanisms, and reliable remote operation capabilities when applicable.
- Vehicle manufacturers and operators should provide documentation on system architecture, sensor fusion algorithms, decision logic, and testing results.
- Regular maintenance and software updates are essential to maintain safety performance over the vehicle’s lifecycle.
- A clear change-management process helps ensure that system updates do not introduce new risks.
- Best practices for AV testing in Georgia include establishing a transparent testing plan, maintaining an incident log, and sharing anonymized performance data with regulators.
