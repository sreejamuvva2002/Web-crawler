# Rivian Automotive

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-04-20

**Source:** [Rivian electric car plant blasted by foes at Georgia meeting | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/technology-business-environment-georgia-atlanta-4deaa001f0ffdcb52aa9a785d383a9ea?email=83c09028683c0388df3dfdd0ffad2af3a45ab796&emaila=bc5b4a354cd4a1e0ffc96177bdd09020&emailb=f0a233dfa02cfee25332ff597b067d0eaebd1d47eec4138197465c806882f4bd)  ·  `page_059172`

## Facts

- Opponents are trying to derail a $5 billion, 7,500-job electric truck plant in Georgia.
- A state meeting was held to gather suggestions on how to design the plant to mitigate any impact on the environment.
- The state assumed oversight over the Rivian Automotive project after opponents overwhelmed Morgan County planning and zoning officials.
- The plant was announced by the company and Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp in December.
- The plant is the biggest single industrial project in state history.
- The first meeting of one of the oversight committees was held Monday in the city of Monroe.
- The Irvine, California-based electric vehicle manufacturer announced last year that it would build the facility on a 2,000-acre (809-hectare) site in Morgan and Walton counties.
- The site is located about 45 miles (72 kilometers) east of Atlanta along Interstate 20.
- The plant plans to produce up to 400,000 vehicles a year.
- Rivian also operates a plant in Normal, Illinois.
- Rivian hopes to break ground as early as this summer and begin production in 2024.
- The state panel overseeing the project was led by John Eunice, deputy director for the state Environmental Protection Division.
- A hostile crowd gathered at Athens Technical College in Monroe for the meeting.
- Opposition to the plant has been heavy from Rutledge-area residents who say it will spoil their rural quality of life.
- Residents criticized the meeting as a sham, stating it is impossible to make meaningful suggestions without a plant design and claiming the state is only working to get the plant built.
- Pam Jones stated she saw her governor on TV announce the Rivian, 2,000-acre plant coming to Rutledge, Georgia as a done deal.
- Many speakers voiced concerns about possible well-water contamination, light pollution, and the disruption of wildlife habitats and farmland for heavy industry.
- Edwin Snell of Oconee County criticized officials for sitting on Rivian's side of the table instead of asking Rivian and Gov. Kemp to explain how the environmental project is a disaster.
- A Rivian executive was present via video conference but did not speak during the hearing.
- A Rivian spokesperson said the meeting was a valuable opportunity for the company to gather input.
- The company is committed to sharing details of their plans for the site once they are complete and meet its own high design and environmental standards.
- The plant has become a subject of contention in Georgia’s Republican primary for governor.
- Former U.S. Sen. David Perdue attacked Gov. Kemp for agreeing to the Rivian location without support from neighbors.
- John Eunice stated he does not know when Rivian will file for environmental permits needed to build the facility.
- The state Environmental Protection Division will take public comment on the permits.
- Monday’s meeting was the first of four planned for the site design and environmental committee.
- The state plans four meetings each with three other committees tasked with examining quality of life, workforce, and local business engagement issues.
