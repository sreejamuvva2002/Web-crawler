# U.S. Department of Energy (DOE)

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [DOE Technical Targets for Fuel Cell Systems and Stacks for Transportation Applications | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/fuels/doe-technical-targets-fuel-cell-systems-and-stacks-transportation-applications)  ·  `page_002885`

## Facts

- The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) established technical targets for integrated polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) fuel cell power systems and stacks operating on direct hydrogen for transportation applications.
- Targets were developed with input from the U.S. DRIVE Partnership, which includes automotive and energy companies, specifically the Fuel Cell Technical Team.
- Detailed target information is available in the Fuel Cells section of the DOE Fuel Cell Technologies Office's Multi-Year Research, Development, and Demonstration Plan.
- For 80-kWe (net) integrated transportation fuel cell power systems, the 2015 peak energy efficiency status was 60%, with a 2020 target of 65% and an ultimate target of 70%.
- Peak energy efficiency is defined as the ratio of DC output energy to the lower heating value of the input hydrogen fuel, occurring at less than 25% rated power.
- For integrated systems, the 2015 power density status was 640 W/L, targeting 650 W/L by 2020 and 850 W/L ultimately.
- Specific power for integrated systems stood at 659 W/kg in 2015, with 2020 and ultimate targets set at 650 W/kg.
- The cost target for integrated systems was $53/kWnet in 2015, reduced to $40/kWnet by 2020 and $30/kWnet ultimately, projected to high-volume production of 500,000 systems per year.
- Cold start-up time to 50% rated power at –20°C ambient temperature was 20 seconds in 2015, with 2020 and ultimate targets set at 30 seconds.
- Cold start-up time at +20°C ambient temperature was under 10 seconds in 2015, targeting 5 seconds for both 2020 and ultimate goals.
- Start-up and shutdown energy consumption from –20°C ambient temperature was 7.5 MJ in 2015, targeting 5 MJ for 2020 and ultimate phases.
- Start-up and shutdown energy consumption from +20°C ambient temperature targets 1 MJ for both 2020 and ultimate phases.
- Durability in an automotive drive cycle was projected at 3,900 hours in 2015, increasing to 5,000 hours by 2020 and 8,000 hours ultimately.
- Start-up/shutdown durability targets 5,000 cycles for both 2020 and ultimate phases.
- Assisted start from low temperatures targets –40°C for both 2020 and ultimate phases.
- Unassisted start from low temperatures was –30°C in 2015, maintaining a –30°C target for 2020 and ultimate phases.
- System targets explicitly exclude hydrogen storage, power electronics, and electric drive components.
- Cost projections are based on state-of-the-art components developed via the DOE Fuel Cells sub-program at the laboratory scale, noting that additional efforts are required to integrate components into complete automotive systems meeting real-world durability requirements.
- Cold start status values were averaged from data reported at the 2010 SAE World Congress, with a caveat that these systems may not meet other system-level targets.
- Durability metrics represent the average projected time to 10% voltage degradation for the highest durability fleet, based on real-world driving testing rather than simulated cycles.
- Catalyst loading was not reported in durability assessments and did not necessarily match the target value of 0.125 mgPGM/cm2.
- Durability targets must be met or exceeded at temperatures of 80°C up to peak temperature, based on testing protocols requiring less than a 10% drop in rated power after testing.
- Start-up/shutdown durability is measured per protocol with less than a 5% decrease in voltage at 1.2 A/cm2.
- An 8-hour soak at stated temperatures must not impact subsequent achievement of targets.
- Unassisted start status references Honda's FCX Concept vehicle demonstration (September 25, 2006) and Toyota's development of a new fuel cell hybrid (June 6, 2008).
- For 80-kWe transportation fuel cell stacks, the 2015 stack power density status was 3,000 W/L, targeting 2,250 W/L by 2020 and 2,500 W/L ultimately.
- Stack specific power remained at 2,000 W/kg across 2015, 2020, and ultimate phases.
- Performance at 0.8 V targets 300 mA/cm2 for both 2020 and ultimate phases.
- Stack cost targets were $26/kWnet in 2015, reduced to $20/kWnet by 2020 and $15/kWnet ultimately, projected to high-volume production of 500,000 stacks per year.
- Stack durability in an automotive drive cycle mirrors system targets: 3,900 hours in 2015, 5,000 by 2020, and 8,000 ultimately.
- Stack start-up/shutdown durability also targets 5,000 cycles for 2020 and ultimate phases.
- The Q/ΔTi metric (heat transfer efficiency) was 1.9 kW/°C in 2015, targeting 1.45 kW/°C for 2020 and ultimate phases.
- Robustness metrics for cold operation, hot operation, and cold transient all target a ratio of 0.7 for 2020 and ultimate phases.
- Stack targets exclude hydrogen storage, power electronics, electric drive, and ancillary systems (thermal, water, and air management).
- Stacks operate on direct hydrogen and air at up to 150 kPa absolute inlet pressure.
- Power density refers to net power minus projected balance of plant power, with volume measured as box volume including dead space.
- Stack power density status references Toyota's environmental technology development press release (September 24, 2012).
- Stack specific power status references Nissan doubling power density with a new fuel cell stack (October 13, 2011).
- Q/ΔTi calculations assume 90 kW gross stack power for 80 kW net power, measured using polarization curve protocols with up to 40% relative humidity inlet humidification and maximum operating coolant outlet temperature.
- Robustness metrics are calculated as voltage ratios at specific temperatures (30°C, 80°C, 90°C) and dew points using polarization curve protocols.
