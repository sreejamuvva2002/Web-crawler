# Georgia EV Market and Policy Landscape

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2021-06-24

**Source:** [‘Electric vehicle revolution’ in Georgia arrives at crossroads - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2021/06/24/electric-vehicle-revolution-in-georgia-arrives-at-crossroads/)  ·  `page_060329`

## Facts

- Shelley Francis, a former medical school professor focused on environmental health who lives in Smyrna, co-founded EVHybridNoire to encourage Black and Latino drivers to adopt plug-in cars.
- EVHybridNoire has a diverse membership of drivers owning vehicles ranging from used 10-year-old Nissan LEAFs to Ford Mustang Mach-E and Porsche Taycan models.
- Georgia once led the nation in newly registered electric vehicles but now ranks 10th for the number of EVs on roadways.
- There are approximately 24,400 electric vehicles in Georgia, with about one-third located in Fulton County.
- Other top counties for EV adoption include DeKalb, Cobb, Gwinnett, and Forsyth, all within metro Atlanta.
- Electric vehicles make up just over 1% of all newly registered automobiles in Georgia, a percentage that has remained steady for the last three years.
- The state ended a generous $5,000 tax break for electric vehicles in 2015 and simultaneously created an annual EV fee.
- The current annual EV fee is set at nearly $214, which advocates argue exceeds what many motorists pay in fuel taxes annually.
- Georgia's gasoline tax is 28.7 cents per gallon, meaning a compact SUV owner would need to fill up weekly to match the annual fuel tax paid by an EV owner under the new fee structure.
- The EV fee was included in a 2015 transportation bill intended to fund repairs for the state's roads and bridges.
- Electric vehicle owners contributed $5 million in revenue to the state last year according to the Department of Revenue.
- Georgia is one of more than two dozen states with a special electric car fee, which range from $50 to $225 across different states.
- California charges a $100 EV fee while Florida does not have an annual fee for electric vehicles.
- Anne Blair, director of energy efficient transportation for the Southeast Energy Efficiency Alliance, stated that lawmakers were too quick to impose the fee and called it punitive during the industry's early adoption stage.
- Blair noted the fee acts as a deterrent particularly for lower-income individuals purchasing used EVs around $8,000.
- Shelley Francis of EVHybridNoire believes the cumulative costs of new technology, charging logistics, and the additional fee deter potential buyers.
- Legislative attempts to resurrect the tax credit or revisit the fee have failed over the years.
- State Rep. Chuck Martin (Alpharetta Republican) successfully pushed to lower the fee for low-speed vehicles like golf carts this session.
- Martin drives a hybrid car and agrees the state's EV fee is too high, advocating it should correlate more closely with gas taxes for comparable models.
- Martin previously pushed to end the tax credit but now supports a smaller, revamped incentive to help people afford a range of EV models.
- Some buyers previously used Georgia's tax credit to lease a Nissan LEAF for virtually nothing.
- Martin introduced a bill this session to allow more electric vehicle manufacturers to sell directly to consumers in Georgia, similar to Tesla's model.
- The direct-to-consumer bill has bipartisan co-signers and received a hearing but faced strong opposition from auto dealers concerned about undermining the franchise system.
- Ben Jordan, lobbyist for the Georgia Automobile Dealers Association, told lawmakers that dealers thrive on competition rather than fearing it.
- Steve Ewing, president and CEO of Wade Ford in Smyrna, argued that protecting dealership jobs is necessary to compete with manufacturer-owned stores.
- Martin clarified his bill would only apply to zero-emission manufacturers without existing Georgia dealer connections, such as Rivian and Lucid, not established brands like Ford.
- A Lucid representative told lawmakers that over 83% of electric vehicles purchased in Georgia last year were bought directly from manufacturers.
- EV supporters argue the proposal would allow consumers to interact with specialists and avoid driving out of state to purchase from new manufacturers.
- The bill remains active for next year, with Martin planning to limit it to metro Atlanta's seven-county nonattainment zone due to population density and air quality standards.
- Sen. Elena Parent (Atlanta Democrat) unsuccessfully tried to eliminate the EV fee and restore the tax credit but believes interested buyers will switch anyway due to fuel and maintenance savings.
- Parent cited last month's Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack as evidence of the benefits of reducing reliance on the gasoline network.
- Range anxiety remains a deterrent for long trips, though newer models like the Ford F150 Lightning are expected to reach up to 300 miles.
- Most day-to-day EV charging occurs at home, and Parent emphasized that charging infrastructure is critical until drivers feel confident on long trips.
- Georgia Power included charging station costs in its recent rate case before the Public Service Commission.
- The commission approved $24 million for two programs to reduce business charger installation costs and fund public fast-charging stations, with 50 already installed statewide.
- Solar-powered charging stations were installed at Atlanta Technical College and Valdosta State University.
- Georgia Power spokeswoman Allison Erdman Gregoire stated Georgia is already the sixth largest market in the nation for EV charging.
- President Biden is pushing to include funding for EV charging infrastructure in his jobs plan, with Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg visiting Atlanta last month to champion it.
- Buttigieg stated the infrastructure plan would trigger an electric vehicle revolution made in America.
- U.S. Sen. Jon Ossoff told reporters during Buttigieg's visit that EVs are coming in force and surface transportation infrastructure must be prepared with adequate charging station density.
- A recent iteration of a federal infrastructure deal included about $10 billion for charging stations, funded by a fee on EV users, though some lawmakers have objected to the fee.
- Anne Blair stated federal investment could significantly accelerate America's shift to electric vehicles from public health, climate, and economic perspectives.
- A poll in Georgia's competitive 6th and 7th congressional districts showed bipartisan support for the American Jobs Plan among voters.
- About two-thirds of respondents supported investment in EVs and charging stations to reduce pollution and help Americans buy clean cars.
- Support for federal spending on roads, bridges, and public transit was higher at 83% in the 6th district and 82% in the 7th district.
- Jennette Gayer, director of Environment Georgia, noted that support for EV adoption measures is higher among young voters who are more excited about new technology.
