# Ascend Elements

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Battery recycling still isn’t easy. Just ask Ascend… | Canary Media](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/batteries/battery-recycling-ascend-elements)  ·  `page_052545`

## Facts

- The startup Ascend Elements has outlasted several rival battery recycling firms that ran out of money.
- It has operated a battery-disassembly plant near Atlanta since 2023.
- It is building a facility in Kentucky to turn ground-up batteries into useful ingredients for new units.
- The company filed for bankruptcy on April 9, with leadership citing “insurmountable” financial difficulties.
- Ascend Elements faced financial headwinds as buyers of recycled battery materials pushed back their timelines.
- The firm nixed plans to produce cathode active material after agreeing to give up a $164 million federal grant in 2025.
- The company lost another $110 million from a separate federal grant.
- President and CEO Linh Austin cited a “long history of fiscal and operational mismanagement” in a LinkedIn post.
- Leadership promised to use Chapter 11 proceedings to “reset and move forward,” not give up.
- The broader battery recycling industry is struggling to figure out what to do with old batteries piling up from the electric vehicle revolution.
- Early in the Biden administration, venture capitalists funded lithium-ion recycling startups aiming to retrieve nearly 100% of useful material from spent batteries and sell it back into the supply chain, but these entrepreneurs struggled to achieve that performance at commercial scale.
- The Trump administration killed electric vehicle incentives for consumers last year, prompting many U.S. automakers to lose their nerve and pivot away from assertive EV plans.
- Faltering American demand for EVs and batteries has rippled through the supply chain.
- Redwood Materials has been grinding up gigawatt-hours of used batteries at its campus outside Reno, Nevada, located down the road from a Tesla Gigafactory.
- Redwood Materials confirmed it will expand its flagship used-battery installation that powers modular data centers.
- Redwood Materials announced it will repurpose 100 Rivian packs to store energy for the EV company’s factory in Illinois.
- Georgia Public Service Commission approved a BYONCE program allowing tech companies and major power customers to directly pay for solar, battery, and other clean energy projects to keep data center boom costs off households.
