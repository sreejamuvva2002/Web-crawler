# Edwin I. Hatch Nuclear Power Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Edwin I. Hatch Nuclear Power Plant - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_I._Hatch_Nuclear_Power_Plant)  ·  `page_018353`

## Facts

- The Edwin I. Hatch Nuclear Power Plant is located near Baxley, Georgia, in Appling County on a 2,244-acre site.
- It operates two General Electric boiling water reactors (BWR-4 Mark 1) with a nameplate capacity of 1,759 MW and previously listed a combined capacity of 1,848 MW.
- Unit 1 has an operational capacity of 876 MW and Unit 2 has an operational capacity of 883 MW.
- The plant's thermal capacity is 2 x 2804 MWth.
- Cooling is provided by six mechanical draft cooling towers drawing from the Altamaha River.
- Construction began on Unit 1 on September 30, 1968 and on Unit 2 on February 1, 1972, with a total construction cost of $3.214 billion (2007 USD).
- Unit 1 went online in 1974 and was commissioned on December 31, 1975; Unit 2 went online in 1978 and was commissioned on September 5, 1979.
- In 2002, the Nuclear Regulatory Commission extended operating licenses for both reactors for an additional twenty years.
- In 2026, the plant was cleared to operate for 80 years, with Unit 1 licensed until August 2054 and Unit 2 until 2058.
- The plant is operated by Southern Nuclear Operating Company, a subsidiary of Southern Company.
- Georgia Power holds a 50.1% ownership stake in the plant.
- Oglethorpe Power Corporation holds a 30% ownership stake in the plant.
- The Municipal Electric Authority of Georgia holds a 17.7% ownership stake in the plant.
- Dalton Water & Light Sinking Fund Commission holds a 2.2% ownership stake in the plant.
- The plant was named for Edwin I. Hatch, who served as president of Georgia Power from 1963 to 1975 and chairman from 1975 to 1978.
- Annual net output reached 14,165 GWh in 2021, with production data provided monthly and annually from 2001 through 2026.
- Capacity factors were recorded at 94.30% in 2017 and 81.25% over its lifetime.
- Spent nuclear fuel is stored on-site in concrete casks, with the DOE estimating the plant has generated 1,446 metric tons of spent fuel.
- The NRC defines a plume exposure pathway emergency planning zone with a 10-mile radius and an ingestion pathway zone of about 50 miles around the facility.
- The 2010 U.S. population within 10 miles was 11,061, and within 50 miles was 424,741, with cities like Vidalia located 19 miles from the city center.
- An August 2010 NRC study estimated the annual risk of an earthquake intense enough to cause core damage at 1 in 454,545.
