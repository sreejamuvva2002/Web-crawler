# Kia Corporation

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-04-25

**Source:** [Mexican Engineers Hired by Kia, Hyundai in US for Jobs that ‘Didn’t Exist’ | Mexico News](https://www.bloomberglinea.com/english/mexican-engineers-hired-by-kia-hyundai-in-us-for-jobs-that-didnt-exist/)  ·  `page_010226`

## Facts

- Isidro Arellano logged 60-hour-plus weeks at a Kia assembly line in West Point, Georgia.
- Arellano is one of nine Mexican engineers who are plaintiffs in a federal racketeering lawsuit against KIA, an affiliate of South Korea-based Hyundai Motor Group, and Hyundai Mobis.
- The Mexican nationals allege they were lured to the companies’ assembly lines by Korean-led labor brokers dangling professional jobs that didn’t exist.
- Last year Georgia gave its largest incentive package ever, $1.8 billion, to Hyundai for an EV plant near Savannah, promising 8,100 direct jobs and thousands more from suppliers.
- Benjamin Botts, law director for Centro de los Derechos del Migrante, asked why companies engaged in deceptive practices in existing facilities while making large promises to the state.
- According to the lawsuit, staffing companies misled both the engineers and the US State Department by coaching them on how to obtain TN visas and providing employer letters detailing fictitious US jobs.
- The plaintiffs were among hundreds of Mexican nationals brought to West Point under the visa program by labor brokers under contract to supply workers to the automakers.
- Federal officials do not track TN visa holders in the US, making the exact number elusive.
- The plaintiffs ended up doing manual labor for less pay and longer hours than their domestic counterparts, the suit alleges.
- Kia and Hyundai MOBIS both strongly deny the lawsuit allegations.
- Georgia Governor Brian Kemp set a torrid pace of recruiting EV projects, making promised jobs a centerpiece of his 2022 reelection campaign.
- The ceremonial groundbreaking for the $5.5 billion Hyundai EV plant came weeks before Governor Kemp's November victory.
- Since October, Kemp has announced 1,500 jobs from Hyundai MOBIS; 630 from Joon Georgia Inc., a subsidiary of Ajin USA; 740 from Seoyon E-HWA and 456 from Ecoplastic.
- There are an additional 3,500 new jobs at a battery manufacturing plant, a joint venture from Hyundai Motor Group and SK On, a spinoff of Seoul-based SK Innovations.
- In March, Kemp announced 400 more jobs from a Hyundai supplier.
- Georgia’s economic development community announced more than 1,900 new jobs for hardworking Georgians in a single month, most related to the Hyundai plant.
- State economist Jeffrey Dorfman warned there may not be enough Georgians to fill the jobs.
- Georgia’s unemployment rate was 3.1% in March, consistently lower than the US average.
- Nationwide, unemployment in transportation manufacturing stood at 1.8% while the national average averaged 3.3%.
- Plants are located in rural areas with a far-flung labor pool and lack a dense, experienced manufacturing workforce around them.
- Legally hiring foreign line workers is difficult because there is not a manual labor visa for those jobs; non-agricultural manual labor visas are for temporary seasonal work, capped at 66,000 annually.
- The TN visa is granted by the US State Department, typically applied for by workers instead of companies, has no annual cap, and lacks worker protections such as work contracts or prevailing wage requirements.
- There have been more TN visa workers in the US than any other category of temporary visa holder every year except one since 2012.
- The TN visa is for Mexicans and Canadians in specific fields including engineers and scientific technicians, but no agency systematically checks to ensure they work in their specified jobs.
- According to the lawsuit, Mexican engineers learned of Georgia opportunities through LinkedIn, job fairs and universities, with a recruiting company coaching them through the application process.
- Luis Salazar, an engineer with seven years of experience, was supposed to be a quality control engineer at Hyundai MOBIS but ended up on an assembly line installing bumpers, suspension systems and speakers.
- Veronica Olan, an engineer for six years, was supposed to develop and improve operations processes but was put on a KIA assembly line tightening door screws and pouring antifreeze.
- Arellano was supposed to be a scientific production technician making $40,000 annually with two weeks of paid vacation and a year’s free housing, but ended up doing manual labor in a two-bedroom apartment with three other engineers.
- Neither state nor local officials would comment on the specific allegations or future implications for Georgia’s labor supply.
- Officials said they were taking steps to ensure an adequate workforce, including a labor shortage action plan from the Savannah Joint Development Authority, a state rural housing initiative and continued investment in training.
- Garrison Douglas, a spokesman for Kemp, said Georgia is leading the way in growing and developing its workforce to fill jobs created by Kia and Hyundai.
- An estimated 4 million manufacturing jobs will open over the decade, of which 2.1 million will remain unfilled, according to a 2021 report by Deloitte and the Manufacturing Institute.
- Laine Mears, a professor of automotive engineering at Clemson University in South Carolina, called labor among the biggest challenges for EV manufacturing.
- The lawsuit raises questions about automaker responsibility for labor violations when workers are recruited and paid by outside firms.
- When Alabama fined eight companies in Hyundai’s supply chain late last year for child labor violations, seven were third-party staffing companies.
- Third-party staffing companies have become a staple of the US manufacturing industry.
- The Obama administration broadened the definition of responsibility to include companies where violations occur along with third-party recruiters due to high coordination between them.
- Former President Donald Trump reversed the provision, and it was restored by President Joe Biden.
- David Weil, a Brandeis economist who headed the US Department of Labor’s Wage and Hours Division under Obama, noted similar problems arose with the J-1 visa program.
