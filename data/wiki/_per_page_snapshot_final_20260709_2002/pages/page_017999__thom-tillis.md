# Thom Tillis

**Type:** person  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [In N.C., all eyes on Senator Tillis as IRA tax credits… | Canary Media](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/clean-energy/ira-tax-credits-senator-tillis)  ·  `page_017999`

## Facts

- The U.S. House of Representatives has passed a measure to kill nearly every federal tax credit there is to support the clean energy transition.
- North Carolina was home to the fourth most solar capacity in the country last year, supporting over 7,000 jobs, according to the Solar Energy Industries Association.
- Vietnamese panel manufacturer Boviet Solar opened its first U.S. plant in Greenville, North Carolina, in April, with plans for expansion next year that would create over 1,300 local jobs.
- North Carolina is a leader in the emerging electric vehicle supply chain, with lithium mines, Toyota’s massive battery plant in Randolph County, and EV factories all in the works.
- North Carolina boasts over 109,000 clean energy jobs, the ninth most in the country, per the national nonprofit E2.
- The Inflation Reduction Act extended tax credits for large wind and solar projects and rooftop solar through 2032, then tapered them down over several years.
- The Inflation Reduction Act enacted new and expanded incentives for rooftop solar, EV purchases, and energy-efficiency improvements, set to end around the same time.
- The Inflation Reduction Act included an advanced manufacturing incentive designed to spur domestic production of minerals, battery components, and more.
- After the passage of the climate law in August 2022, E2 says 27 new projects were announced in North Carolina alone, representing an investment of over $21 billion.
- Tax credits for large clean energy projects will last until 2028 only for those that begin construction within 60 days of the bill’s passage.
- Developers must document that no component of their project is linked to a “Foreign Entity of Concern” such as China.
- This foreign component requirement applies to the advanced energy manufacturing tax credit, rendering it all but useless even though it is extended beyond 2028.
- The House bill abruptly eliminates at year’s end an array of tax credits for EVs and charging infrastructure.
- Termination of those incentives could kneecap North Carolina’s burgeoning EV sector.
- Removing these credits would pull the rug out from under the auto and aligned battery industries at a critical time, immediately putting North Carolina jobs at risk.
- Credits for rooftop solar and energy-efficiency improvements would be eliminated at the end of this year.
- Studies show the resulting spike in demand for gasoline and increased electricity use will raise costs for everyone.
- Senator Thom Tillis signed an April letter urging a targeted approach to reforming renewable energy tax incentives rather than a wholesale repeal.
- The four signatories alone could sink the House measure if they joined Senate Democrats in opposing it.
- The other signatories of Tillis’s April letter are Sens. Lisa Murkowski of Alaska, Jerry Moran of Kansas, and John Curtis of Utah.
- Repeals of energy credits providing a direct passthrough benefit to ratepayers would translate into immediate utility bill increases.
- A full-scale repeal of current credits could lead to significant disruptions for the American people and weaken the U.S. position as a global energy leader.
- A complete repeal would inject uncertainty into the market, jeopardizing capital allocation, long-term project planning, and job creation.
- Tillis told Politico he has a lot of work to do on the timeline and scope of the production and investment tax credits and that undoubtedly there will be changes.
- Polls show 72% of all voters in North Carolina, including 55% of Republicans, favor politicians who support policies that encourage renewable options.
- Only a quarter of North Carolina voters, including just under half of Republicans, agreed with President Trump’s plan to expand fossil fuel production while limiting renewable energy sources like wind and solar.
- The oil and gas sector has access to tax deductions worth up to 80% of drilling costs.
- Conservatives for Clean Energy covers six Southeast states, including North Carolina.
- 21 House Republicans went on record supporting clean energy tax incentives, but none voted against the final bill.
