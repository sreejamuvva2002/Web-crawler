## Facts

- The Oyakhire Lab studies and controls ion and electron transfer at solid-liquid interfaces in electrochemical systems.
- Interfacial charge-transfer processes determine the macroscopic performance of batteries, electrocatalytic cells, and related technologies.
- Using insights from charge transfer measurements, the lab designs next-generation energy storage and conversion devices.
- The lab utilizes hypothesis-informed materials such as thin films and small molecules using tunable methods.
- Research focuses on ion- and electron-transfer mechanisms to determine how these materials influence device properties such as efficiency.
- Material design is performed recursively using mechanistic insights to optimize performance.

- The lab studies and controls charge transfer at solid-liquid interfaces to advance batteries, electrocatalysis, and electrochemical separations.
- During charging, lithium- and sodium-metal batteries undergo electrically driven metal deposition and growth.
- Electrodeposition plays a central role in battery performance and safety but remains poorly understood.
- Using well-defined thin-film substrates, the lab elucidates key steps in metal growth within batteries to enable long-lasting, stable batteries.
- Recycling minerals from waste is essential for environmental stewardship and equitable resource distribution.
- Conventional approaches such as chemical leaching, hydrometallurgy, and pyrometallurgy are limited in selectivity and efficiency.
- The lab exploits precise control over electron transfer and the tunability of electrochemical free energy to recover valuable resources from complex waste mixtures.
- The electric double layer (EDL), solid electrolyte interphase (SEI), and cathode–electrolyte interphase (CEI) mediate dynamic charge-transfer processes in batteries and electrocatalytic devices.
- These interfaces have historically been measured using ex-situ, post-mortem techniques.
- The lab develops operando spectroscopy and acoustic sensing methods to provide real-time insights into interface evolution.
- These methods guide the design of passivation layers that enhance battery cycle life.
- In liquid cells, ion transport and electrode passivation are governed by the properties of small molecules.
- The vast design space of small molecules necessitates artificial-intelligence tools to identify optimal electrolytes.
- The lab develops Hypothesis-Informed, Physics-coupled Artificial Intelligence (HIP-AI) to design new molecules for batteries and resource-recovery cells.
- HIP-AI enables discovery of new molecules through filtering and generative capabilities.
- HIP-AI provides interpretability via physics-coupled algorithms.
- HIP-AI delivers experimentally actionable outputs by linking experimental design parameters to HIP-AI features.
