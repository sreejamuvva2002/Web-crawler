# Celina Mikolajczak

**Type:** person  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Battery Veteran's Hard Truths About US Manufacturing](https://www.batterytechonline.com/battery-manufacturing/-you-have-to-do-the-work-battery-industry-veteran-s-hard-truths-about-us-manufacturing)  ·  `page_009749`

## Facts

- Celina Mikolajczak draws on her experience leading engineering at both Tesla and Panasonic to explain why the US can succeed in battery manufacturing.
- During an October 7 panel at The Battery Show North America titled "The Commercial Compass: Navigating Advanced Technologies to Market Leadership," she stated that the US can lead in batteries but must decide to do it for real.
- A 10 gigawatt hour (GWh) battery factory costs about $2 billion to build.
- Research and development to commercialize battery manufacturing requires about a billion dollars.
- Constructing a dry room for battery production costs between $1,000 and $1,500 per square foot.
- A facility needs approximately 18 to 22 gigawatt hours of capacity to achieve profitability.
- Traditional venture capital models fail in battery manufacturing because investors will not fund projects requiring $1 billion and a 10-year timeline.
- Ordering equipment for a battery factory results in delivery after two and a half years.
- Commissioning battery factory equipment takes about one year after delivery.
- The total lead time from placing an equipment order to actually making product is three and a half years.
- During the COVID-19 pandemic, Panasonic's Gigafactory faced a crisis that became a proving ground for American manufacturing capability.
- The factory operated with approximately 300 Japanese advisors and expats working alongside about 4,000 local employees across all shifts.
- Japanese advisors typically stayed for 90 days to support a specific area before returning to Japan.
- A mentorship system running since 2016 had Japanese experts serve as teachers for American workers over a three-year period.
- When almost all Japanese advisors returned to Japan during the pandemic, the American team was forced to shut down the factory for two weeks.
- The cell factory had been running continuously for three years without shutdown and was designed to operate 24/7 indefinitely.
- Shutting down the factory took about 20 days, involving stopping production, halting cathode and electrolyte coating, and drying out the remaining lines.
- The American team successfully restarted the factory independently without Japanese advisors after being down for two weeks.
- Production during the restart was lower because customer Tesla was making fewer packs at the time.
- The team later completed a whole factory conversion for new technology on schedule and below budget.
- US staff achieved better defect rates than originally planned by planners in Japan during the factory conversion.
- Mikolajczak emphasized that becoming great cell makers in the US is entirely possible but requires doing the work and learning systematically.
- She compared learning battery manufacturing to learning math, noting that while some can self-teach, most need structured classes and homework.
- The US should invite teachers from China, Korea, or Japan to help with battery manufacturing, but American workers must still do the work.
- Battery factory teams face severe psychological challenges when yield rates drop to 50% and coating issues cause inter-departmental conflict.
- Mikolajczak advised that during grim periods with low yields, teams must keep working and cannot run away from the problem.
- Success requires determination and patience with capital, as Panasonic's leadership initially noted regarding the American team's struggles.
- Government demand acts as an industrial policy tool, and failing to deploy it has limited supply for national interests.
- Guaranteed government purchases can create a market floor that makes private investment more attractive.
- The path forward requires internalizing that battery manufacturing will be expensive and take time, with no shortcuts.
- Mikolajczak noted that Tesla made EVs commercial and real, and she personally drives a Model 3 and a Model S while owning four Powerwalls.
- When properly supported and given time to learn, American workers and engineers can match or exceed global standards in battery manufacturing.
- The core lesson is that capability exists but requires commitment, investment, systematic learning, and persistence through difficulties.
