# Georgia Department of Economic Development / Georgia Automotive Sector

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2018-05-01

**Source:** [Auto Evolution - Georgia Trend Magazine](https://www.georgiatrend.com/2018/05/01/auto-evolution/)  ·  `page_053341`

## Facts

- The automotive industry in Georgia represents $2.8 billion in gross state product.
- Since 1909, when the first automobile was assembled in the state, Kia Motors Manufacturing located there a hundred years later.
- In the last five years prior to publication, Georgia located 97 automotive companies creating 7,376 jobs.
- The state is home to more than 300 automotive-related facilities and six headquarters, including Cox Automotive, Genuine Parts Co., Mercedes-Benz USA LLC, Novelis, Exide Technologies, and Porsche Cars North America Inc.
- In January 2018, Groupe PSA, the French manufacturer of Peugeot and Citroën and the second-largest carmaker in Europe, announced it will open its North American headquarters in Atlanta.
- Automotive suppliers view Georgia as central to supplying manufacturing plants for BMW in South Carolina, Mercedes-Benz in Alabama, and Volkswagen in Tennessee.
- Toyota, Honda, and Hyundai have additional manufacturing operations in Alabama, while Volvo and Mercedes-Benz are located in South Carolina.
- Honda Precision Parts of Georgia builds transmissions in Tallapoosa.
- TD Automotive makes air conditioning compressors in Pendergrass.
- HL-A Co. (Honda) produces locks and related door accessories in Bremen.
- IMS Gear manufactures electronic brake and steering systems in Gainesville.
- voestal-pine Automotive Components forges fenders in White.
- Kumho Tire fabricates tires in Macon.
- Hitachi Automotive Systems is Georgia's largest metal equipment and components manufacturer, located in Monroe, and employs 1,200 people.
- Hitachi produces valve timing controls, drive shafts, and electric power steering for customers including Ford, GM, Honda, Hyundai, Nissan, Subaru, and Aston Martin.
- Hitachi celebrated its 20th anniversary in Walton County in July 2017.
- Hitachi operates over 590,000 square feet of manufacturing space on 70 acres with annual revenue exceeding $500 million.
- Hitachi credits Georgia's pro-business environment, transportation network, favorable tax strategy, lower land costs, and the Quick Start job training program for its success.
- Hitachi is the largest employer in Walton County and works with the local school system to provide career paths for non-college-bound students.
- In 2016, Hitachi received the "Community Impact Award" from the Georgia Department of Economic Development.
- West Point is home to Kia Motors Manufacturing, which transformed the former textile town into a job hub.
- Kia's suppliers in the area include Daesol Material Georgia LLC, Hyundai Dymos, and Sewon America.
- In December 2017, Daesol completed construction of a new 120,000-square-foot manufacturing facility in Northwest Harris Business Park, creating 110 jobs with a $35 million investment.
- Daesol manufactures automotive fiber padding at the Harris County facility.
- In 2014, Hyundai Dymos began manufacturing seats for Kia Sorentos at its West Point plant, creating 350 jobs with a $35 million investment.
- In 2008, Sewon America located operations in LaGrange, investing $300 million and growing its workforce from 360 in 2009 to 750.
- Kia operates on 2,200 acres, employs more than 3,000 workers, and produced its one-millionth vehicle at the West Point facility in 2017.
- Since Kia's arrival in 2009, more than 40 new businesses including restaurants and automotive suppliers have opened in the county.
- The city of West Point's population swells to 10,000 people a day due to Kia and its suppliers, compared to a 2016 resident population of 3,766.
- Georgia boasts more than 300,000 skilled production workers and a central location with access to international airports, ports, rail lines, and interstates.
- Georgia's culture of innovation dates back to 1935 when Asa Phillips invented a ventshade that became standard equipment on Volkswagen Beetles for 25 years.
- The state is home to two innovation centers and companies exploring connected cars, autonomous vehicle technologies, advanced diagnostics, and fuel efficiencies.
- Companies in Georgia's auto tech space include AT&T Drive Studio, Car360, Elan Technology, Verizon Connect, and Wheego Technologies.
- GM and Panasonic Automotive operate innovation centers in Atlanta and Peachtree City, respectively.
- GM announced plans for its Atlanta innovation center in 2012, starting with 1,000 employees and growing significantly since.
- Georgia's talent pool has spawned startups like Wheego, which develops self-driving/autonomous cars using artificial intelligence and machine learning.
- Another Georgia Tech-spawned startup is Parkmobile, which helps users find parking spaces and processed more than 50 million transactions in North America in 2017 before being acquired by BMW in January 2018.
- Fort Valley has been home to Blue Bird Corp., one of Georgia's two automotive manufacturers, since 1927.
- Blue Bird employs about 2,400 people (nearly all in Georgia) and posted $991 million in revenue for fiscal year 2017.
- In January 2018, Blue Bird moved its corporate headquarters to Macon to make room for a plant expansion.
- In July 2018, Blue Bird unveiled two new electric-powered school buses following a $4.4 million Department of Energy grant awarded in December 2016.
- Blue Bird built its first electric-powered bus in 1994 for a Southern California demonstration project and slated production of the latest iteration for 2018.
- Blue Bird launched its propane-powered school bus in 2012, delivering its 10,000th propane bus in 2017, with school districts saving $2,000 to $3,000 per bus annually compared to diesel.
- A school bus rolls off Blue Bird's Fort Valley production line every 13 minutes.
- The Ray is an 18-mile stretch of I-85 serving as a testing ground for new transportation, safety, and energy generation technologies with a goal of net zero environmental impact.
- The Visitor Information Center in West Point within The Ray hosts Georgia's first solar-powered PV4EV charging station and a tire safety check station that texts drivers their tire pressure.
- The visitor center features a 7,000-square-foot pollinator garden for honeybees, monarch butterflies, and other species.
- Adjacent to the visitor center is a 50-square-meter solar-paved highway using photovoltaic techniques to generate electricity while allowing traffic.
- The Ray's right-of-way is farmed with Kernza plants that enrich soil, retain water, and sequester carbon, alongside bioswales to capture particulate pollutants from runoff.
- The Ray is scheduled for complete repaving in 2019, with future iterations potentially featuring rubber roads made with recycled tires to reduce noise and increase durability.
