# VinFast

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-01-01

**Source:** [NC sues VinFast in effort to regain control of Chatham County megasite | WHQR](https://www.whqr.org/2026-05-21/nc-sues-vinfast-in-effort-to-regain-control-of-chatham-county-megasite)  ·  `page_000518`

## Facts

- North Carolina is suing to regain control of a nearly 2,000-acre Chatham County economic development site from VinFast.
- The lawsuit alleges VinFast cannot open its planned car manufacturing facility by July 2026.
- The lawsuit alleges VinFast cannot employ 1,750 people at the Moncure site by the end of this year.
- These deadlines are part of an option in the state's sales agreement for three parcels that make up the megasite.
- By failing to meet the deadlines, the state says it should be able to re-purchase the site.
- North Carolina officials point to a clause requiring vertical construction on buildings and structures to begin by Jan. 1, 2024.
- The state alleges VinFast has only graded the site and poured some concrete, with nothing built above ground.
- Attorney General Jeff Jackson stated VinFast agreed to build a factory and create jobs for North Carolinians but did neither.
- Attorney General Jeff Jackson noted that when North Carolina makes a deal, it builds in protection for taxpayers because VinFast broke the deal.
- Attorney General Jeff Jackson said the state is using that protection to find a new project for the site that will create jobs.
- VinFast has publicly stated it could open the factory by 2028, two years after the agreement deadline.
- On May 10, 2024, VinFast asked North Carolina for an extension on the 2026 target.
- During the May 10, 2024 meeting, VinFast cited a slowdown in global electric vehicle sales as making it difficult to secure financing.
- State officials told VinFast they could not change the agreed-upon timeline during the May 10, 2024 meeting.
- The lawsuit expresses skepticism that VinFast can meet its proposed 2028 opening target.
- Even if possible, building by 2028 falls outside the maximum 18-month cure period the state can grant for compliance.
- State officials allege VinFast cleared trees from the site in 2023 but stopped further work in December 2024.
- A site development agreement clause allows the state to claw back site prep funds if work halts for more than 12 consecutive months before 2032.
- The lawsuit states VinFast failed to take concrete action, abandoned work for over a year, and made zero progress on buildings or structures.
- The lawsuit states VinFast failed to create jobs and never provided photos of upright columns or vertical structures built into building footers.
- A request for comment sent to VinFast's general communications portal was not returned Thursday afternoon.
- VinFast was founded in 2017 and quickly looked to open a factory in the United States.
- The Chatham County factory was announced in 2022, with VinFast expecting to finish the first phase by 2024.
- At the time of the 2022 announcement, VinFast called it the largest economic development project in North Carolina history.
- VinFast planned to invest $4 billion and employ 7,500 people for the Chatham County project.
- The site was planned to feature a car factory, a bus manufacturing facility, and an electric vehicle battery production plant.
- VinFast's agreement required starting vertical construction on its first phase by January 1, 2024, which has not happened.
- When asked for proof of vertical construction, VinFast sent photos of concrete footers being poured or retaining walls instead of free-standing structures above ground.
- North Carolina officials argue that finishing a foundation for the facility would require hundreds more footers to be poured.
- The lawsuit states VinFast cannot begin vertical construction until it has completed the underground foundation of the building.
- The lawsuit states VinFast has yet to advance beyond the preliminary stage and has run out of time to do so.
- VinFast has never actually been issued a building permit for the facility.
- Chatham County was poised to issue a building permit on May 29, 2024, but required VinFast to inform the county which contractors would work on the site.
- VinFast never responded to the county's request to name contractors.
- The January 1, 2024 deadline is critical because a potential 18-month cure period under the agreement has already lapsed.
- In a March 6 building permit application for a foundation submitted to Chatham County, VinFast reduced the scope of the project.
- The March 6 application stated the facility would cover about 236 acres of the site and be built to employ 1,400 people.
- This projected employment is fewer than the 1,750 people VinFast's agreement requires it to employ at a manufacturing facility on the site by this year.
- Governor Josh Stein stated VinFast has not fulfilled its commitments and that today's action is about protecting taxpayers.
- Governor Josh Stein said the goal is getting the Chatham County mega-site back on the market to support future good-paying manufacturing jobs.
- The N.C. General Assembly appropriated $450 million of taxpayer dollars to the project for site preparation as part of the economic development package.
- The appropriation included up to $125 million to VinFast, up to $250 million to the N.C. Department of Transportation, and up to $75 million to the City of Sanford for water and sewer extensions.
- The site development agreement states that if construction halts for at least a year, the state can claw back any funds sent to VinFast.
- North Carolina alleges VinFast failed to do anything at the site for a full calendar year, allowing the state to claw back about $80 million sent for grading and site prep.
- Clayco, VinFast's previous contractor, terminated its contract on June 28, 2024, due to delays from VinFast and its failure to prove financing.
- In a lawsuit, Clayco officials alleged that while contracted for construction, the facility's design was only 50% complete in June 2024.
- VinFast terminated its agreement with construction company Barnhart in December 2024.
- In April 2025, VinFast told North Carolina that no work had been done since Barnhart left and said it was considering using the site as a supplier park instead of a manufacturing facility.
- On December 11, 2025, VinFast executives met with North Carolina officials about the lack of progress at the site.
- During the December 11, 2025 meeting, North Carolina told VinFast it was ready to begin discussions about triggering its option to purchase the megasite.
- VinFast officials pushed back during the December 11, 2025 meeting, saying they still planned to build a manufacturing facility on the site.
- Attorney General Jeff Jackson sent VinFast a letter on Jan. 6, 2026, formally informing the company that North Carolina believed it was in violation of the economic development agreement.
- In the Jan. 6, 2026 letter, Jackson told VinFast the state would seek to claw back $80 million if the company did not comply with the state's exercise of the option.
- VinFast officials pushed back in February, arguing work was ongoing at the site, including erosion control.
- VinFast officials argued in February that they were working to secure financing and sign new agreements with contractors for the project.
- Over ensuing months, VinFast made claims about the scope of the project, potential financing, and work being conducted.
- None of VinFast's subsequent claims satisfied North Carolina officials, particularly its insistence that it plans to finish construction in 2028.
- The lawsuit states VinFast repeatedly repudiated its agreements throughout its responses rather than assuring the state of its ability to fulfill them.
