# Hyundai

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-08

**Source:** [Two types of visas that didn’t keep South Korean Hyundai workers out of jail](https://san.com/cc/two-types-of-visas-that-didnt-keep-south-korean-hyundai-workers-out-of-jail/)  ·  `page_021122`

## Facts

- A Sept. 4 raid on Hyundai’s battery plant in Georgia resulted in 475 workers being placed in handcuffs, with more than 300 of them being South Korean.
- In 2022, Hyundai announced plans to build a $5.5 billion electric vehicle plant in Georgia.
- One year after Hyundai's announcement, LG announced it would invest billions to build electric batteries.
- Attorney Charles Kuck, representing more than a dozen detained workers, stated the individuals were brought from abroad to install products and oversee the internal building of the plant rather than perform jobs Americans can do.
- President Donald Trump stated the workers were in the country illegally and that the U.S. needs arrangements with countries like South Korea to train U.S. citizens for this work.
- Kuck argued that training Americans properly takes three to five years before authorization, whereas using a visa allowed since at least 1990 would allow Hyundai to complete plant installation quickly and hire U.S. workers within four years.
- The detained workers were on either ESTA B visas or B-1 visas.
- ESTA visas allow individuals from Visa Waiver Program countries, like South Korea, to enter the U.S. for up to 90 days for business or tourism without applying for a visa.
- A B-1 visa is roughly equivalent to an ESTA but applies to individuals from countries not on the Visa Waiver program.
- Immigration lawyer Rajiv Khanna noted that H-1B visas are annual lotteries with less than a 50% chance of winning, and H-2B visas require months of legal procedures.
- B1 and B2 visas are typically classified as tourists-slash-business visas rather than work visas for the United States.
- Kuck asserted that the B-1/ESTA visas were the correct and only choice for Hyundai to bring in specific equipment not manufactured in the U.S. to avoid import taxes and build the plant per Georgia's request.
- Law professor Kevin Johnson stated that Hyundai did not directly hire the workers, who were instead hired by labor contractors secured by Hyundai, likely preferred for their product knowledge, Korean language skills, and familiarity with Korean corporate operations.
- South Korea is reportedly working on chartering a plane to transport the detained workers out of a south Georgia jail back to South Korea.
- Johnson noted that government intervention to repatriate citizens is extraordinary but consistent with the Trump administration's stated effort to enforce workplace immigration laws.
- Hyundai has not publicly commented on the raid or the next steps at the plant.
- Johnson highlighted that the U.S. solicited investment under the previous administration to attract Hyundai to Georgia, citing economic benefits and job creation for U.S. workers as key factors.
- The warrant for the Sept. 4 raid originally targeted four Latino workers, and enforcement officials were unprepared for South Korean nationals, bringing no Korean interpreters and only expecting to arrest around 200 Latino workers.
- Officials made an on-the-spot decision to arrest all individuals present rather than vet each person's visa status and job duties individually before the raid.
- At least one detained individual has a reported criminal history, while none of the others have reported records at this time.
- Khanna observed that the administration shifted from targeting criminal aliens to establishing an arrest quota regardless of who is caught.
- Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem stated the raid was intended to send a message to other countries about employing workers legally and does not deter foreign investment.
- Noem encouraged companies coming to the U.S. to employ U.S. citizens and follow immigration laws when bringing people to work in the country.
- Department of Homeland Security data indicates that Asians have not been targeted as heavily as South Americans in immigration enforcement actions.
- Johnson compared the raid to ICE operations targeting Latinos in car washes and day labor pickup points, noting this case is different but aligns with the Trump administration's commitment to enforcing immigration laws.
- The situation involves South Korea, which Johnson described as very integral to U.S. efforts to navigate challenges in Asia involving China and other nations.
