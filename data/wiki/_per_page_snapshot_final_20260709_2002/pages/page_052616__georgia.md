# Georgia

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-04-19

**Source:** [The unlikely center of America's EV battery revolution](https://www.yahoo.com/lifestyle/unlikely-center-americas-ev-battery-194436514.html?guccounter=1)  ·  `page_052616`

## Facts

- The battery plant just north of Commerce, Ga. looms over Interstate 85 with sheer gray walls many stories high and a vast parking lot extending almost half a mile.
- Online employees complain about having to trek long distances to reach their posts at the Commerce facility.
- On a Friday afternoon in late March, dozens of workers lined up at the guardhouse to start shifts building batteries for vehicles like the Ford F-150 Lightning and the Volkswagen ID.4.
- The factory opened in early 2022.
- The plant employs over 2,600 people, which is about a third of Commerce's population.
- The battery plant is operated and owned by the U.S. wing of the South Korean company SK Group.
- The facility is part of a bonanza of electric vehicle investments transforming rural Georgia into a bustling "battery belt."
- Gov. Brian Kemp (R) aims to make Georgia the "electric mobility capital of America."
- Kemp and other Republicans do little to put more EVs on state roads or acknowledge the climate reasons behind the switch.
- Many major projects announced since President Biden signed the Inflation Reduction Act are landing in congressional districts represented by Republicans.
- The Inflation Reduction Act includes numerous clean energy incentives.
- Georgia is one of the biggest winners in the quest for clean energy jobs.
- Since 2020, Georgia has attracted 35 electric-vehicle-related projects and an estimated 27,400 jobs.
- With $15.27 billion pledged so far, Georgia leads the country in new clean energy investments.
- Battery and vehicle manufacturers, battery recycling plants, and auto parts suppliers have flocked to Southern states chasing expanded federal tax credits and generous state incentives.
- About an hour and a half southwest of Commerce, excavators are tearing up ground for Rivian's 20-million-square-foot, $5 billion factory.
- Rivian's factory reportedly will include a driving test track and an "adventure trail."
- Just 30 miles outside Savannah, Korean automaker Hyundai is building a "metaplant" that promises to produce 300,000 electric vehicles every year.
- Georgia Republican lawmakers haven't fully embraced electric vehicles; last month the state Senate passed a tax on public EV charging.
- About 90 minutes south of the SK factory, three workers heaved silver-plated lithium-ion battery modules onto a conveyor belt at Ascend Elements.
- The modules came from car companies and battery manufacturers around Georgia.
- A machine with long, rotating metal teeth crushes and tears the modules into small shreds of plastic, metal, and "black mass."
- Black mass consists of dustings of lithium, manganese, and other valuable rare earth elements that could be recycled into new batteries.
- The Ascend Elements battery recycling facility is newly opened and the largest of its kind in North America.
- At full capacity, the facility will be able to recycle 70,000 electric vehicle batteries per year.
- The process can recover up to 98 percent of the critical metals used in those batteries, including nickel, cobalt, manganese, and lithium.
- Recovered materials can be made into new battery cathodes by Ascend or sold back to battery manufacturers.
- Ascend came to Georgia largely because SK needed a recycler to process its battery scrap into new materials.
- Ascend executives also made a strategic decision to follow the growth of the battery belt and a jackpot of state incentives.
- "We wanted to geographically align ourselves with battery manufacturing," said Mike O'Cronley, Ascend's CEO.
- Companies like Toyota, Ford, and BMW have descended on South Carolina, Tennessee, and Kentucky investing billions in battery plants and EV manufacturing facilities.
- In Georgia, the Department of Economic Development helps companies find a site for their plants and guides them through the permitting process.
- Big players in Georgia can receive tax abatements and employee training.
- Rivian's factory near Rutledge, Ga. received an estimated $1.5 billion in tax abatements, funding for the factory, and employee training.
- The SK factory in Commerce pulled in around $300 million from state and county governments.
- Pat Wilson, commissioner of the Georgia economic development agency, said the state wants to be ready for any opportunity for job development with free land, ready-to-go permitting, and workforce training.
- Georgia Quick Start, a program affiliated with the Technical College System of Georgia, can rapidly spin up a training program to provide workers for newly opened factories.
- "While they are building their plant, we will in parallel find and train employees here in Georgia. So on day one, when they turn on their facility, they have employees," Wilson said. "That is our secret sauce."
- Quick Start has sent representatives to Korea to learn from Hyundai's training program.
- Almost all Southern states, including Georgia, Kentucky, Alabama, and Tennessee, are "right to work" states where employees can't be required to join a union as a condition of employment.
- Right-to-work states have gotten the lion's share of clean energy and EV money, including four of the six that have received the most new investments: Georgia, Tennessee, South Carolina, and Texas.
- Michigan recently repealed its right-to-work law.
- A study by researchers at Georgia Tech examining almost 20,000 collective bargaining agreements found that right-to-work laws are associated with lower wages among unionized employees.
- "The right-to-work aspect is compelling to a certain number of CEOs," said Mike Carr, partner at the consultancy Boundary Stone.
- With a tight labor market, Carr noted that competition for talent in Georgia will be pretty fierce.
- Even as Georgia becomes a hub for EV manufacturing, its lawmakers have been hesitant to fully embrace electric cars or mention how going electric could help curb global warming.
- In March, Georgia legislators approved a plan to tax electricity delivered by public chargers at the rate of 2.84 cents per kilowatt-hour.
- The tax is meant to compensate for lost revenue from the gas tax, but environmental and climate groups say it amounts to double-charging Georgians who drive electric cars.
- EV drivers in Georgia already pay a $211 registration fee to compensate for lost gas taxes, one of the highest such fees in the nation.
- Jennette Gayer, state director for Environment Georgia, said she worries the tax will dissuade people from switching to electric vehicles.
- "This extra tax is kind of galling because it's like, 'Wait, then what is this registration fee that we're paying?'" Gayer said.
- Georgia ranks 10th in the country with 34,000 fully electric vehicles registered, according to the Department of Energy.
- Gayer says Georgia's high EV ranking is largely because the state once had a $5,000 tax credit for electric cars that was discontinued in 2015.
- Other Southern states in the battery belt have almost no electric vehicles and similarly unfriendly policies.
- South Carolina has only around 7,000 EVs registered.
- Alabama and Kentucky have less than 5,000 EVs registered.
- South Carolina has been ranked one of the top 10 least EV-friendly states due to its paucity of charging stations.
- Tennessee will soon impose the highest registration fee for electric cars in the nation.
- Georgia is among a group of states that outlaw direct sales of vehicles, stymying companies like Tesla and Rivian that don't sell through dealerships.
- Tesla has received a carve-out to allow the company to operate five direct-to-consumer stores in the Atlanta area.
- Rivian will not be able to sell its vehicles in Georgia despite locating its second factory there.
- "If the products of the manufacturers you're attracting can't be readily sold or purchased in that state - well, you've got a challenge," said Stan Cross, electric transportation policy director at the Southern Alliance for Clean Energy.
- Gov. Kemp champions the shift to e-mobility but stops short of mentioning climate change or pushing residents to buy EVs, arguing Georgia is simply allowing the free market to operate.
- "He's not going to force anyone to get an EV," said Garrison Douglas, the governor's press secretary. "He's not looking to force the market in any one direction or another."
- State Sen. Steve Gooch (R), majority leader and co-sponsor of recent EV legislation, said he continues to be unsure whether electric cars are better for the environment than gas-powered ones.
- "I think the jury's still out on that," Gooch said.
- Gooch helped oversee a comprehensive report on electrifying Georgia's transportation system that stretches to 513 pages.
- The 513-page report mentions "climate change" only once.
- Democratic U.S. senators Jon Ossoff and Raphael G. Warnock point to the Inflation Reduction Act as the reason for the boom in battery manufacturing.
- The Inflation Reduction Act includes a generous tax credit for the production of battery components in the United States and a consumer tax credit for electric vehicles assembled in the country.
- According to Mike Carr, "It's hard to see even a third of these investments happening" without the IRA.
- State Republicans point to the generous incentives offered by the state itself as the driver for attracting top-level investments from folks like Rivian and Hyundai prior to the Inflation Reduction Act.
- Republican voters are more likely to live in areas where jobs are closely tied to carbon-emitting industries like auto manufacturing or coal-fired power generation.
- Democratic voters are more likely to live on the coasts and work at a desk in "knowledge-based" jobs.
- "If you get a growing number of people employed in the clean energy sector, that could increase support for climate policy in red and purple states," Oatley said.
- Former GOP senator David Perdue ran for governor against Kemp in 2022 and was a vocal critic of the Rivian plant, calling the carmaker a "woke California company."
- Perdue's anti-EV message failed to gain much support and he was soundly defeated in the primary.
- "If Georgians start to think of themselves as the heart of clean energy manufacturing - I don't know how you run against that and win," Carr said.
