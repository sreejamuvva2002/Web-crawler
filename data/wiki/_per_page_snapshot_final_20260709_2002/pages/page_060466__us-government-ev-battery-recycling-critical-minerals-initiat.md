# US Government EV Battery Recycling & Critical Minerals Initiative

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2022-06-01

**Source:** [EV battery recycling: As EV sales rise, recyclers are getting ready](https://www.energymonitor.ai/sectors/transport/as-ev-sales-are-rising-recyclers-are-getting-ready-for-spent-batteries/)  ·  `page_060466`

## Facts

- The US government announced in early May 2022 that it will provide more than $3bn to boost domestic production of electric vehicle (EV) batteries.
- The proposal was announced as the US car industry pivots to meet President Joe Biden’s goal for half of vehicle sales in the country to be electric by 2030.
- Experts warn of a gap between future production and demand for the critical minerals needed for EV batteries.
- Scaling up mining in the short term is difficult, as on average it takes 16.5 years to take a new mine from planning to production.
- Most minerals needed for EVs are produced in a handful of countries, making the supply chain vulnerable to price volatility and disruptions.
- The Biden administration announced a separate $60m programme for battery recycling in early May 2022.
- By reusing materials from end-of-life batteries, a secondary domestic supply chain independent of the mining supply chain could be created.
- US-based analysts Allied Market Research valued the global EV battery recycling market at around $139m in 2017 and expected it to grow to $2.27bn by 2025.
- The need for independence and security of supply became more urgent since Russia invaded Ukraine, causing halts to gas deliveries to Poland, Bulgaria, and Finland.
- A month before announcing the $3bn EV battery boost, Biden invoked the Defense Production Act to increase the mining of EV battery minerals.
- The Defense Production Act gives the government more access to funding to support mining, processing and recycling of critical minerals.
- According to the US Geological Survey, the country had a net import reliance of 100% for graphite and manganese in 2021, followed by 75% for cobalt, almost 50% for nickel and more than 25% for lithium.
- Shirley Meng, professor of molecular engineering at the University of Chicago and chief scientist at Argonne National Laboratory, stated that government funding can help bring more companies in North America to responsibly manufacture and recycle batteries.
- Shirley Meng emphasized that batteries should be made next to where consumers are and reused/recycled, noting how they are made and used makes a fundamental difference to their carbon footprint.
- US Secretary of Energy Jennifer Granholm stated in a 2 May press release that positioning the United States front and centre in meeting growing demand for advanced batteries boosts competitiveness and electrifies the transportation system.
- Jennifer Granholm added that President Biden’s historic investment in battery production and recycling will give the domestic supply chain the jolt it needs to become more secure and less reliant on other nations.
- The International Energy Agency (IEA) reported that clean energy technologies are becoming the fastest-growing segment of demand for critical minerals.
- The IEA estimates that in a world on track to meet Paris Agreement goals, demand for copper and rare earth elements will rise by more than 40% in the next two decades, more than 60% for nickel and cobalt, and almost 90% for lithium.
- Without a steady supply of critical minerals, the energy transition is at risk of slowing down.
- IEA executive director Fatih Birol stated in May 2021 that data shows a looming mismatch between strengthened climate ambitions and availability of essential critical minerals, warning that unaddressed vulnerabilities could make global progress toward a clean energy future slower and more costly.
- Recycling is well established globally for some metals like lead and iron, but not yet for many critical minerals due to the young EV market and current low prices of key minerals.
- Policymakers play an important role in encouraging investment in EV battery recycling now.
- The IEA stated that policies can play a pivotal role in preparing for rapid growth of waste volumes by incentivising recycling for products reaching the end of their operating lives.
- Increasing collection and sorting rates is a crucial starting point to scale up recycling, with government policies facilitating waste collection to ensure a sufficiently large waste stream to justify infrastructure investment.
- Governments can fund research into new recycling technologies and provide incentives for manufacturers to recycle.
- The European Commission recognizes the need for improved collection and recovery of valuable materials from batteries in its European Green Deal Circular Economy Action Plan.
- Securing access to secondary raw materials through recycling is a priority action for the European Battery Alliance.
- The US has allocated funds for recycling but has yet to target domestic recycling of lithium-ion batteries in regulation.
- Kunal Phalpher, chief strategy officer at lithium-ion recycling company Li-Cycle, stated that domestic manufacturing and recycling of lithium-ion batteries is critical to accelerating EV adoption.
- Li-Cycle was founded in 2016 in Canada and has expanded its recycling hubs to the US in recent years.
- Kunal Phalpher noted that continued commitment from the Biden administration to support EV battery manufacturing and recycling can help fuel growth and support sustainable electrification.
- With an average EV battery life of between one and two decades, there are not yet large numbers of lithium-ion batteries available for recycling.
- Large volumes of EV batteries will become ready for recycling from 2030 due to booming EV sales.
- One study indicates that the number of EVs sold in 2019 alone could result in 500,000 tonnes of battery waste when they reach their end of life.
- Scrap from battery manufacturing can already be recycled before end-of-life batteries become available.
- Manufacturing scrap is collected from a manufacturer, recycled, and battery-grade minerals are returned for use in new EV batteries.
- Kunal Phalpher explained that recycling scrap generated during battery production is an important component of the lithium-ion battery recycling industry and will ramp up as EV production keeps pace with demand.
- Almost all of the valuable material contained within manufacturing scrap and end-of-life batteries can be used to make new EV batteries.
- The US Department of Energy (DOE) reported that less than 5% of lithium-ion batteries were collected and recycled in 2019.
- The DOE estimates that recycled material could provide one-third of United States cathode material needs for lithium-ion batteries by 2030.
- The low recycling rate is explained by the limited supply chain for collecting, sorting, storing and transporting spent lithium-ion batteries, as well as the cost of recycling compared with the value of end-of-life batteries.
- Recycling company Redwood Materials was founded by JB Straubel, the former chief technology officer of Tesla.
- Redwood Materials states that batteries need an end-of-life solution to make EVs fully sustainable and affordable.
- Redwood Materials explained in an email to Energy Monitor that this means not just collecting and recycling batteries but also fully refining recovered metals and turning them back into precision battery materials to reuse.
- Recycling companies are teaming up with car and battery manufacturers.
- Redwood Materials is working with Ford.
- Panasonic will use recycled material from Redwood to make new batteries for Tesla.
- Glencore and Britishvolt teamed up to build a recycling plant for lithium-ion batteries in the UK.
- General Motors and LG partnered with Li-Cycle to process and return their scrap material.
- Li-ion batteries are more difficult to recycle than average batteries partly due to their design.
- Shirley Meng noted that traditional recycling methods designed for lead-acid batteries are not optimal for lithium-ion batteries, which operate near 4V using non-aqueous electrolytes, are often hermetically sealed, contain flammable materials, and require recycling of light and reactive lithium metal.
- Recyclers initially used high-temperature smelting to melt material, but it takes a lot of energy and does not recover all valuable materials.
- Li-Cycle uses a wet-chemistry process that first processes batteries by 'submerged shredding' to create a ‘black mass’ containing critical minerals.
- In another facility, battery-grade minerals are subsequently extracted from the black mass using Li-Cycle's method.
- This wet-chemistry method reduces the risk of batteries catching fire and recovers more valuable materials than smelting.
- Partnerships between car/battery manufacturers and recyclers can help improve EV battery recycling and move towards a circular economy.
- Rethinking how to design for recycling, such as making it easier to take batteries apart, would improve the sustainability of EVs beyond current priorities of durability and range.
- Li-Cycle and multinational Umicore state that technological advances mean around 95% of materials in an EV battery can already be recovered.
- Unlike plastic, there is no limit to how many times critical minerals in Li-ion batteries can be recycled without a loss of quality.
- Caroline Jacobs, media relations manager at Umicore, stated that indefinite recycling avoids unnecessary pressure on natural resources and mining.
- EV battery recycling could help solve mineral deficits long term and contribute to the overall sustainability of EVs.
- Getting to a circular economy for batteries requires investments and continued evolution of battery and recycling technologies.
- Government policies and funding are essential to establish an EV battery recycling supply chain now.
