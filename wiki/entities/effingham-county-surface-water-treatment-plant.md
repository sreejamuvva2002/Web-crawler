# Effingham County Surface Water Treatment Plant

**Type:** Facility  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[vehicle-manufacturing|Vehicle Manufacturing]]  ·  **Location:** Effingham County, Georgia, United States

## Overview

A new surface water treatment plant planned for Effingham County to provide up to 14 million gallons per day (expandable to 28 MGD) of treated water, funded by state loans and grants, supporting regional growth and the Hyundai electric-vehicle manufacturing complex.

## EV supply-chain relevance

Supplies critical water for Hyundai Motor Group's Metaplant America EV manufacturing complex in nearby Bryan County and future industrial development.

- **Effingham County Surface Water Treatment Plant** — Funded by $365 million in state loans and grants; will pull water from the Savannah River at Georgia Power’s Plant McIntosh property.
- **Savannah Industrial & Domestic Water Plant** — Built in 1945, last major upgrade in 1995; serves Savannah and regional customers.

## Investment

- **$365 million** (2024) — Funding for Effingham County surface water treatment plant construction and related infrastructure.
  - State loans and grants as part of a $501 million regional water package.
- **$146 million** (2025 (mid-year budget)) — Initial phase of upgrades to Savannah’s surface water treatment plant.
  - Expected completion over the next two years.

## Timeline

- **1930s** — Union Bag paper plant arrived in Savannah, prompting early large-scale water withdrawals.
- **1945** — Georgia General Assembly created the Industrial and Domestic Water Supply Commission to build a new filtration plant.
- **1947** — Savannah’s Industrial & Domestic water plant constructed, establishing regional water supply.
- **1995** — Last major upgrade of Savannah’s water treatment facility.
- **August 2024** — Savannah plant treated 57.5 million gallons in a single day, near its 58 MGD rating.
- **September 2024** — Consultant Jonathan Ladd (Black & Veatch) testified to Savannah City Council about capacity needs.
- **January 2024** — Effingham County Manager Tim Callanan noted the water-treatment facility idea surfaced in the county’s master plan.
- **April 2024** — Effingham County hired consulting firm GMC to conduct a feasibility study for the new plant.
- **November 2024** — Tim Callanan submitted a memo to the Environmental Protection Division outlining redundancy benefits of the proposed plant.
- **March 2025** — Georgia state legislature approved a $501 million water-infrastructure package, directing $365 million to Effingham’s plant and $146 million to Savannah upgrades.
- **2005** — Georgia Power closed the Riverside coal-fired plant, reducing river withdrawals.
- **2015** — Georgia Power closed the Kraft coal-fired plant, further freeing river capacity.
- **2019** — Georgia Power shut down its coal unit at Plant McIntosh in Effingham, decreasing cooling water demand on the Savannah River.

## Key facts

- Hyundai Motor Group’s Metaplant America EV manufacturing complex in Bryan County will need 4 million gallons of water per day when fully operational.
- Additional industrial and residential development in the region is expected to consume another 2.6 million gallons daily.
- Effingham County currently purchases 4 MGD of treated surface water from Savannah and operates two wells supplying about 370,000 gallons per day.
- The new Effingham plant will initially process 14 MGD, with potential expansion to 28 MGD.
- State funding totals $501 million for regional water infrastructure, split between $365 million for Effingham’s plant and $146 million for Savannah upgrades.
- Savannah aims to raise its surface-water plant capacity from 58 MGD to 65 MGD in the short term and ultimately to 100 MGD to meet long-term demand.
- The project is intended to provide redundancy, allowing either city to supply water to the other in emergencies.
- Environmental groups view the plan as a step toward reducing withdrawals from the Floridan Aquifer and protecting groundwater resources.
- Georgia Power’s coal-plant closures (Riverside 2005, Kraft 2015, Plant McIntosh 2019) freed up river water capacity for municipal use.
