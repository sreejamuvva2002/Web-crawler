# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-04-09

**Source:** [State funding reshapes Savannah-area water market - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2025/04/09/state-funding-reshapes-savannah-area-water-market/)  ·  `page_000376`

## Facts

- The push to increase the Savannah area's water supply grew from efforts to satisfy the demands of the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America site.
- The Bryan County electric vehicle manufacturing complex will need 4 million gallons of water a day when it’s fully up and running.
- Nearby industrial and residential developments are expected to guzzle another 2.6 million gallons daily.
- An on-site well covers Hyundai’s needs for its construction and launch.
- Wells in Bulloch County are expected to provide the water needed for the plant until more surface water is available.
- Bulloch was chosen because it’s the so-called green zone where additional groundwater use is not limited.
- The state capped or reduced the total amounts that can be withdrawn from the aquifer in Chatham, Bryan and part of Effingham counties.
- The Bulloch wells were initially planned to provide water to Hyundai for its first 25 years.
- Bulloch County residents objected over fears for the continued productivity of their own agricultural wells, and the timeline was shortened to 15 years.
- Savannah’s chief of water resources is Ron Feldner.
- In 1945 the General Assembly created the Industrial and Domestic Water Supply Commission to administer the construction of the water filtration plant in what was then Port Wentworth.
- Using water drawn from 10 miles away in the Abercorn Creek in Effingham County, the plant could treat 35 million gallons a day when it opened.
- The facility gave Savannah greater ability to supply not just its own needs, but also to sell water to smaller municipalities in the three-county area.
- City Manager Jay Melder told Savannah City Council at a workshop in the fall that the City of Savannah has been a regional water supplier at least since 1947 when the Industrial & Domestic water plant was constructed.
- Upgrades over the years have increased the Industrial & Domestic plant’s capacity to its current flow of 58 million gallons a day, or MGD.
- The last major upgrade to the facility was in 1995 and the aging facility is strained by increasing demand.
- One day last August, the plant treated 57.5 million gallons.
- Water infrastructure consultant Jonathan Ladd of Black & Veatch told Savannah City Council in September that the plant is rated for 58 MGD with not a whole lot of fluff left, and additional standby units are needed for routine maintenance or unexpected repairs.
- Savannah plans to increase the facility’s capacity to 65 MGD as quickly as possible.
- The planned upgrades include a new settling basin plus filtration system improvements at the water treatment plant as well as new water storage tanks there.
- At the water intake on Abercorn Creek, the city plans to install an oxygen injection system to meet state regulatory requirements.
- The governor’s mid-year budget authorized $146 million for Savannah’s water issues, enough to cover this initial phase of upgrades, which is expected to be completed over the next two years.
- The city ultimately expects to increase the capacity at its surface water plant to 100 million gallons per day to meet long-term demand.
- Effingham County currently buys 4 million gallons of water a day from Savannah, all of it treated surface water.
- Effingham also operates two wells that provide about 370,000 gallons a day.
- Cities and private systems in Effingham serve their customers with groundwater totaling almost 4 million gallons a day.
- The county population more than doubled over the last three decades to an estimated 73,000 last year, and growth continues.
- Effingham officials expect half of Hyundai’s more than 8,000 employees to reside in the county by 2035.
- Funded by $365 million in loans and grants from the state, Effingham plans a water treatment plant that can process 14 million gallons a day, and could eventually expand to 28 MGD.
- Effingham County Manager Tim Callanan said the idea for the water treatment facility surfaced in January 2024 when the county updated its water and sewer master plan.
- Callanan said they didn’t bring it up related to Hyundai, but looking regionally, it’s safer to have two intakes and one further away from the coast to avoid salt water.
- Effingham is planning to pull water directly from the Savannah River at Georgia Power’s Plant McIntosh property.
- That intake location is about 8 miles inland from Savannah’s intake on Abercorn Creek.
- In April 2024, the county hired a consultant, GMC, to do a feasibility study.
- Feldner said the city welcomed the partnership with Effingham County that evolved in early 2024.
- Callanan welcomed the chance to cooperate with Savannah, his county’s main water purveyor.
- In a November memo to state water regulators at the Environmental Protection Division, Callanan stressed the benefits of its proposed surface water treatment plant.
- He cited redundancy as a benefit, noting they could provide a backup water supply for the City of Savannah and vice versa, as well as potential to provide water to the western portion of Savannah and to Bulloch County.
- Environmentalists have been calling for better water planning.
- Savannah Riverkeeper Tonya Bonitatibus supports the Bryan, Effingham and Savannah collaboration but is uneasy about how long the good feelings will last.
- She criticized the governor for giving money to three different entities without any significant agreement on a regional entity to figure out how to get along, calling it a red flag for just throwing money at the problem.
- Ogeechee Riverkeeper Damon Mullis advocates for diverting surface water to industry to preserve more pristine aquifer water for drinking.
- He sees the plan and its $500 million in state funding as a good start and a foundational step in future regional water planning.
- The move should benefit the Floridan Aquifer by helping to wean industrial water users off it and allow it to begin recovering.
- The Savannah River provides drinking water to more than 60,000 retail customers in South Carolina’s Beaufort and Jasper counties.
- Its harbor is dredged to allow massive ships to call on the port, and it’s a waste receptacle for industrial pollution.
- Observers say the 313-mile-long Savannah can handle the additional withdrawals.
- The closing of Georgia Power’s coal-fired plants Riverside in 2005 and Kraft in 2015 removed almost 400 million gallons a day of demand on the river.
- In 2019, Georgia Power closed its coal unit at Plant McIntosh in Effingham, reducing its need to withdraw river water for cooling.
- The plant closures on the lower Savannah River give suppliers like Savannah and now Effingham more wiggle room to request new or expanded water withdrawal permits.
- Bonitatibus compared the water demand to one more straw in a camel’s back of an overloaded camel, warning that things will start breaking at some point.
- She pointed out that International Paper’s two facilities in Chatham County last year used more than 20 million gallons a day of water from the aquifer.
- Using aquifer water saves the paper mill money because it would have to pay to treat river water to a manufacturing standard, even if it had a permit.
- Bonitatibus questioned why industries should get access to that water when International Paper is the linchpin of the pollution source in the lower river.
