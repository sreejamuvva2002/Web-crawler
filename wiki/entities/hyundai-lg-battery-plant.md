# Hyundai-LG Battery Plant

**Type:** Investment Announcement  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[battery-cell-manufacturing|Battery Cell Manufacturing]]  ·  **Location:** Ellabell, Georgia, Bryan County, Georgia, United States

## Overview

Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solution announced a joint $4.3 billion investment to build a battery manufacturing facility in Ellabell, Georgia that will supply batteries for up to 300,000 electric vehicles per year.

## EV supply-chain relevance

Battery production for electric vehicles in Georgia

## Investment

- **$4.3 billion** (2024) — Construction of battery manufacturing plant
  - Investment split between Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solution
- **$5.5 billion** (2022) — Vehicle assembly plant and associated battery production in Ellabell
- **$12.6 billion** — Overall investment in the Georgia battery plant
  - Hyundai's total planned investment for the joint-venture battery facility.
- **$2.7 billion** (September 19, 2025) — Phase two expansion of the Georgia plant
  - Committed to expand the plant and create 3,000 new jobs.
- **$4.3 billion** — Build electric-vehicle battery manufacturing facility
  - Investment split between Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solution; aims to start production as early as late 2025.

- **3,000 jobs**

## Key facts

- The battery plant will create a strong foundation to lead the global EV transition.
- Georgia aims to become the EV capital of the nation, according to Governor Kemp.
- Auto-parts suppliers have pledged more than $2 billion and 4,800 jobs in the region surrounding the Hyundai site.
- Under the Inflation Reduction Act, EVs must be assembled in North America and a portion of battery parts/minerals must come from North America or a U.S. free-trade partner to qualify for the full $7,500 tax credit.
- Hyundai opposed excluding foreign-made vehicles from the tax credit, aligning with Governor Kemp’s position.
- The state of Georgia and local governments have pledged $1.8 billion in tax breaks-the largest subsidy package ever promised by a U.S. state to an automotive plant.
- The plant is described as a cornerstone of U.S. efforts to secure domestic EV supply chains.
- Construction will remain on hold until the first half of 2026, delaying production by several months.
- 475 workers were detained, including more than 300 South Korean nationals.
- ICE agents arrested workers without Korean interpreters; arrests were linked to a White House-imposed quota.
- The raid could discourage future foreign investment in U.S. battery manufacturing.
- Visa challenges for skilled foreign workers in the battery sector have been highlighted by the incident.
- Governor Kemp suggested the raid may ultimately lead to improvements in the visa system for South Korean employees.
- LGES North America President Bob Lee noted that difficulty obtaining visas has long been a challenge for companies like LG Energy Solution.
- The battery plant will create about 3,000 jobs as part of an overall 8,100-job Hyundai complex.
- Production capacity is initially enough to supply batteries for 300,000 electric vehicles per year, with potential expansion to support 500,000 vehicles annually.
- Georgia has pledged $1.8 billion in tax breaks and incentives for the project, the largest subsidy package ever promised by a U.S. state for an automotive plant.
- Auto parts suppliers have pledged more than $2 billion and 4,800 jobs in the region surrounding the Hyundai site.
- The project aligns with requirements of the Inflation Reduction Act, which mandates North-American assembly and component sourcing for full EV tax credits.
