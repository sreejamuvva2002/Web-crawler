# HL-GA Battery Company

**Type:** Company  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[battery-cell-manufacturing|Battery Cell Manufacturing]]  ·  **Location:** Ellabell, Georgia, Bryan County, Georgia, United States

## Overview

Federal agents executed a search warrant at HL-GA Battery Company, LLC in Ellabell, Georgia, uncovering hundreds of illegal workers and detaining over 400 individuals as part of Operation Low Voltage.

## EV supply-chain relevance

Battery manufacturing facility contributing to the electric-vehicle supply chain in Georgia.

## Key facts

- Hundreds of illegal workers were found working at the HL-GA Battery Company facility.
- Law enforcement identified over 475 people who were unlawfully working at the location.
- Over 400 agents participated in the operation and over 400 illegal workers were detained.
- "This was a significant undertaking with substantial results. Over 400 agents participated in this massive event, and over 400 illegal workers were identified and detained," said U.S. Attorney Margaret E. Heap, highlighting the goal to reduce illegal employment and protect unauthorized workers from exploitation.
- During the search, several individuals attempted to flee into a sewage pond; agents used a boat to retrieve them.
- The investigation was led by Homeland Security Investigations with assistance from ICE, ERO, FBI, ATF, DEA, IRS Criminal Investigations, U.S. Department of Labor, Office of Inspector General, USMS, and Georgia State Patrol.
- The case is part of Operation Take Back America, a nationwide DOJ initiative to combat illegal immigration and transnational criminal organizations.
- Approximately 500 agents from ICE, Homeland Security Investigations, Georgia State Patrol, FBI, ATF, DEA, and IRS participated in the raid.
- 475 individuals were detained; about 300 were South Korean nationals.
- Detained workers were held at an ICE detention centre in Folkston, Georgia.
- The raid prompted South Korea’s Foreign Ministry to investigate alleged human-rights violations.
- Some detained workers were employed by contractors and subcontractors rather than directly by Hyundai or LG ES.
- The abrupt pause to factory operations could create short-term problems for both the BESS industry and the EV industry.
- A market expert noted that the U.S. currently relies on foreign expertise to develop its battery industry in the short term.
- South Korean battery manufacturers produce 45% of all Li-ion batteries sold in the United States.
- The U.S. stationary storage market is expected to need around 50 GWh of batteries this year, growing to 200 GWh by 2028.
- South Korean firms are repurposing nearly completed EV cell factories (about 90% complete) for BESS production.
- Ultium Cells’ $2.3 billion upgrade, announced in 2021, will convert its Spring Hill plant to LFP cells with commercial production expected by late 2027.
- LG Energy Solution expects its new LFP lines to reach 17 GWh annual capacity by end-2025 and over 30 GWh the following year.
- Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) restrictions limiting Chinese equipment may benefit non-Chinese competitors such as South Korean manufacturers.
- SK On and L&F signed an MOU to supply LFP cathode materials for the North American market.
- Trip Tollison, president and CEO of the Savannah Economic Development Authority, said the U.S. relies on proprietary technology that must be installed by certain individuals from South Korea.
- Jose Munoz warned the raid would cause a two-to-three month delay in production and create staffing challenges.
- LG Energy Solution pledged to minimise impact on its business and resume essential personnel travel starting 13 October.
- Potential delays could reduce available BESS capacity by up to 70%, potentially driving up prices and delaying projects.
- Hyundai updated its expansion plans for the HMGMA campus, increasing projected EV output from 300,000 to 500,000 vehicles per year.
- HL-GA Battery Company is excited to call Bryan County home and drive the EV transition in America.
- Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solution committed an investment of more than $4.3 billion to establish HL-GA Battery Company.
- The battery cell manufacturing joint venture is strategically located outside beautiful Savannah, Georgia and is scheduled to start production early 2026.
- HL-GA Battery Company will supply next-generation battery technology to manufacturing facilities for production of Hyundai, Kia and Genesis EV models.
- The new facility will help create a stable supply of batteries in the region and allow for a quick response to global EV demand.
- HL-GA Battery is committed to creating a more sustainable future powered by American workers.
- It is one of the largest projects in Georgia's history.
