# Volvo Cars

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-06-04

**Source:** [Volvo’s little-used U.S. assembly plant is $1.4 billion migraine](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/volvo-little-used-u-assembly-173348450.html?fr=sycsrp_catchall)  ·  `page_014027`

## Facts

- Volvo Cars powered up a sprawling U.S. assembly plant in Ridgeville, S.C., in the summer of 2018 with a $1.4 billion investment.
- The 2.3 million-square-foot factory was designed to produce 150,000 vehicles annually.
- According to Automotive News Research & Data Center estimates, Volvo built only 20,000 vehicles at the plant last year, representing 13 percent of its capacity.
- Production of the S60 sedan ended a year ago, leaving production of just two electric crossovers: the Volvo EX90 and Polestar 3.
- Analyst Jeff Schuster stated that auto assembly plants require a utilization rate of at least 50 percent to break even.
- The factory faced challenges from the start due to difficulties finding skilled production talent in the largely rural area of South Carolina.
- There has been significant churn in plant leadership.
- Sustained losses at the factory, combined with a slump in Volvo's global sales and financial performance, have raised questions about whether Volvo has the financial capacity to continue its U.S. manufacturing effort.
- Analyst Jeff Schuster noted that while Volvo can minimize damage by cutting shifts and slowing lines, doing so does not reduce fixed costs.
- A Volvo spokeswoman stated the company is "fully committed" to the South Carolina plant, calling it a "key strategic advantage" in its plan to "build where we sell."
- To boost factory utilization, Volvo is considering building a higher-volume model, potentially the midsize XC60 hybrid crossover, in the U.S.
- Volvo Cars CEO Hakan Samuelsson said in mid-May that the company wants to bring in a midsize core vehicle selling in good numbers rather fast.
- Producing the XC60 would require an additional investment of several hundred million dollars into the factory.
- Analyst Jeff Schuster questioned whether investing more is the right plan to reach necessary volume or if Volvo should cut its losses now.
- Volvo reported a 60 percent plunge in first-quarter operating income, partly due to a global sales slide taxing its balance sheet.
- The automaker, which is majority-owned by China's Geely Holding, has lost more than two-thirds of its value since its 2021 initial public offering and has become a target for short sellers.
- In May, Volvo announced a corporate restructuring to cut expenses by 1.5 billion Swedish crowns ($140 million), which includes jettisoning about 15 percent of its salaried workforce.
- President Donald Trump's imposition of a 25 percent tariff on imports creates an existential crisis for Volvo, which currently ships 90 percent of the vehicles it sells in the U.S.
- The tariffs also provide Volvo with a reason to keep the South Carolina plant operational.
- CEO Hakan Samuelsson views Trump's tariffs as an opportunity to revive U.S. production.
- During the automaker's annual general meeting on April 3, Samuelsson stated that globalization is being "dismantled" and that to avoid high import tariffs, Volvo needs to localize more, increase factory volumes, and lower costs.
- Analyst Sam Fiorani suggested it would be prudent for Volvo to make additional investments and build the right products in South Carolina rather than abandoning its U.S. manufacturing investment early.
- Fiorani, vice president at AutoForecast Solutions, noted that given the current tariff climate, Volvo could find it challenging to continue profitably in the U.S. without domestic production.
- Production in South Carolina began with the third-generation S60 in a market shifting away from sedans.
- Automotive News estimated the factory produced nearly 40,000 sedans at its peak in 2019.
- Analyst Jeff Schuster criticized the S60 as "the wrong vehicle for the market" because it could not generate the volume required to reach capacity.
- Volvo had originally planned to build the XC90 large crossover in Charleston in 2021, a vehicle more aligned with U.S. preferences.
- The XC90 plan was derailed when Volvo pivoted to an all-electric strategy to capitalize on post-pandemic demand and government backing for zero-emission vehicles.
- Last year, Volvo began building the battery-powered EX90 at the plant, followed by the Polestar 3.
- This EV bet proved incorrect as early demand for electric vehicles fizzled.
- Volvo eventually abandoned its EV-or-bust strategy in favor of leaning on plug-in hybrid models.
- Analyst Sam Fiorani observed that Volvo established the plant to grow as the market transitioned to electric vehicles, but the EV market has not developed to the level the automaker planned.
