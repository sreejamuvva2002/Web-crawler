# Aspen Aerogels

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** thermal_management  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Aspen Aerogels cancels its LPO loan and its Georgia factory | Latitude Media](https://www.latitudemedia.com/news/why-aspen-aerogels-cancelled-its-lpo-loan-and-its-georgia-factory/)  ·  `page_052645`

## Facts

- Aspen Aerogels conditionally received a large Department of Energy loan in October.
- Aspen Aerogels is canceling its planned manufacturing facility in Statesboro, Georgia.
- The company makes thermal barriers for electric vehicle batteries, including for General Motors.
- The conditional commitment from DOE’s Loan Programs Office was for $670 million, dedicated specifically to the Georgia plant.
- In its February earnings call, the company told investors it had withdrawn from the negotiation process for the loan.
- Instead, the company will focus on expanding its production elsewhere, including at an existing factory in Rhode Island.
- The company will also expand production in China and Mexico.
- In China, Aspen is planning to increase annual production capacity by $200 million by 2026.
- The company’s manufacturing partnerships in China have already proven effective in delivering products for other business arms.
- CFO Ricardo Rodriguez said these partnerships are “capable of delivering increased aerogel production capacity” to help make up for the loss of the plant in Georgia.
- Rodriguez added there are “literally no limits” to how much Aspen can increase capacity in China.
- The earnings call came after the Trump administration’s first 10% tariff increase on China, but before the second, which took effect just last week.
- OEM customers aren’t worried about the strategic shift, according to Rodriguez.
- Rodriguez stated: “They don’t care where it is made.”
- To qualify for various tax credits under the Inflation Reduction Act, EV batteries must meet a threshold of components manufactured in the United States.
- The fate of those tax credits remains in flux.
- In February, Aspen officially opened two assembly plants outside of Monterrey, in Mexico.
- One plant will start the process of creating aerogel rolls.
- The second plant will complete assembly of the thermal barrier.
- Those factories are currently owned and managed by a consulting company.
- Aspen has notified that company of its intent to purchase the “automated fabrication facility.”
- This comes as the Trump administration pushes to cut taxes on domestic manufacturing and levy steep tariffs against competing products, including from China and Mexico.
- In late January, the president addressed the World Economic Forum.
- The president told delegates that under his second administration, there would be “no better place on Earth to create jobs, build factories, or grow a company” than the United States.
- The reality of the administration’s first few months has been an environment of regulatory uncertainty, especially for the clean energy industry, that has made it hard to do business.
- Aspen Aerogels did not cite political factors among its reasons for the Georgia project cancellation.
- The company declined to provide additional comment to Latitude Media on its decision.
- Thanks in part to the cancellation of the Georgia plant, and the “reduction of various positions” in the last quarter, Aspen is aiming to reduce its fixed costs by over $35 million per year.
- Nixing the DOE debt from the balance sheet is a big plus, Rodriguez said.
- Rodriguez said: “It feels great to not take on an incremental $671 million of debt on the balance sheet and to still be able to fulfill all the expected demand.”
- Working with external manufacturing — including those in China — has allowed Aspen to “pick up a couple of points of margin” in its other business areas, Rodriguez added.
- Doing so for the EV thermal barrier business “really is a no-brainer,” he said.
- Canceling a project that was already underway comes with costs of its own.
- As of the end of 2024, Aspen had paid around $323.6 million for engineering, design, and construction costs for the Georgia facility.
- Some of the factory’s assets will be moved to Rhode Island to increase that facility’s throughput, Rodriguez said.
- There’s also the potential, he said, to send some of the equipment “to the external manufacturing facility as well, which we’ll be evaluating.”
- The project was expected to come online in 2027.
- It was originally designed to supply thermal barriers for more than 200 million EV batteries each year.
- The project would have created more than 500 construction jobs and 255 full-time operations jobs.
- The site is located in one of the swing states that clinched Trump’s election victory.
- Despite Trump’s enthusiasm for domestic manufacturing, his policies since taking office are at times at odds with the goal of onshoring.
- The IRA and the lucrative tax credits it offers, like 45X, has lured more clean energy manufacturing to the U.S.
- Trump paused funding disbursements related to the law.
- GOP lawmakers have said they plan to take a scalpel to the law.
- 21 House Republicans today issued a letter in support of clean energy tax credits contained within the IRA.
- Those Republicans warned that they may vote against the budget bill if the credits are cut.
- The Trump administration has also discouraged EV adoption, including through halting a $5-billion federal EV charger program.
- Rodriguez told investors that Aspen is concerned about the relatively slow rate of EV adoption in the U.S. and its implications for the company.
- Rodriguez stated: “China is at 50% EV penetration already. In North America and Europe, we continue to dabble in this 10% to 15% level.”
- He added: “So you do start wondering, is that progress?”
- Most of Aspen’s thermal barrier customers are global OEMs.
- Rodriguez said these customers are mostly “staying the course” regardless of the Trump administration’s policies.
- The customers are also staying cautious about how they deploy capital during his second term in office.
- Rodriguez stated: “Four years within a 20-year transition of electrification is not a ton.”
- Aspen Aerogels is at least the second loan recipient to walk back its domestic manufacturing plans.
- In January, Kore Power, which had received a conditional commitment for an $850 million loan from LPO, scrapped plans to build a battery manufacturing plant in Buckeye, Arizona.
- In Kore Power’s case, the company told Latitude Media that it still plans to make its batteries domestically.
- Kore Power will do it faster and more cheaply via a retrofit, rather than building its planned greenfield plant.
