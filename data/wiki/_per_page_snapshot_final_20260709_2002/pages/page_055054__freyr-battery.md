# Freyr Battery

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-02-07

**Source:** [Battery firm abandons plan for a $2.6 billion plant in Georgia](https://ca.finance.yahoo.com/news/battery-firm-abandons-plan-2-204124656.html)  ·  `page_055054`

## Facts

- A clean energy company is abandoning a plan to build a giant electric battery factory in Atlanta's suburbs after shifting to buy a solar panel plant in Texas.
- Freyr Battery told officials in Newnan on Thursday it would not build a $2.6 billion plant that was supposed to hire more than 700 people.
- Freyr sent a Jan. 21 letter to the Coweta County Development Authority announcing its plans to end the project.
- The factory would have built batteries to store electricity produced by renewable sources and release it later.
- It would have been the second-largest battery factory worldwide when announced in 2023.
- Freyr is a startup founded in 2018 that never began construction on the 368-acre (149-hectare) site.
- Freyr moved its corporate headquarters from Norway to Newnan in part to maximize eligibility for U.S. tax benefits of President Joe Biden's climate law.
- Freyr is shifting focus to a newly opened solar panel factory bought last year for $340 million from top Chinese solar panel maker Trina Solar.
- Freyr spokesperson Amy Jaick wrote that the company is grateful for support in Coweta County and Georgia but is focusing on the solar module manufacturing facility in Texas.
- Newnan Times-Herald first reported the story regarding Freyr senior vice president of business development Jason Peace meeting with local officials on Thursday.
- Peace told Coweta County Development Authority board members the decision was driven by rising interest rates, falling battery prices, a change in company leadership, and a shift in goals.
- Authority President Sarah Jacobs wrote an email Friday confirming the meeting and reasons for the project's end.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development conveyed a $7 million grant to buy a site for Freyr in Newnan, about 35 miles (55 kilometers) southwest of Atlanta.
- Department spokesperson Jessica Atwell said the state and company are working together to ensure the grant money is repaid expeditiously.
- Freyr may also owe money to Coweta County.
- Georgia's incentives process protects taxpayers, ensuring discretionary incentives are repaid when a company's plans change, according to Atwell.
- Planning for the project made Coweta County a stronger candidate for future projects, according to Jacobs.
- The company had planned battery factories in Norway and Finland but said in November it will try to sell its European business.
- Freyr is terminating its license for technology to make batteries, paying $3 million to the company it was licensed from.
- Tom Einar Jensen, then CEO, told investors in August that raising money to make batteries had grown difficult due to a surplus of Chinese batteries produced at lower costs.
- The company is switching its strategy into businesses that would allow it to raise cash, including solar panel manufacturing.
- Freyr saw its cash on hand fall from $253 million at the end of 2023 to $182 million on Sept. 30.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp has targeted recruitment of the electric vehicle industry.
- Korean firm SK Innovation built a $2.6 billion battery plant in Commerce, northeast of Atlanta and hired 3,000 workers, but later laid off or furloughed some workers.
- Hyundai Motor Group has started production at a $7.6 billion electric vehicle and battery plant near Savannah, with plans to hire 8,500 workers.
- Electric truck maker Rivian revived its plans to build a plant east of Atlanta after a $6.6 billion loan from the Biden administration.
