# State of Georgia

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2027-01-01

**Source:** [Alternative Fuels Data Center: Electricity Laws and Incentives in Georgia](https://afdc.energy.gov/fuels/laws/ELEC?state=ga)  ·  `page_002469`

## Facts

- Distributors who sell or use motor fuel are subject to an excise tax of $0.26 per gallon, which may be adjusted based on vehicle efficiencies and the consumer price index.
- Motor fuels not commonly sold or measured by the gallon used in public highway vehicles are taxed according to their gasoline gallon equivalent (GGE).
- Effective January 1, 2027, a GGE of electricity may not exceed 11 kilowatt-hours.
- Effective January 1, 2027, a GGE of hydrogen must be at least 2.2 pounds.
- Effective January 1, 2027, a GGE of compressed natural gas (CNG) must be at least 110,000 British thermal units.
- Effective January 1, 2027, a GGE of liquefied natural gas (LNG) must be at least 6.06 pounds.
- CNG is defined as a mixture of hydrocarbon gases and vapors, consisting principally of methane in gaseous form compressed for use as motor fuel.
- LNG is defined as methane or natural gas in cryogenic or refrigerated liquid form for use as motor fuel.
- Propane and special fuels sold in bulk to a licensed consumer distributor are exempt from the excise tax.
- The excise tax for electricity begins January 1, 2027, and only applies to electricity sold at public electric vehicle (EV) charging stations.
- The Georgia Department of Revenue may assess, levy, and collect tax for other motor fuels used on public highways using a GGE rate.
- All-electric vehicles are subject to an annual licensing fee in 2025 of $352.56 for commercial vehicles and $234.97 for non-commercial vehicles.
- AFV annual fees also apply to plug-in hybrid electric vehicles and flexible fuel vehicles, but only if they have an AFV license plate.
- AFV license plates are subject to a one-time manufacturing fee of $25, an annual $20 registration fee, and a $35 special tag fee.
- Electric, natural gas, propane, bi-fuel, and dual-fuel vehicles are eligible for an AFV license plate.
- Effective January 1, 2027, all public EV charging stations must be capable of accurately measuring and displaying electricity dispensed to actively charging EVs on a per kilowatt-hour (kWh) basis.
- The Georgia Department of Revenue has the authority to conduct EV charging station inspections to ensure accurate delivery and display of electricity per kWh.
- Violators of EV charging station inspection requirements may be subject to a fine of up to $1,000.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development is authorized to establish the Georgia EV Manufacturing Commission (Commission).
- The Commission will seek to advance the state’s interest in developing, marketing, promoting, and recruiting the growth of the EV industry through continued investment and job creation in Georgia.
- The Commission will provide recommendations to other State agencies via consulting Georgia’s public and private sectors.
- A person or entity that provides EV charging services and is not otherwise subject to the jurisdiction of the Georgia Public Service Commission is not defined as a public utility.
- The Joint Study Committee on the Electrification of Transportation (Committee) must study the growth of the EV market in Georgia and address concerns regarding public and business needs for public EV charging infrastructure and economic preparedness.
- The Committee submitted a report of their findings to the Georgia Legislature in November 2022.
- Bulk sales of propane by licensed distributors are exempt from the state motor fuel excise tax.
- Beginning Jan. 1, 2027, the sale of electricity dispensed from EV chargers owned by nonprofit organizations, located on federal interstates, and providing electricity without payment is exempt from the state motor fuel excise tax.
- The Georgia Department of Education provides grants to local educational agencies for the purchase of new electric, compressed natural gas, or propane school buses.
- School districts must coordinate with a transportation field consultant from the Georgia Department of Education to apply for alternative fuel school bus grants.
- Alternative fuel school bus grant amounts vary and are available on a first-come, first-served basis.
- An income tax credit is available for businesses for 10% of the cost to convert a vehicle to natural gas, electricity, propane, and hydrogen, up to $2,500 per vehicle.
- Converted vehicles must be fueled solely by an alternative fuel and must meet emissions standards for low-emission or zero emission vehicles defined by the Board of Natural Resources.
- Unused alternative fuel vehicle conversion tax credits may be carried forward for up to three years.
- A business that manufactures alternative energy products for use in battery, biofuel, and electric vehicle enterprises may claim an annual tax credit for five years.
- The alternative fuel and advanced vehicle job creation tax credit amount is based on the number of eligible new full-time employee jobs and the county.
- Qualified entities for the job creation tax credit must be defined as business enterprises, excluding retail businesses.
- Job creation tax credit amounts differ depending on how the county in which the business is located ranks based on unemployment rates and income levels.
- The Georgia Environmental Protection Division (EPD) provides U.S. Environmental Protection Agency Diesel Emission Reduction Act funding for eligible projects that replace existing school buses with alternative fuel or zero-emission school buses.
- Eligible alternative fuels for diesel emission reduction grants include electricity, natural gas, or propane.
- An eligible business enterprise may claim an income tax credit for the purchase or lease and installation of qualified EV charging stations located in Georgia.
- The EV charging station tax credit is for 10% of the cost of the station, up to $2,500.
- Unused EV charging station tax credits may be carried forward for up to three years.
- The U.S. Department of Transportation’s NEVI Formula Program required the Georgia Department of Transportation (GDOT) to submit an annually updated EV Infrastructure Deployment Plan for fiscal years 2022 through 2026.
- Georgia's submitted NEVI plans must describe how the state intends to distribute NEVI funds and their physical and cybersecurity strategies, including a Community Engagement Outcomes Report.
- Submitted NEVI plans must address updated NEVI Guidance.
