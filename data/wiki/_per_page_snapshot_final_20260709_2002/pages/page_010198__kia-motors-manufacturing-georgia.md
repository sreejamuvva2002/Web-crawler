# Kia Motors Manufacturing Georgia

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-09-30

**Source:** [Kia plant CEO: Affordable housing shortage hurts worker recruitment - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2022/09/30/kia-plant-ceo-affordable-housing-shortage-hurts-worker-recruitment/)  ·  `page_010198`

## Facts

- Kia’s assembly plant in West Point opened in 2008 with more than enough workers available to produce the first cars.
- The plant is now struggling to fill 500 new jobs needed to get a new compact SUV off the assembly line due to an affordable housing shortage.
- Kia Georgia CEO Stuart Countess testified before a committee of Georgia legislators that many interested applicants for jobs paying $17-$23 per hour are having trouble finding affordable housing near the west Georgia plant.
- Kia has been in close communication with Troup County and other government officials about housing development plans.
- Countess urged other large employers to collaborate with government and community leaders to prepare for 50,000 manufacturing jobs expected to be created in Georgia in the next decade.
- Kia Georgia explored creative solutions by talking to a developer about building workforce housing.
- The housing shortage worsened during the pandemic when home development stalled due to supply chain issues, making it harder to buy or rent a house.
- Countess stated at Thursday’s meeting held at the Georgia State Capitol: “The housing market creates additional complications for families considering a relocation to pursue employment as we know the housing market is in an exceedingly high demand with most affordable homes in the area lasting hours before asking prices are being offered to buyers.”
- Before the new legislative session begins in January, the Georgia House Study Committee on Regulation, Affordability and Access to Housing will have met multiple times to identify solutions for a lack of affordable housing in the state for renters, entry-level homeowners, and middle-class families.
- Macon Republican state Rep. Dale Washburn offered support for workforce housing, noting it will take a multilayer approach to address the shortage primarily below $300,000.
- Washburn recalled a Macon neighborhood where cotton mill employees once lived in company-owned housing and stated: “Ideally I think for big industries, investing in housing could end up being a good investment.”
- Barbara Holmes, president of the Albany Area Chamber of Commerce, noted that south Georgia is working to identify what constitutes affordable housing in the region.
- There was a 6% drop in home ownership in Albany and surrounding Dougherty County from 2009 to 2020, and home prices have risen by 17% over the past couple of years.
- Holmes said the market presents a hardship for renters, first-time buyers, and middle-class families, and for many people looking to move out of public housing.
- It is hard to find nice housing between $100,000 and $150,000 in the Albany area.
- Holmes stated: “Bidding wars in Dougherty County’s real estate market is not what I say is (typical) and so you’re seeing prices that are squeezing out homeownership that is truly affordable.”
- Holmes added: “What we have is an old stock of homes but nothing newer for our recent college graduates, first-time home buyers.”
- Tax credits are being used in south Georgia to build a multifamily workforce housing complex six miles from a Hyundai manufacturing plant planned near Savannah, according to Department of Community Affairs Commissioner Christopher Nunn.
- Due to the loss of federal funding, DCA has been unable to provide as many Georgians loans and down payments for homeowners who earn 120% or less than other areas’ median household income.
- DCA officials expect a surge in interest in the loan program when the house price limit is increased to $297,000 for most of the state and even higher in Atlanta and Savannah.
- The committee discussed how building codes and government design standards drive up developer costs.
- Chatsworth Republican state Rep. Jason Ridley asked Nunn if any cost-benefit analyses had been conducted on new regulations.
- Dallas Republican state Rep. Martin Momtahan said an international code standard created a major burden for builders in Paulding County and throughout the state.
- The DCA is currently involved in a statewide advisory group composed of representatives of general contracting, code enforcement, city and county government leaders, and trade associations.
- Nunn stated: “We don’t just take a blanket approach and say Georgia, adopt all these codes as is.”
