# Adient plc

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Adient (ADNT) - The Complete Story | Spin-off. Turnaround. Auto Seating Empire.](https://empor.top/us/ADNT)  ·  `page_016559`

## Facts

- Adient plc is the world's largest automotive seating supplier, with products ending up in tens of millions of vehicles annually across Detroit, Shanghai, Stuttgart, and São Paulo.
- The company operates more than two hundred manufacturing and assembly plants across twenty-nine countries and employs north of sixty-five thousand people.
- In fiscal year 2024, Adient reported consolidated revenue of nearly fifteen billion dollars.
- Six years prior to the article's reference point, Adient's stock had cratered ninety-two percent from its all-time high, and it had piled up more than three billion dollars in cumulative losses over two fiscal years.
- Adient officially began independent operations on October 31, 2016, following its separation from Johnson Controls International, entering the market as the industry's biggest player with $16.6 billion in revenue.
- At its spin-off, Adient was born with a poison pill of $3.5 billion in debt strapped to its balance sheet before it even opened its doors.
- In connection with the spin-off, Adient obtained $3.5 billion in debt financing; approximately $500 million stayed for working capital while three billion dollars was distributed directly to Johnson Controls as a pre-separation dividend.
- The average interest rate on this debt was 3.4 percent, translating to roughly $125 million in annual interest expense that could never be invested in new manufacturing technology or talent retention.
- Adient acknowledged in regulatory filings that its significant leverage put it at a competitive disadvantage relative to less-indebted peers.
- R. Bruce McDonald was tapped as Adient's first chairman and chief executive officer, serving as a finance-oriented leader rather than an operations specialist.
- Adient spent $100 million to renovate the historic Marquette Building in downtown Detroit and relocate roughly five hundred employees there, including about one hundred new positions.
- The Marquette Building had been purchased for $16.9 million from Mexican billionaire Carlos Slim, who acquired it for just $5.8 million in 2014.
- Adient also paid approximately $19 million for a nearby parking deck and surface lot as part of its Detroit headquarters plan.
- In fiscal year 2017, Adient delivered $16.2 billion in revenue and $877 million in net income, with its stock reaching an all-time high of $84.06 on October 24, 2017.
- Bruce McDonald's compensation package for fiscal year 2017 hit $24.3 million, making him metro Detroit's highest-paid public company CEO.
- In September 2017, Adient acquired Futuris, an Oak Park, Michigan-based automotive seat manufacturer owned by Clearlake Capital, adding fifteen manufacturing facilities across Asia and North America and expected to contribute $500 million in additional annual revenue.
- In January 2018, Adient and Boeing announced the formation of Adient Aerospace, a joint venture to design and manufacture airliner seats, with Adient holding 50.01 percent and Boeing 49.99 percent.
- The airline seat market was valued at $4.5 billion in 2017, with forecasts projecting $6 billion by 2026.
- Adient Aerospace headquarters were established in Kaiserslautern, Germany, near Frankfurt, with customer service operations in Seattle.
- In the second quarter of fiscal 2018, Adient's Seat Structures and Mechanisms division recorded a $279 million non-cash goodwill impairment charge, admitting it overpaid for the asset.
- In June 2018, Adient scrapped its Detroit headquarters renovation plan entirely and sold the Marquette Building and surrounding properties at an eleven-million-dollar loss.
- Detlef Juerss, Adient's VP of engineering and CTO, later stated that Detroit was not the right timing for the headquarters move.
- Fiscal 2018 results showed a devastating net loss of $1.7 billion on revenue of $17.4 billion, with the fourth quarter alone producing a $1.36 billion loss driven by $809 million in restructuring and impairment charges.
- The cash dividend was suspended as Adient's stock entered freefall.
- Bruce McDonald stepped down as chairman and CEO on June 11, 2018, remaining as an adviser through September 30 while collecting his $1.5 million salary and $250,000 in relocation expenses.
- His 2017 equity award of approximately $17 million was forfeited upon departure.
- Frederick "Fritz" Henderson served as Adient's interim CEO for roughly three months to stabilize the company while the board hunted for a permanent leader.
- Doug DelGrosso became Adient's president and CEO on October 1, 2018, bringing experience from rebuilding Chassix after its March 2015 Chapter 11 bankruptcy.
- Under DelGrosso's turnaround strategy, approximately thirteen thousand employee positions were targeted for elimination, representing roughly one out of every six employees at the company.
- Premium freight costs were systematically attacked as a key metric to drive down, reversing the ballooning expenses caused by mismanaged launches under previous leadership.
- At the Warren Bridgewater plant in suburban Detroit, customer disruptions had been running at more than ten per month during the first quarter of fiscal 2019 but fell below one by the fourth quarter.
- Fiscal 2019 results showed the restructuring was working, with the net loss narrowing dramatically from $1.7 billion to $491 million on revenue of $16.5 billion.
- The fourth-quarter fiscal 2019 loss was reduced to just $4 million compared to $1.36 billion a year earlier.
- On November 14, 2019, DelGrosso announced that roughly 1,300 non-plant salaried employees would not work or be paid during Thanksgiving and New Year's due to a GM strike severely impacting the Americas region.
- In March 2020, COVID-19 caused the most severe demand shock in the global automotive industry since the Great Depression, shutting down assembly plants worldwide.
- Adient's stock plunged to an all-time low of $6.53 on March 18, 2020, a decline of approximately ninety-two percent from its October 2017 high.
- Revenue for fiscal 2020 collapsed to $12.67 billion, a twenty-three percent decline from the prior year, while operating margins contracted to roughly five percent.
- Competitors Lear and Magna operated above eleven percent operating margins during the same period, highlighting Adient's competitive disadvantage.
- The company accumulated more than three billion dollars in cumulative losses since its spin-off, with interest expense consuming an ever-growing share of diminished earnings.
