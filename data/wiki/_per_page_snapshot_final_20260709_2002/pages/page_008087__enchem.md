# Enchem

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Enchem's Aggressive Expansion in North American Secondary Battery Market... Smooth Progress in Market Share Growth - The Asia Business Daily](https://www.asiae.co.kr/en/article/2024012508342937399)  ·  `page_008087`

## Facts

- Enchem announced on January 25 that it has consecutively secured product supply contracts with various global top-tier customers in the North American market, including K-battery companies LG Energy Solution and SK On.
- Since establishing its local subsidiary, Enchem America, in 2019, Enchem has continued a bold “proactive investment” strategy and succeeded in establishing itself as a key material company in the North American secondary battery market.
- In 2020, Enchem purchased the Toyota plant site located in Georgia and completed its first North American factory within two years.
- Through this acquisition, Enchem became the only domestic company at the time to establish a large-scale “local production-local supply” system.
- Enchem rapidly grew based on its existing large-scale factory operation know-how and large-scale production capacity (CAPA) capable of immediate response, currently leading electrolyte supply within the North American market.
- Enchem’s Georgia plant has an annual electrolyte production capacity of 40,000 tons, making it the largest electrolyte factory in North America.
- The Georgia plant site covers approximately 350,000 pyeong (1,141,000㎡), which is one-quarter the size of Yeouido.
- A 20,000-ton annual capacity NMP plant is also located at the Georgia site.
- Enchem plans to initially expand the Georgia plant to an annual capacity of 100,000 tons by the end of this year.
- Subsequently, Enchem aims to increase production capacity to a total of 200,000 tons by the end of next year, covering the entire North American region until the completion of a second plant currently planned for construction in the western region.
- An Enchem official explained that the Georgia plant was the greatest competitive advantage that allowed Enchem to rapidly expand its market share in North America at an early stage.
- The official added that the Georgia plant remains the only large-scale electrolyte factory in the North American market, serving as a strong entry barrier.
- The official noted that the Georgia plant has been receiving more attention following the disclosure of detailed guidelines for the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) Foreign Excluded Overseas Concern (FEOC).
- The official emphasized that the already secured large-scale expansion site, automated processes, and unparalleled large-scale factory operation capabilities are invaluable assets unique to Enchem.
- Enchem’s Georgia plant began initial electrolyte supply to SK On’s Georgia plant in March last year.
- The Georgia plant started supplying products to the Ohio plant of Ultium Cells, a joint venture between LG Energy Solution and GM, in August of the same year.
- This year, Enchem is preparing to supply products to a Japanese battery company located in Nevada, BlueOval (Kentucky), a joint venture between SK On and Ford, and Ultium Cells’ second plant in Tennessee.
- Enchem is pursuing a “pinpoint strategy” focused on the U.S. East and West coasts to establish a more efficient local production and supply system.
- This reflects Enchem’s strategic intention to further segment the North American market and implement construction and expansion plans accompanied by customer-tailored sales strategies.
- The company explained that since it is currently securing early references through a first-mover advantage in the North American market, operational strategies will also become a crucial point going forward.
- A company representative added that Enchem aims to rapidly expand its North American market reach under a site strategy centered on the main Georgia plant, covering the eastern, western, and northern regions.
- The representative added that in addition to the Tennessee plant currently underway, Enchem is also considering building an additional electrolyte plant in Ontario, Canada.
