# HL-GA Battery Company / Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America (HMGMA)

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-11-13

**Source:** [What an ICE raid on a Georgia EV factory could mean for BESS - Energy-Storage.News](https://www.energy-storage.news/what-an-ice-raid-on-hyundai-and-lg-es-ev-cell-factory-in-georgia-could-mean-for-bess-manufacturing/)  ·  `page_000337`

## Facts

- On 4 September, US Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), along with multiple other law enforcement organisations, raided Hyundai Motor and LG Energy Solution’s electric vehicle (EV) battery cell plant in Ellabell, Georgia.
- Hyundai and LG ES each have a 50% stake in the US$4.3 billion HL-GA Battery Company.
- HL-GA is part of the larger Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America (HMGMA) campus.
- The HMGMA campus cost US$7.6 billion and has over 16 million square feet of factory floor space.
- HMGMA began producing the Hyundai Ioniq 5 in October 2024.
- The Associated Press noted the project as “the largest economic development project the state has ever seen.”
- The Savannah Morning News reported that HMGMA had plans to hire 8,100 people by 2031.
- According to Politico, 17 suppliers in the surrounding area announced factories to support HMGMA.
- These supplier factories represent an estimated additional investment of US$2.7 billion and an additional 7,000 jobs.
- The large-scale investments spurred upgrades to surrounding infrastructure from the Georgia Department of Transportation.
- Earlier this year, Hyundai said it would invest an additional US$5 billion in the US overall.
- A reported 500 agents from ICE, Homeland Security Investigations, Georgia State Patrol, the FBI, ATF, DEA, and IRS raided the factory.
- 475 individuals were detained during the raid, of whom approximately 300 were South Korean nationals.
- The detained workers were held in an ICE detention centre in Folkston, Georgia.
- Treatment of the workers led South Korea’s Foreign Ministry to investigate claims of human rights violations.
- Immigration attorney Charles Kuck said two of his clients were working under a visa waiver programme for tourism and business without violating it.
- Some detained workers allegedly illegally crossed the US border, while others had entered legally but had expired visas or visa waivers that prevented them from working.
- Some detained individuals were employed by contractors and subcontractors at the construction site, not directly by Hyundai or LG ES.
- On 10 September, The Guardian reported that one detained Korean worker had a valid working visa but was “mandated” to agree to removal from the US.
- ICE claimed that all 475 individuals were either working illegally or in violation of their visas, which has not been proven true.
- South Korean battery manufacturers currently produce 45% of all Li-ion batteries sold in the US.
- South Korea’s stationary storage market is expected to require around 50GWh of batteries this year, with projected growth to 200GWh by 2028.
- Multiple South Korean companies have outfitted factories made for EV cell manufacturing for the production of BESS cells.
- A market expert source noted that South Korean battery manufacturers partnering with US entities are repurposing existing operational or nearly finished facilities to avoid sunk costs due to potential EV demand decline.
- These repurposed factories are about 90% complete, with manufacturers shifting some capacity to stationary storage applications.
- In July, LG ES and General Motors joint venture Ultium Cells announced plans to upgrade its battery cell plant in Spring Hill, Tennessee, to increase production of lithium iron phosphate (LFP) cells.
- The Ultium Cells upgrade is based on a US$2.3 billion investment initially announced in 2021.
- Conversion of battery cell lines at Spring Hill to manufacture LFP cells will begin later this year, with commercial production expected by late 2027.
- Spring Hill was established to double Ultium’s production of nickel manganese cobalt (NMC) battery cells for vehicles like the Cadillac LYRIQ.
- LG ES stated that its newly opened LFP production lines are expected to reach an annual capacity of 17GWh by the end of 2025.
- LG ES plans to increase this LFP capacity to over 30GWh by the end of the following year.
- LG ES expects continued energy storage growth and a continued slowing in the EV market, citing increased renewable energy deployment and AI data centre demand as key drivers.
- New foreign entity of concern (FEOC) restrictions limiting Chinese equipment in US energy storage projects eligible for tax credits will likely benefit non-Chinese competitors like LG ES.
- In July, South Korean companies SK On and L&F agreed on a memorandum of understanding regarding the supply of LFP cathode materials for the North American market.
- SK On stated its partnership with L&F aims to address rising demand for LFP batteries in the US and strengthen its position in the ESS market.
- In September at the RE+ trade show in Las Vegas, Samsung SDI debuted its new Battery Box (SBB) 1.7 and SBB 2.0 BESS products, scheduled to begin US-based production next year.
- An anonymous source noted that importing batteries from China is complicated by tariffs and FEOC restrictions, while domestic batteries produced by South Korean companies pose no FEOC issues and may qualify for advanced manufacturing tax credits.
- There are no tariffs on finished batteries imported from South Korea, only on inputs like graphite or lithium from China.
- If South Korean factories face delays of three years, available capacity could drop to 30GWh against demand, creating a 70% gap.
- A one-year delay would reduce available capacity from about 25% to 40%, potentially causing prices to go up and projects to be delayed.
- Trip Tollison, president and CEO of the Savannah Economic Development Authority, said there is no other entity in the world with the proprietary technology that has to be installed by certain individuals, stating “We are relying on South Korea.”
- Jose Munoz, president and CEO of Hyundai Motor Company, told CBS News the raid would cause a minimum two to three months delay because displaced workers want to return and need replacement, mostly not being in the US.
- A representative from LG ES stated there are no plans to delay the factory and that they aim to start production at HL-GA Battery Company next year.
- Following a US-Korea working group meeting agreement that B-1 and ESTA holders can perform installation, inspection, and maintenance of equipment at US factories, essential personnel business trips resumed starting 13 October.
- In the weeks following the raid, Hyundai announced it would move forward with expansion plans at the Ellabell plant.
- Hyundai updated its original production plans at HMGMA from 300,000 EVs per year to 500,000 EVs per year.
- The New York Times reported that South Korea’s Chosun Ilbo editorial urged officials to recognize the seriousness of the situation and questioned whether South Korea should continue investing in America.
- Donald Trump wrote on Truth Social calling on all foreign companies investing in the US to respect immigration laws and encouraging them to legally hire and train American workers.
- In July, the US agreed to reduce tariffs on South Korean exports from 15% to 25% in return for Korean companies pledging US$350 billion in investments in the US.
- Of the pledged investment, US$150 billion is specifically allocated to boost America’s shipbuilding industry.
- On 27 October, Trump stated he was opposed to the raid, noting that complex factory equipment requires brought-in expertise to teach American workers.
- About 200 individuals detained during the weeklong ordeal are planning to sue ICE, accusing the organisation of racial discrimination, human rights abuses, and excessive force, while pursuing compensation for damages.
