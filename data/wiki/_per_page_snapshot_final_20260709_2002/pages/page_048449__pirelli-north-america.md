# Pirelli North America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2002-08-18

**Source:** [Pirelli tire plant comes on lin | Local New |  | northwestgeorgianews.com](https://www.northwestgeorgianews.com/pirelli-tire-plant-comes-on-lin-local-new/article_d8bbb496-7891-5bb5-aa5f-fe3b5ade7d88.html)  ·  `page_048449`

## Facts

- Gov. Roy Barnes started production at the Pirelli tire plant in Rome on Saturday.
- The first production P Zero Nero tire came off the company’s modular integrated robotic system (MIRS) less than 10 minutes after start.
- Gaetano Mannino is president and chief executive officer of Pirelli North America.
- This is the fourth MIRS operation in the world for Pirelli.
- Georgia’s long history of automotive leadership was a key determining factor for locating Pirelli's headquarters in Rome, according to CEO Gaetano Mannino.
- Pirelli decided to build a new MIRS facility and headquarters in the United States approximately two years prior to the article's publication.
- Pirelli investigated many possible sites but chose Rome, Georgia because state, county, and city representatives did a fantastic job promoting the area.
- Northwest Georgia has attracted a growing number of automotive suppliers and support facilities since Mercedes-Benz and Honda opened plants in Alabama.
- Gov. Roy Barnes touted his education reform package, which aims to produce highly skilled workers for future technology jobs.
- Barnes stated that a highly skilled workforce, not land or buildings, brings businesses to Georgia.
- Barnes said fostering high-tech industrial growth solidifies Georgia’s standing in the automotive industry and places Rome on the map as a high-tech manufacturing center.
- Garry Fricks is chairman of the Floyd County Commission.
- Ronnie Wallace is chairman of the Rome City Commission.
- The plant is 400,000 square feet and includes Pirelli’s North American headquarters and research and development facility.
- The project represents a $140 million investment in Greater Rome.
- The highly automated MIRS module reduces the 14-step tire manufacturing process to three steps.
- The MIRS system can turn out a tire every three minutes.
- MIRS requires only about one-fifth of the space and uses one-third of the energy as conventional tire manufacturing.
- A MIRS line can be changed over to produce a new product in about 20 minutes.
- A MIRS line can make as few as four tires for specific applications.
- When all 20 MIRS modules slated for the Rome plant are installed, it will be Pirelli’s largest MIRS operation.
- The Rome plant currently has three MIRS modules: one in production, one in final commissioning, and one under installation.
- Andrew Kearton is director of manufacturing at Pirelli.
- Pirelli plans to have 15 MIRS modules in production within three years.
- There is room to add five additional modules beyond the planned 15.
- The plant is located on enough land in the Floyd County Industrial Park to eventually have 40 MIRS modules in production.
- Pirelli expects to have 300 workers in Rome within three years.
- About 250 of the future workers will be production workers, and the remaining 50 will be corporate personnel.
- All headquarters employees are currently in Rome.
- Approximately 35 production workers are currently on the job.
- Others are currently in training and more are being recruited every month.
- Andrew Kearton praised Georgia’s Quick Start training program for providing training to all of Pirelli’s first Rome workers.
- Pirelli sent its first 13 people to the United Kingdom to train on MIRS modules, but they participated in a Quick Start training program before going.
- Craig McDaniel is chairman of the Greater Rome Chamber and president of Coosa Valley Technical College.
- Pirelli officials had temporary offices at Coosa Valley Tech’s Business Expansion Center while the new plant was under construction.
- Pirelli announced its plans to build the Rome plant in April 2001.
- Ground was broken in September 2001.
- Corporate personnel moved into the new headquarters in April 2002.
- Test production began in June 2002, three weeks ahead of schedule.
