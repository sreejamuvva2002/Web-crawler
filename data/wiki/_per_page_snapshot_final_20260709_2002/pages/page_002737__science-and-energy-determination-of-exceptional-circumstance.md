# Science and Energy Determination of Exceptional Circumstances (S&E DEC)

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2021-06-08

**Source:** [U.S. Manufacturing | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/gc/us-manufacturing)  ·  `page_002737`

## Facts

- On June 8, 2021, the Science and Energy Determination of Exceptional Circumstances (S&E DEC) was announced as part of a series of new policy actions to support U.S. job creation and bolster the domestic manufacturing supply chain.
- The S&E DEC was announced in connection with the 100-day review of critical supply chains as directed under E.O. 14017, America’s Supply Chains.
- The S&E DEC expands on DOE’s experience in strengthening U.S. manufacturing requirements in DOE-funded grants, cooperative agreements, and research and development contracts by extending the enhancement of U.S. manufacturing requirements to most of the DOE Programs.
- The S&E DEC ensures that all innovations, including those relating to advanced batteries, developed with taxpayer dollars through DOE Science and Energy Programs are substantially manufactured in the U.S.
- The S&E DEC authorizes inclusion of the U.S. Competitiveness provision, which requires substantial U.S. manufacture of products embodying DOE-funded subject inventions, in agreements funded by DOE Science and Energy Programs.
- DOE expects to release additional guidance on the implementation of the Science and Energy DEC in early FY2022.
- FAQs for Applicants and Awardees of DOE Financial Assistance and R&D Contracts regarding the Department’s Determination of Exceptional Circumstances (DEC) for DOE Science and Energy Technologies were issued in June of 2021.
- DOE includes domestic manufacture requirements for subject inventions in its funding agreements, such as financial assistance awards, Lab contracts, and CRADAs, to help revitalize domestic manufacturing, secure U.S. supply chains, and create good-quality American jobs.
- The authority for U.S. manufacturing requirements is statutory, typically taking the form of U.S. Preference (the baseline standard per the Bayh-Dole Act) or U.S. Competitiveness (for CRADAs and entities obtaining patent waivers).
- Since 2013, DOE has used the statutory determination of exceptional circumstances process in the Bayh-Dole Act to elevate the U.S. manufacturing requirement to U.S. Competitiveness for Bayh-Dole entities in select programs.
- Under the U.S. Preference Provision, recipients agree that neither they nor any assignee will grant exclusive rights to use or sell a subject invention in the United States unless products embodying it are manufactured substantially in the United States, and this provision does not impose a manufacturing commitment on the recipient itself, only on assignees or exclusive licensees.
- Under the U.S. Competitiveness Provision, any products embodying a waived invention or produced through its use will be manufactured substantially in the United States regardless of source, and if foreign manufacture is agreed upon, the government’s support must be recognized appropriately.
- Recipients may request specific, fact-based, and tailored modifications to or waivers of U.S. manufacturing requirements to accommodate situations like a lack of domestic manufacturing capacity.
- DOE evaluates modification requests based on factors including mission support objectives, commercial feasibility of U.S. manufacturing, reasonable efforts to manufacture in the U.S., proposed alternative benefits to the U.S. economy (e.g., investment in U.S.-based plant and equipment, creation of high-quality U.S.-based jobs), scope of the waiver compared to proposed benefits, agreement to provide non-exclusive licenses with commercially reasonable terms, and other relevant factors.
- DOE provides a suggested form to assist requestors in identifying and providing necessary information for modification requests.
