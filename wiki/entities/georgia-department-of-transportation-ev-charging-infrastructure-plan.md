# Georgia Department of Transportation EV Charging Infrastructure Plan

**Type:** Government Program  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[charging-infrastructure|Charging Infrastructure]]  ·  **Location:** Georgia, United States

## Overview

Revised state plan approved in September 2025 that retains $135 million in federal funding to install electric vehicle charging stations along key interstate corridors in Georgia.

## EV supply-chain relevance

Allocates federal funds for the deployment of EV charging infrastructure on Interstates 16 and 95, supporting long-distance electric vehicle travel across the state.

## Timeline

- **2021** — U.S. Congress passed a $5 billion National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure program.
- **2022** — Georgia Governor Brian Kemp praised the EV charging plan, noting GDOT's critical role.
- **Early 2025** — President Donald Trump’s Department of Transportation halted funding for the program and demanded new state plans without equity emphasis.
- **September 2025** — Federal government approved Georgia's revised EV charging stations plan, allowing the state to retain $135 million in funds.

## Key facts

- Georgia can keep spending $135 million in federal funds allocated by the Biden administration for electric vehicle charging infrastructure.
- The revised plan removed language on equity, safety, and workforce considerations that were present in earlier drafts.
- Emphasis remains on installing chargers along Interstate 95 in Liberty or McIntosh counties and Interstate 16 in Bryan and Chatham counties.
- Georgia awarded its first funds last year to new EV charging stations in Brunswick, Dublin, Byron, Metter and Tifton.
- Love’s Travel Center received $650,000 federal grant matched by $435,000 company money for a four-charger station expected to open in 2026.
- Sixteen states and Washington D.C. sued the Trump administration's funding freeze; Georgia did not join the lawsuit.
