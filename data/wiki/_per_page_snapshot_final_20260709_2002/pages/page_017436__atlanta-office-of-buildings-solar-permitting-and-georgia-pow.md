# Atlanta Office of Buildings Solar Permitting and Georgia Power Interconnection Program

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Complete Guide to Solar Panel Permits in Atlanta, GA (2026)](https://www.doineedapermit.org/permits/solar-panels/atlanta-ga)  ·  `page_017436`

## Facts

- Atlanta's Office of Buildings processes residential solar permits through a dedicated track with a specific submittal checklist.
- An express permit option is available for qualifying smaller residential solar systems using an expedited PV inspection checklist.
- Express solar permits must be submitted in person starting in February 2025 after Atlanta discontinued online express submission.
- Georgia Power's BTM interconnection process is a critical step beyond city permitting for all solar installations.
- Installers must submit a Facility Interconnection Application to Georgia Power including system design, total AC inverter capacity in kW, total panel nameplate capacity in kW DC, specification sheets, and a one-line diagram.
- Georgia Power verifies grid compatibility and signs off before any system can be energized.
- Only IEEE 1741SB and IEEE 1547-2018 compliant inverters are accepted for interconnection; older certifications are rejected.
- Residential systems at or below 10kW peak generating capacity fall under Georgia Power's Renewable and Non-Renewable Tariff.
- The RNR Tariff provides compensation based on the Renewable Cost Benefit Framework adjusted avoided cost, resulting in a buy-back rate lower than retail electricity rates.
- Right-sizing solar systems to cover annual consumption maximizes value by offsetting retail rates rather than exporting at low buy-back rates.
- Adding battery storage allows homeowners to capture solar production for self-consumption during peak rate hours, improving system economics.
- Properties in designated historic or landmark districts require a Certificate of Appropriateness from the Urban Design Commission before a building permit can be issued.
- The Urban Design Commission evaluates panel placement for visibility from public streets and compatibility with historic character.
- Atlanta's historic neighborhoods including Inman Park, Grant Park, Virginia-Highland, Druid Hills, and West End have mature tree canopy that may impact shading analyses for solar viability.
- Roof planes facing away from streets are generally more approvable than front-facing installations on historically significant streetscapes.
- The Certificate of Appropriateness process adds 4–8 weeks to the permitting timeline for historic district properties.
- The federal Investment Tax Credit under Section 48D/25D provides a 30% tax credit of total system cost for residential installations, available through at least 2032.
- A $20,000 solar system qualifies for a $6,000 federal tax credit.
- Georgia's statewide Home Energy Rebates administered by GEFA include electrification packages that combine solar with heat pump installations for qualifying households.
- These rebates focus more on heating and cooling equipment but solar plus heat pump combinations may unlock larger package rebates based on income eligibility.
- Georgia's Residential Solar Energy Credit previously provided a state income tax credit of 35% of costs up to $10,500 for several years but has undergone changes requiring confirmation with the Department of Revenue.
- Atlanta does not offer utility fee exemptions for solar permits, unlike Sacramento which waives all building permit fees for solar with interconnection agreements.
- Standard building and electrical trade permits in Atlanta cost approximately $300 to $500 total.
- Atlanta has no mandatory solar requirement for new residential construction, and Georgia has not adopted a statewide solar mandate.
- Solar installation remains a voluntary decision for Atlanta homeowners and ADU builders, contrasting with California cities that require solar on new detached ADUs under the 2025 Energy Code.
- Standard residential solar permit review through the Office of Buildings takes 1–3 weeks.
- Georgia Power's BTM interconnection application review typically takes 2–4 weeks.
- The overall pre-installation wait for non-historic properties is usually 3–5 weeks, determined by the longer of the two parallel processes.
- Total permitting and interconnection timeline for historic district properties ranges from 8–14 weeks from project initiation to Permission to Operate.
- Non-historic properties typically complete the process in 4–7 weeks.
- Atlanta has no tree-canopy height restrictions on solar mounting, unlike some other cities.
