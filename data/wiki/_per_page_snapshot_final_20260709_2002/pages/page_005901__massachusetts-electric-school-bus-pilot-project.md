# Massachusetts Electric School Bus Pilot Project

**Type:** event  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2020-07-17

**Source:** [Alternative Fuels Data Center: Massachusetts School Fleets Get Answers through Electric Bus Testing](https://afdc.energy.gov/case/3092)  ·  `page_005901`

## Facts

- A $2 million pilot project funded through the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative allowed three school districts to purchase Type C electric school buses and Level 2 bidirectional vehicle-to-grid (V2G) charging stations to test the technology in cold weather environments.
- Massachusetts Department of Energy Resources (DOER), which houses the Massachusetts Clean Cities (MCC) coalition, administered the project.
- The project included $400,000 grants for Amherst Regional Public School District, Cambridge Public School District, and Concord Public School District to invest in the vehicles and electric vehicle supply equipment (EVSE).
- Combined, the three buses traveled approximately 14,000 miles and provided transportation for 279 days.
- Though the pilot has ended, the school districts still operate the electric buses and DOER continues to evaluate their impact.
- During the pilot, life cycle air pollution was about half of what pollution from diesel buses would have been.
- As the Massachusetts grid mix incorporates larger sources of alternative energy sources, those pollution benefits will increase.
- Bus efficiency ranged from 1.3 to 1.4 kilowatt-hours (kWh) per mile, providing a range of 70 to 80 miles.
- The implementation team noted that districts would have each benefitted from having more than one bus for redundancy.
- Stephen Russell, former MCC director, stated one bus per district gave a good flavor but recommended piloting a few buses in the same location to make it easier to manage issues, trained staff, and parts.
- Once each district selected an equipment manufacturer, it took approximately three months to build the buses.
- Charging infrastructure installation lagged behind bus arrival because each school had to bid out installation separately after purchasing stations from a state contract.
- Brian Foulds, an electric technology champion for Concord, noted a bus sat for approximately five months while charging station contracts were pulled together.
- Fleets benefit from coordinating with their local utility to understand electrical upgrades, rate structures, and demand charges.
- Demand charges apply to commercial electricity usage customers based on the highest usage recorded at any time in a month and accounted for a significant portion of operating costs.
- One way to avoid demand charge costs is to charge the buses outside of peak demand times.
- The buses consumed excess energy from fans and heaters used to cool and heat the batteries during charging.
- Fueling costs were anticipated to be lower, but unmanaged charging shifted the scales; energy costs were estimated that 63% higher than necessary.
- A lesson learned is that buses should be charged at night rather than during the day when electricity use is already high.
- When the electric fleet grows, scheduled charging and V2G will be addressed with software to manage state of charge based on bus duty cycles and reduce peak load at the substation level.
- The pilot sought to determine whether the technology was market ready, real-world pollution reductions, fuel efficiency benefits, operational costs compared to diesel buses, and V2G capabilities for a school district.
- Concord's mission was to explore a fuel that can reduce the health and environmental impact of busing students.
- For Concord, the school bus fleet accounts for approximately 8% of all municipal sector pollution.
- To maximize pollution benefits, Concord deployed the bus along a route for students using the wheelchair lift because there are no fumes when using the lift and diesel buses have to idle to lift.
- While the purchase price is high, electric buses can help lower maintenance and fueling costs.
- Concord's long-term goal is to reduce electrical utility peak load while buses aren't in use by pairing with V2G capabilities in the future.
- Amherst Regional Public School District operates its own vehicles with a relatively small, 10-vehicle fleet supported by a single technician; their electric bus traveled an average of 34 miles per day.
- Cambridge Public School District is privately operated and transports 2,500 students; their electric bus was incorporated into regular operations and covered roughly 45 miles a day.
- Concord Public School District buses operate in up to three-hour intervals to transport over 3,400 students from Concord, Carlisle, and two local private schools; their electric bus traveled approximately 45 miles per route.
- Vermont Energy Investment Corporation (VEIC) assisted DOER with the project by evaluating available technology, developing procurement materials, providing ongoing support, tracking vehicle reliability, energy efficiency, and energy consumption, and providing an evaluation of the experience.
- Each fleet used a well thought out request for proposal developed by VEIC covering battery size, range, safety regulations, and warranty terms and conditions.
- The Lion Electric Company was the only original equipment manufacturer with electric school buses in 2015 and was awarded the contract for all three demonstration sites.
- As of 2020, four manufacturers are at the table including Lion, Blue Bird Corporation, IC Bus, and Thomas Built Buses.
- The Lion buses used in the pilot included four batteries that provide 104 kWh of energy storage with an estimated range of 75 miles.
- The batteries are warrantied for 160,000 kWh or eight years.
- Amherst, Cambridge, and Concord all had reliability or mechanical issues ranging from noisy cooling fans to charger failures to bus breakdowns while in service.
- One of the biggest challenges was working with a company that did not have a local presence; because Lion is based in Canada, parts took three days to two weeks to arrive and mechanics had to be sent from Canada extending downtime.
- Even over the course of the pilot the technology advanced as the buses received were early models different from the Lion buses in production at the pilot's conclusion.
- Lion learned from the issues the school districts experienced and worked to improve their technology.
- Almost all the technical issues stemmed from being one of the first electric school bus projects in the United States.
- After two years of operation Lion took each bus back to the factory for up to two months and rewired them to fix the issues reported by the districts.
- In the year since the buses were redelivered there were no operational issues.
- Lion recently opened a parts and maintenance facility in Albany New York which will allow for easier servicing.
- Russell advised fleets interested in deploying electric school buses to ask the manufacturer to provide an experienced electric bus technician and driver for training at the start.
- Following the rebuild each bus was equipped with telematics which transmit use data to DOER.
- Amherst Cambridge and Concord continue to operate the electric school buses and report data weekly to DOER including kWh consumed and miles travelled per week.
- MCC is working to analyze the data and provide an update on pollution impacts and maintenance costs.
- Through a vehicle lifecycle cost comparison Concord is developing a plan to switch 80% of its bus fleet to electric.
- The second bus is expected to arrive in 2020 for Concord.
