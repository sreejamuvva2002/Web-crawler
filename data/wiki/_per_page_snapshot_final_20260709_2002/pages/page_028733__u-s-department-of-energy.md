# U.S. Department of Energy

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [DOE Report Finds Hundreds of Retiring Coal Plant Sites Could Convert to Nuclear | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/doe-report-finds-hundreds-retiring-coal-plant-sites-could-convert-nuclear)  ·  `page_028733`

## Facts

- The U.S. Department of Energy released a report showing hundreds of U.S. coal power plant sites could convert to nuclear power plant sites.
- The coal-to-nuclear transition could add new jobs, increase economic benefit, and significantly improve environmental conditions.
- The transition could add substantial clean electricity to the grid, helping the U.S. reach its net-zero emissions goals by 2050.
- The study investigated the benefits and challenges of converting retiring coal plant sites into nuclear plant sites.
- After screening recently retired and active coal plant sites, the study team identified 157 retired coal plant sites and 237 operating coal plant sites as potential candidates for conversion.
- Of the identified sites, 80% are good candidates to host advanced reactors smaller than the gigawatt scale.
- A coal-to-nuclear transition could significantly improve air quality in communities around the country.
- A case study found greenhouse gas emissions in a region could fall by 86% when nuclear power plants replace large coal plants.
- This emissions reduction is equivalent to taking more than 500,000 gasoline-powered passenger vehicles off the roads.
- The transition could also increase employment and economic activity within those communities.
- When a large coal plant is replaced by a nuclear power plant of equivalent size, jobs in the region could increase by more than 650 permanent positions.
- Long-term job impacts from the case study could lead to additional annual economic activity of $275 million.
- This implies a 92% increase in tax revenue for the local county compared to operating coal power.
- Assistant Secretary for Nuclear Energy Dr. Kathryn Huff stated this is an important opportunity to help communities preserve jobs, increase tax revenue, and improve air quality.
- Huff emphasized delivering place-based solutions and ensuring an equitable energy transition that does not leave communities behind as the U.S. moves to a clean energy future.
- The reuse of coal infrastructure for advanced nuclear reactors could reduce costs for developing new nuclear technology by saving 15% to 35% in construction costs.
- Coal-to-nuclear transitions could save millions of dollars by reusing electrical equipment such as transmission lines and switchyards, cooling ponds or towers, and civil infrastructure like roads and office buildings.
- Argonne National Laboratory, Idaho National Laboratory, and Oak Ridge National Laboratory conducted the study.
- The study was sponsored by the Department of Energy’s Office of Nuclear Energy.
