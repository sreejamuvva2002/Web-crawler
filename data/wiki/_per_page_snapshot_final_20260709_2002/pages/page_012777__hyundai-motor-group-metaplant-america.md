# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Hyundai Metaplant America gestaltet die EV- und Hybridproduktion mit Smart-Factory-Automatisierung neu](https://www.automobil-produktion.de/smartfactory/das-sind-hyundais-plaene-fuer-die-metaplant-in-den-usa/2691518)  ·  `page_012777`

## Facts

- Hyundai wants to showcase the car factory of the future in Georgia with its new Metaplant America, bringing together electric cars, hybrids, AI, robotics, and local battery manufacturing.
- Hyundai's US Metaplant complex covers almost 1,200 hectares of land in Ellabell, Georgia.
- While many manufacturers discuss EV/hybrid/ICE production mixes, Hyundai has already acted in Georgia with the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America (HMGMA), one of the group's most ambitious industrial projects.
- The HMGMA facility costs $7.6 billion and is intended as a blueprint for the next stage of vehicle production.
- Hyundai describes HMGMA as a software-defined factory that integrates production, logistics, data, AI, and robotics more closely than classic assembly lines.
- The facility bundles the most advanced manufacturing competencies of Hyundai Motor Group to show how flexible modern vehicle production can be when processes are digitally connected and automated.
- Currently, the Hyundai Ioniq 5, Hyundai Ioniq 9, and Kia Sportage Hybrid are rolling off the line in Ellabell.
- At full capacity, the plant will produce up to 500,000 electric and hybrid vehicles per year for Hyundai, Genesis, and Kia.
- The facility is designed for a model mix of ten different vehicles.
- At the official opening in March of last year, Euisun Chung, Executive Chair of Hyundai Motor Group, called the plant a showcase for advanced manufacturing capacities and the company's innovation claims.
- Robotics and image processing systems are used at HMGMA to enable high-quality, flexible production and support human workers rather than replace them.
- By 2031, 8,500 people will work in Ellabell.
- Hyundai relies on a strongly integrated local production network in Georgia for EV competitiveness.
- A battery factory is located directly on the HMGMA premises, operated by HLBMA, a joint venture of Hyundai and LG Energy Solution (formerly HL-GA Company).
- The HLBMA battery manufacturing represents an investment of $4.3 billion in Georgia.
- The HLBMA facility on the HMGMA complex has an annual cell capacity of 40 gigawatt-hours (GWh).
- Currently, more than 500 people work at the HLBMA facility, mostly from the region.
- According to HLBMA, the focus remains on safe and high-quality processes, including comprehensive training and own emergency capacities directly on site.
- Hyundai is investing a further $6 billion to localize parts more strongly, strengthen logistics, and enter US steel production.
- This $6 billion investment involves digital networking, connecting processes from order entry, procurement, and logistics to production to ensure robust supply chains in the USA.
- The market for battery electric vehicles (BEVs) in the US has been unreliable recently, particularly since the removal of subsidy incentives under the Trump administration in September last year.
- Hyundai was able to integrate hybrid models into production with comparatively few interruptions due to the flexibility of the new manufacturing.
- Jerald Roach (HMGMA) states that the integration of robotics and image processing is very well suited for applications where an assembly process requires image data to position a component precisely on the vehicle.
- Hyundai emphasizes that its long-term focus on electrification has not changed despite the removal of US tax incentives, though short-term production mixes will adapt to demand and political conditions while long-term investment logic remains.
- HMGMA is Hyundai's industrial innovation flagship, with many technologies previously developed and validated at the Hyundai Motor Group Innovation Center Singapore now scaled for mass production in Georgia.
- The plant combines AI, robotics, and data technologies into an automated production system where all processes from order receipt to assembly interlock to react faster, reduce friction losses, and adapt easily to new models.
- Employees at HMGMA are called "Meta Pros", and automation is primarily used for repetitive, physically demanding, or potentially dangerous tasks to increase productivity, reduce injury risks, and improve the work environment.
- Jerald Roach (Head of Department General Assembly at HMGMA) states that robotics and image processing can take over repetitive tasks beyond human physical limits, and HMGMA has integrated a high number of successful technologies into series production by selecting the right applications.
- Hyundai does not disclose details about assembly equipment suppliers, but humanoid Atlas robots from Boston Dynamics will be deployed in HMGMA starting January 2028.
- The Atlas robots are built at Boston Dynamics' headquarters in Boston and will initially be used for external scheduling and planning, specifically unloading and sorting incoming parts and packing boxes with large or heavy components.
- Hyundai tested Atlas already in October last year when sorting roof racks for the assembly line, using an engineer wearing a VR headset to guide the robot's hands and arms, with resulting data used to train the AI models for autonomous operation.
- For automotive production use, Atlas requires standardized parts and robust design, including an externally mounted battery that the robot can change itself, supported by fast-charging technology for entire robot fleets.
- Atlas's range of motion matches factory safety logic for working alongside humans, utilizing camera technology and head mobility to capture its environment, body parts, and spatial position.
- Hyundai Transys has built a high-capacity seat production facility with an annual output of 420,000 units at the site, using advanced automation including an auto-docking system and robots for applying release agents to molds.
- Hyundai WIA supports HMGMA with autonomous robots to improve logistics automation, while Hyundai Rotem has supplied equipment for the press shop.
- Battery cells for EVs come from the on-site HLBMA facility and are delivered to Mobis Battery System Assembly, which packs them onto skateboard chassis transported via conveyor technology to HMGMA's assembly area.
- A second on-site facility, Mobis Modules, produces the frontend module for the vehicles.
- Jerald Roach states that battery subassemblies are fed and mated via a highly automated process ensuring repeatability and precision for critical components like front/rear wheel suspensions, drive systems, and batteries, with all fasteners checked for presence and correct torque.
- HMGMA maintains common positioning schemes for main components so automation can be easily adapted to new models regardless of drive type (ICE, BEV, or hybrid).
- The plant was designed from the start to optimize BEV assembly, recognizing that connecting the large battery to the underbody must be considered during plant planning and vehicle design rather than integrated later.
- Roach notes that plants designed for ICE vehicles would require significant structural changes or compromises to adapt to basic BEV differences.
- Hyundai's modular vehicle design enables building different drive variants in a single plant, with common positioning schemes easing automation adaptation.
- Hyundai uses automated material delivery systems in Georgia, including driverless transport systems and automated trailer unloading systems, reducing safety risks from tractors/forklifts and creating a safer, more efficient assembly plant.
- Roach explains that an aggressive kitting strategy is used to distribute sequenced parts, increasing flexibility for multiple models, reducing decisions for Meta Pros, and improving visual management of assembly processes.
- Vehicles move through the plant on driverless transport systems following programmed routes controlled via QR codes on the hall floor, rather than classic rigid conveyors.
- Sustainability at HMGMA includes sourcing renewable energy, own solar power generation, energy-efficient building design, and lower emissions from producing electrified vehicles.
- The plant features an advanced wastewater treatment and water management system, using treated water for landscaping and systems for collecting and controlled discharge of rainwater to save water.
- In 2024, Hyundai Motor Group signed a 15-year power purchase agreement with energy developer Matrix Renewables, acquiring 147 megawatts of generation capacity from the Stillhouse Solar Project in Texas.
- Hyundai expects to secure around 378 gigawatt-hours of electricity annually from this agreement and reduce CO2 emissions at its US locations by around 140,000 tons per year.
- Despite political uncertainties, Hyundai remains optimistic about BEV production in the USA, with Roach stating the company will continue investing in local capacities and that electrification remains a long-term growth driver driven by localization, economies of scale, and customer choice.
