# Hyundai Motor Group

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-05-23

**Source:** [Hyundai Announces $5.5B Electric Vehicle Plant in Georgia | Manufacturing.net](https://www.manufacturing.net/automotive/news/22248914/hyundai-announces-55b-electric-vehicle-plant-in-georgia)  ·  `page_008795`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group confirmed it will spend $5.5 billion on a huge electric vehicle plant near Savannah.
- The plant will employ thousands of workers, with Georgia’s governor calling the deal the largest economic development project in the state’s history.
- Hyundai Motor Group CEO Jaehoon Chang made the announcement with Gov. Brian Kemp at the site of the future factory in Bryan County.
- State and local officials purchased a flat, sprawling tract for $61 million last year in hopes of luring a major manufacturer.
- Gov. Brian Kemp said hard-working Georgians will have the opportunity to have a really high-paying, advanced manufacturing job with a great company.
- Hyundai said it will employ at least 8,100 workers at the plant near the unincorporated town of Ellabell.
- It will be Hyundai's first U.S. plant dedicated to assembling electric vehicles and will also produce vehicle batteries.
- CEO Jaehoon Chang told more than 100 people that this new, high tech EV plant represents the future of their business.
- Hyundai Motors plans to start construction early next year and in 2025 begin producing up to 300,000 vehicles per year.
- The company did not specify which vehicle models the Georgia plant will make.
- In a video shown at the announcement, Hyundai Motors Chairman Euisun Chung said it will produce “a wide range of exciting, innovative EVs for our American customers.”
- The company and state officials expect suppliers to invest an additional $1 billion in the area.
- Gov. Kemp predicted a ripple effect that will boost businesses from Savannah’s already booming seaport to restaurants and convenience stores.
- The announcement came as President Joe Biden is visiting South Korea.
- Biden was scheduled to meet with Hyundai’s CEO in Seoul on Sunday.
- Jake Sullivan, the president’s national security adviser, told reporters Biden “will have the opportunity to say thank you for this significant investment that will occur in the United States.”
- The timing was fortunate for Kemp, who is being challenged by former U.S. Sen. David Perdue in a Republican primary election that will be decided Tuesday.
- It’s the second huge electric vehicle plant announced in Georgia in less than a year.
- Rivian Automotive announced in December plans for a $5 billion electric truck plant east of Atlanta that’s expected to employ about 7,500 workers.
- Kemp declined to discuss details of what incentives and tax breaks the company was offered for locating in Georgia.
- Rivian received an incentive package worth $1.5 billion.
- The state of Georgia and partner local governments bought 2,200 acres (890 acres) in Bryan County a year ago to lure a large manufacturer.
- That initial tract wasn't large enough for Hyundai.
- The state and its partners purchased two additional neighboring tracts to expand the site to more than 2,900 acres (1,170 hectares).
- That's more than 4.5 square miles (11.6 square kilometers) dedicated to the Hyundai project.
- Officials declined to give the sale price of the extra acreage, saying the property was under contract but the deal hadn't been finalized.
- The plant site sits adjacent to Interstate 16 that links Savannah and Macon, not far from its intersection with Interstate 95 that spans the Eastern Seaboard.
- It’s also near to the Port of Savannah, the fourth-busiest U.S. seaport.
- Kemp predicted the Hyundai plant will become one of the biggest customers for Savannah’s port, which has already seen explosive growth in recent years.
- Hyundai Motor Group sells cars under the Hyundai and Kia brands.
- The South Korean automaker already operates two American assembly plants in Montgomery, Alabama, and in West Point, Georgia.
- Perdue stopped Friday in Savannah to campaign with Sarah Palin, the former Alaska governor and onetime GOP vice presidential candidate.
- Palin borrowed one of former President Donald Trump's slogans to praise Perdue as a Republican who “was America First before America First was cool.”
- Perdue has accused Kemp of trying to buy his reelection with the Rivian deal and its big incentives.
- Kemp brushed off the criticism, noting Perdue’s record of outsourcing U.S. jobs to overseas manufactures as a corporate executive.
- The governor said incentives help Georgia compete for big projects with states such as South Carolina, Tennessee and Texas.
- He pointed to the Kia plant in West Point as one that filled a void when the local textile industry shut down years earlier.
- Kemp said, “You’ve seen downtowns that used to be ghost towns, they’re vibrant communities now.”
- Kemp added, “These are investments that we’ll be reaping the benefits of 20 to 30 years down the road.”
