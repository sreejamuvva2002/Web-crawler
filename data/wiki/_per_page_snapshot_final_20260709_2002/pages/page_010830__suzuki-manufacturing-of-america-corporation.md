# Suzuki Manufacturing of America Corporation

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [ATV Factory Tour | HowStuffWorks | BlogNews](https://ferrochromiums.netlify.app/atv-factory-tour-howstuffworks/)  ·  `page_010830`

## Facts

- The Suzuki Manufacturing of America Corporation ATV factory is located in Rome, Georgia.
- An ATV's frame starts out as straight pieces of pipe that are bent into shapes by hydraulic presses and cut to length by robotic plasma cutters.
- It takes about six hours for one of these frame pieces to make its way into a finished ATV.
- Small frame pieces known around the factory as "kiddie parts" combine to make a "mother part."
- Human welders perform some welding work where robot arms cannot fit into very small spaces, using special masks with light-sensitive windows that automatically darken during welding.
- Once frames are fully assembled and welded, an automated system cleans, paints, and bakes them.
- People assemble handlebars and other components before sending them to the assembly line.
- The Rome facility manufactures red and black fenders using an injection-molding machine that uses 3,300 tons of pressure to mold melted resin granules.
- Some of the black fenders receive a camouflage coating at another facility.
- After fenders come off the conveyor, people trim them and apply plastic to protect them while they cure.
- Stickers and logos are applied to the fenders at another station before they go to the assembly line.
- Different fender shapes require different molds that are extremely heavy and require a 25-ton crane to move.
- Employees use pneumatic and battery-powered tools on the assembly line to mount the engine, shock absorbers, fenders, and tires.
- When the article was written, an ATV-in-process spent 102 seconds at each station on the assembly line.
- As of July 2006, an ATV-in-process spends 89 seconds at each station.
- At this speed, the Rome plant can make about 310 ATVs per day, which is equivalent to approximately 80,000 ATVs per year.
- The last two stations on the assembly line are for inspection and packaging.
- A seesaw-like device moves each ATV off of the assembly-line pedestal and onto its wheels.
- Employees add gasoline to the tank and drive the ATV on a drum tester to ensure it runs properly at high speeds without actually moving.
- The factory uses between 200 and 300 gallons of gasoline a week for testing purposes.
- Each finished ATV is placed on a wooden pallet, boxed with heavy-duty staple guns, and carried by a forklift directly to a shipping truck.
- The Rome plant does not include storage space for completed ATVs.
- More than 250 people work in the Rome factory.
- The entire manufacturing and assembly process, including the manufacturing of engines and other parts at other factories, takes more than 300 people.
- If you include the people who design the ATVs, plan the production process and perform other support roles, it takes more than 350 people to build one ATV.
