# Rivian

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-16

**Source:** [Landowners near Rivian plant defeat state demand for legal costs](https://www.onlineathens.com/story/news/state/2025/09/16/landowners-near-rivian-plant-defeat-state-demand-for-legal-costs/86170285007/)  ·  `page_047019`

## Facts

- A half dozen landowners filed a lawsuit that might have halted development of the massive Rivian auto plant near Social Circle.
- Lawyers hired by Georgia and a local development authority asked a Morgan County judge to make the landowners pay for taxpayer dollars spent defending the project, arguing the lawsuit was frivolous.
- The state had sought $338,000 from the landowners for legal costs in the Morgan County lawsuit.
- A Morgan County judge ruled against the state, determining the suit was an earnest attempt to clarify if Georgia officials had authority to waive local land use rules for a private development project on public land.
- The court's decision means the landowners do not have to pay the state’s $338,000 legal costs in the Morgan County lawsuit.
- The state bypassed agricultural zoning for the 2,000-acre property by purchasing it, then leasing it to the Joint Development Authority for Morgan and nearby counties, which in turn leased it to Rivian.
- Nearby landowners sued asking whether a for-profit corporation that makes automobiles on public land was a “governmental purpose” that would trigger legal immunity.
- The state has filed a similar motion in a related Fulton County case, seeking to recover another $203,000 in legal costs.
- The state could push forward with its motion for legal costs in the Fulton County case and could appeal the Morgan County decision.
- A spokeswoman for the Georgia Department of Economic Development did not answer questions about the state’s next legal steps but issued a joint statement with the local development authority about the Rivian project.
- The joint statement said Rivian will soon break ground on its plant construction, marking the beginning of a generational project that will bring long-term economic opportunity to the region and the state.
- The joint statement added that courts have consistently sided with the state and the local authority, and this ruling does not change the status of the Rivian project.
- Landowners’ attorney John Christy said it would have been financially devastating for the landowners to pay the legal costs.
- Christy thinks the judge issued a well-reasoned order that will discourage an appeal by the state and lead to dismissal of the Fulton County demand for legal costs.
- Christy stated, “I think that any appeal would be frivolous,” and noted the standard on appeal would be very high.
- Morgan County Superior Court Judge Stephen Bradley wrote in an 11-page order filed Friday that the state’s claim about a frivolous lawsuit was undermined by its own actions.
- Judge Bradley noted the state voluntarily joined the case as a defendant and hired a large team of lawyers, signaling an expectation for a much more pitched battle.
- A lawyer for the local development authority displayed apparent misgivings about whether the state’s sovereign immunity would transfer to the Rivian project.
- The authority’s lawyer explained in court that she was withdrawing applications for a change in local restrictions because it would have required 34 public hearings.
- Judge Bradley interpreted the withdrawal of applications as a tacit admission that the law governing the matter was unclear.
- Bradley wrote that neither suit filed by the plaintiffs was based on bad faith or short of justiciable issue, calling it an unprecedented case.
- The ruling highlights new and unanswered questions of law concerning governmental land use regulations and public immunity, specifically focusing on sovereign immunity to shield a private, for-profit corporation.
- Bradley wrote that the government’s handling of the property transaction seems clearly designed to circumvent resistance from local voices opposing the Rivian Project.
