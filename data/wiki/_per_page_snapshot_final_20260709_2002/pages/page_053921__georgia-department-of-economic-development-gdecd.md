# Georgia Department of Economic Development (GDEcD)

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2023-12

**Source:** [GDEcD: 50 Years of International Presence - Tifton CEO](https://tiftonceo.com/features/2023/12/50-years-japan-and-europe/)  ·  `page_053921`

## Facts

- The State of Georgia has become a center of international business, with Atlanta and Savannah serving as primary global gateways for people and products through air, sea, and rail.
- Fifty years ago in 1973, state leadership under then-Governor Jimmy Carter began establishing Georgia’s physical presence abroad to kickstart its trajectory to becoming a global hub for business and innovation.
- Coryn Marsik, Director of International Investment at the Georgia Department of Economic Development, stated that having international representation for over 50 years has helped give Georgia's economy remarkable resiliency.
- Georgia’s international offices work to recruit diverse investments that create jobs for Georgians and help local companies reach new global markets.
- The State of Georgia’s office in Tokyo was first established in 1973, providing direct access to rapidly developing markets in East Asia.
- Over the next few decades, Georgia expanded its international presence to include new offices dedicated to serving individual countries.
- Japanese companies continue to be the top international employer in Georgia today.
- Ties with Japan are supported by organizations such as the Japan-America Society of Georgia and the Japan External Trade Organization, alongside Georgia’s role as a charter member in the Southeast U.S.-Japan Alliance.
- The State of Georgia’s Europe Office was established shortly after the Japan office, with professionals serving the European market from Munich, Germany.
- Germany is one of Georgia’s top investor nations and has a robust mobility industry that anchors Georgia’s mission to attract innovative technologies from Europe.
- In 2020, Governor Brian Kemp and a State of Georgia delegation met with automotive companies headquartered in Germany, where electrification and alternative fuels were repeatedly discussed.
- Following the 2020 meetings, the state established a new goal to identify opportunities at every step of the mobility and battery supply chain.
- Just three years later, the mobility and battery supply chain announced more than $25.5 billion in investments.
- Just three years later, the mobility and battery supply chain announced an estimated 30,700 new jobs over the next few years.
- European-based companies enhancing Georgia’s mobility ecosystem include GEDIA Automotive, Aurubis AG, and Heliox.
- The success of Georgia’s mobility and clean tech industries led to representation at the 2023 World Economic Forum in Davos-Klosters, Switzerland.
- Governor Brian Kemp served on a panel at the 2023 World Economic Forum, sharing that investors can count on stability and a great economy and business environment in Georgia.
- Ellen Kraft, Managing Director of the Europe Office, noted that the European market offers opportunities for attracting innovative companies, helping Georgia businesses connect with new customers, and establishing new ties like the North Rhine-Westphalia office in Atlanta.
- International businesses operating in Georgia tap into university-backed research, innovative ecosystems, and solutions-oriented partnerships to stay up-to-date on business trends.
- Commissioner Pat Wilson explains how Georgia is reaping the seeds sown decades ago by visionary leaders regarding the state's industrial success.
