# US EV Battery Manufacturing Sector

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-05-13

**Source:** [Automakers and towns make a $100 billion bet on EV batteries](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2024/05/13/opinion/ev-battery-plants-toyota-ford/)  ·  `page_050070`

## Facts

- Ford Motor is constructing a Blue Oval electric battery plant on 1,500 acres of former farmland in Marshall, Mich.
- Marshall officials believe the plant will diversify the local economy and attract newcomers to the town of just under 7,000 residents.
- James Durian, chief executive of the Marshall Area Economic Development Alliance, warns that without fighting for economic development, the community will continue to decline and young people will leave.
- Across the United States, at least $100 billion has been invested or promised for EV battery plants.
- These investments will create capacity to produce 18 million battery packs per year.
- TechCrunch estimates the plants will employ nearly 65,000 people in total.
- The investment surge is due in part to generous tax credits in the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022.
- Economic development agencies have been chasing battery investments for much of the past decade to give their communities an edge on future technology.
- EV battery plants in the 2020s are comparable to international car plants from the 1980s through the 2000s as billion-dollar investments providing thousands of jobs.
- A major peril for these projects is Americans’ reluctance to commit to electric vehicles.
- When the United Auto Workers struck Ford for six weeks last fall, construction on the Marshall battery plant was halted.
- After the strike, Ford pushed back the launch of the Marshall plant (one of three planned) and reduced its expected workforce due to weaker than expected EV demand.
- In April, Ford slashed production of the F-150 Lightning electric pickup and pushed back the introduction of a new electric SUV planned for Canada.
- Marshall Area Economic Development Alliance CEO James Durian states that communities are at the whim of the economy and electric vehicles but believes the future is electric vehicles.
- Toyota has begun hiring workers for a vast battery complex in Liberty, N.C., featuring 10 production lines.
- The Toyota Liberty plant will employ 5,100 people working in a high-tech clean-room environment.
- The facility will manufacture batteries for both electric cars and hybrid vehicles powered by batteries and gasoline engines.
- Since its initial announcement in 2021, Toyota has boosted its Liberty investment twice, increasing it from $1.9 billion to nearly $14 billion.
- The Liberty plant will be the sole battery supplier for Toyota’s hybrid vehicles made in North America, including the top-selling Camry sedan and 13 other models.
- By 2030, company officials project that about 30 percent of Toyota’s US sales will be EVs.
- Sean Suggs, president of the North Carolina venture, notes that Toyota is preparing for customer demand but acknowledges customers are not yet asking for all EVs.
- The CEO of General Motors once declared her company would go entirely electric by 2035.
- North Carolina previously pushed hard to win a Toyota-Mazda joint venture factory but lost out to Alabama in 2018 before shifting focus to the battery plant.
- Greensboro Chamber of Commerce president Brent Christensen helped lobby for the plant and describes EV batteries as "the gasoline of the future."
- The Environmental Defense Fund estimates that national capacity for 18 million battery packs will far exceed the 10 million needed to power all vehicles announced for the US market.
- There are currently 30 plants under construction with new investments still being announced, alongside potential foreign plants serving the US market.
- Honda recently announced plans to spend $11 billion on a factory for EVs and batteries in Canada, where Volkswagen has also made a major battery investment.
- Although 2.5 million EVs are now in use in the United States, that represents less than 1 percent of the 282 million vehicles registered there.
- A Gallup Organization survey found that only 35 percent of respondents might consider buying an EV in the future, down from 43 percent a year ago.
- The proportion of people who say they’d never buy an EV jumped to 48 percent from 41 percent in 2023.
