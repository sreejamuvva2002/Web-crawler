# Thomson Plastics Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Restructuring gives Thomson Plastics new lease on work | Plastics Machinery Manufacturing](https://www.plasticsmachinerymanufacturing.com/injection-molding/article/13003361/restructuring-gives-thomson-plastics-new-lease-on-work)  ·  `page_011267`

## Facts

- Custom injection molder Thomson Plastics Inc. has reinvented itself through a comprehensive restructuring plan.
- Five years ago, the company's operation reflected its largest customer's production cycle, molding parts for six to eight months and laying off most workers for four months until production restarted.
- The restructuring plan is now paying off with year-round employment for processing staff.
- The company has opened two new plants.
- Thomson Plastics received supplier awards from customers.
- The company acquired its first brand-new molding machines.
- President and COO Rick Kibbey stated, "We are turning the corner, but this is a new Thomson Plastics," during an interview at the company's headquarters in Thomson, Ga.
- Thomson Plastics was founded in 1973 in Mississippi as CAE Plastics to supply golf cart manufacturer Club Car LLC, located near Augusta, Ga.
- In 1995, at the request of Club Car, CAE moved to Thomson, about 30 miles west of Augusta, and changed its name to Thomson Plastics.
- Thomson Plastics has been owned by Altus Capital Partners since 2005.
- Club Car tended to build golf carts from January to June, so Thomson Plastics previously followed that seasonal timeline.
- Other lawn and garden customers followed a similar seasonal schedule.
- The restructuring plan started with changing the seasonal nature of the business by finding additional year-round business.
- Molding automotive parts became a key part of the strategy for Thomson Plastics, which previously had no automotive business.
- Thomson Plastics now has 12 percent to 15 percent of its total business in the Tier 2 automotive sector.
- President Rick Kibbey stated he would like to see the automotive business increase to about 25 percent because it is not seasonal.
- Director of sales and program management Tom Talboys said, "We select our customers very carefully," noting they usually work with customers that have significant programs to launch and form a relationship before moving to the next.
- Until this year, Thomson Plastics purchased used molding machines for all three of its plants.
- Due to the restructuring program, the company purchased seven new presses with 220 tons of clamping force from Borche.
- One new press was delivered to the plant in Thomson earlier this year, and two more were delivered in June.
- Two new presses were delivered to each of the company's plants in Lexington, N.C., and Howell, Mich., in June.
- Between September 2017 and September 2018, Thomson Plastics plans to launch 150 new molds for golf cart, recreational, and automotive products.
- Three of the new Borche machines are earmarked for new projects.
- President Rick Kibbey explained that price, reliability, and performance were key factors in choosing Borche presses over well-known names.
- The company had a relationship with Nationwide UPM Machinery Sales in Troy, Mich., which represents Borche and offered a competitive price for well-equipped machines.
- Kibbey visited three molders who use Borche presses and spoke to their maintenance people about performance before deciding to put them on the floor.
- Regarding future press purchases, Kibbey noted they are looking at others as well, specifically mentioning Engel's economical Wintec model.
- Kibbey commented that while high-priced green Engels look impressive, not every part needs that capability, and the Wintec looks promising.
- Thomson Plastics performs sophisticated molding with machines it purchased and rebuilt, with an average machine age of about 15 years.
- Nearly all 38 presses in the Thomson plant, which have clamping forces ranging from 165 tons to 3,150 tons, are equipped with Wittmann robots.
- The headquarters' three new machines will sit side by side and be run by one operator, also equipped with Wittmann robots.
- In January, Thomson Plastics volunteered to be a beta test site for RJG Inc.'s new CoPilot product, which is currently operating on six presses.
- CoPilot functions as a notification system, communications tool, and coach that standardizes troubleshooting procedures, automatically performs process audits, logs process changes, and notifies operators if variables move outside set tolerances.
- The CoPilot system is designed to be used by operators of any skill level.
- Kibbey stated that when there is a problem, the processor does not have to twist dials because CoPilot tells them what to do, calling it a "road map."
- Kibbey expects CoPilot will eventually be placed on every press companywide, noting the investment price versus losses from not having it makes it a simple decision.
- Thomson Plastics has been using RJG for training for the past six years.
- President Rick Kibbey emphasized that technical expertise is critical, stating, "Technical expertise makes you or breaks you."
- All processing technicians and tool setters have gone through a knowledge assessment by RJG, which conducts training in the plant once a year.
- Employees are sent to RJG training facilities in Georgia and North Carolina, and the company currently has four master molders certified by RJG.
- In addition to CoPilot, Thomson Plastics uses an IQMS reporting system for tracking production and providing reports to support lean manufacturing efforts.
- Kibbey stated the company does not have an allegiance to particular brands of auxiliary equipment because performance is equal across user-friendly options.
- The Thomson plant staff is comfortable with Wittmann robots, while the other two plants use Sepro, Star, and some Wittmanns.
- One collaborative robot from Universal Robotics is used on overmolding a steering wheel.
- The collaborative robot process involves a 700-ton press making the first shot, the robot extracting the part, dropping it on a conveyor for water bath and air drying, then inserting it into a second press for overmolding.
- The use of the collaborative robot enabled Thomson Plastics to reduce the number of operators for the manufacturing cell from three to one per shift.
- The Thomson plant operates three shifts per day, five days a week.
- Kibbey expects to use more collaborative robots in the future.
- Another insert molding project involves a cover for a deep-cycle battery where a robot picks up battery terminal inserts and places them into the mold.
- Automation of the battery cover process improved cycle time by 25 percent and provided more consistency compared to the original manual process.
- Another automation example involves 6-volt battery cases that are molded, dropped onto a conveyor for gate seal-off testing, and then sorted.
- The company has extensive experience with gas-assisted molding using the Cinpres gas-assist process.
- Kibbey stated they have 25 to 30 different types of gas-assist parts being molded at the Thomson plant every day.
- The company has taken over many gas-assist projects that were in trouble elsewhere.
- Kibbey credits the company's success to staff's ability to use equipment to its full potential, with the company acting as its own integrator to put together work cells.
- Kibbey views buying pieces and assembling work cells as an advantage because the workforce learns while doing it and owns the finished systems.
- The team understands the capabilities of each press and does not try to mold parts the machine is not capable of running.
- Club Car recently named Thomson Plastics its supplier of the year for 2017.
- Kibbey said the supplier award is evidence that they have turned the corner.
