# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-05-23

**Source:** [2 workers killed in separate fatal workplace accidents in Georgia - World Socialist Web Site](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2025/05/23/bjmo-m23.html)  ·  `page_000908`

## Facts

- Two workers were killed this week in separate workplace accidents at two of Georgia’s most high-profile manufacturing sites: the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America battery plant near Savannah and the Hanwha Qcells solar panel plant in Cartersville.
- The deaths occurred within six weeks of the death of Ronald Adams, Sr. at the Stellantis Dundee Engine Complex in Michigan.
- Adams, a 63-year-old worker, was killed on April 7 when an overhead gantry engaged, pinning him to a conveyor and causing fatal crushing injuries to his upper torso.
- On Tuesday, May 20, a construction worker was killed at the HL-GA Battery facility, part of the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America complex in Ellabell, Georgia.
- The worker was fatally injured when an unsecured load fell from a forklift during loading operations shortly before 11:00 a.m.
- The identity of the worker, who was employed by a subcontractor involved in the construction of the battery plant, has not been released.
- The HL-GA Battery plant is not yet operational and is a key part of Hyundai’s $12.6 billion investment in Georgia.
- The HL-GA Battery plant is slated to supply batteries for Hyundai’s new electric vehicle assembly plant.
- In March of this year, Sunbok You, a 67-year-old worker, was struck and dragged by a forklift at the Hyundai Metaplant construction site, resulting in fatal injuries.
- In April 2023, Victor Gamboa, a 43-year-old contractor, died after falling 60 feet when his safety line failed at the same site.
- An OSHA investigation cited contractors for failing to provide workers with adequate safety gear.
- Repeated workplace deaths indicate that worker safety is being subordinated to Hyundai Motor Group’s drive to speed up the project and cut costs ahead of the battery facility opening.
- On Monday, May 19, a fatality occurred at the Hanwha Qcells solar panel plant in Cartersville, Georgia.
- Firefighters responded to a call at 7:15 p.m. and found Marion Jose Rugama, a 33-year-old resident of Norcross, dead on top of a tank.
- It has not been established if Rugama was a direct employee of Hanwha Qcells or a contractor, though contract labor use is widespread at the facility.
- Local authorities reported the oxygen level at the top of the tank was only 15 percent, far below the minimum required to sustain life.
- A nitrogen leak is suspected as the cause of Rugama’s death, pending autopsy and toxicology screening.
- The Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America (HMGMA) in Ellabell, Georgia is the largest single economic development project in Georgia’s history.
- HMGMA is backed by Hyundai and partners including LG Energy Solution and SK On.
- The sprawling complex is expected to employ 8,500 workers by 2031 and includes both vehicle assembly and battery manufacturing operations.
- The facility is being constructed at breakneck speed with the goal of making Georgia a hub for electric vehicle and battery production.
- The Hanwha Qcells plant in Cartersville is part of a $2.5 billion investment by the South Korean clean energy giant.
- Hanwha Qcells also operates a major facility in Dalton, Georgia.
- Together, the Hyundai and Hanwha plants form the largest solar manufacturing operation in the Western Hemisphere.
- There are plans to expand the Cartersville facility even further in 2025.
- Neither the workforce at Hyundai Metaplant nor the Hanwha Qcells Cartersville facility are unionized.
- The use of contract labor allows companies to cut costs, evade responsibility, and fragment the workforce.
- Contract workers are often paid less, receive fewer benefits, have less job security than direct employees, and are less likely to receive adequate safety training.
- According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, 5,486 workers died on the job nationwide in 2022, with transportation, construction, and manufacturing ranking among the deadliest industries.
- In February 2024, OSHA reported an investigation of the Columbus, Georgia facility of Aludyne Columbus LLC, a prominent global manufacturer of auto parts with 30 production facilities worldwide.
- OSHA’s findings uncovered 22 serious violations and were followed by proposed penalties of $182,344.
- Violations included failure to provide protective gear and enforce safety protocols where employees worked dangerously close to energized power lines without insulated tools.
- Employees at the Aludyne facility were exposed to airborne silica at levels up to 15 times higher than the permissible limit.
- Workers lacked proper respiratory equipment, and the company failed to conduct fit testing for those exposed to silicosis hazards.
- OSHA Atlanta-West Area Office Director Jeffery Stawowy stated that Aludyne Columbus LLC’s failure to prioritize employee safety nearly cost a worker their life and allowed overexposure to silica.
- Workers at StarPlus Energy, a joint venture between Stellantis and Samsung SDI in Kokomo, Indiana, exposed dangerous working conditions on social media.
- Workers reported no medical emergency response, no safety data sheets, and no workers’ comp doctors in the facility.
- Injured workers at StarPlus Energy must climb four flights of stairs to seek assistance.
- The International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees (IWA-RFC) launched an independent investigation into the death of Ronald Adams.
- Stellantis, the UAW apparatus, and MIOSHA have refused to release information about the circumstances or official investigation into Adams’s fatality.
