# Georgia Economic Development Deal Evaluation Process

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2021-07-27

**Source:** [How Georgia evaluates economic development deals to bring in more jobs](https://www.poughkeepsiejournal.com/story/news/2021/07/27/how-ga-potential-economic-agreements-impact-jobs-pay/7964894002/)  ·  `page_050111`

## Facts

- Carl Campbell serves as executive director of the Dalton-Whitfield Joint Development Authority.
- The Hanwha Q Cells solar manufacturing facility was pursued with a larger strategic goal beyond immediate financial return.
- After the recession, the Dalton-Whitfield area lost 10,000 jobs in the carpet and flooring industry.
- Campbell stated that bringing in Q Cells was intended to prove the area could attract manufacturers besides flooring.
- Local authorities were willing to offer generous incentives for the Hanwha project to demonstrate economic diversification.
- Carl Campbell described the Hanwha deal as "richer than we've done in the past" but believed it paid off.
- Hanwha Q Cells moved into its building in December 2018.
- Hanwha Q Cells hired 500 people within three months of moving in.
- State and local governments calculate if money given to companies is worth it by running cost-benefit analyses.
- These analyses look at economic impact from new jobs, wages, and potential economic impact from new residents, including school and public safety costs versus property and sales tax revenue.
- Factors are summed over time to create a total economic impact known as the net present value (NPV) of the project.
- The Hanwha plant received a payment in lieu of taxes (PILOT) agreement guaranteeing no property taxes for 10 years and half property tax payments for the next five years.
- Dalton-Whitfield did not run a standard cost-benefit analysis on the Hanwha project.
- Campbell explained that most Hanwha workers lived outside the community, making standard software (LOCI) inaccurate for counting expected costs and benefits of expanded employment.
- Alfie Meek is principal and chief economist at the Economic Impact Group, which licenses LOCI software.
- Meek disagreed with skipping cost-benefit analysis, stating LOCI can use community-specific patterns and remains best practice.
- Meek noted that communities often do not understand local financial costs related to economic development projects, especially regarding new households.
- The SK Battery project in Jackson County had a state-estimated NPV of $5.7 million.
- The SK Battery project was estimated to create up to 2,700 more jobs through increased economic activity.
- The Wayfair facility in Athens had an estimated NPV of about $150,000.
- Many Georgia municipalities use LOCI software for cost-benefit analyses using community-specific data.
- Meek stated that anything over $100K NPV is probably quite positive when evaluating deals at the local level.
- LOCI can analyze cost to specific entities, such as total county tax revenue impact, as well as overall economy.
- Meek noted that a negative fiscal impact on a jurisdiction does not necessarily mean a project should be rejected if it meets larger strategic goals.
- Local communities need to know the cost to achieve strategic goals before moving forward with projects.
- Major industrial projects are part of Georgia's economic development and can bring thousands of jobs and significant local government investments.
- The USA TODAY Network-Georgia examined six major economic development projects across Georgia using open records requests, state data, and official interviews.
- The examined projects included the Hanwha Q Cell facility, SK Battery plant in Jackson County, Amazon facilities in Bibb County, Wayfair facilities in Athens and Savannah, and a warehouse development project under development in Savannah.
