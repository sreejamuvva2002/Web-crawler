# Hyundai Motor and LG Energy Solution Joint Venture Plant in Ellabell, Georgia

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-10-16

**Source:** [[ED] Gov. Kemp of Georgia should address Korea's collective sense of betrayal - The Korea Times](https://www.koreatimes.co.kr/opinion/editorial/20251016/ed-gov-kemp-of-georgia-should-address-koreas-collective-sense-of-betrayal)  ·  `page_056155`

## Facts

- Governor Brian Kemp is expected to arrive in Korea soon.
- Georgia was not well-known to Korea until events on Sept. 4.
- U.S. immigration officials searched, shackled, and detained more than 300 Korean nationals during a raid on a plant under construction in a joint venture by Hyundai Motor and LG Energy Solution (LGES) in Ellabell, Georgia.
- The plant is part of a massive $7.6 billion investment.
- LGES was in the process of constructing a battery factory at the site when the immigration raid took place.
- Detained Korean nationals included individuals directly employed by LG and its suppliers.
- Immigration officials stated the detained workers lacked proper visas or had overstayed them.
- Most detained workers were on a B-1 (temporary business visitor) visa or through the visa-free Electronic System for Travel Authorization (ESTA).
- The U.S. suddenly and unexpectedly deemed the visa-free ESTA improper for anyone seeking to work in the United States.
- Korea and the U.S. worked together to seek the release of the detained workers and find better visa solutions.
- Seoul and Washington officials agreed that Korean workers investing in the U.S. could stay under B-1 visas or ESTA for installing, servicing, and repairing foreign-purchased equipment at their U.S. plants.
- Gov. Kemp called for visa reform in the aftermath of the raid.
- LGES has resumed sending workers to the Georgia plant.
- Department of Homeland Security officials promoted the Georgia plant raid as 'the largest single-site enforcement operation in U.S. history'.
- Koreans with family members among the approximately 300 detainees spent days waiting for news of their release.
- The Korean public was shocked by the raid, and anti-U.S. sentiment percolated.
- Kemp is familiar with Seoul's economic investments and plans in Georgia from his third trip to Korea.
- The U.S. has yet to allot an appropriate H-1B visa quota for Korean nationals comparable to countries like Singapore.
- The two sides have not yet agreed on setting up a dedicated visa for highly skilled Korean workers.
- Trump issued an order requiring anyone newly applying for an H-1B visa to pay a $100,000 fee to encourage businesses to hire Americans.
- Kemp should continue pressing for improved visa policies for Korean workers staffing plants and creating jobs in Georgia.
- Kemp should apologize for the September raid to assuage Korean concerns and ensure Georgia's business environment remains vibrant.
- The governor's visit was planned well before the raid.
