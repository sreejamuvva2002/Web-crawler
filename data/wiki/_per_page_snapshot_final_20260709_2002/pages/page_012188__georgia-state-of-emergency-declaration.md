# Georgia State of Emergency Declaration

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** 2022-04-29

**Source:** [Q&A: Untangling Georgia’s supply chain emergency - Terry College of Business](https://www.terry.uga.edu/qa-untangling-georgias-supply-chain-emergency/)  ·  `page_012188`

## Facts

- On April 14, Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp declared a state of emergency stemming from ongoing disruptions to the state’s supply chain infrastructure.
- The emergency measure eases regulations on the trucking industry and prevents price gouging on gasoline and diesel fuel.
- The declaration aims to remedy supply chain disruptions stemming from the COVID-19 pandemic and Russia’s invasion of Ukraine.
- UGA supply chain expert and Terry College of Business management lecturer Marty Parker explained that supply chain problems are bigger than one state or set of regulations.
- Georgia's supply chain is difficult, impacted by labor shortages, truck shortages, the war in Ukraine, and recent COVID outbreaks in China.
- The supply chain has not fully recovered from original shocks in 2020.
- The crisis is described as a perfect storm involving rapid economic rebound post-pandemic, COVID lockdowns in China, the Ukraine war, booming U.S. economy, Great Resignation labor shortages, and truck shortages.
- Bottlenecks include lag time between rising prices and decreasing demand for goods, port congestion, warehouse space shortage, and growth in e-commerce.
- Supply chain issues originate with raw materials flowing through China, Brazil, and other countries facing manufacturing issues, followed by port congestion and trucker shortages.
- The trucking industry faces labor shortages as drivers reach retirement and few young people enter the field.
- Trucking is highly regulated regarding when drivers can sleep, eat, drive, and park, with electronic devices monitoring them at all times.
- Companies are investing in better analytics and automation to improve truck driver culture, as discussed at the Trucking Profitability Strategies Conference.
- Gov. Kemp's order removes some trucking restrictions but includes anti-price gouging provisions that Parker criticizes for creating shortages, comparing them to 1970s gasoline price caps and NYC rent controls.
- Georgia ports increased the number of containers brought in by 50 percent between 2015 and 2021.
- Ports expect to grow capacity now that dredging is complete.
- There is a shortage of warehouse space, though more space will be built out over time.
- Companies trying to build warehouse space face shortages of building materials and labor.
- A large warehouse project in Savannah is on hold because the builder could not get roofing material despite having all other parts.
- Farming faces a rough season due to record drought in the west, tractor shortages, fertilizer shortages from the Ukraine war, and shortages of other farming equipment.
- Small companies making up the bulk of Georgia's economy are hurt by needing to pay upfront for imported goods well in advance of manufacturing or shipping, depleting working capital before sales.
- Money is getting tighter as the Fed fights inflation.
- Supply chain problems may improve due to a looming economic slowdown from higher interest rates slowing home building and large purchases.
- The crisis is viewed as an anomaly in a generally healthy system caused by the pandemic and demand changes, but it is leading to a long-term shift.
- Long-term shifts involve solving supply chain problems through better use of data, automation, and robotics for a more resilient supply chain with better information processing.
- Conditions are expected to improve once COVID is controlled globally.
