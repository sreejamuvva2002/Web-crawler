# Georgia Electric Vehicle Incentive Programs

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-12-31

**Source:** [EV Incentives in Georgia – Electric Vehicle Rebates, Tax Credits & Charging Programs | Net-Zero USA – Energy Incentive Calculator](https://www.usa-net-zero.com/ev-incentives/ga)  ·  `page_017703`

## Facts

- EV tax credits, charging rebates, and utility programs are available for Georgia residents to save on electric vehicles.
- Charging cost in Georgia ranges from $0.10–$0.15/kWh.
- Home charger costs range from $700–$2,400.
- Annual fuel savings for EVs in Georgia are $800–$1,500 compared to gas vehicles.
- Georgia drivers can charge an EV at home for the equivalent of roughly $1.00–$1.50 per gallon of gasoline.
- The federal Clean Vehicle Credit (Internal Revenue Code Section 30D) historically offered up to $7,500 for qualifying new electric vehicles.
- The Used Clean Vehicle Credit (Section 25E) offered up to $4,000 for qualifying used EVs.
- Both federal EV tax credits were terminated for vehicles placed in service after December 31, 2025 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.
- Georgia residents who purchased an eligible EV before that date may still claim the credit on their 2025 federal tax return.
- The Alternative Fuel Vehicle Refueling Property Credit (Section 30C) provided up to $1,000 for home EV charger installation.
- Commercial Section 30C credits for businesses installing chargers in low-income or non-urban census tracts ended on the same schedule as the residential credit.
- Georgia state-level EV programs vary year to year as legislatures appropriate or sunset funding.
- Common Georgia state incentive categories include income tax credits for EV purchase, sales-tax exemptions on vehicles or charging equipment, reduced registration fees, HOV-lane access stickers, and rebates funded through the Volkswagen Diesel Settlement or NEVI corridor programs.
- Some states route incentives through their Department of Energy, Department of Environmental Quality, or Department of Transportation rather than through the tax code.
- Funding pools for state EV incentives are often capped and close once the budget is exhausted.
- Many states fund equity-focused EV rebates that target lower-income households with larger amounts, often $2,500–$7,500.
- Public charging in Georgia is expanding rapidly thanks to the federal NEVI (National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure) program.
- The NEVI program directs roughly $5 billion across all 50 states over five years to build DC fast-charging stations along Alternative Fuel Corridors.
- Georgia's NEVI allocation funds charging stations roughly every 50 miles along designated interstates.
- Each NEVI-funded site in Georgia is required to have at least four 150 kW DC fast chargers.
- A Level 2 (240V) home charging station typically costs $400–$900 for the unit.
- Home charger installation costs range from $300–$1,500 depending on panel capacity, garage wiring distance, and required electrical service upgrades.
- Some Georgia utilities offer rebates of $250–$1,000 toward home charger installation.
- Utility charger rebates often require customers to agree to off-peak charging or allow demand-response control during grid peaks.
- Georgia utilities increasingly offer EV-specific time-of-use (TOU) rate plans that significantly reduce home charging costs.
- Overnight electricity on TOU rates is typically priced at half or less of the standard daytime rate, often dropping to $0.05–$0.08 per kWh.
- Charging an EV exclusively during off-peak hours can cut effective per-mile fuel cost to $0.02–$0.03.
- Other utility programs include managed-charging credits of $50–$150 per year for allowing utilities to delay charging during grid peaks.
- Utilities also offer bill credits for installing smart chargers that report usage data.
- Pairing rooftop solar with an EV is a cost-effective way for Georgia homeowners to lock in low transportation costs.
- A typical 7–10 kW solar array generates enough surplus electricity to cover an additional 10,000–12,000 miles of EV driving per year.
- Once installed, the marginal cost of driving an EV charged with rooftop solar is essentially zero.
- Combining solar with battery storage and a TOU rate plan can result in a combined payback period of 7–11 years on the full system for Georgia homeowners.
- At average Georgia residential electricity rates, charging a typical EV for a full 250-mile range costs roughly $9–$13.
- On time-of-use overnight rates, charging for a 250-mile range drops to $4–$7.
- Compared to a gasoline vehicle averaging 30 mpg at $3.50/gallon ($29 for the same 250 miles), home charging cuts fuel costs by 50–80%.
- Georgia's public charging network is growing through federal NEVI funding, private investment from networks like Tesla Supercharger, ChargePoint, EVgo, and Electrify America, and utility-led infrastructure programs.
- DC fast chargers in Georgia are clustered along interstates and in metro areas.
- For most Georgia EV owners with daily commutes over 30 miles or a household with two EVs, a Level 2 charger is recommended.
- A Level 2 charger cuts charging time from 40+ hours (Level 1) down to 4–8 hours overnight.
- Total installed cost for a home Level 2 charger is typically $700–$2,400.
- A 7–10 kW solar system in Georgia can offset both home electricity and EV charging, effectively giving free fuel after the system is paid off.
