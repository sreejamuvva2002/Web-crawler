# Chatham County Public EV Charging Station Initiative

**Type:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-25

**Source:** [Chatham County plans new EV charging stations](https://www.savannahnow.com/story/news/local/2026/03/25/chatham-county-plans-new-ev-charging-stations/89303701007/)  ·  `page_000530`

## Facts

- Chatham County is planning to install public electric vehicle (EV) charging stations in unincorporated areas outside Savannah.
- The project aims to serve all residents fairly and equitably, addressing EV adoption barriers for lower-income residents.
- Nine potential locations were identified based on criteria including accessibility, flood risk, and service to low-income populations.
- The project was funded last year by a $21 million grant from the Southeast Crescent Regional Commission for infrastructure and economic development projects across the Southeastern United States.
- Site recommendations followed three stakeholder meetings and two public engagement forums.
- Consultants visited 50 spots throughout the county and announced nine locations on Mar. 24 for Georgetown, Garden City, Thunderbolt, and the islands.
- Proposed locations had to be accessible along evacuation routes, unlikely to flood, serve low-income populations, and feel safe.
- Chatham County Resilience Program Administrator Jackie Jackson stated the goal is to look at areas where residents cannot afford to install chargers at home.
- As of Mar. 19 records from the Georgia Department of Revenue, Chatham County has over 3,000 active EV registrations and over 8,000 hybrid registrations out of more than 245,000 total registrations.
- The Savannah Morning News reported just over 2,000 EV registrations in Chatham County in 2024.
- Market research firm Luth Research found EVs are commonly used by high-income households attracted by government rebate incentives who live in cities with ample charging ports.
- Jackie Jackson noted that some residents cannot get an EV because there is nowhere to charge, or their apartment complexes lack charging systems.
- The county aims to build a foundation of charging infrastructure that is fair and equitable to all residents regardless of income.
- Building many stations will require third-party buy-ins due to the difficult business case of building without existing customers.
- Chris White from Kittelson and Associates highlighted the project is addressing interesting public-private partnerships.
- A pilot project possibility involves lower-income qualified housing developments where residents can rent EVs in addition to using chargers.
- Federal rollbacks to incentives make lower-income buyers less likely to purchase their own cars.
- Consultants used Google Maps, Georgia Power energy grid maps, and evacuation routes to ground survey nearly 50 locations across unincorporated Chatham County.
- Locations were required to be out of the floodplain and along evacuation routes, with chargers also capable of powering phones and household items during emergencies.
- The county aims to build Level 2 and Level 3 chargers, while noting Level 1 takes about 12 hours and Level 3 (DC Fast Charging) takes about an hour.
- Garden City Library will receive Level 2 chargers with fees based on kilowatt-hours.
- Charles Brooks Park Lot #2 will receive Level 2 chargers.
- Islands Library and High School will receive Level 2 chargers for employees, students, visitors, and nearby police station vehicles, with hourly fees.
- Lake Mayer Park will host a DC Fast charging hub on three major evacuation routes, sized for larger vans and CAT buses.
- Thunderbolt Town Hall will have Level 2 charging for condo residents and visitors, potentially for electric bikes and watercraft, with hourly pay.
- Island Town Center will host a DC Fast Charging Hub owned and operated by iONNA, co-owned by Hyundai, at a junction of major evacuation routes.
- Thrift Store Plaza will host a DC Fast Charging Hub.
- A former paint store near the intersection of Ogeechee Road and Chatham Parkway will host a DC Fast charging hub.
- A retail plaza under development near the intersection of Highways 204 and 17 will host a DC Fast Charging Hub.
- Areas of high-flood risk, including Tybee Island, were avoided for site selection.
- Resident Azania Heyward-James raised safety concerns at the potential site near Highways 204 and 17 intersections, noting residents may not leave vehicles for hours and need adequate lighting for students, elderly people, and women.
- Chris White responded that resident safety is paramount but charging areas could transform scary spaces into exciting ones, using stations as space activators.
- The City of Savannah was the first to participate in Georgia’s “Make Ready” program, a Georgia Power initiative covering installation costs.
- In 2021, Savannah partnered with Georgia Power to install four EV stations, each equipped with eight chargers.
- Jackie Jackson stated the county is building on what Savannah already has.
- Chris White noted the county must find infrastructure funding and may need to build roads and stores to attract third-party owners and operators.
- Sidewalks and road widenings might be required to compel third-party owners and operators for some stations.
- Changes to codes and ordinances may be necessary, as lot sizes may not currently allow fire department trucks to enter in case of an electric fire.
