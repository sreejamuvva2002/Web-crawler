# Solvay Specialty Polymers USA, LLC

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-01

**Source:** [Augusta communities speak out against Solvay chemical plant expansion - Southern Environmental Law Center](https://www.selc.org/press-release/augusta-communities-speak-out-against-solvay-chemical-plant-expansion/)  ·  `page_050158`

## Facts

- Residents, environmental organizations, and members of local faith communities joined a virtual hearing held by the Georgia Environmental Protection Division to convey concerns about Solvay's plans to significantly expand its operations and drastically increase toxic air emissions in Augusta.
- Solvay Specialty Polymers USA, LLC operates a large chemical plant in South Augusta located in the middle of a predominantly Black community where half of the population earns a lower income.
- Decades of legacy pollution in the areas surrounding Solvay's facility have led to residents facing serious health problems, including asthma and other respiratory conditions, increased risk of heart disease, and a lower overall life expectancy.
- Solvay’s facility is a major source of hazardous air pollutants with the potential to emit approximately 50 tons of hazardous air pollutants per year if allowed to expand operations.
- The proposed expansion would potentially include more than 25 tons of toxic chlorobenzene emissions annually.
- Nearly 80 people attended the hearing to ask state regulators about public health implications, citing Augusta’s air quality issues and high asthma rates.
- Hearing participants questioned how the plant’s emissions would further impact Augusta’s noncompliance status with federal air pollution standards.
- The Augusta metro area has been named by the American Lung Association as one of the nation’s worst 25 metro areas for particle pollution.
- The Augusta metro area was ranked as the worst in the southeastern United States for particle pollution.
- SELC submitted written comments on behalf of Savannah Riverkeeper and 13 local organizations and churches regarding the permit application.
- Comments pointed out that Solvay has been improperly classified as a minor rather than major source of criteria pollutants.
- Comments highlighted significant issues with Solvay’s compliance with the Clean Air Act more broadly.
- SELC Staff Attorney Patrick Anderson stated that especially given the potential for additional significant pollution, Solvay should be classified as a major source and held to stricter emissions standards.
- Patrick Anderson urged EPD to deny the permit or address glaring issues and lack of information in the company’s application, and to release a revised draft permit for additional public notice and comment.
- Savannah Riverkeeper Tonya Bonitatibus noted that while communities have heard from the company that it wants to be a good neighbor, the permit application omits critical data on how much more pollution residents will face.
- Tonya Bonitatibus stated that EPD should require Solvay to provide all information in its application to the public before allowing any expansion to move forward.
