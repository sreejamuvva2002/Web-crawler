# Federal Clean Vehicle and Charging Infrastructure Tax Credits

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-30

**Source:** [Alternative Fuels Data Center: Tax Credits for Electric Vehicles and Charging Infrastructure](https://afdc.energy.gov/laws/ev-tax-credits)  ·  `page_002550`

## Facts

- Federal tax credits were available to consumers, fleets, businesses, and tax-exempt entities that acquired new, used, and commercial clean vehicles before September 30, 2025.
- Credits were also available for alternative fuel refueling infrastructure placed in service before June 30, 2026.
- As of July 5, 2025, clean vehicle tax credits were available for vehicles acquired before September 30, 2025.
- Taxpayers purchasing an eligible vehicle may qualify for a tax credit of up to $7,500 for qualified new vehicles and up to $4,000 for qualified pre-owned vehicles.
- Eligibility for the clean vehicle tax credit is based on income and vehicle requirements.
- Buyers of pre-owned clean vehicles must obtain a copy of the vehicle history report during the purchase and keep it for their records.
- Starting Jan. 1, 2024, purchasers may lower a vehicle’s purchase price by transferring the clean vehicle tax credit to a registered dealer at the time of sale.
- The credit transfer mechanism is available at over 9,500 registered dealers rather than waiting to claim it on next year’s tax return.
- Commercial fleets and tax-exempt organizations buying qualified commercial clean vehicles may qualify for a credit per vehicle.
- The maximum commercial credit is $7,500 for vehicles with gross vehicle weight ratings under 14,000 pounds and $40,000 for all other vehicles.
- The commercial credit equals the lesser of: 15% of basis for plug-in hybrid vehicles or 30% of basis for full battery or fuel cell EVs, or the incremental cost depending on the year placed in service.
- There is no limit on the number of commercial clean vehicle credits an organization can claim.
- IRS Notices 2023-9 and 2024-5 created a safe harbor allowing eligible entities to identify tax credit values for commercial clean vehicles acquired in 2023 and 2024 using a U.S. Department of Energy incremental cost analysis.
- The form to claim the commercial clean vehicle tax credit was under development at the time of publication.
- For the first time, tax-exempt entities can qualify to receive direct payment (elective pay) for a federal clean vehicle tax credit if they meet requirements and register with the IRS.
- Dealers and sellers must register with the IRS using the Energy Credits Online tool and report clean vehicle sales to be eligible for credits.
- Eligible clean vehicles include all-electric and plug-in hybrid EVs under 14,000 pounds GVWR with at least 7 kilowatt hours battery capacity, as well as fuel cell EVs.
- Dealers can identify specific vehicle eligibility by entering the VIN into the dealer portal webpage.
- Dealers must provide purchasers with a copy of the accepted seller report (time of sale report) documenting vehicle eligibility.
- IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-26 Section 6 provides rules for updating seller reports in cases of errors, cancelled sales, or vehicle returns.
- Transferring the tax credit at sale allows customers to immediately save up to $7,500 per new vehicle and up to $4,000 per pre-owned vehicle.
- Eligible customers transfer their credit to the dealer so the dealer provides a reduced purchase price.
- Dealers submit transfer information electronically via the Energy Credits Online tool and receive an advance payment for the credit value.
- The IRS expects to issue advance payments to dealers within 72 hours of when the customer elects to transfer the credit.
- EV manufacturers must enter a written agreement with the IRS and report information for each vehicle to qualify for clean vehicle tax credits.
- Manufacturers can submit documentation demonstrating compliance for upfront review by the IRS per Revenue Procedure 2024-26.
- Dealers cannot sell vehicles for a clean vehicle credit until qualified manufacturers have submitted the VIN and the vehicle is determined eligible.
- Fuel cell EVs are not required to be made by a qualified manufacturer, but doing so makes it easier for consumers to claim credits.
- As of July 5, 2025, the alternative fuel infrastructure tax credit is available for property placed in service by June 30, 2026.
- Home EV charging equipment purchases may be eligible for a 30% tax credit up to $1,000 covering the port, essential components/parts, and installation labor.
- A credit in the same amount is available for energy storage associated with the home charger.
- Home charging eligibility requires the installation location to be in an eligible census tract.
- Businesses, fleets, or tax-exempt entities purchasing EV charging equipment may be eligible for a 6% tax credit of the cost starting Jan. 1, 2023, with a maximum of $100,000 per single item of property.
- A single item of property is defined as each charging port or energy storage property, and the credit also applies to fueling dispensers and storage for hydrogen, natural gas, propane, E85, or B20 biodiesel blends.
- Projects meeting prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements may be eligible for a 30% tax credit instead of 6%.
- Tax-exempt entities can qualify to receive full payment for the federal alternative fuel infrastructure tax credit via direct pay starting Jan. 1, 2023.
- Tax-exempt entities may obtain the credit indirectly by having sellers or installers treated as the taxpayer and disclosing the amount to negotiate a lower upfront cost.
- Tax-exempt entities seeking technical assistance for EV and zero emission vehicle infrastructure can refer to the Joint Office of Energy and Transportation Technical Assistance resources.
- The page notes that summaries are not formal IRS guidance and taxpayers should consult professionals regarding eligibility.
