# Rivian

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-03-12

**Source:** [Senators question economic development chief on delayed EV plant | Georgia Public Broadcasting](https://www.gpb.org/news/2024/03/12/senators-question-economic-development-chief-on-delayed-ev-plant)  ·  `page_060644`

## Facts

- Georgia Commissioner of Economic Development Pat Wilson expressed disappointment with Rivian's decision to delay building a new plant east of Atlanta but said the company remains committed to Georgia.
- Pat Wilson told members of a state Senate budget subcommittee that "It stings," and noted that Rivian has said they will live up to all their promises and commitments, calling it an active project until heard otherwise.
- Rivian executives announced last Thursday a pause in the company’s plans to invest $5 billion in an EV plant near Covington that would create 7,500 jobs.
- Instead of building in Georgia, Rivian will manufacture its new R2 midsize SUV model at an existing plant in Normal, Ill.
- To land the deal, the state and the local development authority offered Rivian $1.5 billion in incentives including tax credits, a 25-year no-cost lease, and $198.1 million in site and road improvements on 1,978 acres.
- The state and the Joint Development Authority of Jasper, Morgan, Newton, and Walton Counties have spent $141 million to acquire the property, prepare the site and drain wetlands.
- Rivian has made two payments in lieu of taxes (PILOT) to the development authority totaling $3 million.
- Sixty percent of those PILOT payments will go to local school systems.
- Pat Wilson stated that the payments don’t stop because they’re paused, and the community will receive a benefit even though there’s no active construction.
- Under an agreement Rivian signed off on in 2022, the company must fulfill at least 80% of its investment and jobs commitments by the end of 2030.
- Rivian has committed to maintaining those timelines.
- Several senators expressed discomfort with letting the site sit empty potentially until the end of 2030 waiting for Rivian to fulfill its part of the deal.
- Pat Wilson expects Rivian’s intentions for the site likely will become clear well before 2030.
- If Rivian doesn’t come through on the project, Wilson said the site is attractive enough that it won’t be hard to find another manufacturing tenant, stating, "We would have no trouble at all filling that spot."
- Sen. Jason Anavitarte, R-Dallas, chairman of the Senate Republican Caucus, noted there is a distinct difference between a pause and a pullout.
- Senate Majority Whip Randy Robertson, R-Cataula, expressed less hope, saying he would be surprised if Rivian comes back to Georgia and starts making good on their promises.
