# Salient Energy

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2021-01-04

**Source:** [Zinc-ion Batteries Are a Scalable Alternative to Lithium-ion](https://www.powermag.com/zinc-ion-batteries-are-a-scalable-alternative-to-lithium-ion/)  ·  `page_008215`

## Facts

- Lithium-ion batteries control more than 90% of the global grid battery storage market according to some estimates.
- The lithium-ion supply chain is becoming constrained due to insufficient raw material production to meet imminent demand from EVs and renewable energy storage installations.
- A comparison between adoption projections and miner investments shows that lithium-ion’s supply chain will soon be very constrained.
- This shortage will become more severe as governments around the world pass legislation accelerating the transition to EVs and renewables.
- Lithium is unlikely to be replaced for EVs because its position on the periodic table guarantees it will remain the king of energy density.
- Renewable energy storage primarily requires a low lifetime cost rather than high energy density.
- Lithium-ion’s safety risks make it a poor fit for markets seeking to place massive battery packs in people’s homes and businesses.
- Non-lithium batteries are far more likely to succeed in renewable energy storage markets.
- Many companies have tried building new energy storage batteries over the past decades, but none have really succeeded.
- Technologies that made it out of the lab were eroded by rapidly decreasing lithium-ion manufacturing costs before they could scale up.
- Any alternative to lithium-ion batteries needs to adopt standard manufacturing processes to allow for rapid and low-cost scale-up.
- Zinc-ion batteries are the only non-lithium technology that can adopt lithium-ion’s manufacturing process to make an attractive solution for renewable energy storage.
- Salient Energy’s zinc-ion battery cell functions using intercalation, with zinc ions reacting at both electrodes and traveling through a water-based electrolyte.
- During discharge, zinc metal at the anode dissolves into the electrolyte as zinc ions while zinc ions are absorbed into the cathode; this process reverses during charge.
- Lithium-ion batteries use intercalation where lithium reacts at both the anode and cathode through a liquid electrolyte.
- In lithium-ion batteries, the electrolyte only serves as a conduit between electrodes rather than storing large amounts of ions, allowing for compact designs.
- Most other battery chemistries do not use intercalation and depend on each electrode reacting with the electrolyte, requiring large quantities of electrolyte to store reactants.
- Lithium-ion electrodes can be built with relatively thin coatings of active material totaling less than 0.1 millimeters in thickness.
- Lead-acid batteries have electrodes multiple millimeters thick, whereas lithium-ion's thin coatings allow for higher energy efficiency and better performance in high-power applications.
- Lithium-ion manufacturing involves applying thin coatings to metal substrates, placing a thin separator between electrodes, winding them together, injecting electrolyte, sealing the cell, and performing formation cycling.
- Formation cycling causes reactions within the cell that protect its longevity.
- A novel battery chemistry must store large amounts of energy in a small amount of active material at both electrodes to be compatible with lithium-ion manufacturing processes.
- Non-active components like metal substrates and separators represent about one-third of a lithium-ion cell’s material costs.
- Using excess electrolyte requires thicker separators and limits the amount of energy that can be stored in a cell container of a given size.
- Very few battery chemistries meet the requirements for thin electrodes and minimal electrolyte while also meeting market cost and performance demands.
- Zinc-ion batteries meet all requirements for lithium-ion compatibility by using intercalation to limit the electrolyte's function to an ion conduit.
- Active materials in zinc-ion batteries are very energy dense, allowing for sufficiently high energy storage even in thin electrodes.
- Zinc-ion batteries can improve on lithium-ion manufacturing because they do not require the highly controlled atmosphere needed for lithium’s violent reactivity with water.
- As a water-based battery, zinc-ion avoids the cost and complexity constraints associated with lithium's production steps.
- Zinc-ion batteries do not require formation cycling at the end of life, allowing them to move more quickly from manufacturing lines to customers.
- The ability to use lithium-ion manufacturing enables the rapid and inexpensive scale-up of zinc-ion battery production.
- Zinc-ion’s key short-term differentiators from lithium-ion are safety and supply chain security.
- Zinc-ion’s intrinsic safety comes from using water as an electrolyte, enabling it to gain traction in markets where lithium-ion adoption has been limited due to safety concerns.
- Dense urban centers with fire regulations preventing lithium-ion adoption are an example market for zinc-ion batteries.
- Zinc-ion can be built with materials that are produced in abundance in North America.
- Customers will seek to develop domestic supply chains as energy storage plays an increasingly important role in critical infrastructure.
- The need for domestic supply chains will become more acute if U.S.-China relations worsen or if government subsidies enforce strong “Buy American” requirements.
- As zinc-ion production ramps up and takes advantage of economies of scale, zinc-ion batteries will become a lower-cost alternative to lithium-ion.
- Paired with long service life, zinc-ion batteries will offer a far lower cost of storage than can be achieved with lithium-ion today.
- Zinc-ion has demonstrated substantial improvements in supply chain security and safety compared to lithium-ion.
- Salient Energy is a battery energy storage technology company based in Halifax, Nova Scotia, Canada.
- Ryan Brown serves as the co-founder and CEO of Salient Energy.
