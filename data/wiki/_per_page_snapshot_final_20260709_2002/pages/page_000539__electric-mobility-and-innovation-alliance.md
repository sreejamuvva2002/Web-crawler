# Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2021-08

**Source:** [Georgia partnership to steer through electric vehicle disruption - AI Online -](https://ai-online.com/2021/08/georgia-partnership-to-steer-through-electric-vehicle-disruption/)  ·  `page_000539`

## Facts

- A visit to Mercedes-Benz and Porsche plants in Stuttgart, Germany, served as a wake-up call for Georgia regarding the electric vehicle manufacturing race.
- Pat Wilson, Commissioner of the Georgia Department of Economic Development (GDEcD), stated that the EV revolution is not theoretical and Georgia decided it needs to participate or risk getting left behind.
- Governor Brian P. Kemp and Pat Wilson recognized that approximately 55,000 Georgian jobs are on the line due to industry disruption.
- A statewide initiative was launched to strengthen Georgia’s position as a national leader in the electric mobility industry.
- The Electric Mobility and Innovation Alliance (EMIA) is driven by the GDEcD and includes partners from government, industry, electric utilities, education, nonprofits, and other stakeholders.
- Governor Brian P. Kemp stated at the launch that Georgia has a proven record of investing early in resources and infrastructure to connect to the world and develop future jobs.
- EMIA's scope covers the entire EV industry transition, from rare earth metals to vehicle assembly, aiming to create jobs across the breadth of the industry.
- Work resumed on a nearly US$2.6 billion battery plant in Commerce, Georgia, following an agreement between two rival Korean companies.
- The Commerce battery plant will supply batteries for electric vehicles to Ford and Volkswagen.
- Pat Wilson noted that the United States currently produces around 10% of its lithium-ion batteries while China produces 70% or more.
- The presence of a battery plant attracts additional supply chain opportunities, with five suppliers already announcing plans to establish plants around the Commerce facility.
- Investors in the Commerce battery plant include Dutch e-mobility charging systems leader Heliox, Turkish EV-parts manufacturer TEKLAS, German-owned lightweight automotive-body parts manufacturer GEDIA, and SK-supplier EnChem of Korea.
- School bus manufacturer Blue Bird introduced a range of all-electric buses made in Georgia in 2018.
- Blue Bird recently reported its 500th delivery of an electric bus.
- Non-profits focused on automotive innovation within EMIA include The Ray, a non-profit public-private partnership and living highway testbed located on Georgia’s I-85 between LaGrange and the Alabama state line.
- The Ray is involved in Peachtree Corners, Georgia, which is home to Curiosity Lab, a 5G-enabled autonomous vehicle and smart city living laboratory.
- Curiosity Lab features a 1.5-mile test and demo track as its centerpiece for exploring emerging technologies in a real-world environment.
- Additional infrastructure at Curiosity Lab includes a network operations center, smart poles, DSRC units, dedicated fiber, and a 25,000 square foot tech incubator.
- EMIA stakeholders face the challenge of making policy and investment recommendations to ensure Georgia can deliver workforce, business policies, infrastructure, and supply chain elements needed for industry growth.
- EMIA has been divided into different work streams to focus efforts and generate actionable suggestions for the state government's transition to electric vehicles.
- A key focus area is ensuring the workforce is fully equipped through school-level education and retraining of currently employed automotive sector workers.
- Pat Wilson emphasized that success in the transition requires a pipeline of the right skills.
