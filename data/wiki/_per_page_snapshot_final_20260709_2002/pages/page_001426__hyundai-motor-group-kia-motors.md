# Hyundai Motor Group / Kia Motors

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [‘I Expected to Work in an Office:’ Engineers Recruited by Carmakers End Up on the Assembly Line - Human Trafficking Search](https://humantraffickingsearch.org/resource/i-expected-to-work-in-an-office-engineers-recruited-by-carmakers-end-up-on-the-assembly-line/)  ·  `page_001426`

## Facts

- Mexican nationals recruited to work white-collar jobs at Hyundai and Kia plants in Georgia instead ended up on the factory floor, lawsuit alleges.
- Isidro Arellano was recruited by recruiters at Mexico’s Universidad Tecnológica de Torreón looking for engineers interested in career-enhancing jobs in the auto industry in the US South.
- Arellano, 26, ended up lugging steering columns and installing bumpers, logging 60-hour-plus weeks at a Kia assembly line in West Point, Georgia.
- Arellano returned to Mexico last year after being fired for complaining about what he describes as bait-and-switch practices.
- Arellano expected to work in an office, attend meetings with executives, and have a friendly environment as promised.
- Arellano is one of nine Mexican engineers who are plaintiffs in a federal racketeering lawsuit against KIA, Hyundai Motor Group, and Hyundai Mobis.
- The Mexican nationals allege they were lured to the companies’ assembly lines by Korean-led labor brokers dangling professional jobs that didn't exist.
- The case highlights an ongoing battle between business and US agencies charged with enforcing labor laws, including the role of third-party staffing companies in enabling alleged abuse.
- Georgia gave its largest incentive package ever, $1.8 billion, to Hyundai for an EV plant near Savannah, promising 8,100 direct jobs and thousands more from suppliers.
- Benjamin Botts, law director for Centro de los Derechos del Migrante, questioned why companies make such promises to the state while engaging in deceptive practices at existing facilities.
- Staffing companies allegedly coached engineers on how to obtain TN visas and provided employer letters detailing fictitious US jobs.
- The plaintiffs were among hundreds of Mexican nationals brought to West Point under the visa program by labor brokers under contract to supply workers to automakers.
- Federal officials do not track TN visa holders in the US.
- Plaintiffs ended up doing manual labor for less pay and longer hours than their domestic counterparts, the suit alleges.
- Kia strongly denies the lawsuit allegations and states it requires business partners to strictly adhere to all applicable laws, including immigration laws.
- Hyundai MOBIS strongly denies the allegations, stating it is an equal employment opportunity employer that will vigorously defend the claim.
- Georgia Governor Brian Kemp set a particularly torrid pace of recruiting and winning EV projects, making promised jobs a centerpiece of his 2022 reelection campaign.
- The ceremonial groundbreaking for the $5.5 billion Hyundai EV plant came weeks before Governor Kemp's November victory.
- The Hyundai EV plant is the biggest economic development project in state history.
- Since October, Kemp announced job creations including 1,500 from Hyundai MOBIS; 630 from Joon Georgia Inc., a subsidiary of Ajin USA; 740 from Seoyon E-HWA; and 456 from Ecoplastic.
- There are an additional 3,500 new jobs at a battery manufacturing plant, a joint venture from Hyundai Motor Group and SK On, a spinoff of Seoul-based SK Innovations.
- In March, Kemp announced 400 more jobs from a Hyundai supplier.
- Kemp stated that in a single month, Georgia’s economic development community announced more than 1,900 new jobs for Georgians, most related to the Hyundai plant.
- State economist Jeffrey Dorfman warned there may not be enough Georgians to fill the jobs, telling state legislators in January they need to bring in more people.
- Georgia’s unemployment rate was 3.1% in March, consistently lower than the US average.
- Nationwide labor crunch has plagued automakers for years, according to Kristin Dziczek, automotive policy adviser for the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago.
- Federal data show the unemployment rate for the general transportation equipment sector has been lower than for both manufacturing and overall unemployment nearly every quarter since 2012.
- As the national unemployment rate averaged 3.3%, unemployment in transportation manufacturing stood at 1.8%.
- The South faces challenges because plants are in rural areas with a far-flung labor pool and lack a dense, experienced manufacturing workforce around the plant.
- Legally hiring foreign line workers is difficult due to the lack of a manual labor visa; non-agricultural manual labor visas are for temporary seasonal work, time-consuming, costly, require documentation that no US workers are available, mandate prevailing wage payment, and are capped at 66,000 annually.
- The TN visa is granted by the US State Department, typically applied for by workers instead of companies, has no annual cap, and lacks worker protections such as work contracts or prevailing wage and travel reimbursement requirements.
- Employers provide a job offer letter to U.S. consular officials for TN visas but nothing else, resulting in scant federal oversight.
- There have been more TN visa workers in the US than any other category of temporary visa holder every year except one since 2012.
- The TN visa is for Mexicans and Canadians in specific fields including engineers, scientists, lawyers, doctors, dentists, accountants, architects, astronomers, meteorologists, and “scientific technicians” with enough expertise to assist professionals in 10 specified fields.
- No agency systematically checks to ensure TN visa holders work in the jobs they were brought to the country to do.
- Mexican engineers learned of Georgia opportunities through LinkedIn, job fairs and universities.
- A recruiting company identified candidates that met TN visa requirements and coached them through the application process, while an affiliated staffing company provided critical hiring letters attesting to US jobs.
- Luis Salazar, an engineer with seven years of experience, was supposed to be a quality control engineer at Hyundai MOBIS applying statistical methods and performing mathematical calculations, but ended up on an assembly line installing bumpers, suspension systems and speakers.
- Veronica Olan, an engineer for six years, was supposed to develop, evaluate and improve operations processes, analyze workflows, and help with budget, specifications, schedule, management, start-up, evaluations and presentations, but was put on a KIA assembly line tightening door screws and pouring antifreeze.
- Arellano was supposed to be a “scientific production technician” making $40,000 annually with two weeks of paid vacation and a year’s free housing, but learned the reality when landing in a two-bedroom apartment with three other engineers doing manual labor.
- Neither state nor local officials would comment on the specific allegations in the lawsuit or the future implications for Georgia’s labor supply.
- Officials said they are taking steps to ensure an adequate workforce, including a labor shortage action plan from the Savannah Joint Development Authority, a state rural housing initiative, and continued investment in training related to EVs.
- Garrison Douglas, spokesman for Kemp, stated Georgia is leading the way in growing and developing its workforce to fill jobs of tomorrow created by partners like Kia and Hyundai.
- An estimated 4 million manufacturing jobs will open over the decade, of which 2.1 million will remain unfilled, according to a 2021 report by Deloitte and the Manufacturing Institute.
- Laine Mears, professor of automotive engineering at Clemson University, called labor among the biggest challenges for EV manufacturing, noting the talent pipeline has slowed to a trickle.
- The lawsuit raises questions about automaker strategies for staffing assembly lines and company responsibility for violations when workers are recruited and paid by outside firms.
- Alabama fined eight companies in Hyundai’s supply chain late last year for child labor violations, seven of which were third-party staffing companies that found and hired the children for Hyundai’s suppliers.
- Third-party staffing companies have become staples of the US manufacturing industry, according to David Weil, Brandeis economist and former head of the US Department of Labor’s Wage and Hours Division.
- The Obama administration broadened the definition of responsibility to include companies where violations occur along with the third-party recruiter due to high coordination between them.
- Former President Donald Trump reversed the provision, which was later restored by President Joe Biden.
- Weil noted similar problems arose with the J-1 visa program a decade ago, which also resulted in foreign students ending up on factory floors.
