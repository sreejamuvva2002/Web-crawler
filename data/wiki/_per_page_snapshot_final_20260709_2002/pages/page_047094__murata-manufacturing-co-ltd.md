# Murata Manufacturing Co. Ltd.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** power_electronics  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Murata banks on ceramic technology - EE Times](https://www.eetimes.com/murata-banks-on-ceramic-technology-2/)  ·  `page_047094`

## Facts

- Since its founding in 1940, Murata has followed a strategy of organic growth using research and development to build capacitors, filters, resistors, microwave parts, and other components.
- The prolonged industry downturn forced Murata to trim its workforce and lower capital investment.
- Increasing OEM pressure has forced Murata to continue reducing commodity part prices.
- Murata is leveraging core ceramic expertise to develop higher-margin products such as Bluetooth modules and higher-performance capacitors for emerging applications.
- The company is increasing its presence in China.
- John Denslinger, senior vice president of Murata Electronics North America Inc., Smyrna, Ga., stated: “Everything is changing in terms of what people do and where.”
- Murata remains the world's leading manufacturer of multilayer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs), accounting for more than 20% of the global market based on revenue.
- The company considers itself a market leader in SAW filters with a 20% share, and dielectric filters with a 40% share.
- Murata remains vertically integrated, forming its own ceramic materials and building its own machines, eschewing outsourcing trends.
- The company consistently reinvests 5% to 8% of its revenue in R&D.
- Murata's last notable acquisition was in 1981.
- For fiscal year 2001 ended March 31, 2002, Murata's revenue was $2.97 billion, down 32.4% from the previous year.
- Net income for fiscal 2001 was $394 million, down 66% from the prior year.
- Murata projects revenue and earnings for fiscal 2002 to be roughly flat with that of fiscal 2001.
- Capital investment for fiscal 2002 is projected at $200 million, down from $260 million in fiscal 2001.
- Denslinger expects Murata's revenue to slowly improve over the next year, citing price erosion in commodity parts as the main hurdle to recovery.
- A lot of OEMs now negotiate price contracts on a quarterly rather than yearly basis.
- Capacitors have experienced 12 consecutive quarters of price reductions.
- Denslinger expects prices to erode 10% the first half of 2003.
- Murata's largest market segment is communications products, but its percentage of revenue from this sector slid to 37% in fiscal 2001 from 41% in fiscal 2000.
- Jiro Miyazaki, vice president of marketing for Murata Electronics North America, expects the company to supply EMC filters, thermistors, and DC/DC converters to OEMs building broadband, home networking, and digital access equipment.
- Capacitors account for 40% of Murata's revenue.
- The company is expanding offerings in capacitors with low-equivalent-series resistance as well as capacitance values exceeding 1 microfarad.
- Murata hopes to target the RF market and applications in which tantalum capacitors now dominate.
- Denslinger expects the focus on advanced technology to fend off low-cost Far East suppliers like Taiwan's Yageo and Korea's Samsung.
- Murata is leveraging low-temperature co-fired ceramic technology to manufacture integrated Bluetooth modules.
- Last week, Murata announced a new Bluetooth module measuring 9.3 x 7.8 x 1.8mm, which is 25% smaller than the previous version introduced in August.
- The Bluetooth module is for wireless handset and PDA applications and sells for $15 to $20.
- Scott Klettke, Murata's group product manager, said the higher price is justified since the module speeds time-to-market for designers of Bluetooth products.
- Murata expects its percentage of revenue from China, 14% in fiscal 2001, to increase as more OEM and EMS customers locate there.
- Last month, the company began mass production of capacitors, isolators, and thermistors at a plant in Wuxi.
- Murata's other China plants are located in Beijing, Suzhou, and Shenzhen.
