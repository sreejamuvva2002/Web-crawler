# SK Innovation

## Facts

- The Biden administration avoided one of the more challenging early tests of its climate agenda Saturday, when two South Korean battery giants feuding over trade secrets reached a settlement to keep a massive Georgia factory complex open.
- The White House faced a Sunday deadline to decide whether to veto a U.S. International Trade Commission decision from February that barred SK Innovation from importing the materials needed to build electric vehicle batteries at a $2.6 billion pair of plants in Commerce, Georgia.
- The case illustrates the new administration’s dueling priorities as it seeks to jumpstart efforts to eliminate the U.S. economy’s planet-heating pollution while going head-to-head with China over intellectual property protections.
- As late as Friday night, SK Innovation looked far from a deal with Seoul-based rival LG Energy Solutions, which convinced the federal trade court that SK Innovation destroyed evidence of stolen trade secrets.
- In February, the ITC imposed a 10-year import ban on SK Innovation.
- The import ban jeopardized the supply of batteries for Ford’s electric F-150 pickup and Volkswagen’s Crossover series.
- The ruling threatened the 2,600 jobs the company planned to fill in Georgia over the next few years.
- The ruling also risked delaying President Joe Biden’s plans to electrify the nation’s 276 million some-odd automobiles, the top U.S. source of climate pollution.
- A $1.8 billion cash deal will end other ongoing lawsuits between SK Innovation and LG Energy Solutions in the U.S. and South Korea.
- The cash deal includes ending a lawsuit in a federal court in Delaware that had been on pause until the ITC saga reached its completion.
- SK Innovation CEO Jun Kim and LG Energy Solution CEO Jong Hyun Kim issued a joint statement Sunday morning saying they decided to settle and compete amicably for the future of the U.S. and South Korean electric vehicle battery industries.
- The CEOs stated they will work together to strengthen an EV battery domestic supply chain and support the U.S. Biden Administration’s effort to advance green energy policies and environmental responsibility.
- SK Innovation’s plant will comprise upward of 35% of the U.S. production capacity of electric vehicle batteries by the end of Biden’s first term.
- By the end of Biden’s first term, the country is expected to have roughly 11 big powerpack-making factories online.
- The settlement will likely help reverse the ITC ruling and allow SK Innovation to keep its plant open.
- Leaders across the partisan spectrum pressed for an outcome that would allow the plant to remain open.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp (R) pleaded with Biden to veto the decision if a settlement wasn’t reached.
- In recent weeks, Sen. Jon Ossoff (D-Ga.) held at least one meeting seeking to broker a deal.
- The looming deadline and high stakes of a decision that should define the future of a U.S. set for near exponential growth in the next decade led to an eleventh-hour agreement.
- Losing the factory without a deal or veto would have sent a chill through the U.S. battery market as it looks to start attracting more players, according to Caspar Rawles.
- Caspar Rawles is an analyst at Benchmark Mineral Intelligence, a London-based research firm that specializes in lithium-ion batteries and electric vehicle supply chains.
- Rawles stated before the deal was announced that shutting down a plant in the U.S. due to legal reasons does not send a good message.
- Rawles noted it is not the most inviting investment environment for someone looking to spend several billion dollars with the risk of legal intervention causing them to lose it all.
- A presidential veto would have been a rare move, with the last time used in 2013 when President Barack Obama blocked an ITC ruling that would have prevented Apple from importing some iPads.
- Ronald Reagan set the record for vetoing four ITC decisions, including one that coincidentally dealt with batteries.
