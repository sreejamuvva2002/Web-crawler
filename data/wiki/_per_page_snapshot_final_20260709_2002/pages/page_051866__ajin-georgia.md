# AJIN Georgia

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2024-07-19

**Source:** [Opening celebration guests see AJIN Georgia factory in action - Statesboro Herald](https://www.statesboroherald.com/local/opening-celebration-guests-see-ajin-georgia-factory-action/)  ·  `page_051866`

## Facts

- About 285 invited guests – including many South Koreans and Americans – attended the grand opening celebration luncheon Thursday at AJIN Georgia’s automotive metal structural parts factory in Bulloch County’s Bruce Yawn Commerce Park.
- Many hundreds of robots and a few hundred employees inside the 853,000-square foot facility have been making parts for the Ioniq 5, an all-electric Hyundai Motor Group crossover SUV, on a trial basis for about a month.
- The parts produced include the main metal portions of a vehicle’s doors, dash, fenders, and floorboard.
- The facility represents a $317-million corporate investment with a Register, Georgia address.
- When production begins in earnest, tractor-trailer trucks will haul parts from the AJIN plant to Hyundai Motor Group’s Metaplant America in northern Bryan County about 29 miles down Interstate 16.
- Hyundai announced in June that it would begin production at the Metaplant with the 2025 IONIQ 5 in the fourth quarter of 2024.
- So far, with no market-ready assembly of vehicles yet underway at the Metaplant, AJIN has undertaken “control production” of parts used for calibration and testing.
- Human Resources General Manager Jamie Calloway explained that during Hyundai's calibration and assembly process, AJIN sends them parts, and they also build components that are destroyed for testing and certification purposes.
- Guests observed hundreds of one-armed robots ranging from a couple of feet tall to 20 feet or more in blue, yellow, and orange colors swiveling, flexing, lifting and lowering in robotic sub-assembly and welding areas.
- The plant features massive stamping presses that exert up to 1,500 tons of force and rolls of steel that feed them.
- Cavernous empty areas remain in the plant with only safety lines painted on the floor and some bases for equipment yet to be installed.
- These empty areas will soon be filled with equipment for production of a second vehicle line, referred to simply as “the SUV.”
- AJIN Georgia currently employs about 200 people and plans to hire more in August and September.
- The company expects about 350 employees on the job by the end of the year.
- This hiring trajectory leads toward the plant’s eventual full-capacity employment of about 630 people, as predicted in the late-November 2022 plant announcement.
- The AJIN plant's headcount is far fewer than the 8,500 jobs expected to be created eventually on-site at Hyundai's Metaplant.
- AJIN is the first tier-one supplier to get up and running, meaning it sends parts directly to the Metaplant.
- Supplier plants in a number of counties, along with a battery factory also at the Bryan County Mega Site, should boost the total number of jobs associated with the Metaplant to more than 15,000 over the next few years, according to state officials.
- Two other tier-one suppliers are building plants in Bulloch County and are expected to be completed soon.
- Hanon Systems is a maker of "automotive thermal and energy management" equipment, which includes air-conditioning components.
- Ecoplastic America Corporation will manufacture injection-molded plastic automotive body parts for Hyundai Motor Group vehicles.
- AJIN USA Chief Executive Officer Jungho Sea welcomed guests in Korean while another staff member translated his remarks to English.
- Sea stated: “As long as I continue operating my humble organization and factory here, I will always take the local community into my considerations. I will always take my employees as my top priority, as well.”
- Local community guests included Bulloch County commissioners and staff, officials of Statesboro’s city government, state officials, representatives of the Development Authority of Bulloch County, the Statesboro-Bulloch Chamber of Commerce, and media organizations.
- Some Korean attendees were AJIN executives and plant employees in specialized roles, such as technicians and electricians.
- AJIN USA already operates two plants in Alabama, including one that makes the same kinds of parts now to be made at the Register plant.
- The Republic of Korea’s Consul General Sangpyo Suh from the Atlanta Consulate attended and spoke during the ceremony.
- Suh congratulated Sea and other AJIN corporate officials on a successful site selection, noting the plant’s proximity to the interstate highway and direct path to the Metaplant.
- Billy Allen, chair of the Development Authority of Bulloch County, thanked DABC staff, county commissioners, and others for making the commerce park presentable to attract companies like AJIN Georgia.
- Sea and Development Authority CEO Benjy Thompson exchanged plaques, with both AJIN and DABC expressing continuing appreciation to each other.
- Sydne Smith, chief operating officer of the Georgia Department of Economic Development, noted that next year the state will celebrate the 40th anniversary of its trade office in South Korea.
- Smith reported that for the last three years, Korea has been the top international source of investment for Georgia.
- Georgia District 4 State Sen. Billy Hickman, R-Statesboro, cited a recent Georgia Southern University study valuing the annual economic impact of manufacturing in Bulloch County at $1.17 billion.
- Hickman, a certified public accountant, appreciated the economic impact number but might argue with the multiplier economists used, thinking it should actually be higher.
- Hickman stated: “This is economic activity that helps other businesses in the community be successful, helps employ thousands of our citizens and enhances our quality of life.”
- Georgia Southern, Ogeechee Technical College, Bulloch County Schools, and some neighboring school systems were represented by their top officials at the celebration luncheon.
- Sizeable groups of students from Seoul National University and Daegu High School, both in South Korea, also attended.
- A group of educators from Bulloch County, Candler County, and Evans County school systems who took part in a 12-day visit to South Korea in June as part of the company’s “Korean Corner” program gathered around Sea to publicly thank him for the opportunity.
- The educators had visited schools and cultural sites, as well as AJIN worksites during their trip.
- Bethany Gilliam, CTAE director for Bulloch County Schools, stated that the program helps community members understand Korean culture when they have new neighbors or work in Korean-owned businesses.
