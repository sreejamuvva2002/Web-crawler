# Global Lithium-Ion Battery Market

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [China Already Makes as Many Batteries as the Entire World Wants | BloombergNEF](https://about.bnef.com/blog/china-already-makes-as-many-batteries-as-the-entire-world-wants/)  ·  `page_045661`

## Facts

- The US is ramping up efforts to onshore the lithium-ion battery supply chain.
- Global lithium-ion battery demand across EVs and stationary storage came in at around 950 gigawatt hours last year.
- Global battery manufacturing capacity was more than twice demand, at close to 2,600 GWh.
- China’s battery production in 2023 alone was similar to global demand.
- Canada is matching US incentives, while Europe, India and others are awarding subsidies to grow their battery industries.
- The oversupply picture is going to get worse before it gets better.
- BNEF is tracking 7.9 TWh of annual battery manufacturing capacity announced for the end of 2025.
- Demand projections are 1.6 TWh, assuming steady EV demand growth and very rapid growth in batteries for storage applications.
- Even half the total announced capacity would be enough to equip almost every car sold in the world next year with a 50 kWh battery pack.
- Much of the announced capacity will either be behind schedule or outright cancelled as scaling realities become clearer and contenders thin out.
- Other parts of the supply chain cannot deliver at that level, and EV batteries are not yet a commodity product due to different formats, chemistries, automaker preferences, and planned model launches.
- Plants typically do not run at their intended capacity, and utilization rates have been falling for the last few years.
- The market is heading for even more serious oversupply even with a hefty de-rating factor for actual online capacity.
- Prices will fall and margins will get squeezed, already evidenced by a 14% dip in average battery pack prices in 2023.
- China’s CATL expects to be able to sell battery cells at the equivalent of less than $60 per kWh this year.
- Average capacity utilization of existing plants will continue to fall after dropping in 2023.
- Battery prices are converging across sectors as manufacturers look for new markets for their products.
- The price gap between commercial-vehicle batteries and passenger car batteries has narrowed as the market swung in favor of buyers and should continue to close.
- Low prices will make it difficult for new entrants to compete, even with generous support provided by the US Inflation Reduction Act and other means.
- The production phase involves challenges including cutting down scrappage rates, which typically start out high and gradually come down over time.
- There is a scenario that battery makers receiving generous support in the US and Europe are undercut by their Chinese rivals.
- China’s BYD and CATL both continue to invest billions in R&D, are steadily launching new and better products, and behave more like scrappy startups than bloated corporate fiefdoms.
- Lower prices will benefit consumers with cheaper EV models planned for launch in 2025 and 2026.
- The recently released Xiaomi SU7 EV uses CATL’s new battery technology, boasting up to 830 kilometers (516 miles) of range for the equivalent of $34,000 in China.
- The stationary storage market is already growing very rapidly and will also benefit from lower battery prices.
- Battery supply dynamics are acting as a moderating factor for EV demand: when EV demand slows, battery prices fall, leading to lower EV prices and helping spur a recovery in demand.
- Some current low battery prices are driven by lower raw material costs for oversupplied materials like lithium, which may not hold long since it is easier to build a battery plant than bring on new mining or refining capacity.
- Some price cuts are likely due to established players looking to protect market share and force smaller ones out.
- There is definitely an element of technology and manufacturing improvement at play in the price cuts.
- Overcapacity is a boon for potential EV buyers but marks a challenging time ahead for new entrants to the battery industry.
