# Great Dane

## Facts

- Great Dane’s new refrigerated trailer plant is located in Statesboro, Georgia.
- The facility operates under a 450,000-square-foot roof.
- It is America’s newest truck trailer production facility.
- Great Dane also builds refrigerated trailers in Brazil, Indiana, and Wayne City, Nebraska.
- Michael Chitty serves as sales engineering for Great Dane.
- The plant was designed with objectives including efficiency, product quality, environmental concerns, and ergonomics.
- The company relied heavily on automation and designing the plant to minimize travel distance for raw materials, subassemblies, and components.
- The company’s Savannah plant (now closed) required parts to travel between multiple buildings, resulting in excess travel that was reduced by a factor of nine in the Statesboro facility.
- Management allowed the plant to gradually ramp up production following last year’s grand opening.
- The plant built its first custom trailer late last summer for Rowland Transportation of Dade City, Florida.
- Throughout the fall and winter, assembly increased as employment grew and the new workforce became increasingly familiar with the new plant layout and its technology that includes robotic welding and automated foaming.
- Production is building momentum, especially with Great Dane’s receipt of a 200-unit order for Celadon.
- The plant is approaching its capacity—50 refrigerated trailers per week with one shift.
- The plant has room to grow toward targeted production of 100 trailers per week at full two shift capacity.
- The building includes 12,000 square feet of office space and a 3,000-square-foot climate-controlled customer preview center.
- The Statesboro plant is tooled to manufacture Great Dane’s new Everest refrigerated trailers, the TL model reefer for truckload carriers and the CL model reefer built especially for the needs of multi-temp and foodservice operations, especially those in the Southeast.
- The Statesboro plant received a big boost recently with the receipt of a 200-unit order from Celadon.
- The plant completed the first unit last month.
- The reefers represent the first refrigerated trailers that Celadon, one of North America’s largest truckload carriers, has purchased from any manufacturer.
- Dave Gilliland is Great Dane’s vice-president of national accounts.
- The reefer trailers being built for Celadon are based on Great Dane’s Everest TL model.
- The fleet also specified ThermoGuard reefer liner, which features a patented metal barrier layer in the sidewalls, ceiling and floor subpan to minimize thermal degradation of the trailer’s insulation over time.
- Other features of Celadon’s customized Everest TL order include a wedge design for maximum rear opening height, Hendrickson HD air ride suspension, Bridgestone R197 Ecopia tires, Transtex side skirts, Carrier 2100 APX reefer unit with auto fresh air exchange and Intelliset temperature control, rear door switch, StarTrak GPS, side air chute, air return bulkhead and pallet stops.
- The trailers are also designed for occasional rail usage.
- The Statesboro plant was scheduled to complete the 200-trailer order at the end of July.
- Engineers placed an L-shaped assembly line within the square 450,000-square-foot structure.
- The trailer assembly line starts along the back wall of the plant and turns 90 degrees as it flows along the adjacent wall and back toward the front of the building.
- Subassemblies flow from the front of the plant to the trailer assembly line, with termination points at the exact spot where they are needed on the assembly line.
- Raw materials are delivered and unloaded close to where they are needed for subassemblies.
- The plant has two sets of doors for deliveries—one set near the start of subassembly production through which about 70% of materials and components enter the facility.
- A second set of doors primarily feeds materials used for floor assemblies.
- Supplied parts are kept in a supermarket inside the plant, slashing the amount of motion inherent in any sort of outside storage.
- The company continues to work on ways to reduce inventories by improving real-time communications.
- Computer monitors are being installed on the plant floor to enable production workers to view drawings and specifications in order to improve accuracy and reduce lost time.
- Great Dane shopped the world to develop specialized equipment for producing its trailers, including its proprietary foaming system.
- Under controlled conditions, each cavity is automatically injected with the exact amount of foam required to fill it.
- The foaming presses at the Statesboro plant are designed to resist several hundred tons of force generated during the foaming operation.
- The foaming presses are 60 feet long, giving Great Dane the capability of manufacturing a 60-foot-long trailer or sidewalls for two 28-foot pups.
- A robotic work cell automates the welding of rear door frames, and gantry-mounted guns take the work out of welding reefer flooring.
- Automated rivet inserting equipment has been performing reliably, enabling conveyors to carry sidewalls filled with rivets to the rivet press for upsetting.
- Automated torque equipment tightens lug nuts and U-bolts to required specifications, monitoring and recording actual torque specifications and generating a record of the torque applied on each trailer serial number at the time it was assembled.
- Trailers move from one workstation to the next on conveyors to workstations throughout the plant.
- “Air skates,” which use a thin cushion of compressed air to virtually eliminate friction, move trailers through areas where such devices offer advantages over the conveyor line.
- The company tries to recycle 100% of what it doesn’t use.
- The plant has different colored dumpsters for various types of scrap that the plant generates, including mild steel, stainless, and aluminum.
- A building management system automatically controls lights, air quality and building temperature.
- Load monitors are used to ensure optimal energy consumption.
- There is extensive use of energy-saving compact fluorescent bulbs.
- A water reclamation system is used for the wash bays.
- Evaporative coolers add comfort in the plant on summer days, dropping temperatures 15 degrees from ambient but without the extensive electrical consumption of standard air conditioning systems.
- Four large fans change the air inside the plant every 15 minutes.
- The grand opening was held a little more than a year after Great Dane broke ground on the 118-acre site.
- Bill Crown is president and chief executive officer of Great Dane.
- During the planning process, the trailer industry suffered through its most significant downturn in a generation.
- Great Dane’s newest manufacturing facility is built on a 118-acre site in Bulloch County, just 50 miles from the company’s roots in Savannah, Georgia.
- The location was selected for a variety of reasons, including its economy.
- As the state unemployment rate hovers above eight percent, the plant will bring more than 400 greatly needed jobs to the area.
- Statesboro was selected for several reasons including availability of suitable space, easy access to the Interstate highway, and access to a qualified workforce (without heavy competition from other manufacturing concerns).
- Proximity to Savannah was a key factor in selecting Statesboro.
- Great Dane stopped manufacturing trailers in Savannah in 2009, but corporate offices remain there, including sales, marketing, customer service, administration, and engineering, along with research and development functions.
- The company has further committed to the Savannah area by breaking ground on a new research and development campus, on the existing corporate office facility in Savannah.
- Brandie Fuller is vice-president at Great Dane.
- Before discontinuing manufacturing in Savannah, Great Dane had previously stopped producing dry vans there around 2001 due to the availability of five other strategically located Great Dane van plants across the US.
- The plan for Statesboro was to keep a reefer manufacturing presence in the Southeast, which is described as their historical home market.
