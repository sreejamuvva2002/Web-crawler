# Georgia Institute of Technology

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** 2024-09-22

**Source:** [New Battery Cathode Material Could Revolutionize EV Market and Energy Storage | Research](https://research.gatech.edu/new-battery-cathode-material-could-revolutionize-ev-market-and-energy-storage)  ·  `page_000350`

## Facts

- A multi-institutional research team led by Georgia Tech’s Hailong Chen has developed a new, low-cost cathode for lithium-ion batteries (LIBs).
- The new cathode material could radically improve LIBs and potentially transform the electric vehicle (EV) market and large-scale energy storage systems.
- Hailong Chen stated that researchers have long sought a lower-cost, more sustainable alternative to existing cathode materials and believe they have found one.
- Chen is an associate professor with appointments in Georgia Tech’s George W. Woodruff School of Mechanical Engineering and the School of Materials Science and Engineering.
- The revolutionary material developed by the team is iron chloride (FeCl3).
- FeCl3 costs a mere 1-2% of typical cathode materials while storing the same amount of electricity.
- Cathode materials significantly affect battery capacity, energy, efficiency, performance, lifespan, and affordability.
- Chen described the cathode as a potential game-changer that would greatly improve the EV market and the whole lithium-ion battery market.
- The team's work detailing this invention was published in Nature Sustainability.
- Lithium-ion batteries were first commercialized by Sony in the early 1990s, initially sparking growth in personal electronics before advancing to electric vehicles.
- Batteries currently account for about 50% of an EV’s total cost, making clean-energy cars more expensive than internal combustion vehicles.
- The Chen team's invention has the potential to change the high cost of LIBs relative to EVs.
- Compared to alkaline and lead-acid batteries, LIBs store more energy in a smaller package and power devices longer between charges.
- Conventional LIBs contain expensive metals including semiprecious elements like cobalt and nickel, along with high manufacturing costs.
- Only four types of cathodes had been successfully commercialized for LIBs prior to this development; Chen’s would be the fifth type.
- The invention represents a significant step forward in battery technology through the development of an all-solid-state LIB.
- Conventional LIBs use liquid electrolytes to transport lithium ions, which impose hard limits on energy storage and pose risks of leaking and catching fire.
- All-solid-state LIBs use solid electrolytes, which dramatically boost efficiency and reliability while making batteries safer and capable of holding more energy.
- All-solid-state technology is currently in the development and testing phase globally.
- Using the FeCl3 cathode paired with a solid electrolyte and a lithium metal anode reduces the cost of the entire battery system to 30-40% of current LIBs.
- Chen noted this advancement could make EVs much cheaper than internal combustion cars, provide new large-scale energy storage options enhancing grid resilience, and improve sustainability and supply chain stability for the EV market.
- The team's research into FeCl3 originated in 2019 when they attempted to create solid-state batteries using chloride-based solid electrolytes with traditional oxide-based cathodes.
- Initial attempts failed because the traditional cathode and electrolyte materials were incompatible.
- Researchers hypothesized that a chloride-based cathode would pair better with the chloride electrolyte for improved battery performance.
- Chen explained that FeCl3 was chosen because its crystal structure is potentially suitable for storing and transporting ions, and it functioned as expected.
- The most popularly used EV cathodes are oxides requiring large amounts of costly nickel and cobalt, which are heavy, toxic elements posing environmental challenges.
- In contrast, the Chen team’s cathode contains only iron and chlorine, which are abundant, affordable, and widely used elements found in steel and table salt.
- Initial tests showed FeCl3 performed as well as or better than other, much more expensive cathodes.
- FeCl3 demonstrated a higher operational voltage than the popularly used LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) cathode.
- The technology may be less than five years away from commercial viability in EVs.
- The team will continue investigating FeCl3 and related materials moving forward.
- The research was led by Chen and postdoc Zhantao Liu, who is the lead author of the study.
- Collaborators on the project included Ting Zhu (Georgia Tech Woodruff School), Yuanzhi Tang (Georgia Tech School of Earth and Atmospheric Sciences), Jue Liu (Oak Ridge National Laboratory), and Shuo Chen (University of Houston).
- Chen stated the team aims to perfect the materials in the lab, understand underlying mechanisms, and remains open to scaling up the technology for commercial applications.
- The work was funded by the National Science Foundation under Grant Nos. 1706723 and 2108688.
