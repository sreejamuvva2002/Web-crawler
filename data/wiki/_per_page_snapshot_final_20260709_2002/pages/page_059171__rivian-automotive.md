# Rivian Automotive

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Biden administration to loan $6.6 billion to EV maker Rivian, $RIVN, to build Georgia factory that automaker paused](https://unusual-whales.ghost.io/unusual_blog/post/biden-administration-to-loan-6-6-billion-to-ev-maker-rivian-rivn-to-build-georgia-factory-that-automaker-paused/)  ·  `page_059171`

## Facts

- President Joe Biden's administration announced a $6.6 billion loan from the U.S. Department of Energy to Rivian Automotive to support the construction of a factory in Georgia.
- The Georgia factory project had been stalled as the electric vehicle startup struggled to achieve profitability.
- It remains uncertain whether the loan can be finalized before Donald Trump assumes office in less than two months or if the incoming administration might attempt to revoke it.
- Donald Trump has previously pledged to eliminate federal electric vehicle tax credits, currently valued at up to $7,500 for new zero-emission vehicles and $4,000 for used ones.
- Rivian began producing its R1 series of electric SUVs, pickup trucks, and delivery vans in 2021 at a repurposed Mitsubishi factory in Normal, Illinois.
- Shortly after beginning Illinois production, the California-based company announced a $5 billion investment to build a second plant near Social Circle, Georgia, approximately 40 miles east of Atlanta.
- The R1 vehicles were priced at $70,000 or more and targeted a premium market.
- Rivian intended to use the Georgia facility to produce the R2, a smaller SUV with a lower price point aimed at broader consumer adoption.
- The plant’s first phase was projected to produce 200,000 vehicles annually.
- A second phase of the Georgia plant was projected to double that capacity.
- At full scale, the Georgia plant would create 7,500 jobs.
- Rivian's inability to meet production and sales targets led to financial strain.
- In March, the company announced a halt to construction at the Georgia site, opting to begin R2 production at its Illinois facility instead.
- Rivian’s CEO, RJ Scaringe, cited a need to accelerate R2 availability and reduce capital expenditures by $2.25 billion for the pause decision.
- In June, Rivian’s cash flow improved after German automaker Volkswagen AG invested $5 billion in a joint venture with Rivian.
- The joint venture with Volkswagen AG includes an agreement to share software and electrical technology.
- The partnership with Volkswagen provided crucial financial relief to the startup.
- Following the loan announcement, Rivian stated that construction of the R2 and a future R3 model will now proceed at the Georgia site.
- Production of the R2 and R3 models at the Georgia facility is slated to begin in 2028.
- CEO RJ Scaringe stated that the loan would enable Rivian to more aggressively scale its U.S. manufacturing footprint for competitively priced R2 and R3 vehicles that emphasize capability and affordability.
- The Department of Energy emphasized that the loan would significantly bolster domestic electric vehicle production.
- The loan aligns with President Biden’s goal for zero-emission vehicles to comprise half of all new U.S. sales by 2030.
