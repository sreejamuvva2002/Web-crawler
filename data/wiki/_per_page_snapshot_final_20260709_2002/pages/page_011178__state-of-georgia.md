# State of Georgia

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-03-31

**Source:** [Insult To Injury? GA Kills Electric-Car Incentive, Adds Last-Minute Luxury-Car Tax Break](https://www.greencarreports.com/news/1097641_insult-to-injury-ga-kills-electric-car-incentive-adds-last-minute-luxury-car-tax-break)  ·  `page_011178`

## Facts

- Georgia passed a highway-funding bill that killed its $5,000 income-tax credit for the purchase of battery-electric vehicles.
- Illinois suspended its own EV rebates to close a budgetary hole.
- Texas may suspend its EV rebates as well.
- Georgia residents have less than three months to take advantage of the state's EV tax credit before it expires.
- The Georgia EV tax credit will no longer be effective as of July 1st.
- State sales of electric cars in Georgia are expected to soar for April, May, and June, then decline thereafter.
- The extent of the sales decline is unknown, though data from British Columbia may prove instructive.
- Georgia added a $200 registration fee for battery-electric vehicles.
- The $200 fee exceeds the $165 in state gasoline taxes a Georgia driver in a 25-mpg vehicle would pay for driving 15,000 miles per year at $0.0275 per gallon.
- Georgia becomes only the second state among 50 (after Virginia) where EV owners pay more in taxes for driving than gasoline and diesel car owners.
- The state rushed through a hasty tax break for employees of Mercedes-Benz who lease cars from the company.
- Under the new tax break, Mercedes-Benz will pay only a small application fee and a small charge for specialized license plates instead of its employees paying the standard car tax.
- The total cost of the Mercedes-Benz tax break to Georgia will exceed $1 million, according to an analysis by the Fiscal Research Center of Georgia State University.
- Mercedes-Benz North America is relocating its operations from New Jersey to Georgia.
- Mercedes-Benz states its goal in relocating is to be closer to its large SUV assembly plant in Tuscaloosa, Alabama.
- Industry observers suggest Mercedes-Benz will lose a majority of its current employees during the relocation.
- Not all current MBNA employees were invited to relocate, and some invited may decline the offer.
- The tax break for driving cars registered to the company (possibly on manufacturer or "MFR" plates) replicates one in New Jersey that relocating employees were used to.
- Political commentators note Georgia may be shuffling various tax incentives to contribute to the rehabilitation of a former General Motors assembly plant in Doraville, which is set to become film studios.
- The Georgia tax break for Mercedes still requires signing by Governor Nathan Deal.
- According to an Associated Press report, the tax break's legality may be questionable because it was passed four minutes after the legislature's midnight deadline and may have violated a rule requiring lawmakers at least two hours to review new legislation.
- The electric-car tax credit is conclusively dead.
- Plug-in car owners in Georgia will start paying the $200 annual fee later this year.
