# BYD

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-01

**Source:** [BYD battery breakthrough crashes Indonesia's nickel cartel dream - Asia Times](https://asiatimes.com/2026/07/byd-battery-breakthrough-crashes-indonesias-nickel-cartel-dream/)  ·  `page_010115`

## Facts

- BYD’s new Datang SUV recently racked up 150,000 pre-orders in a 53-day period in China, marking a pre-order record for any BYD model.
- The Datang is booked without a single gram of nickel in its premium EV battery.
- The industry's working assumption that killing range anxiety in long-range EVs required high-nickel ternary batteries has collapsed in China.
- Datang’s global rollouts for Europe and Southeast Asia are scheduled to begin around the turn of the year.
- BYD historically leaned on its now-discontinued F3 compact sedan, which retailed for about 50,000 yuan, or roughly US$6,900.
- The Datang’s price band runs from 239,900 to 309,900 yuan, or roughly $36,000 to $46,500.
- A single Datang generates the top-line revenue of five to six F3s.
- Across its initial 150,000 pre-orders, the aggregate sales value is roughly in line with 900,000 entry-level cars.
- The margin pivot is achieved entirely on a manganese-rich, nickel-free battery.
- Every Datang runs on what BYD calls its second-generation Blade Battery.
- Analysts believe the battery uses Lithium Manganese Iron Phosphate (LMFP) chemistry based on patent filings and its 3.8-volt platform.
- BYD has not officially confirmed the cathode material, and a regulatory filing for an earlier Blade 2.0 vehicle described a standard LFP pack.
- The formulation drops nickel and cobalt in favor of lithium, iron, phosphorus, and manganese.
- BYD rates Datang’s top-tier pack at 950 kilometers of CLTC range.
- The top-tier option features a 130.15-kWh pack that reportedly delivers real-world highway range well above 600 kilometers.
- Indonesia pursued an OPEC-style mineral cartel to weaponize its vast nickel reserves and extract outsized profits from the global EV transition.
- Chinese battery-maker CATL’s integrated $6 billion nickel mining, refining, and cell-manufacturing complex in Indonesia was designed as a showcase for downstream ambitions but has become a case study in sovereign risk.
- Throughout 2026, Indonesia implemented abrupt mandates including sudden mining-quota cuts, arbitrary export levies, rigid foreign-exchange retention rules, and last-minute policy reversals.
- Major Chinese nickel smelters are already throttling output, and planned ambitious capacity expansions are now on hold.
- Appetite for a nickel-centric industrial model has cooled fast, hitting major capital commitments from Japan’s Sumitomo Metal Mining, LG Energy Solution, and Singaporean resource houses.
- The Indonesian rupiah recently touched a record low of over 18,000 per dollar.
- Fitch Ratings cut Indonesia’s sovereign credit outlook to negative, reflecting broader capital flight.
- Indonesia’s policy instability has accelerated a technical substitution already underway in EV battery-making.
- Advanced solid-state pipelines remain deeply committed to high-nickel cathodes, modifying only the electrolyte layer rather than cathode chemistry.
- The real existential threat to Indonesia’s model is the rapid, mass-market scaling of manganese-rich platforms like LMFP, which can compete with or displace high-nickel ternary cells even in premium vehicles.
- Automakers increasingly treat de-nickelization not just as cost-cutting, but as a geopolitical hedge against unpredictable resource-exporting governments.
- Within Southeast Asia’s domestic auto markets, real growth is going to low-cost, zero-nickel systems split between sodium-ion platforms and manganese-rich chemistries.
- CATL’s 15-gigawatt-hour high-nickel ternary facility in Indonesia depends almost entirely on premium export markets in Europe and North America.
- That niche is shrinking as automaking giants including BMW, Volkswagen, and Volvo aggressively diversify their cell procurement to reduce exposure to Indonesian supply chains and ESG risks.
- Strict US Foreign Entity of Concern rules and Inflation Reduction Act tax credits explicitly bar Chinese-majority joint ventures from subsidy eligibility.
- CATL’s Indonesian project phase-one production is pushed to late 2026, with full capacity delayed until 2031.
- The US IRA subsidy window closes in 2032, leaving at most six years of subsidized, high-margin operations for the facility.
- Large battery plants typically need an eight-to-10-year runway to break even.
- Battery-industry forecasts broadly agree that manganese-rich, nickel-free cathodes will dominate mainstream EV production by decade’s end.
- Indonesia’s mining sector generates more than $32 billion a year in overseas revenue but remains tied to low-margin, primary downstream processing.
- Optimistic backers point to the operational flexibility of local Rotary Kiln Electric Furnace (RKEF) facilities, which could theoretically be reverse-converted from battery-grade nickel matte back into raw Ferronickel or Nickel Pig Iron for stainless steel production.
- Trading high-margin energy-transition feedstock for cyclical, low-margin bulk steelmaking undercuts the investment case for Indonesia’s nickel push.
- Western and Chinese steel mills are absorbing regional nickel intermediates due to scrap shortages.
- Wood Mackenzie’s Global Nickel Supply-Demand Outlook puts traditional stainless steel demand at roughly 72% of total global nickel supply.
- BYD’s second-generation LMFP blade battery is the vanguard of a broader zero-nickel transition alongside sodium-ion and high-voltage manganese platforms scaling across major automotive platforms.
- Long-term passenger vehicle nickel demand faces a steep decline.
- The reported collapse of an Indonesia-Philippines nickel alliance over clashing commercial interests will further weaken Jakarta’s leverage over global nickel supply.
- S&P Global commodity analysis found that Jakarta’s mining-quota cuts triggered faster nickel output growth in the Philippines, Madagascar, and parts of Africa.
- Indonesia retains steady low-grade nickel demand from stainless steel production, but its ambition to monopolize high-value EV battery materials has collapsed.
