# Georgia General Assembly

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2024-04-30

**Source:** [GA Tax Legislation Update | Individual Income Tax Deduction](https://windhambrannon.com/blog/georgia-tax-law-2024/)  ·  `page_009902`

## Facts

- During its 2024 legislative session ending March 28, 2024, the Georgia General Assembly passed several key tax bills impacting state policy.
- HB 1015 lowers Georgia’s individual income tax rate from 5.49 percent to 5.39 percent for tax years beginning after Jan. 1, 2024.
- HB 1023 matches the corporate income tax rate to the individual income tax rate set in HB 1015 at 5.39 percent, applicable to every domestic and foreign corporation paying taxable income in Georgia and pass-through entity owners electing entity-level taxation.
- HB 1181 reduces the carryforward period for most income tax credits from 10 years to five years, specifically affecting the research and development credit, jobs tax credit, and investment tax credit.
- HB 1181 reduces certain other credits, such as those for student scholarships and film production, from five years to three years, with changes applying only to credits earned after Jan. 1, 2025.
- HB 1181 adds sunset dates to some tax incentives and exemptions in Georgia.
- HB 1162 conforms Georgia’s revenue code to the federal Internal Revenue Code (IRC) with no additional decoupling from federal tax law.
- HB 598 transitions the Georgia Tax Tribunal from an administrative court in the executive branch to a judicial branch court, with voters deciding on a ballot question in November 2024 whether to grant it superior court authority.
- If the November 2024 referendum passes, HB 1267 will enable the Georgia Tax Court to begin operations on Jan. 1, 2026, and receive cases starting Aug. 1, 2026.
- HB 1019 allows voters to decide in November 2024 whether to increase the homestead exemption from $2,000 to $10,000.
- HB 1022 proposes a state constitutional change allowing local governments to offer property tax relief for homeowners by limiting how much the taxable value of a primary residence can increase.
- HB 581 proposes capping annual increases in home taxable value, likely based on inflation, and allows counties to enact a new flexible local sales tax with a generally three percent cap, separate from existing tourism (HOST) and education (LOST) taxes.
- HB 808 increases the ad valorem tax exemption for tangible personal property in a county from $7,500 to $20,000, exempting taxpayers if their total tangible personal property fair market value is under $20,000.
- HB 1180, which would have revamped Georgia’s film tax credit program with stricter criteria and limits on sold or transferred credits, died in the Senate without a vote despite committee approval.
- Legislation passed by the General Assembly requires action from Governor Brian Kemp within 40 days of the session's close to become law; otherwise, it lapses.
