# Kennesaw State University

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2026-02-03

**Source:** [Kennesaw State researcher develops safer, faster solid-state battery design  - News](https://www.kennesaw.edu/news/stories/2026/researcher-develops-safer-solid-state-battery.php)  ·  `page_000346`

## Facts

- A Kennesaw State University researcher is leading development of a new materials approach to make next-generation solid-state batteries safer and more sustainable.
- Beibei Jiang, an assistant professor in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at Kennesaw State University, leads the research team developing a sulfur-modified solid electrolyte for solid-state batteries.
- The sulfur-modified solid electrolyte is designed to improve lithium ion movement through solid-state batteries, increasing performance while reducing safety risks.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang stated the team's goal is to replace flammable components in batteries to make them much safer.
- The team aims to reduce the risk of overheating, short circuits, and fires by removing the liquid electrolyte and redesigning the solid materials inside the battery.
- Solid-state batteries replace flammable liquid electrolytes with a solid material that separates electrodes and allows lithium ion movement during charging and discharging.
- The solid-state battery design improves safety but limits charging speed because lithium ions move more slowly through solids.
- Jiang's team addressed slow ion movement by adding sulfur-based chemical groups to a composite solid electrolyte made of ceramic and polymer components.
- The added sulfur improves bonding between the ceramic and polymer components, reducing resistance and allowing lithium ions to move more easily.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang compared lithium ions to cars on a highway, stating their sulfur modification smooths the path for faster ion movement, enabling faster charging and better performance.
- The research team discovered a strong interaction between sulfur and zirconium in the ceramic portion of the electrolyte.
- Dr. Jiang's group is the first to identify and document this strong sulfur-zirconium interaction.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang stated they believe the sulfur-zirconium interaction is the main reason for the improved battery performance.
- The discovery originated from students noticing a chemical reaction occurring far more quickly than expected during early experiments.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang described the discovery as almost accidental, noting the reaction happened in just a few seconds and got out of control before they investigated how to slow it down and make it controllable.
- Lawrence Whitman, dean of the Southern Polytechnic College of Engineering and Engineering Technology at Kennesaw State University, stated the work reflects the college's applied research focus.
- Dean Lawrence Whitman stated Dr. Jiang's research addresses a pressing challenge in energy storage by improving safety and performance to move solid-state battery technology closer to real-world application.
- The research project is supported by a $200,000 grant from the National Science Foundation.
- The project also receives seed funding from Kennesaw State University's Office of Research.
- Most of the research takes place in Dr. Jiang's lab on Kennesaw State University's Marietta Campus.
- Students at the lab synthesize materials, assemble prototype batteries, and test performance using small coin-cell designs.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang stated the team's current focus is proving the design works, is stable, and is reliable before considering scaling and manufacturing.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang believes the solid-state battery research has strong potential applications in electric vehicles, renewable energy storage, and consumer electronics.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang encourages students from a range of engineering backgrounds to get involved in the research.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang stated that students from any background can contribute and emphasized the importance of getting started and gaining hands-on experience.
- Dr. Beibei Jiang's work represents a promising step toward making solid-state batteries a practical alternative to current lithium-ion technology as demand for safer and more efficient energy storage grows.
