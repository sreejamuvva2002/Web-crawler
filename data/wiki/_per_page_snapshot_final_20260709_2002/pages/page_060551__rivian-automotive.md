# Rivian Automotive

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-10-01

**Source:** [Rivian plant in Georgia loses property tax break after judge's ruling | Fortune](https://fortune.com/2022/10/01/rivian-georgia-plant-property-tax-break-judge-ruling-5-billion/)  ·  `page_060551`

## Facts

- A Georgia judge rejected an agreement that would have provided a huge property tax break to Rivian Automotive.
- The ruling clouds the upstart electric truck maker’s plans to build a plant east of Atlanta.
- Morgan County Superior Court Judge Brenda Trammell rejected a routine request by a local government to validate a bond agreement.
- The judge ruled that the development authority hadn’t proved that the $5 billion plant, projected to hire 7,500 people, was “sound, reasonable and feasible” as required under state law.
- Trammell ruled that Rivian should be required to pay regular property taxes because of its level of control over property it would be leasing from the development authority.
- Rivian declined to comment on the ruling.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development and a local four-county joint development authority that recruited Rivian expressed disappointment and respectfully disagreed with Judge Trammell’s decision.
- They stated they aren’t giving up on their plans and are considering an appeal.
- The groups said they remain undeterred in efforts to bring high-paying, American manufacturing jobs to Georgia and are currently assessing all legal options.
- The Irvine, California-based electric vehicle manufacturer announced last year that it would build the facility on a 2,000-acre (800-hectare) site in Morgan and Walton counties.
- The site is located about 45 miles (70 kilometers) east of Atlanta along Interstate 20.
- Rivian plans to produce up to 400,000 vehicles a year at the facility.
- Rivian also has a plant in Normal, Illinois.
- The company had hoped to break ground as early as this summer and begin production in 2024.
- By maintaining ownership of the property and leasing it to Rivian, local governments would have exempted Rivian from a projected $700 million in property taxes over 25 years.
- Rivian agreed to make $300 million in payments in lieu of taxes during that period.
- The property tax break is a key part of the $1.2 billion in tax breaks and incentives that Georgia and local officials offered for Rivian to build a plant in the state.
- The long-used maneuver circumvents a ban in Georgia’s state constitution on giving “gratuities” to companies or individuals.
- If Trammell’s order requiring normal property taxes is upheld, it could call other big tax breaks into question and keep officials from using the tool in the future.
- The state plans to spend $200 million to buy the site and prepare it.
- Rivian could claim a projected $200 million income tax credit.
- Rivian could claim $280 million in sales tax breaks on machinery and construction materials.
- The state plans to spend $90 million to build a job training center and train workers.
- The judge found persuasive the arguments of a group of local residents who oppose development of the plant, saying it will spoil their quality of life in a rural area that Atlanta’s sprawl is now encroaching upon.
- JoEllen Artz, president of opposition group Morgan Land, Sky & Water Preservation, said it was very fulfilling that local citizens were able to band together to do research and bring a legal team on board to deliver fantastic results.
- Artz and other opponents intervened in the lawsuit to question the appropriateness of the tax break deal proposed by the four-county joint development authority.
- The company has encountered difficulties in ramping up production in Illinois.
- Rivian’s once soaring stock price has tumbled with some key investors dumping shares.
- Trammell wrote that local and state officials appeared not to have considered the higher costs of services that local governments would incur.
- The judge questioned whether Rivian had the money to complete the project.
- Trammell wrote that “Rivian’s cash reserves are quickly drying up, thus casting serious doubt on whether it will be able to commence, let alone complete, the project.”
