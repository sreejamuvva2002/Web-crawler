# SK Innovation

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [The Georgia GOP Is Begging Biden To Act On Battery Plant, While Dems Stay Silent | HuffPost Latest News](https://www.huffpost.com/entry/georgia-skinnovation_n_605a0947c5b6cebf58d2123a?origin=article-related-nonlife)  ·  `page_050002`

## Facts

- A U.S. International Trade Commission ruling threatens to shut down a giant electric vehicle battery factory set to employ upward of 2,600 workers in the suburbs north of Athens, Georgia.
- Georgia’s Republican lawmakers have emerged as the project’s most vocal defenders.
- President Biden has just 19 days left to veto trade authorities’ ruling before it takes effect.
- The situation is enormously important for jobs in Georgia and the administration’s plans to electrify the nation’s automobiles over the next decade.
- Last month, the ITC ruled in favor of South Korean battery giant LG Chem, finding rival SK Innovation guilty in a trade secrets case.
- The ITC ruling bans SK Innovation from importing what it needs to run its planned Georgia factory for the next decade.
- There has been no sign from the White House that it will block the decision.
- SK Innovation has hired consultants to draft a plan to shut down its $2.6 billion plant in Georgia.
- The potential shutdown threatens one of the largest economic development projects in Georgia’s history.
- Pat Wilson, the GOP commissioner of Georgia’s Department of Economic Development, stated: “There’s nothing partisan about the jobs of the future.”
- Georgia’s Republican leaders have been very public in their call for Biden to act to save the Commerce, Georgia, plant.
- None of the six Democrats representing Georgia in the House of Representatives responded to requests for comment.
- The Georgia Democratic Party did not respond to requests for comment.
- Five emails to Democratic Sen. Jon Ossoff’s office went unanswered.
- Former gubernatorial candidate Stacey Abrams declined an interview request on the topic.
- Sen. Raphael Warnock (D-Ga.) declined an interview but provided a link to a March 4 confirmation hearing video where he pressed Polly Trottenberg, Biden’s pick for the No. 2 job at the Department of Transportation, on whether the agency would provide an analysis of the ITC ruling’s effect on U.S. battery supplies.
- During the hearing, Warnock said: “It’s not a good position for U.S. automakers if they’re only relying on almost one cell supplier.”
- Warnock stated during the hearing (which took place a month after the ruling): “An adverse ruling by the International Trade Commission threatens seriously the future of that project, whether it’ll happen at all,” and noted it would be “one of the largest economic investments in Georgia’s history and a severe punch in the gut, if you will, for folks who are counting on those jobs, not to mention President Biden’s own goals.”
- Warnock faces reelection in two years amid mounting pressure from the right in Georgia.
- Conservative Atlanta-based pundit Erick Erickson called the ITC’s decision “a bad day for all of us here in Georgia” and compared the potential job loss to construction workers laid off after Biden blocked the Keystone XL oil pipeline.
- Erickson wrote: “You would think Biden would want to salvage this because Georgia is a state where Raphael Warnock and Jon Ossoff have gotten elected,” adding that if they lack political clout to reverse the decision, it will “help contribute to the loss of over 2,000 jobs in Georgia” going into 2022.
- Carol Browner, former Clinton-era EPA administrator, served as an expert witness for SK Innovation and testified before the ITC that eliminating its Georgia plant put the U.S. electric vehicle supply chain at risk.
- Former Obama-era deputy attorney general Sally Yates told The New York Times’ Dealbook that the case could “seriously undermine critical progress on the climate crisis” and risked “killing jobs and setting back the Georgia economy.”
- Civil rights leader and former UN ambassador Andrew Young warned in an opinion piece that the ITC ruling “imperils thousands of direct and indirect Georgia jobs and postpones the future of the new era auto industry” over what he called a technicality.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp (R) repeatedly urged Biden to “support thousands of hardworking Georgians and their communities.”
- On March 12, Gov. Kemp sent a letter to Biden asking him to “exercise the authority granted to you under law to disapprove the ITC ruling on grounds that it is contrary to the public interest and will seriously jeopardize your administration’s environmental and economic goals.”
- Kemp wrote: “Simply put: the livelihoods of thousands of Georgians are now in your hands,” and “This critical opportunity requires us to work together for the good of my state and our country.”
- GOP Rep. Buddy Carter hoped Biden was helping broker an agreement between LG Chem and SK Innovation to allow manufacturing plans to proceed.
- Carter said: “We don’t want to lose those jobs in Georgia and we don’t want to lose those jobs in the electric vehicle industry,” and expressed hope that the president has staff engaged in trying to broker a deal.
- The White House’s U.S. Trade Representative stated it was “currently reviewing the policy implications of the ITC determination.”
- Spokesman Adam Hodge said the review will be completed within the 60-day deadline outlined by federal law.
- The dispute started in 2019 when LG’s battery subsidiary, LG Energy Solutions, lodged a complaint with the ITC.
- The underlying dispute began in 2017 when SK Innovation poached engineers in Seoul, prompting LG to file lawsuits accusing them of violating noncompete contracts.
- Around the time SK Innovation announced plans to open its massive Georgia factory to supply Ford Motor Company and Volkswagen with batteries, LG turned to the ITC.
- LG filed a separate lawsuit in Delaware federal courts for monetary damages for alleged intellectual property theft, pending the conclusion of the ITC case.
- Going to the ITC is considered “the nuclear option” because guilty verdicts typically carry trade bans that restrict imports.
- SK Innovation denied stealing trade secrets but on Feb. 10, the ITC found it guilty of destroying documents allegedly detailing what it learned from LG hires.
- SK Innovation argued that South Korean laws do not require companies to retain the same documents as U.S. statutes.
- The commission ruled unanimously to ban SK Innovation from importing raw materials for the next 10 years, which LG said it would take to develop batteries independently.
- The ITC granted a carve-out allowing SK Innovation to build batteries for the next four years for Ford’s electric F-150 pickup truck and two years for Volkswagen’s Crossover series.
- The commission determined this timeframe should give automakers enough time to find alternative sources or for SK Innovation to reach a settlement with LG.
- U.S. manufacturing capacity for electric vehicle batteries is currently nascent and fragile.
- Tesla is the largest battery maker in the U.S., but its Nevada Gigafactory only produces packs for its own vehicles.
- LG operates projects across the Midwest that, when completed, will produce roughly 30 gigawatt-hours per year to provide batteries to General Motors and other automakers.
- SK Innovation’s Georgia plant would add another 22 gigawatt-hours of annual capacity.
- The rest of the battery market is made up of much smaller companies.
- LG sent a letter on March 10 to Warnock vowing to “do whatever we can to help the people and workers of Georgia,” hinting it was prepared to make investments in the state to offset the closure of SK Innovation’s factory.
- David Callahan, a lawyer for LG, stated the company is preparing to invest $4.5 billion in the U.S., resulting in at least two more factories, one of which could be in Georgia.
- SK Innovation stated that LG is “using the ITC to try to muscle SK out of the US EV battery market, period.”
- A SK Innovation spokesperson said: “SK has always been open to good-faith settlement talks with LG,” but criticized LG as “inflexible and unreasonable” and noted it “doesn’t want competition.”
- James Frith, an analyst at BloombergNEF, warned that allowing LG to dominate the battery market would pose risks to producing EVs at the speed and affordability the Biden administration wants.
- Frith stated: “Competition within the battery industry has been one of the key factors that helped to drive down cell and pack prices over the past decade,” and noted it’s not a good position for U.S. automakers to rely on almost one cell supplier that could set prices as it liked.
- Other manufacturers that could fill SK Innovation’s void include Japan’s Panasonic Corporation, South Korea’s Samsung, and China’s CATL or BYD.
- Callahan denied LG was seeking a monopoly and accused SK Innovation of bluffing about its willingness to shutter the plant to avoid a costly settlement.
- Callahan said: “If SK Innovation intends to be in this hugely growing market in the long haul, which they have said they are, it would be suicide to leave Ford and Volkswagen in the lurch,” and called the threat to walk away an “idle threat.”
- Callahan added: “They’re threatening Georgia. If I were Georgia, I would be mad. I understand for political reasons not everyone is going to acknowledge that.”
- SK Innovation already started hiring hundreds of workers, and it could take years for another company to replace the plant or increase supply to levels needed to electrify the U.S. auto fleet.
- Pat Wilson stated: “We need batteries in order to keep up with the demand, this was not only an investment into Georgia but an investment into electric vehicles in the United States and a diversification of the supply chain outside of Asia,” adding, “We saw this as a win-win for the state and the country.”
