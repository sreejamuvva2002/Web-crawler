# State of Georgia

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-01-01

**Source:** [Georgia Enacts Income Tax Reduction Bill | BDO](https://www.bdo.com/insights/tax/georgia-enacts-income-tax-reduction-bill)  ·  `page_009907`

## Facts

- Gov. Brian Kemp signed H.B. 463, which makes many taxpayer-favorable changes to the state’s income tax code.
- H.B. 463 reduces Georgia’s personal and corporate income tax rates from 5.19% to 4.99% for 2026.
- The bill allows for potential further annual rate reductions of 0.125% down to a floor of 3.99%, pending the state meeting revenue collection targets.
- H.B. 463 increases the standard deduction and dependent exemptions for individual income tax purposes for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2026.
- The bill provides a temporary reduced exclusion of overtime pay and tips subject to state tax compared to the amounts exempt from federal income tax under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.
- Taxpayers subject to ASC 740 should account for the currently effective 2026 tax rate change in their income tax provisions under the accounting standard.
- For deferred income tax purposes, taxpayers must measure deferred income taxes using enacted rates expected to apply to taxable income in the periods when deferred tax items reverse.
- The contingent nature of subsequent years’ tax rate reductions leaves open whether and when such reduced rates will go into effect.
- Taxpayers should be cognizant of deferred income tax implications associated with tax rates that have yet to be enacted and therefore have uncertain effective dates.
- Due to the tie-in with state revenue collection targets, taxpayers should not automatically expect additional decreases to the individual and corporate income tax rates to be immediately effective for tax years beginning January 1, 2027.
- Taxpayers should consider the contingent nature of any future rate reductions in the context of deferred income tax accounting.
- H.B. 463 is one of several developments during Georgia’s 2026 legislative cycle.
