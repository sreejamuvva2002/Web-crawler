# Hyundai-LG Energy Solution Battery Plant at Ellabell, Georgia (Metaplant America)

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09

**Source:** [South Korea-US Relations Suffer the Aftermath of ICE’s Georgia Raid – The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2025/09/south-korea-us-relations-suffer-the-aftermath-of-ices-georgia-raid/)  ·  `page_000420`

## Facts

- In early September, U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) raided the Hyundai-LG Energy Solution’s battery factory in Ellabell, Georgia.
- Approximately 300 South Korean workers were on site during the raid, which involved choppers, drones, armored vehicles, and ICE agents.
- Most of the detained South Korean workers were automation programmers, mechanical engineers, and information technology specialists.
- At the time of the raid, construction of the electric vehicle battery factory was 98 percent completed.
- The South Korean workforce had been dispatched to fine-tune machinery and train local workers.
- ICE shackled the ankles and waists of the South Korean workers at gunpoint and forced them onto transportation vehicles.
- ICE alleged that the South Korean workers had overstayed their visas or held permits that excluded manual labor.
- Most of the workers arrived in Georgia with an ESTA visa waiver or a B-1 business visa intended for installing/maintaining imported machinery and educating Americans.
- Hyundai, LG Energy Solution, and their subcontractors insist they followed U.S. Department of State guidelines and communicated with the U.S. Embassy in South Korea regarding visa compliance.
- ICE’s Georgia raid was Homeland Security’s largest single worksite operation ever conducted in its history.
- Georgia and Savannah local officials viewed Hyundai and LG as key to uplifting rural coastal counties.
- Governor Brian Kemp and his economic development team visited Seoul to entice investment to Georgia.
- In 2022, Georgia secured a manufacturing deal with Hyundai.
- Hyundai’s total investment in Georgia has grown to $12.6 billion.
- The Hyundai-LG Energy Solution battery cell plant is valued at $4.3 billion on its own.
- The plant was slated to annually produce enough battery cells to power approximately 300,000 electric vehicles.
- Trip Tollison, head of the Savannah Economic Development Authority (SEDA), stated Hyundai’s presence brings more economic benefit to Georgia than the Atlanta Olympics did.
- Georgia state and local governments have invested more than $350 million in road improvements around the production site.
- The area’s ports have been enlarged to facilitate shipment of manufacturing parts and cars.
- South Korea is Georgia’s third-largest international trading partner.
- Governor Kemp entered crisis management mode, conveying embarrassment and an apology regarding the raid.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development and SEDA met with Hyundai Motor executives to discuss returning deported workers.
- Trip Tollison told the Savannah Morning News that no other entity in the world possesses the proprietary technology required to install the battery equipment, emphasizing reliance on South Korea.
- Governor Kemp planned to visit South Korea again to meet with Hyundai executives for damage control.
- The Ellabell battery plant is losing $2.3 million per day due to the forced construction stall.
- On September 14, U.S. Deputy Secretary of State Christopher Landau visited Seoul and expressed “deep regrets” over the detention incident.
- Landau assured that deported workers would face no disadvantages upon returning and that no further similar incidents would occur.
- President Donald Trump posted that he does not want to frighten off or disincentivize foreign investment into America, welcoming companies and their employees.
- The Congressional Research Service noted the raid raised concerns in South Korea about bilateral relations and whether U.S. immigration policy conflicts with increasing U.S. manufacturing jobs through foreign investment.
- The raid instilled fears among South Korean companies with U.S. projects, including Samsung Electronics ($37 billion semiconductor foundry in Texas) and SK Hynix ($3.8 billion Indiana plant for high-bandwidth memory chips by 2028).
- Weeks before the raid, Hyundai pledged to increase its total U.S. investment to $26 billion.
- Since the raid, South Korean companies have repatriated workers, and specialized workers postponed or canceled U.S. trips.
- Workers developed a collective phobia of U.S. business trips after seeing colleagues shackled and forced to survive on bread and canned beans for days.
- Trump offered detained workers the chance to stay and continue work, but only one opted to remain.
- Completion dates for South Korean plants in the U.S. are being delayed, causing economic loss for companies and local communities.
- South Korean President Lee Jae-myung noted that businesses investing in the U.S. will be very hesitant following the raid.
- Seoul and Washington are negotiating a verbal commitment to invest $350 billion into America, with the Georgia detention trauma looming large.
- The incident prompted multinational companies to flood U.S. legal firms with requests for visa advice for employees on similar visas.
- Non-Korean companies halted some planned U.S. trips after seeing South Korean employees treated “like criminals.”
- It is common industry practice for foreign companies to bring specialized workers on short-term business visitor visas (ESTA, B-1/B-2), especially in battery manufacturing, shipbuilding, and semiconductors where U.S. workers lack technical expertise.
- L-1 visas are for nonimmigrant intracompany transferees, but most specialized workers are subcontractors without access to this category.
- H-1B visas have a low annual cap of 85,000, are exorbitant to apply for, highly in demand, and the lottery system prevents quick acquisition for short-notice technicians.
- Previous U.S. administrations allowed high-skilled foreign nationals under visitors’ visas (ESTA for business activities, B-1 for equipment installment/training).
- ICE under the Trump administration took a more restrictive view of business visitor allowances than regulations permit, claiming individuals at Hyundai’s plant were “fraudulently using visitors’ visas.”
- U.S. officials have shown an apologetic stance and willingness to accommodate South Korea’s visa requirements following the raid.
- Deputy Secretary of State Landau proposed talks on issuing proper visas for South Korean workers, citing a need for Washington’s institutional support.
- Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick stated Trump will address tensions caused by the raid.
- The most likely outcome is passage of the Partner with Korea Act (proposed in 2013), which intends to create an E-4 visa category for nonimmigrant specialized South Korean workers to build U.S. manufacturing capacity.
- Experts believe at least 20,000 E-4 visas should be issued annually given investment size and the “Make America’s Shipbuilding Great Again (MASGA)” commitment.
- The Foreign Ministry in Seoul noted the general public in South Korea “felt deeply shocked by the crackdown.”
- The raid engendered rare political unity in South Korea, with politicians across partisan lines deploring images of people carted off in chains.
- Almost 40 percent of South Korean conservatives said they were “deeply disappointed” by the Trump administration.
- South Korean workers remain reluctant to set foot in the U.S., with trust shattered and a lasting sense of humiliation despite Washington's visa offers.
