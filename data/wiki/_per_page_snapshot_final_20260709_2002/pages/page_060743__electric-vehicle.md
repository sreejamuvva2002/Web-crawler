# Electric vehicle

**Type:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Electric vehicle - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_vehicle)  ·  `page_060743`

## Facts

- An electric vehicle (EV) is a vehicle propelled mostly by electric power, encompassing road vehicles, rail, boats, aircraft, and spacecraft.
- EVs originated in the late 19th century but were hindered by limited range until lithium battery advances revived interest in the late 20th century.
- Manufacturers initially switched to hybrids with internal combustion engines and electric motor supplements before plug-in hybrids saw mass production in the late 2000s.
- Battery electric cars became practical for consumers in the 2010s.
- Technological progress in EV batteries, traction motors, and automotive electronics made EVs more feasible and cost-efficient than conventional ICE vehicles in the 21st century.
- Government incentives are available in many areas to promote EV adoption to reduce tailpipe emissions and fossil fuel dependency.
- As of 2026, China's electric vehicle industry produces more than the rest of the world combined.
- In January 1990, General Motors President introduced the "Impact" EV concept at the Los Angeles Auto Show.
- The California Air Resources Board mandated major automaker EV sales starting in phases from 1998.
- From 1996 to 1998, GM produced 1,117 EV1s, with 800 available through three-year leases.
- Chrysler, Ford, GM, Honda, and Toyota produced limited EVs for California drivers during this period.
- GM discontinued the EV1 in 2003 after lease expiration, citing federal court challenges to zero-emission mandates, spare parts regulations, and industry media campaigns.
- A 2005-2006 film titled "Who Killed the Electric Car?" explored roles of automakers, oil industries, the U.S. government, and the public in limiting EV deployment.
- Ford released Ecostar delivery vans, while Honda, Nissan, and Toyota repossessed and crushed most of their leased EVs; Toyota later sold 200 RAV4 EVs at over $40,000 each after protests.
- Citroën stopped production of the Berlingo Electrique in September 2005, and Zenn produced vehicles from 2006 to 2009.
- Renewed interest in EVs emerged in the late 20th/early 21st century due to petroleum infrastructure impacts and peak oil fears.
- EVs consume electricity generated from fossil fuels, nuclear power, renewables like solar and wind, or combinations thereof.
- Recent advancements in battery technology and charging infrastructure have addressed earlier adoption barriers.
- EV carbon footprints vary based on electricity generation fuel and technology.
- Electricity can be stored via batteries, flywheels, or supercapacitors.
- Regenerative braking recovers kinetic energy typically lost as heat during friction braking, restoring it to the onboard battery.
- Over 20% of new cars sold in 2024 were electric-only, while only 2% of trucks were.
- China accounted for more than 70% of global EV production and 67% of global sales in 2024.
- EVs are categorized by fuel source: combustion-only (ICE), micro hybrid, mild hybrid, plug-in hybrid (PHEV), all-electric (BEV/AEV), and fuel cell (FCV/FCEV).
- EVs are much more efficient than ICE vehicles with few direct emissions but rely on grid electricity from non-fossil and fossil plants.
- Some areas allow individuals to request utilities provide renewable energy for EV charging, increasing energy resilience.
- EVs connect to generator plants via direct electric grids (trains/trams), online power strips via electromagnetic induction, or onboard generators (diesel, fuel cell, nuclear, solar).
- Hybrid EVs can combine onboard rechargeable storage with direct land-based grid connections for highway recharging or fueled propulsion sources.
- Full electric vehicles store power externally before disconnecting, using chemical batteries (typically lithium-ion), kinetic flywheels, or static double-layer capacitors.
- Electro-chemical battery conversions are reversible, storing energy chemically and improving efficiency by avoiding intermediate mechanical steps.
- Battery, traction motor, and controller designs vary by vehicle size and application, from shopping carts to industrial forklifts.
- EV batteries typically use lithium-ion technology due to higher energy density, longer lifespan, and higher power density compared to other practical batteries.
- Most BEVs include a separate 12-volt auxiliary battery powered by the traction battery via a DC/DC converter for accessories and controls.
- In 2024, Hyundai and Kia recalls cited Integrated Charging Control Unit (ICCU) failures that could stop 12-volt charging and cause drive power loss.
- Lithium-ion batteries were first commercially released in 1991 and dominate EVs due to safety, durability, thermal breakdown, environmental impact, and cost factors requiring safe temperature/voltage operation.
- Operating a subset of battery cells at a time and switching them is a technique to increase lifespan and reduce costs/environmental impact.
- Nickel-metal hydride batteries were previously used by GM but are outdated due to heat self-discharge, Chevron patent restrictions, and high costs.
- Lithium-ion battery prices declined dramatically over the past decade, reducing EV prices, though critical mineral lithium prices increased from 2021 to late 2022.
- Electric motor power is measured in kilowatts (kW) and can deliver maximum torque over a wide RPM range, often exceeding comparable ICE performance.
- Charging efficiency varies by charger type, with energy lost during electrical-to-mechanical conversion.
- DC electricity is typically fed into a DC/AC inverter to power 3-phase AC motors, though DC or universal motors are used for trains, forklifts, and some cars.
- Tesla vehicles use induction motors, while Nissan Leaf and Chevrolet Bolt use permanent magnet machines.
- Electric motors achieve approximately 90% energy conversion efficiency over full speed/power ranges and can be finely controlled with high stationary-to-moving torque without gearboxes.
- Linear motors drive maglev trains via magnetic levitation, eliminating rolling resistance and mechanical wear but restricting operations to high-speed point-to-point services.
- Regenerative braking in electric traction transforms train motion into electrical power fed back into lines, advantageous for mountainous and start-and-stop city use.
- EVs provide quiet operation with less noise/vibration than ICE vehicles, prompting many countries to mandate slow-speed warning sounds for pedestrian safety.
- Pure-electric vehicles are powered exclusively by electric motors from batteries, solar panels, or fuel cells.
- Hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs) combine conventional ICE with one or more electric engines, using energy recovery technologies like motor-generators and regenerative braking.
- Some HEVs use an internal combustion engine to directly drive a generator for battery recharging or motor power, known as a range extender.
- Many HEVs reduce idle emissions via start-stop systems that shut down the combustion engine at idle and restart it when needed.
- Hybrid-electric systems produce less tailpipe emissions than comparable petrol vehicles due to smaller engine displacement and lower fuel consumption.
- Parallel hybrids mechanically couple engines and motors to wheels, series hybrids use only electric motors (often called EREVs/REEVs), and series-parallel hybrids allow engine-only, motor-only, or combined operation.
- Plug-in electric vehicles (PEVs) recharge from external sources like wall sockets, with stored electricity driving wheels; PEVs include BEVs, PHEVs, and conversions.
- Range-extended electric vehicles (REEVs) use an electric motor and plug-in battery, with an auxiliary combustion engine only supplementing charging.
- On-road EVs include cars, trolleybuses, buses, trucks, bicycles, motorcycles, scooters, neighborhood vehicles, golf carts, milk floats, and forklifts; off-road includes ATVs and tractors.
- Electric trucks transport cargo or perform utilitarian work, using lead-acid batteries historically but now lighter, energy-dense chemistries for broader roles.
- Electric trucks reduce noise/pollution and have dramatically lower ownership/operating costs due to high efficiency, low component counts, no idle fuel burning, and silent acceleration.
- Long-distance freight truck electrification is hindered by battery weight reducing payload and frequent recharging reducing delivery time, while short-haul urban delivery has electrified rapidly.
- Railborne EVs use fixed overhead lines or third rails for power, enabling electric locomotives, multiple units, trams, light rail, and rapid transit common in Europe and Asia.
- Electric trains have high power-to-weight ratios, allowing TGVs to operate at 320km/h (200mph)+ and locomotives to exceed diesel power output, with regenerative braking returning power to the grid.
- Maglev trains are nearly always EVs using magnetic levitation for near-zero rolling resistance.
- Battery electric passenger trains operate on non-electrified rail lines.
- Fuel-cell electric trains are gaining popularity in Europe, with Germany ordering Alstom Coradia iLINT sets since 2018 and France planning orders.
- The UK, Netherlands, Denmark, Norway, Italy, Canada, and Mexico show interest in hydrogen trains; SNCF plans to replace remaining diesel-electric trains with hydrogen trains by 2035.
- Alstom announced in 2018 plans to retrofit British Rail Class 321 trainsets with fuel cells.
- Electric boats saw renewed interest due to quiet/renewable marine transport potential, with solar cells giving motorboats infinite range and electric motors replacing diesel in sailboats.
- Fully electric tugboats operate in Auckland (June 2022), Vancouver (October 2023), and San Diego.
- Electric aircraft include piloted and unpiloted aerial vehicles, with power sources for spacecraft including batteries, solar panels, and nuclear power.
- Spacecraft propulsion methods include arcjet rockets, electrostatic ion thrusters, Hall-effect thrusters, and Field Emission Electric Propulsion.
- Electric rovers are the only option for outer space/exoplanetary exploration due to lack of oxygen, with Apollo missions (1971-1972) using silver-oxide battery-powered Lunar Roving Vehicles traveling up to 35.7 km.
- Solar-powered uncrewed rovers have explored the Moon and Mars.
- Charging stations (EVSE) supply power for recharging plug-in EV batteries, with AC and DC types being main categories.
- EV batteries require DC charging, so most vehicles have onboard AC-to-DC converters; DC chargers integrate this converter to bypass vehicle constraints.
- Battery swapping mechanically replaces batteries at stations in minutes as an alternative to socket recharging.
- Metal-air fuel cells like zinc-air batteries may require mechanical refueling by replacing anodes/electrolytes instead of electrical recharging.
- GM is adding V2H bidirectional charging to 2024-2026 models (Silverado, Blazer EV) to send battery power to homes during grid outages.
- EVs release no tailpipe pollutants, reduce respiratory illnesses like asthma, and could prevent hundreds of thousands of early deaths annually from air pollution.
- EVs significantly reduce urban noise pollution, improving quality of life.
- In most cases, EV lifecycle carbon emissions are lower than conventional vehicles, though cost-responsive charging strategies can increase embedded carbon emissions.
- Urban EVs almost always pollute less than ICE vehicles, but grid electricity sources (especially coal) and battery manufacturing/recycling create negative environmental impacts including toxic metals.
- ICE vehicles use more raw materials over their lifetime than EVs; estimates suggest over a fifth of lithium and about 65% of cobalt for electric cars will come from recycled sources by 2035.
- Switching privately owned car fleets from ICE to EVs does not free up road space for active travel or public transport.
