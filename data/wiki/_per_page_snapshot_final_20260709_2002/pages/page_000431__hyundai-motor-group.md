# Hyundai Motor Group

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [ICE raids Georgia Hyundai plant and detains 475 in massive operation | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/immigration-raid-hyundai-plant-4dd1a6b2ad66d27567b2463c5f3c97bb)  ·  `page_000431`

## Facts

- Hundreds of federal agents descended on a sprawling site where Hyundai manufactures electric vehicles in Georgia and detained 475 people, most of them South Korean nationals.
- The raid occurred on a Thursday and targeted a manufacturing site that state officials have long called Georgia’s largest economic development project.
- The detainment of South Korean nationals is notable because they are rarely caught up in immigration enforcement compared to other nationalities.
- Video released by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement on Saturday showed federal agents directing workers to line up outside the site.
- Some detainees were ordered to put their hands up against a bus while being frisked and shackled around their hands, ankles, and waist.
- Other detainees had plastic ties around their wrists as they boarded a Georgia inmate-transfer bus.
- South Korea’s Foreign Minister Cho Hyun stated on Saturday that more than 300 South Koreans were among the 475 people detained.
- Some detainees worked for HL-GA Battery Co., a joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution slated to open next year, while others were employed by contractors and subcontractors at the construction site.
- Lead Georgia agent Steven Schrank stated that some detained workers had illegally crossed the U.S. border, while others had legally entered but possessed expired visas or entered on a visa waiver prohibiting work.
- An immigration attorney stated that two of his detained clients arrived from South Korea under a visa waiver program allowing tourism or business stays of 90 days or less without a visa.
- Attorney Charles Kuck noted one client had been in the U.S. for a couple of weeks and another for about 45 days, with both planning to return home soon.
- A detainee included a lawful permanent resident kept in custody due to a prior firearm and drug offense record that could jeopardize their status under the "crime of moral turpitude" rule.
- U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement public affairs officer Lindsay Williams denied reports that U.S. citizens were detained, stating the agency has no authority over them once they identify themselves.
- Hyundai Motor Company stated on Friday that none of its direct employees were detained and that it is reviewing supplier and subcontractor practices to ensure compliance with U.S. employment laws.
- LG Energy Solution told the Associated Press it could not immediately confirm how many of its own employees or Hyundai workers were detained.
- The South Korean government expressed "concern and regret" over the operation targeting its citizens and is sending diplomats to the site.
- South Korean Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lee Jaewoong stated in a televised address that "the business activities of our investors and the rights of our nationals must not be unjustly infringed in the process of U.S. law enforcement."
- Most detainees were transported to an immigration detention center in Folkston, Georgia, near the Florida state line.
- None of the detainees have been charged with crimes yet, though the investigation remains ongoing.
- Family members and friends struggled to locate or contact detained individuals, according to James Woo, communications director for Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta.
- Many detainees' families remain in South Korea because the workers were in the U.S. primarily for business purposes.
- The raid resulted from a months-long investigation into allegations of illegal hiring at the site.
- Search warrants and affidavits requested employment records for current and former workers, timecards, and video/photos of workers.
- Court records indicate prosecutors do not know who hired the individuals, with the U.S. Attorney’s Office stating the "actual company or contractor hiring the illegal aliens is currently unknown."
- Hyundai Motor Group began manufacturing electric vehicles at its $7.6 billion Georgia plant approximately one year ago.
- The site currently employs approximately 1,200 people in a largely rural area located about 25 miles (40 kilometers) west of Savannah.
- Agents focused on an adjacent plant still under construction where Hyundai partners with LG Energy Solution to produce EV batteries.
- The plant is located in Bryan County, which had a population of nearly 47,000 in 2023 after increasing by more than a quarter in the early 2020s.
- Bryan County's Asian population grew from 1.5% in 2018 to 2.2% in 2023, primarily driven by people of Indian descent.
- Lead agent Steven Schrank described the Georgia raid as the "largest single site enforcement operation" in his agency's two-decade history.
- According to ICE data, only 46 Koreans were deported out of over 270,000 total removals during the 12-month period ending September 30, 2024.
- Governor Kemp and other Georgia Republican officials, who previously courted Hyundai and celebrated the plant's opening, issued statements on Friday emphasizing that all state employers must follow the law.
- The nonprofit Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta labeled the raid "unacceptable" in a joint statement.
- The advocacy group's statement noted that targeted workers are "everyday people who are trying to feed their families, build stronger communities, and work toward a better future."
- Sammie Rentz, who opened a supermarket less than 3 miles (4.8 kilometers) from the Hyundai site six months ago, reported sharp business declines and fears it may not recover.
- Rentz expressed concern that Korean nationals might "cut and running, or starting an exit strategy" due to the raid's impact on their pride.
- Ellabell resident Tanya Cox, living less than a mile from the site, stated she held no ill feelings toward Korean nationals or other immigrant workers.
- Cox noted that few local neighbors were employed at the site and believed more battery plant construction jobs should have been allocated to local residents.
- Cox stated, "I don’t see how it’s brought a lot of jobs to our community or nearby communities."
