# Rivian

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Rivian Goes Bigger in Georgia With $2.1B Less to Spend | Wheel Front](https://wheelfront.com/rivian-goes-bigger-in-georgia-with-2-1b-less-to-spend/)  ·  `page_059101`

## Facts

- Rivian boosted initial production capacity at its Stanton Springs North plant in Georgia to 300,000 vehicles annually, up from the originally planned 200,000.
- The company's Department of Energy (DOE) loan for the facility was reduced by $2.1 billion, shrinking from $6.6 billion to $4.5 billion.
- The original DOE loan was structured around two production phases targeting 400,000 vehicles per year, but the revised deal collapses this into a single phase to allow earlier fund access and higher initial volume construction.
- Construction of vertical structures at the Georgia plant is scheduled to begin in the spring, with the stamping press area already in preparation.
- Production at the Stanton Springs North plant is targeted for late 2028.
- In its recent first-quarter earnings report, Rivian reported an 11 percent revenue increase to $1.38 billion.
- The company produced 10,236 vehicles and delivered 10,365 in the reported quarter.
- Rivian reported a Q1 net loss of $416 million, improved from a $541 million loss in Q1 2025, though this includes a $506 million accounting gain tied to the Series A capital raise and deconsolidation of Mind Robotics.
- Rivian's 2026 guidance projects vehicle deliveries between 62,000 and 67,000 units and an adjusted EBITDA loss between $1.8 billion and $2.1 billion.
- The Georgia expansion strategy is centered on the R2 mid-sized SUV, which recently began production at Rivian's existing Normal, Illinois plant.
- The R2 is priced to reach a far broader market than the R1T truck and R1S SUV, with early demand signals prompting Rivian to redesign the Georgia factory around higher initial volumes.
- Rivian delivered approximately 50,000 vehicles last year, highlighting the scale challenge of tripling or quadrupling output alongside building a dealer-less retail infrastructure, service network, and brand awareness.
- The reduced DOE loan may reflect a federal lending environment that has become less generous toward EV ventures since the original terms were negotiated.
- Rivian plans to begin drawing on the DOE funds in 2027, requiring it to finance the next 18 months of construction using existing reserves and revenue from the R2 ramp.
- The company is spending more than $4 billion on the new Georgia plant while reporting quarterly losses, relying on its cash position and product sales to sustain operations.
