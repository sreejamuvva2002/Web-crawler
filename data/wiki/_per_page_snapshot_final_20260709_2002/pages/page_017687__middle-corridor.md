# Middle Corridor

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** 2024-03-03

**Source:** [Gateway to development – assessing Georgia’s Middle Corridor opportunity – Investor.ge](https://www.investor.ge/2024/03/03/gateway-to-development-assessing-georgias-middle-corridor-opportunity/)  ·  `page_017687`

## Facts

- The Middle Corridor is a multimodal land and sea freight transport route from China to Europe that was first mooted in the 1990s.
- The route transits through Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Turkmenistan, crosses the Caspian Sea through Azerbaijan and Georgia to the Black Sea.
- The corridor consists of around 4,250 kilometers of rail lines and 500 kilometers of seaway.
- Two paths lead from the corridor to Europe via Turkey or the Black Sea.
- In 2021, there was a 29% increase in container trains along the Middle Corridor.
- The corridor still carries just a small percentage of total China-EU trade.
- An efficient Middle Corridor could offer transport time between China and Europe of 13 to 21 days compared to 35 to 45 days for the sea route, according to the German Economic Research Team.
- The European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) suggested in a report for the European Commission that around €19 billion is needed to make necessary improvements to the route.
- Financing is not the main issue; officials cite soft issues like aligning national visions, coordinating planning, harmonizing standards, enhancing the PPP environment, liberalizing markets, and integrating border management as key hurdles.
- Infrastructure deficiencies require addressing bottlenecks including a lack of capacity in shipping, goods-handling infrastructure, and IT at ports.
- There is a shortage of maritime services at sea.
- On land, there is insufficient modern rolling stock for rails, uncoordinated border crossing points, and unstandardized information technology.
- A World Bank report released in November 2023 forecasts that by 2030, if the Middle Corridor is operational, Georgia’s export tonnage could rise by over 3%.
- The same World Bank report projects an increase in the value of exported goods for Georgia of as much as $107 a ton overall.
- Georgia stands to gain notably from increased exports of prepared foodstuff, especially beverages, mineral waters, and wines, particularly as exports to China via the Middle Corridor.
- Georgia’s mineral exports are projected to grow, notably for construction materials and non-ferrous ores.
- The World Bank forecasts a 44% rise in the regional cargo base between 2021 and 2030.
- This growth will foster the development of logistic clusters and concentration of specialist activities.
- Despite a 33% rise in 2022 container traffic as China rerouted EU-bound shipments, high transport delays drove shippers back to alternative corridors in 2023.
- The first eight months of 2023 saw a decrease of 37% in Middle Corridor container traffic according to the World Bank.
- International logistic giants Maersk, Rail Bridge Cargo, and CEVA Logistics initially rushed to the Middle Corridor after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine but have since held back.
- Bottlenecks make the Middle Corridor take far longer than the Northern Corridor, mostly due to inadequate Caspian and Black Sea shipping and port capacity.
- Tariffs on the corridor are uncompetitive and costs fluctuate.
- In October 2023, 2,000 international delegates attended the Silk Road Forum in Tbilisi to discuss challenges and opportunities for the corridor.
- Georgia, Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan, and Turkey signed a roadmap in 2022 listing priority investments and actions needed.
- In June 2023, an agreement was reached between Georgia, Azerbaijan, and Kazakhstan on the creation of a joint logistics operator.
- The World Bank, EU, ADB, and other international organizations confirmed their support and interest in providing technical assistance and investments.
- Forecasts predict a 30% rise in EU-China trade by 2030.
- Inter-regional trade between Azerbaijan, Georgia, and Kazakhstan is predicted to increase by 37% by 2030.
- Trade between these countries and the EU is anticipated to grow by 28%.
- The UK-US think tank IISS lists an upcoming tender to construct the Anaklia deep sea port as a hard investment for Georgia.
- The IISS also lists the reconstruction of sections of the Baku-Akhalkalaki-Kars railway as a priority.
- Plans exist for the creation of a logistics and industry park near Poti.
- A logistics center is planned in Akhalkalaki.
- APM Terminals has proposed capacity expansion at Poti Port to help ease congestion.
- Georgia has intensified work to finish a new east-west road highway connecting its ports to the Caspian Sea.
- Georgia is reforming the Georgian Railway by raising money for new rolling stock and bringing in a new ownership structure.
- For a 1,500 TEU vessel, a sole ship-call in Poti costs $38,800.
- This equates to around $97 per 40-foot box on average.
- Crossing the whole port costs $275 in Poti and $430 in Batumi for a 40-foot box.
- These port costs are significantly higher than in Aktau or Baku ($148), or European or Asian ports.
- The U.S. Foreign Policy Research Institute expects it will be at least five years before the new Road Map eliminates red tape problems.
- Russia has long sought to be the dominant influence in countries traversed by the Middle Corridor and control trade.
- If Russia wins decisively in Ukraine, it is likely to direct resources towards disrupting the corridor to protect the Northern Corridor’s role in transcontinental trade.
