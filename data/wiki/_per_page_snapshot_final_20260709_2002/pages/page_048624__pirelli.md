# Pirelli

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2007-01-13

**Source:** [Robotic assembly lines help drive tire innovation / Small batches, greater variety have changed profit calculus](https://www.sfgate.com/cars/article/Robotic-assembly-lines-help-drive-tire-innovation-2623887.php)  ·  `page_048624`

## Facts

- Pirelli operates a manufacturing and research complex in Rome, Ga.
- The facility relies exclusively on production from its modular integrated robotized system, or MIRS.
- Pirelli's automation strategy focuses on making smaller batches of a large number of products to keep mixed production runs profitable.
- The system eliminates the need to keep expensive raw materials and finished tires on hand.
- Small production runs can be as few as four tires at a time.
- Gaetano Mannino serves as president of Pirelli Tire North America.
- Mannino stated that they make tires for ultra-big bling-bling wheels in small numbers, noting it has become like a fashion business where being first with a hot design leads the market.
- The facility produces specialized tires, including sets of gigantic tires for Hummers.
- A traditional tire factory may sprawl up to a half-mile in length and can be set up to produce popular models in batches of 1,000 or more.
- Switching molds and setting up machinery for a different model in a traditional factory can take workers eight hours or more.
- A MIRS production unit consists of a dozen robots feeding a group of rubber-extruding and ply-laying machines.
- The system fabricates tires around metal drums that the robot's arms grip powerfully.
- The robot arms have sophisticated wrists that let them hand off the tire-in-progress to the extruding machinery at all sorts of angles as strips of rubber and reinforcing material are built up.
- One MIRS system can simultaneously build 12 different tire models.
- A bar code on each drum tells the equipment which model of tire it is about to make, and the control software supplies the appropriate recipe for the machinery.
- At the end of the process, robots load the unfinished tires into molds that will emboss the tread pattern and sidewall lettering in a 25-minute steam-curing process.
- Robots ensure precise, repeatable movements to build tires with everything in the right place without overlapping rubber strips or joints.
- MIRS ensures every tire is well balanced and quiet running, matching a level of consistency human labor cannot achieve.
- Pirelli engineers originally considered trucking the robotic system to racetracks to quickly make and test racing tires.
- About two years ago, a general from the Pentagon contacted Mannino about taking a MIRS module to Iraq, but it was rejected because the plants must be very clean and cannot operate in desert conditions.
