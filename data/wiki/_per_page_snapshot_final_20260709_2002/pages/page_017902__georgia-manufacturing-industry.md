# Georgia Manufacturing Industry

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-26

**Source:** [Table of Experts: The future of manufacturing in Georgia - Atlanta Business Chronicle](https://www.bizjournals.com/atlanta/news/2025/09/26/table-experts-future-manufacturing-georgia.html)  ·  `page_017902`

## Facts

- The US economy is adjusting to ever-changing conditions, with tariffs and protectionism creating serious headwinds for companies that outsource parts and materials due to rising costs and volatile supply chains.
- Energy scarcity is a looming concern for some parts of the country as sustainability initiatives and climate change concerns drive business outlooks.
- Mid-level businesses are considering private equity as a source of capital but must navigate distinctions between local private equity firms and bigger players along the east coast.
- Atlanta Business Chronicle brought together five leading minds in Georgia manufacturing and infrastructure for a roundtable discussion on where the manufacturing industry is headed statewide.
- Topics covered in the roundtable included marketing in the era of protectionism, understanding private equity, and the unique benefits of the frozen base rate for energy.
- Manufacturing is one of the most energy-intensive industries anywhere, and Georgia’s success as a manufacturing hub is attributed to its robust and affordable energy supply.
- David Wagner, Vice President of Supply and Origination for Gas South, stated that Georgia is well situated to provide cost-effective and reliable natural gas and power due to its proximity to major interstate energy infrastructure.
- Georgia's position between natural gas producing regions in the Appalachians and along the Gulf Coast, connected by extensive pipeline infrastructure, provides a robust supply solution.
- Arthur Tripp, vice president of economic development for Georgia Power, noted that Georgia’s forward-thinking energy policies allow local utilities to capitalize on the state’s position and offer multiple years of frozen base energy rates.
- Tripp stated he is not aware of any other utility in the country freezing base rates and emphasized that this decision creates expanded opportunities for reliability and sustainability because everything is interconnected.
- Georgia operates as a vertically integrated energy market, which provides opportunities for strategic investments in reliability as the power grid expands and efficient new generation sources are added.
- Tripp highlighted that Georgia's market structure is a major reason why utility rates have averaged 15% below the national average since 1990.
- Manufacturing is a talent-driven industry, and Georgia is considered a great place to live and work for attracting and retaining the best talent.
- Annette Sally, executive vice president for marketing agency Brunner, described a renaissance moment happening for manufacturing and noted an opportunity to romance jobs in manufacturing to make Georgia a top destination.
- Brad Beard, senior director of middle market banking at Synovus, stated that business and political climate are huge drivers, but location is vital due to proximity to a major port and significant international airport.
- The state has invested heavily in infrastructure and understands the importance of attracting more diverse industries than it had 40 years ago, with emerging industries now coming to Georgia from California and other parts of the country.
- Jason Hodell, partner and industrial manufacturing & consumer goods leader with Cherry Bekaert Advisory, praised Georgia officials for being very forward-looking in enticing out-of-state and foreign businesses into the state.
- Hodell noted that efforts to help middle-market companies optimize and negotiate with the state, port, or county are extremely lucrative and bring jobs, people, and workforce into the economy.
- Quality of life is a big incentive for employers considering Georgia, including access to the Beltline, beaches, mountains, and the excitement created by Atlanta.
- Sally emphasized that momentum underpins these factors, noting that forward momentum and growth create positive energy that drives both people and companies to want to be in Georgia.
- Natural gas is a foundational fuel here for the long run, supplying power plants for electricity and direct heat, both of which manufacturers need for many processes.
- Gas South is working to partner to enhance local gas facilities and work with upstream infrastructure providers so manufacturers can experience success and avoid disruptions.
- Tripp stated that in 2025, having a reliable energy source is a necessity, not just a commodity, and highlighted how smart investments and infrastructure planning worked wonders after Hurricane Helene knocked out power throughout many parts of the state.
- Following Hurricane Helene, Georgia Power coordinated generators for nursing homes in South Georgia to restore power.
- Tripp noted that across the country providers are raising rates and struggling to meet demand, while Georgia froze base rates and still meets growing energy demands as a rare benefit.
- Hodell considered the immense opportunity facing manufacturers in Georgia, stating this could be the best manufacturing climate ever since World War II, allowing companies to run off an entire project in the first year of deployment.
- Manufacturers can immediately expense R&D and get tax credits under current policies.
- Middle-market companies have access to historic government funding like the roughly $300 billion of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act federal allocation that is still available.
- Tariffs and Made-in-America mandates force nearshorers to open up American distribution and manufacturing to avoid tariffs and reduce logistics costs.
- Beard stated there are significant programs such as the USDA Financing program that can create attractive financing arrangements for companies to expand and bring economic development to areas of the state that may not benefit otherwise.
- Synovus, the largest bank based in Georgia, advises family-owned manufacturers on succession planning routes including strategic sales to competitors, private equity transactions, or forming an ESOP.
- Hodell explained that businesses can protect themselves from tariffs by setting up a foreign trade zone to delay paying tariffs until selling the product in America, providing nine months of cash flow.
- Companies can avoid tariffs altogether if they re-export or materials are scrapped, and many misunderstand how much they can earn in R&D tax credits from the state and federal government for simple process improvements, shop floor investments, and software investments.
- Sixty percent of companies are either rebranding or preparing to rebrand for new protectionist policies, which is now table stakes.
- Sally advised that since very few products are 100% made in America, companies should play offense with transparency on what they make domestically rather than hiding imports.
- Smart companies understand marketing is a long game influenced by every part of the company, and building brand reputation consistently is foundational for success in the turn to protectionism.
- Georgia manufacturers who compete against international players love the new market because protectionism creates winners alongside headwinds.
- Tripp stressed that utilities must be strategic partners growing Georgia deliberately through day-to-day engagement with local development authorities, chambers of commerce, and site selectors worldwide.
- Advanced manufacturing is still leading the way over data centers in attracting businesses to Georgia, and knowing what business fits each community is key.
- Wagner noted that while local infrastructure may provide physical interconnection for manufacturers, expanding upstream natural gas transportation capacity can take months or years, making power availability a key factor in bringing new business to the state.
- Gas South has partnered in multiple circumstances to leverage its portfolio of gas supply deliverability to bridge the gap between short-term needs and long-term solutions for business leaders.
- Georgia’s deregulated gas market puts it in a unique position to find collaborative solutions that bring lower-cost, more reliable energy to manufacturers.
- Wagner stated that infrastructure upgrades throughout Georgia are a step toward keeping everyone’s energy needs filled affordably, with cutting-edge infrastructure being the central focus for continuing to lead in manufacturing.
- Tripp emphasized that affordability is the next priority for manufacturing and that freezing base rates for three years brings predictability, which is critical for businesses and residential customers who make up the workforce.
- Sally noted that building a positive and reliable reputation behind Georgia's benefits is what will get the state on the map, keep it there, and help it reach the number one spot in manufacturing.
