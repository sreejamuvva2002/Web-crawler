# State of Georgia

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-30

**Source:** [Feds OK updated Georgia plan for electric vehicle charging stations - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2025/09/30/feds-ok-updated-georgia-plan-for-electric-vehicle-charging-stations/)  ·  `page_000314`

## Facts

- Georgia can keep spending $135 million in federal funds allocated by the Biden administration for electric vehicle charging infrastructure.
- The funded infrastructure includes sections of Interstates 16 and 95 in Coastal Georgia.
- An edited plan for the funding was approved this month.
- The project to install EV charging points on unserved Georgia interstate and highway corridors has survived largely intact.
- State officials deleted language that referred to equity and safety from the plan.
- President Donald Trump’s Department of Transportation halted funding nationwide for the National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure program earlier this year.
- The federal agency demanded new state plans that omit any emphasis on serving rural, underserved or disadvantaged communities.
- The federal agency also removed requirements that states address emergency evacuation needs, small business participation and labor and safety standards.
- Sixteen states and Washington D.C. sued, arguing a president can’t legally freeze funding approved by Congress.
- A federal judge dealt Trump a setback regarding the lawsuit, though the case is ongoing.
- Georgia did not join the suit against the federal government.
- Georgia was among the states that submitted revised plans to the federal agency.
- GDOT did not respond to numerous requests for comment on this program.
- In 2021, the U.S. Congress passed a $5 billion program with bipartisan support for the goal of easing long-distance trips in electric vehicles.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp, a Republican, praised the program in 2022, saying the state’s Department of Transportation would play a critical role in building infrastructure to match rapidly developing EV manufacturing capacity around the state.
- GDOT’s revised plan leaves out former statements on equity and workforce considerations.
- The deleted equity statement read: “GDOT recognizes the impacts new transportation infrastructure and related energy systems may have on rural and historically disadvantaged communities and is committed to implementing an EV program that is convenient, reliable, affordable, and equitable for all users.”
- The new plan also leaves out chapters of technical detail about existing road conditions.
- What remains emphasizes the state’s second round of federal project spending on Interstate 95 in Liberty or McIntosh counties and on Interstate 16 in Bryan and Chatham counties.
- Georgia awarded its first funds last year to help pay for new EV charging stations in Brunswick, Dublin, Byron, Metter and Tifton.
- Love’s Travel Center in Brunswick is getting $650,000 to help pay for an EV charging station.
- The Love's funding is matched by $435,000 in company money.
- Contracting and design for the Love's station are underway.
- The four-charger station at Love's is likely to open in 2026.
