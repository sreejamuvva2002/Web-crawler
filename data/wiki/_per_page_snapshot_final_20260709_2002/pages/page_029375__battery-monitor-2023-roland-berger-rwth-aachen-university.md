# Battery Monitor 2023 (Roland Berger & RWTH Aachen University)

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [A sustained boom: Global battery market continues on a growth trajectory | Roland Berger](https://www.mynewsdesk.com/rolandberger/pressreleases/a-sustained-boom-global-battery-market-continues-on-a-growth-trajectory-3291669)  ·  `page_029375`

## Facts

- Global battery market is set to grow at 34% per year until 2030.
- Electric vehicles will account for around 80% of demand for lithium-ion batteries over the coming decades.
- Competition is growing from new European and US manufacturers as well as massively expanding Chinese companies.
- Despite the recent deceleration in automotive manufacturers' electric vehicle projections, the global battery market continues to grow at an enormously fast pace and the demand for lithium-ion and sodium-ion batteries is still rising.
- Between 2020 and 2030 alone, demand will increase more than eighteenfold at an annual growth rate of 34%.
- The main reason behind this development is the automotive industry's switch to battery electric vehicles.
- This is having a corresponding impact on the industrial landscape, particularly in the USA and Europe.
- New battery production hotspots are emerging in these regions, as well as new companies to add to those already existing in Asia.
- These conclusions were reached by Roland Berger and the Chair of Production Engineering of E-Mobility Components (PEM) of RWTH Aachen University in their 'Battery Monitor 2023' publication.
- The focus of current developments lies primarily on technical innovations towards more efficient production and alternative battery materials.
- Professor Heiner Heimes, Member of the Institute Management of RWTH's Chair of PEM, states that to thrive on the international market, battery manufacturers need to optimize their production processes and that developing new production technologies is the key to efficient, cost-effective and sustainable battery production.
- Demand for batteries is expected to reach around 4,900 gigawatt hours (GWh) in 2030, significantly up on last year's forecast of about 4,000 GWh.
- This expected demand will be exceeded by announced global production capacities of around 8,900 GWh in 2030.
- It is doubtful whether these capacities will all be realized.
- Wolfgang Bernhart, Partner at Roland Berger, states that they do not expect to see overcapacity outside China.
- The lack of materials, available talent and secured sales is impairing the performance of battery manufacturers and production processes worldwide.
- Market imbalances are arising and are exacerbated by national legislation.
- The development of production capacities and requirements differs starkly between countries, particularly the USA, China and Europe.
- Wolfgang Bernhart states that in North America, new relevant market players will emerge.
- In Europe, large amounts of battery capacity have been announced, but many of the companies are still very new, so the market is expected to consolidate.
- China is building up tremendous overcapacities, resulting in low capacity utilization alongside export pressure.
- Goods from China are unlikely to be imported into the USA due to a sizeable tax hit of 25.4% on battery imports from China and barriers Chinese manufacturers and suppliers face as a result of the Inflation Reduction Act.
- Chinese manufacturers are increasingly focusing on the European market, which in turn increases the cost pressure on local producers.
- Innovative product and process technologies have become increasingly important in the battery sector to counteract the years of experience that Asian factories already have.
- There is a growing trend in patents coming out of the USA and Europe.
- Companies must strike a balance between efficient, cost-effective and sustainable battery production.
- In the medium-term future, striking this balance will be the only way for firms to secure their position in this dynamic market.
- The Chair of Production Engineering of E-Mobility Components (PEM) of RWTH Aachen University was founded in 2014 by StreetScooter co-inventor Professor Achim Kampker.
- PEM focuses on all aspects of the development, production, and recycling of battery systems and their components as well as fuel cells and the production of the electric drive train and entire vehicle concepts.
- PEM employs a total of 78 researchers, 35 non-scientific staff and around 130 student assistants.
- PEM collaborates with numerous renowned industrial partners with a focus on sustainability and cost reduction to aim for a seamless 'Innovation Chain' from basic research to large-scale production.
