# Bridgewater Interiors

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-01

**Source:** [“It’s hard to save for a car with the wages we’re getting”: Widespread opposition as UAW pushes through second sellout deal at Bridgewater Interiors - World Socialist Web Site](https://www.wsws.io/en/articles/2026/07/01/ttuc-j01.html)  ·  `page_057500`

## Facts

- Workers at Bridgewater Interiors in Warren, Michigan, members of UAW Local 400, voted Tuesday to ratify a four-year contract the United Auto Workers bureaucracy rushed through without giving the factory’s 1,000 workers the chance to see the full agreement or study its details.
- The UAW stated in a social media post Wednesday that workers “voted by 80 percent to ratify a new contract that secures a top rate of $35 an hour by 2030 among other major gains...”
- The deal includes a starting wage of only $20 an hour, which would top out at $29 in four years.
- Due to high turnover rates, many workers would remain on the low-wage tier for extended periods.
- According to the union's own contract “highlights,” workers would not make $35 an hour until 2033—three years after the contract expires.
- Inflation is running at 4.2 percent annually, which will wipe out much of any wage increase.
- In May, workers previously voted down a UAW-backed deal by 95 percent.
- The existing contract expired on May 15 before being extended behind workers' backs by UAW International and Local 400 officials who ignored an overwhelming vote to authorize a strike.
- Union officials dangled a $2,000 signing bonus and front-loaded pay increases for lower-seniority workers to exploit economic distress.
- The plant's workers produce seats and interiors for Big Three vehicles, including the Ford F-150 and Dodge Ram pickup trucks.
- The majority of workers make $21 an hour according to the UAW.
- Many workers are hired through temp agencies and earn so little they cannot afford a car.
- Workers travel by bus or Uber to reach the plant due to low wages.
- One worker stated, “They’ve got this signing bonus, but they are going to take half of it away with taxes and union dues.”
- Another worker noted the contract takes away emergency vacation days needed for families.
- Workers demanded $35 an hour immediately rather than in five or six years.
- A veteran with 23 years of tenure said he is just making $26 an hour and recalled when the company was owned by Johnson Controls.
- The company was spun off from Johnson Controls in 2016.
- Bridgewater Interiors generates an estimated $2 billion in annual revenue.
- CEO Ronald E. Hall Jr. has reportedly taken home $50 million over the past three years.
- UAW President Shawn Fain pushed the agreement in a livestream, declaring it ends treating IPS (Independents, Parts and Suppliers) like second-class citizens.
- Fain's pay raise brings his annual compensation to nearly $300,000, roughly seven times what a Bridgewater worker earns.
- The UAW bureaucracy is attempting to prevent a strike that would disrupt Big Three production and profits.
- Fain is aligned with the Trump administration’s trade war policies and increased military production to suppress opposition to living costs and automation-driven job cuts.
- Supporters of the WSWS Autoworker Newsletter distributed hundreds of copies of an article on the snap vote at the factory gate Tuesday.
- One worker noted that using FMLA days would now come out of vacation time or paid days off.
- Another worker pointed out there is no COLA (Cost-of-Living Allowance) and no retro pay back to the contract’s expiration.
- Workers lost three personal days under the new agreement.
- A worker stated that if they were to strike now, it would shut down a lot of the auto industry.
- Another worker denounced the snap vote, noting they got their first glance of the offer yesterday and were asked to vote today.
- Contract talks for roughly 3,000 Dana workers under a UAW master wage agreement resume July 7 in Cincinnati, Ohio.
- Dana workers across the Midwest and South voted down the first contract in June, led by massive “no” votes of 90 percent or more at Toledo Driveline, Warren Axle, Paris, Tennessee and Columbia, Missouri.
- The UAW refused to grant strike authorization for Dana workers and instead extended the contract despite a decisive strike mandate.
- Separate United Steelworkers talks over the Fort Wayne, Indiana plant continue; those workers rejected their contract by 158–15.
- Under the rejected Dana agreement, new-hire pay is frozen at $20 an hour, rising to just $25 after four years, with senior pay capped at $28.
- The Dana contract offered no relief from rotating 12-hour shifts or mandatory weekend work.
- Kamara Bond, who worked at the Warren, Michigan facility, was fired twice for reporting unsafe conditions including chemical exposures, high temperatures, and poor ventilation.
- Dana reported $102 million in net income in 2025 on $7.5 billion in global sales.
- CEO R. Bruce McDonald took home $5.61 million in compensation while the company spent $650 million on stock buybacks—about 23 percent of outstanding stock.
