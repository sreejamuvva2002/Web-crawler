# Georgia Tech Battery Cathode Research Team

**Type:** Research Center  ·  **Supply-chain role:** [[battery-materials|Battery Materials]]  ·  **Location:** Georgia, United States

## Overview

A multi-institutional research team led by Georgia Tech professor Hailong Chen has developed a low-cost iron chloride (FeCl₃) cathode that could dramatically reduce lithium-ion battery cost and improve performance, potentially transforming the electric vehicle market and large-scale energy storage.

## EV supply-chain relevance

The FeCl₃ cathode could lower battery costs-currently about 50% of an EV’s price-and improve sustainability, making electric vehicles more affordable and supporting large-scale energy storage.

## Timeline

- **2019** — Hailong Chen's lab began solid-state battery research focusing on chloride-based electrolytes.
- **2024-09-22** — Georgia Tech announced the development of a low-cost FeCl₃ cathode material.

## Key facts

- The iron chloride (FeCl₃) cathode costs only 1-2% of typical cathode materials while storing the same amount of electricity.
- Batteries account for about 50% of an electric vehicle’s total cost; a cheaper cathode could significantly reduce EV prices.
- The new cathode contains only iron and chlorine, abundant and affordable elements found in steel and table salt.
- All-solid-state lithium-ion batteries using the FeCl₃ cathode, solid electrolyte, and lithium metal anode could cost 30-40% of current LIBs.
- The technology may be less than five years from commercial viability in electric vehicles.
- The research team aims to scale up the material and push it toward commercial applications.
