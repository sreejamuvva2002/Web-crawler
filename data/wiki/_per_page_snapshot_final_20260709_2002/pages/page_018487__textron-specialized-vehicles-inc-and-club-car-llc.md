# Textron Specialized Vehicles Inc. and Club Car LLC

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-08-05

**Source:** [Golf cart companies Club Car, E-Z-Go applaud tariffs on Chinese products](https://golfweek.usatoday.com/story/sports/golf/2025/08/05/golf-cart-companies-club-car-e-z-go-applaud-chinese-tariffs/85526575007/)  ·  `page_018487`

## Facts

- The world's top two golf cart manufacturers are headquartered in Augusta, Georgia.
- The nonpartisan U.S. International Trade Commission announced that Textron Specialized Vehicles Inc. and Club Car LLC, along with their domestic competitors, have been materially injured by unfairly traded imports of low-speed personal vehicles from China.
- The Commerce Department stated in June its commitment to impose antidumping tariffs of between 119 percent and 478 percent on the affected imports.
- Countervailing tariffs will be applied at rates between 31 percent and 679 percent.
- As first reported by The Augusta Chronicle in December 2024, the U.S. Department of Commerce concluded that China's government engaged in antidumping and countervailing activities undercutting domestic vehicle makers.
- Antidumping occurs when foreign manufacturers sell goods at less-than-fair value, while countervailing involves a foreign government subsidizing manufacturers to enable cheap sales.
- Rob Scholl, president and CEO of Textron Specialized Vehicles, stated the ITC's decision will help protect the health of a uniquely American industry and the livelihoods of thousands of American workers who build golf cars, low-speed vehicles, and personal-transportation vehicles for global customers.
- Craig Scanlon, president and CEO of Club Car, described the final determination from the U.S. International Trade Commission as a clear win for fair competition and the thousands of American jobs that power their industry.
- Law firm Wiley Rein counseled the advocacy group American Personal Transportation Vehicle Manufacturers Coalition through its filings of several unfair-trade protests.
- Wiley Rein announced the ITC's decision on July 17.
