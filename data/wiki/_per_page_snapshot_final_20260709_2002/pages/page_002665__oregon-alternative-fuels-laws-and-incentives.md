# Oregon Alternative Fuels Laws and Incentives

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-01-01

**Source:** [Alternative Fuels Data Center: Hydrogen Laws and Incentives in Oregon](https://afdc.energy.gov/fuels/laws/HY?state=or)  ·  `page_002665`

## Facts

- Compressed natural gas motor fuel is subject to the Oregon state fuel excise tax at $0.34 per 123.57 cubic feet.
- Liquefied petroleum gas motor fuel is subject to the Oregon state fuel excise tax at $0.34 per 1.353 gallons.
- Hydrogen motor fuel is subject to the Oregon state fuel excise tax at $0.34 per kilogram.
- Liquefied natural gas motor fuel is subject to the Oregon state fuel excise tax at $0.34 per 1.71 gallons.
- Beginning January 1, 2025, all new light-duty state agency fleet vehicle purchases or leases must be all-electric, plug-in hybrid electric, or hydrogen fuel cell vehicles to the maximum extent possible.
- If purchasing or leasing zero-emission or hydrogen vehicles is not feasible, Oregon state agencies may purchase or lease light-duty alternative fuel vehicles or qualifying low-emission vehicles.
- Each Oregon state agency must develop and report a greenhouse gas reduction baseline and annual reduction targets to the Oregon Department of Administrative Services (DAS).
- Reports to DAS must include the number of purchases or leases of zero-emission vehicles, alternative fuel vehicles, and alternative fuel vehicle conversions, plus the quantity of each type of alternative fuel used annually by state agency fleets.
- The Oregon Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) administers the Oregon Clean Fuels Program, which requires fuel producers and importers to register, keep records of, and report the volumes and carbon intensities of fuels provided in Oregon.
- Phase 2 of the Oregon Clean Fuels Program, implemented in 2016, requires fuel suppliers to reduce the carbon content of transportation fuels.
- A 2022 goal requires reducing the carbon content of transportation fuels by 20% below 2015 levels by 2030, and 37% below 2015 levels by 2035.
- The DEQ continues to update rule-making to achieve the Clean Fuels Program carbon reduction goals.
- All-electric vehicle owners in Oregon must pay an annual fee of $115 or a per-mile road use fee of $0.02 per mile through the OReGo program.
- Hybrid electric vehicles and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles must pay annual fees based on MPG rating: $20 for 0-19 mpg, $25 for 20-39 mpg, and $35 for 40 mpg or greater.
- These vehicle efficiency fees are in addition to standard registration fees.
- Drivers with EVs or vehicles rated over 40 mpg are exempt from additional registration fees if they enroll in the OReGo program.
- Oregon joined California, Colorado, Connecticut, District of Columbia, Hawaii, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Nevada, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, and Washington in signing a memorandum of understanding to support medium- and heavy-duty zero-emission vehicle deployment through a Multi-State ZEV Task Force.
- In July 2022, the Multi-State ZEV Task Force published an action plan to support electrification of medium- and heavy-duty vehicles.
- The multi-state action plan includes strategies to limit all new medium- and heavy-duty vehicle sales in signatory states to zero-emission vehicles by 2050.
- The action plan aims to accelerate medium- and heavy-duty ZEV deployment and ensure deployment benefits disadvantaged communities.
- Oregon has adopted California Advanced Clean Trucks requirements requiring manufacturers to meet zero tailpipe emission production and sales requirements.
- Beginning with model year 2025, manufacturers will be required to sell zero-emission trucks as an increasing percentage of their annual sales for Class 2b through Class 8 vehicles in Oregon.
- Zero-emission vehicles under the requirement include all-electric and fuel cell electric vehicles.
- Oregon will pursue transportation electrification as part of greenhouse gas emissions reduction targets of at least 45% below 1990 levels by 2035, and at least 80% below 1990 levels by 2050.
- Select Oregon state agencies must report actions to reduce emissions annually.
- The Oregon Department of Transportation published a statewide transportation electrification infrastructure analysis report with updates in August 2022.
- Oregon joined California, Connecticut, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, and Vermont in signing a memorandum of understanding to support zero-emission vehicle deployment through a ZEV Program Implementation Task Force.
- In May 2014, the ZEV Program Implementation Task Force published an action plan identifying 11 priority actions to deploy at least 3.3 million zero-emission vehicles and adequate fueling infrastructure within signatory states by 2025.
- The 2014 ZEV Action Plan includes a research agenda to inform future actions.
- On an annual basis, each signatory state must report on the number of registered zero-emission vehicles, public electric vehicle chargers and hydrogen fueling stations, and workplace fueling information.
- In June 2018, the Task Force published a new ZEV Action Plan for 2018-2021 building on the 2014 plan.
- The 2018 ZEV Action Plan makes recommendations in five priority areas: raising consumer awareness of electric vehicle technology, building reliable residential/workplace/public charging and fueling infrastructure, continuing and improving access to purchase and non-financial incentives, expanding public and private sector fleet adoption, and supporting dealership efforts to increase zero-emission vehicle sales.
- The Oregon Department of Energy administers the Small-Scale Local Energy Loan Program, which offers low-interest loans for qualifying alternative fuel projects.
- Eligible alternative fuel projects for the loan program include fuel production facilities, dedicated feedstock production, fueling infrastructure, and fleet vehicles.
- Loan recipients must complete a loan application and pay a loan application fee.
- The Oregon Department of Environmental Quality uses funds from the Volkswagen Environmental Mitigation Trust deposited in the Clean Diesel Engine Fund to award grants for at least 450 diesel school buses.
- Eligible school buses must have at least three years of remaining useful life.
- Grants cover 30% (up to $50,000) for purchasing a new bus or up to 100% of the cost to retrofit a bus with emissions-reducing parts or technology that reduce diesel particulate matter by at least 85%.
- Any unexpended Clean Diesel Engine Fund money will fund grants for diesel engine emission reduction as matching funds under the Diesel Emissions Reduction Act program.
- The Clean Vehicle Rebate Program provides rebates to Oregon residents, businesses, non-profit organizations, and government agencies for purchasing or leasing new electric vehicles, plug-in hybrid electric vehicles, electric motorcycles, or fuel cell electric vehicles.
- Electric vehicles and fuel cell electric vehicles purchased between May 22, 2025, and September 8, 2025, with a battery capacity greater than 10 kilowatt-hours are eligible for a $2,500 rebate.
- Electric vehicles and fuel cell electric vehicles with a battery capacity of less than 10 kilowatt-hours are eligible for a $1,500 rebate.
- Electric motorcycles are eligible for a $750 rebate under the Clean Vehicle Rebate Program.
- Eligible electric vehicles may not have an MSRP over $50,000, and eligible fuel cell electric vehicles may not have an MSRP over $60,000.
- The Oregon Department of Environmental Quality administers the Zero-Emission Fueling Infrastructure Grant Program to support charging and fueling infrastructure for medium- and heavy-duty zero-emission vehicles.
- Eligible applicants for the Zero-Emission Fueling Infrastructure Grants include public agencies, businesses, and Tribal governments.
- The grants reimburse a portion of eligible project costs for designing and installing direct-current fast chargers and Level 2 chargers.
- Priority for Zero-Emission Fueling Infrastructure Grants is given to projects in locations disproportionately affected by poor air quality.
