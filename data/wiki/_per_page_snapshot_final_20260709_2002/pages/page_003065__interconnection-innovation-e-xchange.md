# Interconnection Innovation e-Xchange

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [Tackling High Costs and Long Delays for Clean Energy Interconnection | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/i2x/articles/tackling-high-costs-and-long-delays-clean-energy-interconnection)  ·  `page_003065`

## Facts

- U.S. Department of Energy established the Interconnection Innovation e-Xchange (i2X) to identify and develop solutions that make interconnection fairer, faster, and simpler.
- i2X was announced on May 11, 2023.
- Proposed renewable generation and energy storage projects face lengthy delays and high costs to interconnect to the transmission grid.
- Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL) released reports highlighting how high interconnection costs are impacting deployment of renewables and storage in the United States' two largest ISO/RTO territories.
- PJM and MISO operate wholesale power systems that serve a combined 110 million people across the eastern and midwestern United States.
- LBNL reports examine costs in five ISO/RTOs.
- Transmission interconnection costs have doubled for both PJM and MISO in recent years.
- Proposed renewable energy and energy storage projects face significantly greater interconnection costs than natural gas generators in both territories.
- The administration has a goal of a carbon-free electric grid by 2035, which will require large deployment of new renewable energy generation and storage capacity.
- 31 states and Washington, DC have adopted renewable portfolio standard (RPS) policies setting binding targets for renewable or clean electricity generation.
- Four states in MISO territory (Minnesota, Illinois, Wisconsin, Michigan) and three in PJM (Maryland, Virginia, New Jersey) aim to achieve 100% zero-carbon or net-zero electricity by 2050 or earlier.
- In PJM territory, average costs for projects that completed studies between 2020 and 2022 doubled compared to similar projects between 2017 and 2019.
- MISO saw a doubling of costs for projects completed between 2019 and 2021 compared to projects from before 2019.
- Costs have increased even more for projects remaining in the queue, with MISO seeing costs triple and PJM an extraordinary 800% cost increase for active applications in just the last two years.
- Total capacity of projects waiting in PJM's interconnection queue nearly quadrupled between 2017 and 2021 to 288 gigawatts (GW), with approximately another 40 GW added in 2022.
- Capacity in MISO's interconnection queue nearly tripled between 2017 and 2021.
- 93% of PJM applications and 96% of MISO applications during those periods were for renewable energy or energy storage projects.
- Unprecedented interconnection cost increases are mostly driven by network upgrade costs, while point-of-interconnection facility costs have remained relatively constant.
- Proposed wind, solar, and storage projects have paid higher average network upgrade costs than natural gas projects applying to PJM or MISO networks.
- Many projects are proposed where transmission lines are near capacity, requiring more infrastructure to be built.
- Large wind and solar projects located far from population centers require funding for new substations and long-distance transmission lines.
- Projects that withdrew from interconnection queues faced much higher costs than active or completed projects.
- In PJM's queue in 2022, projects were charged mean interconnection costs of $240/kW of capacity, while withdrawn projects between 2020 and 2022 had mean costs of $599/kW.
- High network upgrade costs force potential energy generators out of queues, imposing costs on other developers and causing cascade withdrawals.
- Only 24% of all projects in MISO's queue and 27% in PJM's queue that applied for interconnection between 2000 and 2016 were in operation by 2021.
- PJM and MISO have adopted clustered first-ready, first served approaches to interconnection studies, studying multiple projects at once based on readiness.
- MISO increased proactive funding for new transmission infrastructure to reduce financial burden on developers for network upgrades.
- PJM adopted an expedited process for projects with low network upgrade costs.
- Both ISO/RTOs have begun publishing maps to give developers insight on potential costs before entering the queue.
- These reforms have not yet slowed the increase in interconnection costs, and additional improvements are needed.
- i2X convenes diverse stakeholder groups including utilities, project developers, regulators, and host communities to develop and test solutions for interconnection obstacles.
