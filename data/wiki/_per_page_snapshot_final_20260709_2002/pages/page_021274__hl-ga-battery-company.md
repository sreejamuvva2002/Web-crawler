# HL-GA Battery Company

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-04

**Source:** [Hundreds arrested in immigration raid at Georgia Hyundai plant site](https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/nation/2025/09/04/hundreds-arrested-in-immigration-raid-at-georgia-hyundai-plant-site/85982109007/)  ·  `page_021274`

## Facts

- Federal agents arrested hundreds of people at a sprawling battery plant construction site in Georgia on Sept. 4.
- The Department of Homeland Security said agents had a judicial search warrant alleging unlawful employment practices.
- The operation was one of the Trump administration’s largest immigration raids.
- Agents from the Department of Homeland Security, FBI, DEA, ATF, and Georgia State Patrol participated in the operation at Hyundai’s battery plant in Bryan County, southwest of Savannah.
- Agents apprehended about 450 people suspected of being in the country illegally, according to an ATF X post.
- DHS stated that Immigration and Customs Enforcement's Homeland Security Investigations executed a judicial search warrant for an ongoing criminal investigation for alleged unlawful employment practices and other federal crimes.
- The agency did not clarify how many agents took part in the operation or the charges those detained face.
- Steven Schrank, special agent in charge of Homeland Security Investigations Atlanta, stated at a news conference streamed by local news stations: "We are making many arrests of undocumented individuals."
- Schrank noted they encountered many lawful employees working there, including United States citizens and lawful permanent residents, who were being released.
- Schrank said there were no injuries or uses of force in the operation.
- Schrank indicated more details on the raid would be released later.
- The facility is the HL-GA Battery Company, a battery plant jointly created by LG Energy Solution and Hyundai Motor Group.
- The facility is under construction next to the Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America at a site spanning nearly 3,000 acres.
- Hyundai’s new $7.6 billion plant in a rural area near Savannah has promised to transform the local economy and workforce.
- The facility is estimated to create around 8,500 jobs by 2030.
- Hyundai spokesperson Bianca Johnson said in an email there was no impact to the metaplant or its production.
- Johnson stated the law enforcement action took place at the HL-GA battery plant's construction site.
- Johnson said Hyundai is cooperating with law enforcement and is committed to abiding by all labor and immigration regulations.
- Mary Beth Kennedy, a spokesperson for the HL-GA Battery Company, stated in a statement that the company was “cooperating fully with the appropriate authorities regarding activity at our construction site.”
- Kennedy said, “To assist their work, we have paused construction,” and added, “We do not have further details at this time."
- The Savannah Morning News was told to exit the facility as state troopers blocked access.
- A helicopter hovered over the plant site amid the raid, while state patrol cars and unidentified black vehicles entered the site.
- Images posted by ATF showed men and women in zip ties being escorted off the site.
- Georgia State Patrol Capt. Crystal Zion said the department assisted federal officials with “the service of a criminal search warrant in an operation at the Hyundai Metaplant.”
- Zion shared no additional information and directed inquiries to Homeland Security Investigations.
- The Georgia raid is one of the largest during President Donald Trump’s second term, as he promises a sweeping immigration crackdown across the country using multiple federal agencies and assistance from local and state law enforcement.
- Raids have taken place across the country on farms and construction sites, as well as restaurants and car washes.
- During Trump’s first term in August 2019, agents arrested 680 people at seven Mississippi chicken processing plants, which officials at the time called the largest single-state immigration crackdown in a decade.
