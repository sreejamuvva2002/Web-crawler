# Hyundai Motor Group EV Manufacturing Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-06

**Source:** [What to Know About a Large-Scale Immigration Raid at a Georgia Manufacturing Plant](https://www.usnews.com/news/us/articles/2025-09-06/what-to-know-about-a-large-scale-immigration-raid-at-a-georgia-manufacturing-plant)  ·  `page_009982`

## Facts

- Hundreds of federal agents detained 475 people at the Hyundai EV manufacturing site in Georgia during a large-scale immigration raid, with most detainees being South Korean nationals.
- The raid occurred on a Thursday and targeted a manufacturing site that state officials have long considered Georgia's largest economic development project.
- U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement released video on Saturday showing federal agents directing workers to line up outside, frisk them, and shackle or tie their hands, ankles, waists, and wrists before boarding Georgia inmate-transfer buses.
- South Korean Foreign Minister Cho Hyun stated on Saturday that more than 300 of the 475 detained individuals were South Korean nationals.
- Some detained individuals worked for HL-GA Battery Co., a joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution for a battery plant slated to open next year, while others were contractors or subcontractors at the construction site, according to Steven Schrank, lead Georgia agent of Homeland Security Investigations.
- Lead agent Steven Schrank stated that some detained workers had illegally crossed the U.S. border, while others entered legally with expired visas or on visa waivers prohibiting work.
- Immigration attorney Charles Kuck stated that two of his detained clients arrived from South Korea under a visa waiver program allowing tourism or business stays of 90 days or less without a visa.
- Attorney Charles Kuck noted one detained client had been in the U.S. for a couple of weeks and another for about 45 days, with both planning to return home soon.
- A detained lawful permanent resident was kept in custody due to a prior firearm and drug offense record that could jeopardize their status under the "crime of moral turpitude" rule, according to ICE public affairs officer Lindsay Williams.
- ICE public affairs officer Lindsay Williams denied reports of U.S. citizens being detained, stating that once citizens identify themselves, ICE has no authority to detain them.
- Hyundai Motor Company stated on Friday that none of its direct employees were detained and that the company is reviewing supplier and subcontractor practices to ensure compliance with U.S. employment laws.
- LG Energy Solution told the Associated Press it could not immediately confirm how many of its own employees or Hyundai workers were detained.
- The South Korean government expressed "concern and regret" over the raid targeting its citizens and is dispatching diplomats to the site.
- South Korean Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lee Jaewoong stated in a televised address that "the business activities of our investors and the rights of our nationals must not be unjustly infringed in the process of U.S. law enforcement."
- Most detained individuals were transported to an immigration detention center in Folkston, Georgia, near the Florida state line.
- Lead agent Steven Schrank confirmed that none of the detainees had been charged with crimes yet and that the investigation remains ongoing.
- Family members and friends struggled to locate or contact detained individuals, according to James Woo, communications director for Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta.
- Many detained workers were in the U.S. solely for business purposes, leaving their families primarily located in South Korea.
- The raid resulted from a months-long investigation into allegations of illegal hiring at the site.
- Search warrants and affidavits requested employment records for current and former workers, timecards, and video/photos of workers.
- Court records indicate prosecutors do not know who hired the individuals, with the U.S. Attorney’s Office stating the "actual company or contractor hiring the illegal aliens is currently unknown."
- Hyundai Motor Group began manufacturing EVs at the $7.6 billion plant approximately one year prior to the raid.
- The site currently employs approximately 1,200 people in a largely rural area located about 25 miles (40 kilometers) west of Savannah.
- Federal agents specifically targeted an adjacent plant currently under construction where Hyundai and LG Energy Solution are partnering to produce EV batteries.
- The plant is located in Bryan County, which had a population of almost 47,000 in 2023 after increasing by more than a quarter in the early 2020s.
- Bryan County's Asian population grew from 1.5% in 2018 to 2.2% in 2023, with growth primarily driven by people of Indian descent, according to Census Bureau data.
- Lead agent Steven Schrank described the Georgia raid as the "largest single site enforcement operation" in Homeland Security Investigations' two-decade history.
- The majority of detained individuals were Korean; ICE data shows only 46 Koreans were deported out of over 270,000 total removals in the 12 months ending Sept. 30, 2024.
- Governor Brian Kemp and other Georgia Republican officials, who previously courted Hyundai and celebrated the plant's opening, issued statements on Friday asserting that all state employers must follow the law.
- The nonprofit Asian Americans Advancing Justice-Atlanta called the raid "unacceptable" in a joint statement.
- Advocacy groups stated that workers targeted at Hyundai are "everyday people who are trying to feed their families, build stronger communities, and work toward a better future."
- Sammie Rentz, who opened a supermarket less than 3 miles (4.8 kilometers) from the plant six months prior, reported sharp business declines and fears it may not recover following the raid.
- Local business owner Sammie Rentz expressed concern that Korean nationals might leave the area due to pride and the raid's impact.
- Ellabell resident Tanya Cox, living less than a mile from the site, stated she held no ill feelings toward Korean or other immigrant workers.
- Resident Tanya Cox noted that few local neighbors were employed at the site and believed more battery plant construction jobs should have been allocated to local residents.
- Local resident Tanya Cox stated she did not see how the plant had brought significant jobs to her community or nearby areas.
