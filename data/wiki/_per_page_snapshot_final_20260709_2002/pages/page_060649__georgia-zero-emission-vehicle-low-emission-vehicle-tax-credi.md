# Georgia Zero Emission Vehicle / Low Emission Vehicle Tax Credit

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2017-10-29

**Source:** [#electricity | Atlanta Electric Vehicle Development Coalition](https://atlantaevdc.com/tag/electricity/)  ·  `page_060649`

## Facts

- Governor Nathan Deal signed the Transportation Bill which repeals the ZEV/LEV Tax Credits.
- The $5000 state tax credit for leasing or purchasing an electric vehicle expires June 30, 2015.
- Qualifying vehicles for the credit include the Nissan LEAF, Tesla Model S or Roadster, KIA Soul EV, VW eGolf, and Mitsubishi Mio.
- To qualify, Georgia residents must take delivery of a qualifying new vehicle by midnight June 30, 2015, with a signed Motor Vehicle Purchase Agreement signifying delivery.
- Tim Echols leases two Nissan LEAFs in Athens, charging them in his garage for about $20 per month on his electric bill.
- The Nissan LEAF has an approximately 100-mile range, which can cause "range anxiety" when pushed to its limit.
- Tesla owners experience less range anxiety but face higher car payments.
- Tim Echols pays about $270 per month in lease payments for each of his two Nissan LEAFs over a 24-month term.
- Nissan incorporates the $7500 federal tax credit into the transaction price, reducing the effective monthly cost to about $208 per car.
- After accounting for the state credit over 24 months, Tim Echols pays approximately $62 out of pocket per month per car.
- A garage charger installation costs about $700 including labor and eliminates the need for oil, water, transmission fluid, or gas.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development estimates that displacing one percent of petroleum-based miles in Georgia with electric vehicles keeps approximately $201 million annually in the state.
- Each pure electric vehicle purchased keeps $2,242 annually in Georgia by fueling with electricity instead of petroleum products.
- Many electric car owners shift charging to overnight hours due to an incentive from Georgia Power.
- A study of 1,000 Georgia electric car owners found they reduced their annual electricity bill by $180 despite not buying gasoline for the entire year.
- This "load shifting" reduces peak load electricity usage, ultimately saving money for all ratepayers.
- Tim Echols serves on the Georgia Public Service Commission and regulates electricity, natural gas, and telecom for the state.
