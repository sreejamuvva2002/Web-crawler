# Qcells

**Source:** [How Dalton, Georgia, went from Carpet Capital to… | Canary Media](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/solar/how-dalton-georgia-went-from-carpet-capital-to-solartown-usa)  ·  `page_000897`

## Facts

- Lisa Nash serves as general manager of the Qcells solar panel factory in Dalton, Georgia.
- Dalton is a town of 34,000 located 50 miles up I-75 from Cartersville, Georgia.
- The Qcells factory opened in January 2019 after the Trump administration imposed tariffs on Chinese-made panels.
- The Korean conglomerate Hanwha owns Qcells.
- Qcells initially planned to hire several hundred people at the Dalton site.
- By the end of 2019, the Dalton factory employed more than 800 people.
- Georgia elected President Joe Biden and Senators Jon Ossoff and Raphael Warnock in 2020, leading to policies promoting domestic clean energy manufacturing.
- The Inflation Reduction Act passed in August 2022, creating comprehensive policies to support domestic clean energy manufacturing.
- Qcells broke ground on a second facility in Dalton in February 2023.
- The Dalton expansion was completed in August 2023.
- The expansion added two football fields' worth of manufacturing space with four new production lines.
- The new lines produce 1.5 times more solar panels than the original three lines due to technological advances.
- The entire Qcells Dalton complex now employs 2,000 full-time people and produces 5.1 gigawatts of solar panels a year, more than any other site in the U.S.
- Since the IRA passed, Georgia has received $23 billion in clean energy factory investment, much flowing to northwest Georgia.
- Both CSX and Norfolk Southern run Class I rail lines through Dalton.
- Local factories consolidated into global carpeting giants Shaw Industries and Mohawk Industries.
- 80 percent of America’s tufted carpet production happens within 100 miles of Dalton.
- The largest Shaw site covers 650,000 square feet, and the new Engineered Floors facility covers 2.8 million square feet.
- Dalton employment peaked at 80,200 in 2006 before the Great Recession cratered demand for carpeting products.
- From June 2011 to June 2012, Dalton recorded the most jobs lost of all 372 metro areas surveyed by the Bureau of Labor Statistics.
- One-quarter of Dalton’s pre-recession jobs vanished, and unemployment surged to 12.3 percent.
- Engineered Floors, Mohawk, and Shaw still dominate local employment with some 14,000 jobs among them.
- Local leaders acquired an undeveloped lot south of town and cleared/leveled it to be shovel-ready for a future tenant.
- Georgia’s Department of Economic Development sent Qcells to the Greater Dalton Chamber of Commerce in February 2018.
- Qcells originally promised to invest $130 million and hire 525 people within five years, fulfilling the promise in three months.
- Qcells brought in helicopters to install air-conditioning units on the factory roof.
- Qcells hourly wages for non-experienced hires start at $17.50 to $22, amounting to $36,400 to $45,760 a year for full-time work.
- Workers with experience in robotics and manufacturing can earn much more through on-the-job training handling the in-house fleet of robots.
- Qcells relied on Quick Start, a Georgia state program that funds worker training for new factories before they open.
- Some 56 percent of Dalton-area students enroll in postsecondary education within 16 months of graduating high school.
- The Greater Dalton Chamber of Commerce runs Project Purpose, a crash course for living-wage careers.
- Last year, seven young adults completed Project Purpose and Qcells hired one; this time, 18 finished and Qcells hired 12 to start on July 1.
- Nash hopes to double the number of Project Purpose hires next year or more.
- 900 apartment units are slated to come online from last August through November to address a long-running housing deficit.
- The plush Carpentry Hotel opened this spring across from the Oakwood Cafe in downtown Dalton.
- Senator Jon Ossoff authored the IRA manufacturing incentives that Qcells is tapping into.
- Qcells is building an even bigger factory compound down in Cartersville, which won a conditional $1.45 billion loan guarantee from the Department of Energy on August 8.
- The Cartersville facility will use Inflation Reduction Act tax credits to onshore slicing silicon wafers, carving them into solar cells, and assembling finished modules with newer robots.
- Workers in Dalton currently complete just the last step of solar panel production: assembling modules.
- 97 percent of the glass that encloses solar panels comes from China.
- Solarcycle is breaking ground on a combination solar-panel recycling facility and solar-glass factory in Cedartown, some 70 miles southwest of Dalton.
- Solarcycle founder and CEO Suvi Sharma expects to invest $344 million in the community and hire 600 full-time employees.
- Solarcycle will use its rail spur to ship in low-iron silica from a Georgia mine, plus soda ash and limestone.
- Over time, Solarcycle will supplement raw ingredients with increasing amounts of glass pulled from decommissioned solar panels, including those made by Qcells.
- The goal is to produce enough glass for 5 gigawatts of panels per year and ship it to nearby customers.
- Hanwha recently became the largest shareholder in REC Silicon to secure access to domestic polysilicon from the Pacific Northwest.
- Hyundai is dropping nearly $1 billion on its “Metaplant” near the deepwater port of Savannah.
- LG is building an adjacent $4.3 billion battery plant with Hyundai.
- Kia erected a new EV9 SUV manufacturing line at its plant in West Point, about halfway down Georgia’s border with Alabama.
- The first EV9 rolled off the line in June, less than two years after the IRA was signed into law.
