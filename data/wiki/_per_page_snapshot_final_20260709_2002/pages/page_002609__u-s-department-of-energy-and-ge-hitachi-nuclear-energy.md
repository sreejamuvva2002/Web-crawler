# U.S. Department of Energy and GE Hitachi Nuclear Energy

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [DOE and GE Hitachi Team Up to Lower Costs of Building New Nuclear Reactors | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/doe-and-ge-hitachi-team-lower-costs-building-new-nuclear-reactors)  ·  `page_002609`

## Facts

- The U.S. Department of Energy announced $5.8 million in funding to develop three construction technologies that together can reduce the cost of new nuclear builds by more than 10 percent.
- Construction costs and schedule overruns have plagued new nuclear builds for decades, according to Dr. Kathryn Huff, Acting Assistant Secretary for Nuclear Energy at DOE.
- Leveraging advanced construction technologies can drive down costs and speed the pace of advanced nuclear deployment to tackle global climate change and meet the President’s goal of net-zero carbon emissions by 2050.
- The project team is led by GE Hitachi Nuclear Energy.
- One technology is vertical shaft construction, a best practice from the tunneling industry that could reduce construction schedules by more than a year.
- Another technology is Steel BricksTM, modular steel-concrete composite structures similar to high-tech LEGO pieces, which could significantly reduce the labor required on site.
- A third technology is advanced monitoring coupled with digital twin technology, which can create a 3D replica of the nuclear power plant structure.
- These technologies can be applied to a variety of advanced reactor designs to significantly improve the economics of bringing advanced reactors to market.
- GE Hitachi Nuclear Energy leads a proposal team that includes Black & Veatch, the Electric Power Research Institute, Purdue University, Caunton Engineering, Modular Walling Systems Limited, University of North Carolina at Charlotte, Nuclear Advanced Manufacturing Research Centre, and the Tennessee Valley Authority.
- Jon Ball, executive vice-president of GE Hitachi Nuclear Energy, stated that the funding will significantly benefit the commercialization of SMRs and pave the way for other advanced reactors.
- The work is funded and managed through DOE’s National Reactor Innovation Center (NRIC), which was established in 2019 to enable advanced reactor demonstration and deployment.
- The project, referred to as the Advanced Construction Technology initiative, will be conducted in two phases.
- The initial phase will focus on technology development and preparation for a small-scale demonstration.
- Pending successful completion of the first phase and future appropriated funds, a second phase is planned to carry out the demonstration within three years of this award.
