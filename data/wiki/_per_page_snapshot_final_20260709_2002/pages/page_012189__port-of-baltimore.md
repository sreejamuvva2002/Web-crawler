# Port of Baltimore

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** 2024-03-27

**Source:** [Supply chains could feel ripple effects from Baltimore's Key Bridge collapse | Hub](https://hub.jhu.edu/2024/03/26/baltimore-key-bridge-collapse-supply-chain-impacts-tinglong-dai/)  ·  `page_012189`

## Facts

- Baltimore is the largest auto port in the U.S. and a vital part of the global automotive supply chain.
- The port handles more than 800,000 vehicles a year.
- A significant portion of vehicles are imported from Europe and Mexico, with many exported by Detroit automakers.
- BMW and Volkswagen distribution centers are located on the side of the harbor beyond the bridge, making them less affected by the collapse.
- President Biden touted the Port of Baltimore as a model port in November 2021 during a deepening supply chain crisis.
- The ongoing transition of global supply chains involves more nearshoring and friend-shoring with neighbors like Mexico and friends like European countries and Japan, positioning the Port of Baltimore perfectly for this shift.
- The bridge collapse created a major disruption to the logistics networks of companies like Amazon, which relies on Baltimore as a major East Coast logistics hub.
- Rerouting decisions for cargo ships are already underway and will continue for months.
- Alternative ports like Brunswick, Georgia, and Norfolk, Virginia, won't be able to fully absorb the volume going through Baltimore.
- Consumers may experience delivery delays depending on how quickly vehicle inventories are depleted, and perhaps price increases in certain cases.
- Supply chain resilience built since the early 2020s means disruptions should be less significant, with supply chains showing robustness in 2024 not as pronounced in previous years.
- The ecosystem surrounding the port includes a trucking industry and intermodal transportation networks that will experience ripple effects from the collapse.
