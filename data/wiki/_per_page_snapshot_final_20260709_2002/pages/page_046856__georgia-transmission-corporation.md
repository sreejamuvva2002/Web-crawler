# Georgia Transmission Corporation

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [RABUN COUNTY v. GEORGIA TRANSMISSION CORPORATION (2003) | FindLaw](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/ga-supreme-court/1457152.html)  ·  `page_046856`

## Facts

- GTC distributes and transmits electric power and is a nonprofit electric membership corporation, organized and incorporated pursuant to OCGA § 46-3-170.
- GTC is comprised of 39 affiliated electric membership corporations and is authorized to exercise the power of eminent domain pursuant to OCGA § 46-3-201(b)(9).
- In 1999, GTC began planning construction of a 115-kilovolt transmission line, known as the North Burton Tap 115-KV Transmission Line Project, to service the electrical demands of the customers of Habersham Electric Membership Corporation (“Habersham EMC”).
- GTC selected route “D,” which crosses approximately seven miles of Rabun County, as the site for the proposed transmission line.
- GTC anticipates that if the line is not completed by April 2003, blackouts and brownouts will occur during the summer of 2003.
- In June, 2000, Rabun County adopted the “Rabun County High Voltage Line Construction Moratorium Ordinance,” which prohibits construction or installation of new high voltage transmission lines transmitting greater than thirty-five (35) Kilovolts in Rabun County for a period of three years.
- The ordinance was enacted due to concerns from citizens about possible harmful effects of electromagnetic fields emanating from the proposed lines, citing scientific studies suggesting potential health risks like leukemia and cancer.
- The ordinance's stated purpose includes that it is technically and economically feasible to provide electric power to meet the needs of the North Lake Burton/Persimmon communities without constructing new high voltage transmission lines.
- Prior to its passage, GTC made a written constitutional challenge to the proposed ordinance before the Rabun County Board of Commissioners.
- On August 31, 2000, GTC and Habersham EMC held a public meeting regarding the project and announced that the final selection of a “corridor” for the transmission lines would be made in October 2000 and that the project would be completed in 2003.
- Eight days later, Rabun County counsel informed GTC's counsel and the President and Chief Executive Officer of Habersham EMC that the county would enforce the ordinance to prohibit construction of the proposed project.
- On December 14, 2001, GTC filed a petition for declaratory judgment and injunctive relief against Rabun County seeking a declaration that the moratorium ordinance is unconstitutional under the home rule and uniformity provisions of the Georgia Constitution.
- The superior court ruled that the only issues for decision were whether the ordinance was unconstitutional as a special law and whether Rabun County could diminish GTC's right to exercise eminent domain, excluding evidence on project necessity or routing.
- The superior court determined the ordinance ran afoul of the home rule and uniformity provisions of the Georgia Constitution by affecting the exercise of GTC's power of eminent domain.
- Route “D” will traverse lands of the United States Forest Service, and GTC was attempting to obtain necessary permitting, with plans to select an alternate route within Rabun County if permitting was denied.
- The case was decided by the Supreme Court of Georgia on January 13, 2003, affirming the superior court's judgment that the ordinance was unconstitutional and permanently enjoining its enforcement.
