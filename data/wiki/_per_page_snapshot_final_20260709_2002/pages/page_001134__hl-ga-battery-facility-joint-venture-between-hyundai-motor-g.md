# HL-GA Battery Facility (Joint Venture between Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solution)

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Hyundai-LG's Georgia battery plant: Workers return amid legal battles and diplomatic tensions](https://www.automotivemanufacturingsolutions.com/news/battery-manufacturing-engineers-return-to-hyundailg-site-in-bitter-epilogue/2333485)  ·  `page_001134`

## Facts

- The Hyundai-LG joint venture between Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solution is developing a $7.6bn ($8.1bn) battery plant in Ellabell, rural Georgia.
- On September 4, the site was targeted by the largest immigration enforcement operation in American history, resulting in 475 individuals being detained.
- More than 100 of the 317 South Korean nationals detained had their B-1 visas reinstated without requiring reapplication by mid-November.
- Approximately 50 detained South Korean engineers had re-entered the United States by mid-November to resume work at the facility.
- Construction at the HL-GA Battery facility was indefinitely suspended following the raid but has now resumed in a phased manner as engineers return.
- LG Energy Solution confirmed that construction and business travel have resumed, with plans to gradually expand business travel to HL-GA and other US production facilities.
- When complete, the battery plant will supply cells for electric vehicles produced at Hyundai's adjacent Metaplant America assembly complex, which remains operational and unaffected by the enforcement action.
- Nearly 200 of the detained South Korean nationals are preparing to file a lawsuit against US Immigration and Customs Enforcement alleging unlawful detention, racial profiling, human rights violations, and excessive force.
- One engineer identified only as Mr Kim testified that workers' phones were confiscated and they were shackled around their wrists, ankles, and chest before being transported to a detention facility in Folkston, Georgia.
- Some detained workers tripped and fell while being loaded onto buses due to the restraints, rendering them unable to walk properly.
- The detained engineers spent seven days in detention before South Korea's government negotiated their release and arranged charter flights home.
- Attorneys for the plaintiffs argue that the restoration of B-1 visas without reapplication demonstrates the workers were lawfully present, contradicting the basis for their detention.
- LG Energy Solution and Hyundai Engineering have stated they are not involved in the lawsuit, describing it as being pursued on an individual basis by the workers.
- The legal proceedings are likely to stretch well into 2026, with plaintiffs seeking undisclosed financial compensation for damages.
- South Korean President Lee Jae Myung raised the incident directly with US President Donald Trump during bilateral meetings in October, describing it as causing severe trauma among workers.
- President Lee warned reporters that some workers no longer wish to return to the United States and cautioned that without safety measures, factory construction projects across America could face disruptions.
- President Trump acknowledged the complexity of the situation, noting foreign companies building advanced manufacturing facilities in the US often need to bring in experts during initial phases, and suggested his administration would work to resolve visa issues while maintaining enforcement priorities.
- Ahead of the meeting with Trump, President Lee told Bloomberg News he expected a solution "in the not-too-distant future," though specifics remain vague.
- According to reports from The Washington Post, several South Korean firms have pulled back or prolonged pauses on US investment projects following the Hyundai raid.
- Three individuals representing South Korean and East Asian firms in America confirmed that companies remain cautious despite reassurances from the Trump administration.
- The detained engineers were specifically brought to Georgia to install highly technical production equipment and train American personnel in battery cell manufacturing processes that remain scarce domestically.
- Construction halted and training stopped following the raid, leaving US workers waiting to assume those roles sidelined.
- Hyundai has since resumed limited international travel for technical teams, but a chilling effect on foreign expertise recruitment persists.
- Federal immigration authorities insisted the enforcement action was lawful and necessary to protect American labour standards.
- Steve Schrank, special agent in charge of Homeland Security Investigations for Georgia and Alabama, stated that individuals entering on short-term or recreational visas are prohibited from engaging in employment.
- Governor Brian Kemp of Georgia called for changes to the visa system to prevent similar disruptions, arguing that clarity and consistency are essential to maintaining investor confidence.
- White House border adviser Tom Homan promised more worksite enforcement operations following the Hyundai-LG raid.
- Hyundai Motor Group has pledged $26bn in American investments through 2028, with much of that capital directed towards vehicle production, battery manufacturing, and supply chain localization.
- The Hyundai-LG Georgia plant is now scheduled to become operational in the first half of 2026, a timeline that assumes continued access to foreign technical expertise during the ramp-up phase.
- Battery cell production requires precision engineering, stringent contamination controls, and process knowledge accumulated over decades, much of it concentrated in South Korea, China, and Japan.
