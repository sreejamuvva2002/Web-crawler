# State of Georgia

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2024-09-16

**Source:** [Despite Bumps, Georgia Has Become a Growing EV Hub](https://www.govtech.com/transportation/despite-bumps-georgia-has-become-a-growing-ev-hub)  ·  `page_003305`

## Facts

- Gov. Brian Kemp set out about five years ago to make Georgia the “e-mobility capital of the world.”
- Georgia has emerged as an e-mobility hub and a clean technology giant.
- The state has recruited a pair of multibillion-dollar electric vehicle plants.
- The state has recruited dozens of parts suppliers to support those factories.
- The state has recruited several facilities across the battery supply chain.
- Georgia has benefited from huge incentives pushed by President Joe Biden to expand EVs and clean technologies.
- State and local leaders have offered up their own bevy of incentives to woo companies.
- Georgia ranks second behind North Carolina for most EV industry investment since the Inflation Reduction Act was adopted in 2022, according to nonpartisan group E2.
- New plug-in vehicle sales had a sluggish start to 2024 due to consumer range anxiety and lack of public charging options.
- EV sales rebounded by midyear as legacy automakers aside from Tesla saw their market share grow.
- Hyundai Motor Group’s $7.6 billion plant near Savannah is Georgia’s largest economic development project.
- The Hyundai plant is already producing test vehicles and could begin full-scale production as soon as October.
- Hyundai is considering adding hybrid vehicle production in Bryan County.
- Development of a planned $5 billion Rivian EV factory an hour east of Atlanta was paused six months ago, with no restart date announced.
- Industry observers hope a recent partnership with Volkswagen Group and promising reservation figures will help ensure the Rivian factory moves forward.
- Pat Wilson is the state’s top economic development official.
- Pat Wilson said speed bumps are to be expected but justified by the industry's potential and change across Georgia.
- Pat Wilson stated: “None of us really knew where the industry was going and how quickly it was going to move.”
- Pat Wilson stated: “It’s an exciting time to be doing what we’re doing in Georgia because we are on the forefront of these technologies that are going to take us into the future.”
- The fast-evolving and climate-friendly technologies represent what Wilson calls a “new industrial revolution.”
- The majority of promised investments and jobs are landing outside of metro Atlanta.
- Pat Wilson stated: “What started with a conversation talking about the future of an industry is now leading to the health and future of rural Georgia and communities all over the state.”
- Gov. Kemp’s focus on clean tech was prompted by a trip to Germany in early 2020.
- During his first term, Kemp met with Mercedes-Benz and Porsche in Germany.
- The automakers focused discussions on EVs instead of gas-powered technologies.
- At the time, Georgia had one major EV player: SK Group.
- SK Group, a South Korean conglomerate, opened a massive lithium-ion battery production facility near Commerce.
- The SK facility has grown to employ thousands.
- SK and Hyundai broke ground last year on a $5 billion battery plant in Bartow County.
- The SK/Hyundai battery plant is on track to open by the second half of 2025.
- Since 2018, Georgia has announced 53 EV and battery projects combining for $27.3 billion in investment and more than 32,000 anticipated jobs, according to the Georgia Department of Economic Development.
- Hyundai’s “Metaplant” in Bryan County was announced in May 2022.
- The Hyundai plant has attracted a network of 17 suppliers combining for another $2.7 billion of investment.
- Some Hyundai supplier facilities have already opened.
- On Sept. 5, state leaders celebrated the opening of Seohan Auto Georgia in rural Liberty County.
- Seohan Auto Georgia develops axles and brake systems.
- Rivian’s factory was originally announced in December 2021 and slated to open in 2024.
- The Rivian factory site is 2,000 acres in southern Morgan and Walton counties.
- Infrastructure around the Rivian site has been built.
- Construction on the Rivian factory was indefinitely paused in March.
- Rivian recently partnered with a local farmer to use idle land to grow and bale hay.
- The Rivian factory is promised to employ 7,000 workers after launch.
- Rivian will build the factory after launching a new crossover model called the R2.
- The R2 crossover is expected in 2026.
- The R2 has a significantly cheaper sticker price than Rivian’s flagship R1 line.
- Production of the R2 is now planned to start in Illinois, but shift to Georgia as Rivian builds scale.
- Rivian says it will save more than $2.2 billion by delaying the Georgia factory.
- Rivian and its partners continue working at the site to ensure readiness for vertical construction after the R2 launch.
- The Joint Development Authority of Jasper, Morgan, Newton and Walton counties oversees the Rivian project.
- The JDA added that Rivian continues to make required payments as part of the state’s incentive agreement.
- The JDA said Rivian has been making meaningful improvements to its site and implementing agreed-upon site maintenance measures.
- Kemp’s camp reiterated confidence that Rivian will deliver the factory despite criticism from some elected officials.
- In June, Rivian announced a potential $5 billion technology partnership with Volkswagen Group.
- Rivian’s leaders aim to enter the green by this year’s fourth quarter.
- The VW partnership is expected to create new revenue streams for Rivian, create capital for expansion, and help Rivian lean on VW’s manufacturing prowess.
- Pat Wilson stated: “Rivian is a great example of a company that has an amazing product, and they’re really learning how to manufacture.”
- Pat Wilson stated: “They’re moving in the right direction, and we’re moving with them.”
- Georgia’s large e-mobility projects received large packages of property tax breaks and other incentives from state and local leaders.
- The incentive packages for Rivian and Hyundai far eclipsed $1 billion each.
- Wilson said companies don’t receive incentive benefits unless they deliver on promises to build factories and employ thousands, which protects taxpayers.
- Wilson said the state isn’t shying away from clean tech projects going forward.
- The days of announcing multibillion-dollar projects every few months are likely over.
- The next wave of projects will likely be smaller endeavors to assist and supply the state’s e-mobility ecosystem.
- Pat Wilson stated: “Those are generational investments. There are only so many of those out there.”
