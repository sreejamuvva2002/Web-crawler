# Georgia Ports Authority

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [GA: Supply Chain Predictability, Cost-Saving Strategies Highlight Georgia Ports Trade Conference | Trade and Industry Development](https://www.tradeandindustrydev.com/region/georgia/ga-supply-chain-predictability-cost-saving-35814)  ·  `page_009719`

## Facts

- Georgia Ports Authority hosted their 57th annual Georgia International Trade Conference bringing together customers, business partners and industry leaders to discuss the latest maritime and logistics developments.
- Governor Brian Kemp met with Georgia Ports customers and logistics partners during the conference.
- Governor Brian Kemp stated that innovation and port investments by the Georgia Ports Authority are helping to keep our state No. 1 for business while also positioning us as a true leader in global trade, and pledged to continue finding ways to further their success and reach.
- Georgia Ports Chairman Alec Poitevint thanked the International Longshoremen’s Association, Gateway Terminals Savannah, the Georgia Department of Transportation, the Georgia Department of Economic Development, and other members in the Georgia supply chain for their valuable contributions.
- Georgia Ports President and CEO Griff Lynch stated that customers require greater supply chain predictability and cost savings opportunities, and that the port has the capacity and connectivity to respond quickly to disruptions and market fluctuations around the world.
- New findings from Georgia Tech researchers show using the Port of Savannah saves shippers more than $1,000 per container to Atlanta, Memphis and Nashville compared to West Coast gateways, along with more predictable transit times.
- Chris Gaffney, managing director of the Supply Chain and Logistics Institute at Georgia Tech, noted that routing cargo through Savannah provides a clear economic advantage compared with West Coast routes by reducing congestion exposure, saving shippers money, and delivering more consistent, predictable transit performance.
- 80 percent of Savannah’s cargo moves inland by truck and 20 percent by rail.
- Rail is increasingly attractive beyond 250 miles from the port due to the cost of diesel fuel.
- Savannah’s supply chain velocity features 40 container ships a week calling Savannah, 42 doublestack trains a week connecting to inland markets, with the lowest rail dwell in the nation of 20 hours or less.
- The port handles 14,000-16,000 truck gate moves a day with dual moves taking less than 50 minutes, and single moves 30 minutes.
- Georgia Ports Authority will be opening the Gainesville Inland Port on May 4th with daily rail service via Norfolk Southern.
- In the first year of operation at the Gainesville Inland Port, 26,000 truck moves are expected to transition to rail and off Atlanta highways.
- Georgia Ports’ 10-year plan will see five new big ship berths added, reflecting a 54 percent growth in container throughput with Ocean Terminal and Savannah Container Terminal.
- Rail service will be a major differentiator as a gateway port serving growth to inland consumer and manufacturing markets.
- The Mason Mega Rail facility had a record year in 2025, handling 591,000 containers.
- On April 15, the Georgia Ports Authority submitted a Letter of Intent to Adam Telle, the Assistant Secretary of the Army for Civil Works (ASACW), to formally begin the study process to further deepen and widen the Savannah Harbor.
- The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers encourages non—federal sponsors to undertake feasibility studies themselves in line with Section 203 of the Water Resources Development Act of 1986 as a way to accelerate dredging timelines and save taxpayer funds.
- Griff Lynch stated they are excited to kick off this first step with the Army Corps of Engineers on the Savannah Harbor deepening and widening feasibility study, with the goal of being the benchmark for Army Corps of Engineering deepening projects.
- Port officials said the shipping channel needs to be improved to better serve the largest ships currently serving Savannah, and to prepare for even larger, more efficient vessels expected to serve the U.S. East Coast in the future.
- The feasibility study will consider deepening the channel and adding passing lanes for two-way ship traffic.
- The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers will provide technical assistance and will retain final review and approval authority, including oversight of environmental and economic analyses.
- The Georgia Department of Transportation (GDOT) will begin construction on a cable replacement project for the Talmadge Bridge over the Savannah River, which will remain operational during the project.
- The Talmadge Bridge work will shorten the bridge’s cables, increasing air draft beneath the bridge from 185 feet to about 205 feet, allowing larger vessels to pass, with completion expected in 2029.
- In August 2026, GDOT will open the $126 million Brampton Road Connector, a four-lane highway providing a direct link between the Port of Savannah’s Garden City Terminal and the interstate system, removing at-grade rail crossings and truck traffic from local neighborhoods.
- Separately, GDOT plans to widen Interstate 95 by one lane in each direction along a 22-mile stretch from Exit 90 to the South Carolina state line, with the project currently in preliminary design.
- The department also plans to reconstruct and widen portions of Interstate 16 west of I-95 toward State Route 67 in Bulloch County.
- This $522 million project will expand I-16 to three lanes in both directions, with construction expected to begin in 2027.
- Griff Lynch stated that all the pieces are coming together in Savannah as the gateway of choice for the Southeast region, with five container berths in the pipeline, a new Gainesville Inland Port, multiple GDOT infrastructure improvements, and Georgia Ports ready for the next 10 years with its $5 billion investment plan as the fastest growing port in the Southeast.
- The port serves 70% of the nation’s population east of the Mississippi River.
