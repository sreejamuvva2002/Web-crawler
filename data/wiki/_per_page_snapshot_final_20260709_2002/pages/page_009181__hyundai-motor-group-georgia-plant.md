# Hyundai Motor Group Georgia Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-03-31

**Source:** [Hyundai’s all-electric auto plant expands into hybrids : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/31/nx-s1-5342658/hyundai-georgia-plant-electric)  ·  `page_009181`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group announced plans to open a new plant near Savannah, Ga., a few years ago.
- The plant was originally planned to exclusively make electric vehicles.
- In 2022, José Muñoz, then CEO of Hyundai Motor North America, stated the project responded to 'the clarion call of [the Biden] administration to hasten the adoption of new electric vehicles and reduce carbon emissions.'
- The plant's grand opening occurred last week (relative to March 31, 2025).
- Hyundai changed its plans over the last few years from a dedicated EV plant to also manufacturing hybrid vehicles.
- José Muñoz, now CEO of the entire global company, said at the opening that initially everyone thought 'the world is going to be fully electric,' but they realized 'the pace of electrification was not going to be as fast as everybody thought.'
- EV sales grew slower than companies had anticipated, while hybrid vehicle sales took off.
- Hyundai executives decided quickly to produce hybrid vehicles at the Georgia plant in response to market trends.
- The industrywide trend of slowing EV plans and focusing on hybrids is also seen in companies such as Ford, GM, and Volvo.
- Under President Trump, regulations pushing automakers to make EVs are being rewritten, tax breaks for consumers buying them are at risk, and funds for EV chargers are frozen.
- Georgia Sen. Jon Ossoff warned during a Zoom call with reporters that 'The Trump administration's war on EVs poses a serious threat to EV sales, to the electric vehicle industry and the EV market, and therefore to the state of Georgia.'
- Hyundai executives maintain that government policy never drove their decision to expand into hybrids.
- José Muñoz stated, 'Building plants on the assumption of incentives is not a good decision,' and noted that if plans change, he would not say, 'Hey, we invested $12.6 billion, so I made a mistake, I'm sorry?' No.
- Hyundai's total investment in the Georgia plant is $12.6 billion.
- While Hyundai's shift toward hybrids started before Trump took office, the timing coincides with federal policy changes.
- The expansion plans were in place long before President Trump announced a new 25% tariff on imported cars.
- Kia COO Steve Center noted that the timing of the tariff announcement was 'convenient' for Hyundai's hybrid shift.
- Kia is part of Hyundai Motor Group and will also build vehicles at the new plant.
- Automakers emphasize that EVs are quiet, fun to drive, and cheap to maintain, and fear ceding an entire market segment to Chinese companies if they stop investing in EVs.
