# U.S. E-Waste and Critical Mineral Recycling Market

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** 2025-07-13

**Source:** [U.S. eyes old tech as ammo in rare earth fight with China - Cryptopolitan](https://www.cryptopolitan.com/us-old-tech-in-rare-earth-fight-with-china/)  ·  `page_001274`

## Facts

- The U.S. is recycling old electronics to reduce its dependence on China for rare earth metals.
- Companies like Glencore, Illumynt, and Cyclic Materials are expanding operations to extract metals from e-waste.
- Tariffs, trade war tensions, and long mining timelines are pushing the U.S. toward faster recycling solutions.
- The Pentagon grabbed a big equity stake in MP Materials, the only company currently digging rare earths out of U.S. soil.
- President Trump tightened his grip on global copper supplies with a 50% import tariff, pushing prices to an all-time high.
- The U.S. is targeting dead phones, broken EV batteries, trashed servers, and ancient laptops piling up in warehouses and landfills for recycling.
- The goal is to recycle metals like neodymium, praseodymium, and terbium out of e-waste instead of waiting 30 years for new mines.
- Companies like Glencore, Full Circle Electronics, Cyclic Materials, and startups like Illumynt are fighting for e-waste as raw material in a geopolitical fight against China.
- These metals are needed for fighter jets, power tools, MRI machines, wind turbines, and high-end tech used by the military or industry.
- Kunal Sinha, global head of recycling at Glencore, stated that recycling was not taken seriously until recently.
- Kunal Sinha noted, “A lot of people are still sleeping at the wheel and don’t realize how big this can be.”
- Glencore has been processing old electronics at its smelter in Quebec for decades, turning waste into copper slabs and extracting gold, silver, palladium, platinum, and more.
- Fifteen percent of Glencore’s Quebec smelter feedstock now comes from recycled electronics.
- Glencore signed a multi-year deal with Cyclic to supply them with recycled copper pulled from EV motors, data center parts, and wind turbines.
- Cyclic is spending over $20 million building a new U.S. plant in Mesa, Arizona.
- Germany’s Wieland started a $100 million recycling facility in Kentucky in 2022.
- Aurubis followed with an $800 million multi-metal plant in Georgia.
- Aurubis CEO Toralf Haag said the Aurubis Richmond facility “will allow us to keep strategically important metals in the economy,” aiming to cut down dependence on China.
- John Mitchell, president of the Global Electronics Association, pointed out that most electronics imported into the U.S. are loaded with metals like copper, gold, and steel.
- John Mitchell stated, “There’s a great opportunity to actually have the tariffs be an impetus for greater recycling in this country.”
- Recycling is fast because waste flows daily, unlike new mines which take decades to scale.
- Full Circle Electronics CEO Dave Daily said they have seen a spike in dumped electronics because businesses are rushing upgrades ahead of rising prices.
- Dave Daily noted, “We’re definitely seeing a bit of an influx of [e-waste] coming into our warehouses.”
- Full Circle tears old gear into 40 to 50 material types and sells them off to recyclers hunting metals for resale.
- Lithium-ion batteries have become a hot feedstock, packed with lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese, and aluminum.
- Recyclers like Ascend Elements, Redwood Materials, Cirba Solutions, and American Battery Technology are trying to tap battery materials to feed EV battery production.
- These recyclers rely on the 45X tax credit created under Biden, a program that could get slashed in Trump’s new spending plan.
- Kunal Sinha warned, “Be careful not to build a recycling company on the back of one tax credit, because it can be short-lived.”
- Illumynt is harvesting rare earths from dead hard drives removed from data centers.
- In April, Western Digital teamed up with Microsoft, Critical Materials Recycling, and PedalPoint Recycling to pull out copper, gold, aluminum, steel, and rare earths from old drives.
- The ITAD industry is booming, with waste disposal firms like Waste Management, Clean Harbors, and specialists like Sims Lifecycle Services and Electronic Recyclers International wiping data and breaking down devices.
- In 2022, nearly 62 million metric tons of e-waste were produced worldwide, up 82% since 2010.
- The U.S. made almost 8 million metric tons of e-waste, but only 15–20% was recycled.
- IBISWorld said the U.S. e-waste recycling market generated $28.1 billion last year, growing 8% a year.
- In April 2025, China restricted exports of rare earth magnets in retaliation for new Trump tariffs.
- Ford had to shut down some factories due to magnet shortages.
- In June, China granted a few six-month export licenses to ease the squeeze, but shipments are still far from normal.
- China still controls 90% of the market, from mining to refining to processing.
- Biden gave MP Materials $45 million before the Pentagon move.
- In April, the Interior Department greenlit development on the Colosseum rare earth project, deep inside California’s Mojave National Preserve.
- The site, owned by Australia’s Dateline Resources, could become the second U.S. rare earth mine.
- Glencore put $327.5 million into Li-Cycle, a battery recycler based in Toronto.
- Li-Cycle filed for Chapter 15 bankruptcy in May after starting construction on a plant in Rochester, New York.
- Glencore is trying to scoop up the failed project with a $40 million bid.
- Kunal Sinha said the current environment will lead to more startups and investments, adding, “We are investing ourselves.”
- Sinha warned against betting too much on hype or just new tech, stating, “Don’t invest on the hype,” but rather “on the fundamentals.”
