# U.S. Department of Energy Office of Electricity

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [Energy Department Advances U.S. Electric Grid Resilience and Reliability with New Transformer and Silicon Carbide Packaging Projects | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/oe/articles/energy-department-advances-us-electric-grid-resilience-and-reliability-new-transformer)  ·  `page_005715`

## Facts

- The U.S. Department of Energy’s Office of Electricity (OE) announced nine projects selected to receive approximately $20 million through its Flexible Innovative Transformer Technologies (FITT) funding opportunity announcement.
- OE also announced eight winners of the Silicon Carbide (SiC) Packaging Prize Phase 1 who will receive $50,000 each in cash prizes from a $400,000 total prize pool.
- The total $2.25 million SiC prize is awarded in three phases as part of the American-Made Challenges Program, which fosters collaboration between entrepreneurs, innovators, DOE’s National Labs, and the private sector.
- Gene Rodrigues, Assistant Secretary for the Office of Electricity, stated that the selected projects address advanced technology and supply chain challenges to drive innovation for the 21st century grid.
- Advanced transformers and semiconductor packaging will help the power grid better serve residents and businesses by ensuring resilient, reliable, secure, and affordable electricity.
- Transformers regulate voltage levels as power enters homes and businesses and are critical for ensuring reliability and security of power delivery.
- Selected FITT projects address technical challenges of advancing transformers through research, development, and demonstration across distribution to transmission scale applications.
- DOE’s Office of Electricity and the Office of Cybersecurity, Energy Security, and Emergency Response (CESER) co-fund the FITT FOA, contributing $18 million and $2 million respectively.
- Clemson University in Clemson, SC received $2.47 million for the Smart Hybrid transformer for Abnormal Power Events (SHAPE) project, which integrates medium voltage power electronics and battery storage into a distribution transformer.
- Eaton Corporation in Menomonee Falls, WI received $2.94 million to research and test a Compact and Reliable Voltage Regulating Hybrid Transformer for utility distribution networks and industrial sites.
- Electric Power Research Institute, Inc. in Palo Alto, CA received $2.88 million for Solid State PowerHub Development and Demonstration to accelerate commercialization of an innovative solid-state transformer accommodating input voltage variations.
- Electric Research and Manufacturing Cooperative, Inc. in Dyerberg, TN received $1.84 million for the Distribution Transformer Extender (DTE) Project to develop devices using power electronics to combine solid-state transformer functionality with small battery energy storage.
- GE Vernova Operations LLC in Schenectady, NY received $1.99 million for Full-scale Alternating Current Solid-State Transformer Demonstration (FASST) to develop and deploy a one megavolt-amperes 3-winding solid-state transformer to a public utility-owned substation.
- GE Vernova Operations LLC in Schenectady, NY received $2.99 million for topic area 2 to design and deploy a single-phase 2-winding power transformer and solid-state tap changer enabling dynamic load balancing, power flow control, and fault current limiting.
- Resilient Power Systems, Inc in Austin, TX received $1.47 million for Direct Current-as-a-Service SolidFlex Power Hub to develop and demonstrate solid-state transformer technology enhancing efficiency, reliability, and flexibility in power distribution.
- Transforma Energy, Inc. in Albuquerque, NM received $1.45 million for Drop-In Replacement Electronically Controlled Transformer (DIRECT) proposing a solid-state transformer architecture to increase system robustness across voltage and power ratings.
- University of Pittsburgh in Pittsburgh, PA received $2.5 million for Enabling Technology and Grid-Tied Testbed for Efficient, Flexible, Intelligent Distribution Transformers to leverage industry partners for evaluating and maturing next-generation intelligent distribution transformers.
- Silicon carbide is used as raw material for semiconductors because it can withstand high electricity voltage fluctuations and temperatures.
- SiC Prize Phase 1 winners will design, build, and test state-of-the-art SiC semiconductor packaging prototypes to enable devices to work more effectively in high-voltage environments such as energy storage.
- Board Breakers from Fargo, North Dakota is creating a replacement for traditional power electronic modules using additive manufacturing to print 3D ceramic packaging.
- LincolnX from Lincoln, Nebraska plans to develop novel ultrafast and scalable SiC modules featuring dual orthogonal cooling to meet prize goals and metrics.
- Marel Power Solutions from Plymouth, Michigan plans to make packaging improvements through thermal management, three-dimensional mechatronic design, and a scalable arrangement of power switches.
- NC Solar Inverters from Cary, North Carolina will utilize a designed symmetric layout to maximize parasitic flying capacitance and minimize parasitic inductance using commercially available topside cooled discrete devices.
- NoMIS-Lux-QPT-UA from Albany, New York will combine technology and products in Smart Metal Core SiC power blocks to create high voltage chip-scale packaging.
- Stony Brook Power Packaging Team from Stony Brook, New York will develop high-voltage, high-current, fast-switching, and cost-effective modules and create a business entity for engineering sampling and commercialization.
- Superior SiC Power Module Team from Gainesville, Florida plans to develop an interdisciplinary approach for SiC power modules with high-speed, high-energy efficiency, and low EMI.
- Team Raiju - University of Arkansas from Fayetteville, Arkansas will embed 128 SiC die in LTCC controlled by an active dV/dt voltage balancer and cooled with integrated microchannel busbars.
- In Phase 2, winning teams from Phase 1 will develop a physical prototype meeting Phase 2 metrics and send it to a national lab for testing validation.
- At the end of Phase 2, up to four winning teams will receive $250,000 each and become eligible to compete in Phase 3.
