## Facts

- Intercalation released a new documentary exploring why U.S. battery manufacturing lags behind China and what it will take to catch up.
- Since 2020, roughly $100 billion has poured into the American battery industry sector.
- The path to commercial-scale production at gigawatt-hour capacity remains blocked for most companies.
- One company stands as the lone exception breaking through what the industry calls the "valley of death."
- The industry landscape features promising laboratory breakthroughs that are shadowed by shuttered factories and failed ventures.
- Chinese manufacturers have cemented their control over the global battery market.
- U.S. policy support for the battery sector remains uncertain under the current administration.
- A determined cohort of entrepreneurs, researchers, and investors continues developing innovations in battery chemistry and manufacturing processes to signal an American resurgence.
- Support networks have formed to help these ventures navigate the path to commercialization.
- Intercalation collaborated with Rahul Dhiman and Spiral Studios on a documentary examining California's battery innovation ecosystem.
- The film poses the critical question of whether American ingenuity can translate into manufacturing competitiveness on the global stage.
- Six industry leaders share their perspectives in the documentary: Kara Rodby (Investor, Volta Energy Technologies), Jonathan Tan (CEO, Coreshell), Brenna Teigler (Co-Founder and CEO, Electrochemistry Foundry), Richard Wang (CEO, Voya Energy; Co-Founder, Electrochemistry Foundry; former CEO, Cuberg), Vivas Kumar (CEO, Mitra Chem), and Asma Sharafi (CTO, Ensurge Micropower).
- The documentary runs just over 20 minutes.
- It examines how China achieved its lithium-ion battery dominance, analyzes American successes and setbacks, and explores emerging opportunities for the U.S. battery sector.
- Several major U.S. battery gigafactories are scheduled to come online in 2025-2026.
