# Global and United States Battery Energy Storage Systems Market

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-12-09

**Source:** [Battery Storage Statistics 2026: The Complete Data Reference](https://bitsfrombytes.com/battery-storage-statistics-data-reference/)  ·  `page_060385`

## Facts

- Global battery storage deployments increased 40% year over year in 2025 and are forecast to grow another 41% in 2026.
- In 2025, global new battery storage capacity deployed was 108 GW (power) / 275.3 GWh (energy) per IEA Global Energy Review 2026 and InfoLink Consulting.
- BloombergNEF reported an independent count of 112 GW / 307 GWh for 2025, considered more inclusive of smaller-scale commercial deployments.
- Global cumulative battery storage capacity relative to 2021 is 11 times higher.
- Utility-scale share of 2025 new global deployments was approximately 80%.
- LFP (lithium iron phosphate) accounted for approximately 90% of 2025 new global battery storage deployments.
- Long-duration storage (4h+) represented 6% of 2025 global installs according to Wood Mackenzie.
- China produced approximately 557 GWh of stationary storage cells in 2025 per BloombergNEF estimates.
- Global BESS shipments (cells + systems) reached 421.2 GWh in 2025 per InfoLink Consulting.
- US battery storage accounted for 28% of all planned new utility-scale capacity additions in 2026, second only to solar at 51%.
- Battery storage set a US record in 2025 with 18.9 GW installed, a 58% increase from 2024's ~12 GW.
- Of the 2025 US installations, approximately 15 GW was utility-scale, 2.7 GW was residential (+92% YoY), and 95.6 MW was commercial & industrial.
- Utility-scale battery storage planned for the US in 2026 is 24 GW per EIA (Feb 2026).
- Texas accounts for 53% (12.9 GW) of 2026 US planned storage additions, California 14% (3.4 GW), and Arizona 13% (3.2 GW).
- US cumulative installed capacity over the 5-year total from 2021–2025 is 40+ GW.
- Wood Mackenzie forecasts ~500 GWh cumulative US storage for 2026–2031.
- BNEF projects US cumulative capacity by 2036 at 334 GW / 1.3 TWh.
- The US faces uncertainty in 2026 due to Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) rules effective January 2026, which disqualify battery storage projects with significant ties to Chinese entities from claiming the federal Investment Tax Credit.
- A 23% utility-scale system price increase from 2024 to 2025 reflects early supply chain restructuring due to FEOC rules.
- BNEF initially forecast a 2026 contraction for US storage but withdrew it after the Q4 2025 construction surge; growth will likely retain 2024–2025 momentum but slow compared to non-US markets.
- The $264/kWh gap between global turnkey average ($110/kWh) and US utility-scale NREL benchmark ($334/kWh) reflects FEOC-compliant supply chains excluding lowest-cost Chinese suppliers, US labor/soft costs, domestic content requirements under the IRA, and Section 301 tariffs on components sourced from China.
- NREL’s $334/kWh figure represents a US-built system with compliant components.
- The IRA standalone storage ITC (30%) is in effect; projects starting construction before mid-2026 secure the full credit, which drove the Q4 2025 construction surge and continues to support demand.
- China removed its mandatory storage-with-renewables rule in 2025, creating a slight headwind for domestic demand and reinforcing export pressure.
- Cobalt export quotas from the DRC in 2025 accelerated LFP adoption and increased flow battery costs by 25%.
- US tariffs on Chinese components (Section 301 + general duties at ~34–37% effective) contribute to the US/global cost gap.
- The 19% learning rate means battery costs fall approximately 19% for every doubling of cumulative production, a trend that has held consistently over the past decade.
- LFP cell price in the Chinese domestic market was $36–$40/kWh as of Nov 2025 per Ember/BNEF survey.
- Global average stationary storage pack price was $70/kWh globally in Dec 2025 per BNEF battery price survey.
- Global average lithium-ion pack price across all uses was $108/kWh.
- Core equipment (BESS enclosure + PCS + EMS) costs approximately $75/kWh in competitive global markets outside the US and China.
- Turnkey BESS project capex global average for 4-hour systems is $110–$117/kWh per BNEF Energy Storage Systems Cost Survey 2025.
- Turnkey BESS in competitive ex-US/ex-China markets costs ~$125/kWh.
- Residential battery storage fully installed in the US costs $700–$1,300/kWh.
- Utility-scale LCOS is $65/MWh for optimal global markets (Ember, Oct 2025) and ranges from $50–$150/MWh for US utility-scale (Lazard, 2025).
- Residential LCOS in the US ranges from $200–$400/MWh.
- Global average pack prices declined from ~$1,200/kWh in 2010 to $108/kWh in 2025, a total decline of 91%.
- BNEF forecasts turnkey 4-hour BESS costs by 2035 at $41/kWh for China, $101/kWh for Europe, and $108/kWh for the United States.
- LFP batteries now account for around 90% of global battery storage deployments, up from below 50% five years ago.
- The shift to LFP reflects cost, safety, and cycle-life advantages; LFP cells tolerate more charge-discharge cycles without significant degradation and contain no cobalt.
- Chinese manufacturers supply close to all global LFP demand, causing a 44–56% price premium for cells in North America and Europe due to lack of local manufacturing at comparable cost.
- Lowest observed LFP cell price in 2025 was $36/kWh.
- Leading suppliers offer LFP cycle life of 6,000–10,000 cycles.
- NMC share is declining due to cobalt export quotas from the DRC and cost pressures.
- Flow battery (vanadium redox) accounted for 2.1% of capacity in 2025.
- Vanadium prices rose from $8/kg to $11.2/kg (+40%) between 2024 and mid-2025, causing a +25% CAPEX impact on flow batteries.
- Sodium-ion batteries crossed a commercial threshold in April 2026, offering no lithium, cobalt, or nickel, with performance stability down to -40°C.
- CATL launched a sodium-ion BESS cell (300+ Ah, 160 Wh/kg, 97% round-trip efficiency) at ESIE 2026 on April 1.
- CATL's sodium-ion cells offer 15,000+ cycles at 80% capacity retention and operate from -40°C to +70°C.
- CATL signed a 60 GWh multi-year deal with HyperStrong for sodium-ion BESS in April 2026, the largest such agreement ever signed.
- CATL plans a 40 GWh sodium-ion capacity expansion backed by a ¥5B / $735M investment.
- Sodium-ion prices are expected to be approximately 30% lower than comparable LFP cells at scale.
- BNEF forecasts sodium-ion will account for ~3% of energy capacity in 2026, growing to 13% by the end of its forecast period.
- The world's first mass-produced sodium-ion passenger EV is the Changan Nevo A06 using CATL cells (Feb 2026).
- Utility-scale grid applications accounted for ~80% of 2025 deployments, with a growing trend displacing gas peakers.
- Behind-the-meter commercial & industrial applications accounted for ~12% of 2025 deployments.
- Residential applications accounted for ~8% of 2025 deployments, showing fast growth in the US (+92%) and dominance in Germany.
- Data center co-located storage is an emerging category with 12.9 GW/51.5 GWh announced or under development, driven by AI infrastructure needs for sub-second power quality.
- EV charging station storage accounted for ~1 GWh installed globally as of the report date.
- Battery storage market value figures range from $14.9 billion to $161 billion depending on whether pumped hydro, behind-the-meter residential, or services/O&M revenue are included.
- Fortune Business Insights values the BESS hardware market at $32.62B in 2025 and projects it to reach $40.45B in 2026, growing to $161.12B by 2034 (18.86% CAGR).
- Cell prices dropped 45% in stationary storage from 2024 to 2025, yet deployment grew 61%, meaning dollar-denominated market size figures chronically understate physical energy transition progress.
- Long-duration storage (8+ hours) accounted for just 6% of global 2025 deployments despite extensive policy support.
- The dominant duration in 2025 new builds is 2–4 hours using lithium-ion.
- The IEA states a need for hundreds of TWh of LDES by 2050 for deep decarbonization.
- Compressed air, gravity, and iron-air technologies account for less than 1% of deployed capacity.
- The largest barrier to LDES is economics versus 2–4 hour Li-ion and intermittency duration mismatch.
