# Rivian

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Rivain's Loan: Rivian Gets $6.6 Billion Conditional Loan Approval To Build Georgia EV Plant | Republic World](https://www.republicworld.com/automobile/rivian-gets-66-billion-conditional-loan-approval-to-build-georgia-ev-plant)  ·  `page_059036`

## Facts

- Rivian has received conditional approval for a loan of up to $6.6 billion from the US Department of Energy to build its production facility in Georgia.
- The announcement comes ahead of the inauguration of President-elect Donald Trump, who is expected to undo many of the Biden administration's EV-friendly policies and incentives.
- Operation of the Georgia plant will begin in 2028.
- Rivian plans to build future vehicles at the Georgia facility, including its smaller, less expensive R2 SUVs and R3 crossovers.
- Rivian shares are down about 50 percent this year as the company has struggled to produce its electric SUVs and pickup trucks while grappling with part shortages and pushing to slash costs.
- To conserve cash and hasten the production of the R2, which is seen critical to Rivian's success amid a slowdown in EV growth, Rivian paused construction of the Georgia plant earlier this year.
- Instead of building the R2 in Georgia, Rivian decided to start production there in 2026 at its Normal, Illinois plant where it makes its flagship R1S SUVs and R1T pickup trucks.
- CEO RJ Scaringe stated that the loan would enable Rivian to more aggressively scale its US manufacturing footprint for competitively priced R2 and R3 vehicles that emphasize both capability and affordability.
- Rivian must satisfy certain technical, legal, environmental, and financial conditions before the energy department grants the loan.
- As part of the loan conditions, Rivian will not actively oppose union organizing efforts at the Georgia plant, though the approval will not guarantee unionization at the facility.
- Rivian will work with the Energy Department to close the loan before the Trump administration takes over.
- The loan comes from the government's Advanced Technology Vehicles Manufacturing loan program, which has previously provided low-cost loans to automakers including Tesla, Ford, and General Motors.
- Rivian had earlier forecast the cost of the Georgia plant at $5 billion.
- Rivian expects the Georgia plant to employ about 7,500 operations staff through 2030.
- The Department of Energy stated in an October assessment that financially supporting the project will help Rivian bring 400,000 electric vehicles to market and into greater use.
- The loan includes $6 billion of principal and $600 million of capitalized interest.
- The loan announcement comes less than two weeks after Rivian closed its $5.8 billion investment from German automaker Volkswagen as part of their technology joint venture.
- Canaccord Genuity analysts noted that the joint venture helps alleviate a significant chunk of capital concerns and likely establishes the Rivian and Volkswagen venture as a platform of choice in the Western world apart from Tesla.
- Rivian still faces challenges including a lack of scale, increasing competition, high capital costs, and Trump's plans to end tax credits for EV buyers.
- In 2022, Rivian secured $1.5 billion in state and local incentives for the Georgia facility.
- In May, Rivian received $827 million in an incentive package from the State of Illinois to expand operations at its Normal facility.
- Earlier this month, Rivian posted its first drop in quarterly revenue since going public three years ago, citing a significant shortage of a part used in the drive unit of its vehicles.
- The company stuck to its forecast of turning its first gross profit in the current quarter, reflecting cost cuts from renegotiated supplier contracts, revamped manufacturing processes, and a sharp increase in green car credits.
