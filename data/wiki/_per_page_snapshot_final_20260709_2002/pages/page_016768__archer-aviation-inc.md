## Facts

- eVTOLs are electric vertical takeoff and landing aircraft that are battery-powered and electrically flown for short-distance urban air mobility spanning 20- to 50-mile urban routes.
- eVTOLs produce zero emissions and are much quieter than helicopters.
- eVTOLs combine vertical takeoff and landing capabilities with the fixed-wing efficiency of airplanes.
- eVTOLs can maneuver through tight spaces in crowded cities and fly at speeds up to 200 miles per hour.
- eVTOLs typically seat four passengers and a pilot, shrinking a 60-minute commute to the airport to under 10 minutes.
- eVTOLs can be used to transport passengers, cargo, or medical products.
- Archer Aviation Inc. is a pre-revenue leading player in the air taxi industry.
- Archer's lead prototype is named Midnight and can fly four passengers and a pilot at speeds of up to 150 miles per hour.
- Archer Aviation has partnerships with United Airlines Holdings Inc.
- Archer plans to launch its air taxi routes in Los Angeles and New York.
- United Airlines agreed to purchase up to 200 aircraft to transport passengers to and from airports.
- Archer has a manufacturing partnership with auto giant Stellantis N.V.
- Archer is building a high-volume production factory in Georgia and is awaiting final certification.
- Archer is burning over $200 million in cash per quarter.
- Joby Aviation Inc. acquired Uber Technologies Inc.’s Elevate division in 2020.
- Joby strengthened its bond with Uber and positioned itself as a leading operator in urban air mobility.
- Joby focused on developing its ElevateOS operating platform, which would be integrated with Uber’s mobility platform.
- Uber invested up to $125 million in Joby.
- Uber plans to offer Joby's air taxi rides through its platform.
- Joby sealed a partnership with Toyota Motor Corporation to provide expertise in scaling production of its aircraft to over 500 per year by 2028.
- Delta Air Lines Inc. is also a partner for Joby.
- Both Archer and Joby are currently in pre-revenue and pre-commercial stages.
- Both companies plan on launching commercial services by early 2026, though there is no guarantee.
- Operations carrying passengers hinge on acquiring FAA certifications to begin operations.
- Both companies are currently losing money and are expected to continue losing money even after commercial operations begin.
- Both companies burn through hundreds of millions of dollars every quarter.
- The financial losses increase the likelihood of additional capital raises, which can further dilute shareholders' interests.
