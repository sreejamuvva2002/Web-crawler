# Hyundai Motor

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2022-07-22

**Source:** [Hyundai accused of using child labor at Alabama factory](https://nypost.com/2022/07/22/hyundai-accused-of-using-child-labor-at-alabama-factory/)  ·  `page_021135`

## Facts

- A subsidiary of Hyundai Motor has used child labor at a plant that supplies parts for the Korean carmaker’s assembly line in Montgomery, Ala.
- Underage workers, in some cases as young as 12, have worked at a metal stamping plant operated by SMART Alabama LLC.
- SMART is listed by Hyundai in corporate filings as a majority-owned unit.
- SMART supplies parts for popular cars and SUVs built by Hyundai at its flagship US assembly plant in Montgomery.
- Hyundai did not respond to phone calls or emails from Reuters seeking comment on the allegations.
- SMART stated it follows federal, state, and local laws and denies knowingly employing anyone ineligible for employment.
- SMART relies on temporary work agencies to fill jobs and expects them to follow recruitment and hiring laws.
- The allegations followed the brief disappearance in February 2022 of a Guatemalan migrant child from her family’s home in Alabama.
- The missing girl, who turns 14 that month, along with her brothers ages 12 and 15, worked at the plant earlier in 2022 and were not attending school.
- Their father, Pedro Tzi, confirmed their employment at the plant.
- Enterprise police identified the missing girl by name in a public alert during a search for her.
- The Enterprise police force, located about 45 miles from the Luverne plant, lacks jurisdiction to investigate labor-law violations and notified the state attorney general’s office instead.
- Mike Lewis, a spokesperson at the Alabama attorney general’s office, declined to comment on whether investigators contacted SMART or Hyundai.
- Interviews with a dozen former/current employees and labor recruiters indicate a larger cohort of underage workers found jobs at the supplier over recent years.
- Several minors forgone schooling for long shifts at the plant, which has a documented history of health and safety violations including amputation hazards.
- Most current and former employees spoke on condition of anonymity.
- The precise number of children who worked at SMART, their pay, and employment terms remain undetermined.
- The child labor revelations could spark consumer, regulatory, and reputational backlash for Hyundai.
- Hyundai’s online human rights policy forbids child labor throughout its workforce and suppliers.
- Hyundai recently announced plans to expand in the US with over $5 billion in investments, including a new electric vehicle factory near Savannah, Ga.
- David Michaels, former US assistant secretary of labor for OSHA, stated consumers should be outraged that cars are being built partly by child workers.
- Labor experts noted heightened risks of undocumented migrants ending up in hazardous workplaces during US labor shortages and supply chain disruptions.
- Alabama and federal laws limit minors under 18 from working in metal stamping and pressing operations like SMART's.
- Alabama law requires children 17 and under to be enrolled in school.
- Michaels warned Hyundai executives in 2015 that heavy demand for "just-in-time" parts was causing safety lapses.
- The SMART plant builds parts for the Elantra, Sonata, and Santa Fe models, which accounted for almost 37% of Hyundai’s US sales through June.
- Federal records show SMART has received repeated OSHA penalties for health and safety violations.
- A Reuters review found SMART assessed with at least $48,515 in OSHA penalties since 2013, including a fine this year.
- OSHA inspections documented crush and amputation hazards at the factory.
- The plant's website states it has capacity to supply parts for up to 400,000 vehicles each year.
- The facility has struggled to retain labor to meet Hyundai’s demand.
- In late 2020, SMART wrote a letter to US consular officials in Mexico seeking a visa for a Mexican worker due to being "severely lacking in labor."
- The late 2020 letter was written by SMART General Manager Gary Sport and stated Hyundai "will not tolerate such shortcomings."
- Attorneys filed a class-action lawsuit earlier this year against SMART and several staffing firms supplying workers with US visas.
- The lawsuit, filed in the US District Court for the Northern District of Georgia on behalf of about 40 Mexican workers, alleges some hired as engineers were ordered to do menial jobs.
- SMART called the lawsuit allegations "baseless" and "meritless" in court documents.
- Many minors at the plant were hired through recruitment agencies.
- Staffing firms are often criticized by labor advocates for enabling large employers to outsource responsibility for verifying employee work eligibility.
- A former adult migrant worker said there were around 50 underage workers across different shifts at SMART.
- Another former US citizen worker said she worked alongside about a dozen minors on her shift.
- Former employee Tabatha Moultry, who worked on SMART’s assembly line through 2019, noted high turnover and reliance on migrant workers for intense production demands.
- Moultry recalled working with one migrant girl who looked 11 or 12 years old but claimed to be 13.
- Tzi contacted Enterprise police on Feb 3 after his daughter did not come home.
- Police issued an Amber Alert and launched a manhunt for Alvaro Cucul, a 21-year-old Guatemalan migrant and SMART worker.
- Using cell phone geolocation data, police located Cucul and the girl in a parking lot in Athens, Georgia.
- The girl told officers Cucul was a friend they traveled with to look for work opportunities; Cucul was later arrested and deported.
- After local news coverage of the disappearance, SMART dismissed a number of underage workers due to fears of an authority crackdown.
- Tzi previously worked at SMART and now does construction and forestry odd jobs, expressing regret that his children worked but noting the family needed the income.
- Tzi stated the kids are no longer working and will be in school in the fall.
