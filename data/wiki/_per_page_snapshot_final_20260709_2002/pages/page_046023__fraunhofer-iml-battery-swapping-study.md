# Fraunhofer IML Battery Swapping Study

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Battery Swapping in Road Freight: Opportunities and Obstacles](https://dhl-freight-connections.com/en/sustainability/battery-swapping/)  ·  `page_046023`

## Facts

- Introduction of battery-electric trucks faces significant challenges including shortage of charging stations, limited available space, and narrow operational windows.
- Growing interest exists in automated battery swapping technology that decouples charging from vehicle operation.
- Fraunhofer Institute for Material Flow and Logistics (IML) produced a study identifying situations where battery swapping creates added value and remaining obstacles.
- Battery swapping involves automated replacement of discharged traction batteries with fully charged ones at specially equipped stations.
- Swappable battery modules are removed from vehicles from below or the side and replaced.
- Key benefit: vehicle spends short time at service station while batteries charge separately, reducing grid strain and costs.
- Decoupling charging from operation is especially beneficial for heavy-duty road freight transport, enabling high vehicle availability while keeping grid load manageable.
- Fraunhofer IML produced white paper titled “Battery Swapping for Heavy-Duty Commercial Vehicles as a Complement to Wired Charging” on behalf of logistics and waste management companies including DHL Group.
- Project supported by Working Group on CO₂-Free Logistics of the Federal Association For Freight Forwarding and Logistics Germany (DSLV).
- Study integrates technological, business, and energy system perspectives based on 22 expert interviews across value chain participants including users, station operators, battery manufacturers, grid operators, and vehicle manufacturers.
- Heavy-duty vehicles account for more than a quarter of greenhouse gas emissions from road transport in the EU.
- Heavy-duty vehicles account for over six percent of the EU’s total emissions.
- In 2024, stricter CO₂ regulations took effect implementing staggered reduction targets for new heavy-duty vehicles: minus 45% by 2030, minus 65% by 2035, and minus 90% by 2040 compared to 2019 reference year.
- Electrifying heavy-duty trucks poses greater technical and operational challenge than passenger cars due to high daily mileage, tight time windows, large energy required per stop, and lack of space at high-traffic locations.
- Industry feedback indicates depot charging remains cornerstone of operations; widespread adoption of electric trucks will likely be driven primarily by fast wired charging.
- Battery swapping is beneficial when three factors converge: time-sensitive processes, predictable routes, and limited grid or space resources.
- Study identifies hub-to-hub transport in multi-shift operation as application area with fixed, predictable routes and daily driving ranges of 400 to 500 kilometers.
- Six- to ten-minute swap replaces wired charging session of about 50 to 70 minutes for hub-to-hub transport.
- Study identifies 24/7 internal transport operations with just-in-time requirements as application area where exchange occurs concurrently with loading dock and yard operations without additional downtime.
- Potentially autonomous 24/7 logistics can use battery swapping to ensure continuous power supply without driver or operational downtime.
- Swapping stations have large storage capacities and controllable energy consumption, relieving pressure on power grid.
- Swapping stations require significantly less space than conventional charging stations for heavy-duty trucks.
- Cost-effectiveness of battery swapping depends heavily on usage profile, system parameters, and business model.
- Key cost factor is interplay between energy price, swap time, and resulting opportunity costs rather than energy price alone.
- Quick battery swap significantly reduces costly downtime.
- European model calculation concludes swap-capable trucks can currently offer cost advantage of up to 17% over vehicles charged at stations.
- Cost advantage may persist through 2035 (up to 10%).
- Data on actual operating costs of heavy-duty battery-swap fleets is currently limited, and study acknowledges uncertainties in parameterization.
- Most significant barrier for newcomers is lack of cross-manufacturer mechanical interoperability.
- Currently no standardized interchange or interface format exists for different vehicle and battery types.
- Outstanding issues include ownership and liability, investment needs for automated swap stations and battery pools, and invoicing compliance with legal calibration requirements.
- Station mechanics pose single-point-of-failure risks, necessitating redundancy and fallback wired charging option.
- Europe is lagging behind by international standards; China uses battery swapping on industrial scale.
- In 2024, approximately 28,700 swap-capable heavy-duty trucks were registered in China.
- Roughly one in three newly registered heavy-duty electric vehicles in China was swap-capable in 2024.
- Faster implementation in China attributed to standardization initiatives, government regulation, and stronger market dynamics.
- Local conditions in China cannot be directly applied to Europe.
- Recommendations include launching open-standard corridor pilot projects with anchor fleets as complement to high-power charging rollout.
- Future expansion can include megawatt charging systems to address technical, regulatory, and business issues under real-world conditions.
- Findings support measures to promote testing of battery swapping outlined in 2030 Charging Infrastructure Master Plan of German Federal Ministry of Transport.
- Plan requires vehicle manufacturers and battery producers to collaborate with freight forwarding and logistics companies to develop binding standards.
- DHL Freight is examining ways to accelerate electrification of heavy-duty vehicles in practice using battery swapping for specific use cases.
- Battery swapping is not intended to replace plug-in charging but serve as potential component for well-defined, predictable transport operations.
- DHL promotes emissions reductions through currently available solutions including using battery-electric trucks for distribution and testing new technologies.
- DHL offers GoGreen Plus Flex sustainable offer allowing customers to reduce shipments’ carbon footprint through insetting measures.
- Contributing to studies like this white paper is part of DHL’s approach to developing practical solutions where need for action is urgent.
- Wired charging remains primary path for widespread adoption, but battery swapping has place in high-frequency, schedule-driven applications.
- Battery swapping reduces downtime to just a few minutes and enables grid- and cost-optimized charging at station.
- Technology requires resolution of interoperability, invoicing, ownership, and liability issues to reach full potential.
- Timing is crucial; if Europe addresses issues too late, non-European standards could set pace.
- Decarbonizing road freight requires combination of technologies including battery swapping, fast-charging infrastructure, and consistent emissions reductions.
- Battery swapping currently takes about five to ten minutes for heavy-duty commercial vehicles.
- Main advantage is significant reduction in downtime compared to 50 to 70 minute cable charging sessions.
- Decoupling energy consumption from route planning increases vehicle availability.
- Battery swapping considered supplementary option to depot and fast charging, particularly useful for predictable routes with frequent traffic.
- Station utilization rate of over 43% represents tipping point for economic viability.
- All calculations on operating costs are subject to uncertainty due to limited data.
