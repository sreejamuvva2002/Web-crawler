# Hyundai Motor Group / LG Energy Solution Joint Venture

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Hyundai and LG will invest an additional $2B into making batteries at Georgia electric vehicle plant | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/hyundai-georgia-lg-electric-vehicle-battery-58bdbe36e34179db41c95dc120851f77)  ·  `page_009338`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group and LG Energy Solution will spend an additional $2 billion and hire an extra 400 workers to make batteries at Hyundai’s U.S. EV plant under construction in Georgia.
- The announcement expands on a partnership launched three months ago to produce batteries at the same site west of Savannah, where Hyundai plans to start EV production in 2025.
- The news brings the companies’ total investment in the Georgia plant to more than $7.5 billion and the site’s overall planned workforce to 8,500.
- José Muñoz, president and global chief operating officer of Hyundai Motor Company, stated that the incremental investment in Bryan County reflects their continued commitment to create a more sustainable future powered by American workers.
- In 2022, Hyundai said it would invest $5.5 billion to assemble electric vehicles and batteries on 2,900 acres (1,170 hectares) in the community of Ellabell.
- The additional investment and jobs do not mean the Hyundai/LG battery plant will produce more batteries than originally planned.
- The companies still expect the plant to supply enough batteries for 300,000 EVs per year, matching the capacity given when the joint venture was first announced in May.
- Hyundai spokesperson Michael Stewart said the plant has potential to expand depending on market conditions and demand.
- Stewart added that Hyundai remains very optimistic about the transition to electric vehicles.
- Hyundai’s Georgia plant initially plans to produce 300,000 vehicles per year but could later expand to build 500,000 annually.
- Landing Hyundai’s first U.S. plant dedicated to EV manufacturing was hailed as the largest economic development project in Georgia’s history when it was first announced last year.
- Suppliers have pledged to invest nearly $2.2 billion and hire 5,000 people for the project.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp stated that they are building on that success as they continue to make Georgia the e-mobility capital of the nation.
- Jessica Atwell, spokesperson for the state Department of Economic Development, said Georgia and local governments will kick in additional incentives to be disclosed next week after documents are finalized.
- State and local governments have already pledged $1.8 billion in tax breaks and other perks.
- The new investment will allow more tax breaks by law, including another $10 million in state income tax credits at $5,250 per job over five years.
- State and local governments could also end up spending more on discretionary incentives like infrastructure aid and equipment subsidies.
- The Hyundai package has been described as the largest subsidy package a U.S. state has ever promised an automotive plant, according to Greg LeRoy, executive director of Good Jobs First.
- The announcements are part of an electric vehicle and battery land rush across the United States.
- Under the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act, EVs must be assembled in North America, and a certain percentage of their battery parts and minerals must come from North America or a U.S. free trade partner to qualify for a full $7,500 EV tax credit.
- Currently, no Hyundai or Kia vehicles are eligible for the tax credit unless they are leased.
- Hyundai opposed having foreign-made vehicles excluded, in part because it is building American factories.
- Hyundai will need batteries for more than just vehicles made in Ellabell.
- The company is already assembling electric vehicles at its plant in Montgomery, Alabama.
- In April, the company announced it would start assembling its electric Kia EV9 large SUV at the Kia plant in West Point, Georgia.
