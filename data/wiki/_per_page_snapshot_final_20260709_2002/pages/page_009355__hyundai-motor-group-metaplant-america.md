# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-03-06

**Source:** [Hyundai fails wastewater standards - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2025/03/06/hyundai-fails-to-meet-wastewater-standards/)  ·  `page_009355`

## Facts

- For months, companies building Hyundai’s electric vehicles and batteries have trucked millions of gallons of wastewater from the Ellabell manufacturing facility to private treatment facilities in Georgia and beyond state lines.
- Savannah city authorities found excessive levels of heavy metals from the plant’s wastewater that violated its permit.
- Correspondence between Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America and Savannah officials shows struggles with wastewater processing and disposal since September 2024.
- Permit violations forced the company to abandon treatment in Coastal Georgia and truck industrial wastewater to sites in Jacksonville and Goose Creek, South Carolina, among other places.
- The difficulties illustrate challenges local communities face from Hyundai’s rapid pace to open Georgia’s largest economic development project.
- Bryan County is building the $129 million North Bryan County Water Reclamation Facility across I-16 from the Hyundai site to accommodate the plant’s industrial waste.
- The new facility will not be complete until the end of 2025.
- HMGMA is the legal entity in charge of manufacturing and permitting at the Ellabell site, which started assembling vehicles last September.
- Two temporary measures—first Savannah treating the wastewater, then a Richmond Hill facility handling it—have failed.
- Ogeechee Riverkeeper Damon Mullis stated HMGMA’s wastewater issues are part of a larger pattern of neglect of state standards and good stewardship of natural resources.
- Mullis noted there seems to be a gap in the system where contents of hauled wastewater are not properly vetted, resulting in industrial pollutants passing through facilities and polluting shared waters.
- Savannah city government agreed to treat HMGMA’s industrial wastewater temporarily while the Bryan County facility was built.
- On Sept. 3, the Travis Field Water Reclamation Facility near the airport received its first wastewater from the Hyundai site through 24 miles of pipe.
- Within weeks, city technicians discovered Hyundai’s wastewater contained more than six times the allowable limit of copper and more than twice the allowable zinc concentration.
- HMGMA failed to keep continuous oversight on wastewater conductivity, with data gaps sometimes reaching 19 hours.
- Savannah has authority from the Georgia Environmental Protection Division (EPD) to operate and enforce an industrial pretreatment program.
- The permit for HMGMA demanded self-reporting from the company.
- City technicians found clear violations resulting in rising levels of phosphorus in discharge from Travis Field, which poses a risk to sensitive biological treatment processes.
- On Sept. 26, the city shut down operations at Travis Field in anticipation of Hurricane Helene, which was also the last day it received wastewater from HMGMA.
- On Oct. 1, the city sent a “Notice of Violation” to Hyundai detailing permit violations and demanding a detailed correction plan.
- Savannah Chief of Water Resources Ron Feldner stated that inability to achieve compliance necessitated alternate approaches for wastewater disposal.
- Rather than restart treatment in Savannah, HMGMA started trucking wastewater to various facilities in late September.
- State regulators at the EPD stepped in on Dec. 6, sending a “Letter of Concern” to HMGMA requiring a permit for government-owned facilities and requesting a list of all receiving treatment plants.
- In the last four months of 2024, HMGMA sent more than 5.2 million gallons of wastewater to private treatment plants operated by Liquid Environmental Solutions in Jacksonville, Fla.; ReWorld in Augusta and Asheboro, NC; Shamrock Environmental Corp in Browns Summit, NC and Oxford, Ga; and US Water Recovery in Goose Creek, SC.
- Communications and PR Manager Joe LaMuraglia said Hyundai’s own investigation showed pipes within its onsite pretreatment system had elevated zinc levels affecting both domestic and industrial wastewater discharge.
- The piping replacement process will not be complete until the fourth quarter of 2025.
- HMGMA received a Notice of Violation on Jan. 27 from the EPD for not applying or receiving a permit prior to disposing wastewater at the Richmond Hill facility.
- The EPD notice threatens fines for any continued noncompliance.
- Correspondence shows companies from the Ellabell site stopped using the Richmond Hill facility in November.
- LaMuraglia stated the company had not received any fines as of March 5.
- LaMuraglia clarified that the wastewater is not solely from HMGMA.
- HMGMA remains the permit holder and subject of both Savannah’s and EPD’s regulatory actions.
- State EPD officials continue to talk with HMGMA to ensure proper wastewater management until the new Bryan County facility is ready later this year, according to EPD spokeswoman Sara Lips.
- Savannah city water experts remain involved in working daily to return to a normal treatment process, according to Feldner.
- The new Bryan County facility will use a membrane bioreactor process and have capacity to treat 5 million gallons of wastewater a day at its projected opening in the fourth quarter of this year, according to spokesman Nicholas Beard.
- LaMuraglia stated the wastewater issues have had “no impact to production” at the megasite.
- Hyundai will start releasing production numbers at the end of March.
- LaMuraglia emphasized Hyundai’s vested interest in keeping local waterways healthy and adherence to all applicable laws and regulations regarding wastewater.
- Ogeechee Riverkeeper Legal Director Ben Kirsch stated that Hyundai’s failure to properly treat industrial wastewater began impacting the biochemical balance at City facilities and could have polluted waterways if not caught.
- Kirsch noted it is likely what happened at each of the smaller facilities Hyundai sent wastewater to, as without a pretreatment permit, they could not know if they could successfully treat the specific needs of Hyundai’s industrial wastewater.
