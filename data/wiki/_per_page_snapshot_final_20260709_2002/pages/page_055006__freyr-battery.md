# Freyr Battery

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-02-06

**Source:** [Freyr Battery scraps plans for $2.5 billion factory in Coweta County, Georgia official says](https://www.atlantanewsfirst.com/2025/02/06/freyr-battery-scraps-plans-factory-coweta-county-georgia-official-says/)  ·  `page_055006`

## Facts

- Norway-based battery manufacturer Freyr Battery has scrapped plans to build a factory in metro Atlanta.
- The Coweta County project would have been Freyr Battery’s first American factory.
- The project promised hundreds of jobs and a $2.57 billion investment in Georgia.
- A spokesperson for the Georgia Department of Economic Development confirmed the state was made aware of Freyr’s decision to discontinue its plans in Coweta County.
- Georgia’s incentives process protects the Georgia taxpayer, and when a company’s plans change, that process ensures discretionary incentives are repaid.
- The company and state are working together to ensure that the state grant used for project site acquisition is repaid expeditiously.
- A Freyr spokesperson stated the company is grateful for the support and partnership from Coweta County.
- Freyr is focusing at the moment on a solar module manufacturing facility in Texas.
- The plant was announced in 2022.
- The planned site was a 368-acre location in Bridgeport Industrial Park off I-85.
- The plan called for creating more than 720 jobs over a seven-year period.
- In 2022, Gov. Brian Kemp stated the plant would have been a massive investment into “Georgia’s sustainable technology ecosystem.”
- The plant was designed to produce lithium-ion battery cells, which are often used in cars and for energy storage.
- Coweta County officials expressed disappointment in Freyr’s business decision.
- The county expressed sincere appreciation to the County staff, Coweta County Development Authority, and the Georgia Department of Economic Development for their assistance, hard work and due diligence throughout the whole process.
- County officials noted that despite the outcome, incentives were structured with safeguards to protect taxpayers and the property will remain valuable for creating jobs and investment to benefit the community.
