# Constellium

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2023-05-30

**Source:** [Police escort scab trucks through pickets at Constellium Automotive in Michigan - World Socialist Web Site](https://www.wsws.io/en/articles/2023/05/30/xcnz-m30.html)  ·  `page_018513`

## Facts

- Police escorted trucks transporting scab parts through a picket line at Constellium Automotive in Van Buren Township, Michigan.
- Workers on the picket line reportedly made each truck wait 15 minutes before allowing it through.
- UAW Local 174 instructed members to allow scab trucks to enter and exit without delay instead of mobilizing solidarity action from Ford workers.
- A picket reported that day shift held trucks for 15 minutes until the company called police and the union ordered them to be let through.
- A co-worker stated that if Ford workers refused to take the scab parts, the strike would end by the next day.
- The 160 workers at the plant were approaching their third week on strike.
- Striking workers are demanding wage increases to offset the soaring cost of living, safe working conditions, and an end to the attendance points system.
- Under the attendance points system, employees are fired after accumulating 10 points, with penalties ranging from half a point for being one minute late to 4 points for a no-call/no-show.
- High turnover at the plant forces team leaders, who train new hires, to take on extra work.
- The hazardous work environment includes tarps covering the plant to catch rainwater, which is directed via hose into a trash barrel.
- Union bureaucracy collaborates with management to enforce speedups, often assigning one person to operate machinery designed for two.
- New hires are frequently forced to work seven days a week for 12 hours a day.
- The facility serves as a key regional supplier of aluminum engine rails and crash control systems to Ford Motor Company’s six flagship assembly plants.
- Scab parts from this supplier are visibly identifiable on the shop floors of Ford's assembly plants.
- On Tuesday, May 16, the union permitted the company to bring in approximately 40 temporary agency workers to be trained as strikebreakers.
- Unqualified and uncertified management personnel are operating hi-los and other heavy equipment during the strike.
- A Constellium spokesperson stated upon the strike's start that they do not anticipate production disruptions and will work with customers to ensure operational continuity.
- The Paris, France-based corporation employs more than 12,000 workers internationally.
- The global producer of aluminum rolled products, extruded products, and structural auto parts reported $8.73 billion in 2022 revenue, including $1.9 billion in automaker sales.
- The company recorded a net profit of $332 million in 2022, an increase from $282 million in 2021.
- CEO Jean-Marc Germain earned nearly $9 million in compensation last year.
- The strike is part of a broader wave of autoworker resistance against automaker plans to force workers to fund the industry's transition to electric vehicles.
- In Holland, Ohio, 525 UAW members have been striking for over three weeks against Clarios, the world's largest auto battery manufacturer.
- Days after receiving a court injunction limiting picketing, Clarios began importing strikebreakers to its Toledo-area factory to maintain battery production for Ford, GM, Stellantis, and others.
- Both Constellium and Clarios are collaborating with UAW leadership to secure wage settlements below inflation and mandate overtime at straight-time pay.
- This trend threatens over 170,000 Big Three auto plant workers in the US and Canada facing contract negotiations in the fall.
- Decades of auto industry concessions, including plant closures, pay cuts, temp labor, and eliminated COLA and personal days, largely originated in parts plants spun off from the Big Three.
- After rejecting a UAW-backed contract with compulsory overtime and low pay, Clarios workers formed a Rank-and-File Committee under the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees.
- In a May 26 open letter, Clarios rank-and-file workers stated that merely saying "no" is insufficient and they must clearly define their strike demands.
- The letter demands UAW President Shawn Fain utilize the union's $800 million strike fund to provide $1,000 weekly strike pay for Clarios and Constellium workers ahead of fall contract expirations.
- Constellium workers face identical issues and are urged to connect with the Clarios Rank-and-File Committee to build mutual strike support.
- Despite UAW leadership's attempts to isolate the strikes, rank-and-file workers from other facilities are joining the picket lines.
- A picket reported that wages remain stagnant while costs rise, overtime requires 40 hours worked, and they are picketing 24/7 as maintenance workers and temps scab around the clock.
- Constellium workers are encouraged to coordinate with the International Workers Alliance of Rank-and-File Committees at Clarios and Dana to develop a counter-strategy.
