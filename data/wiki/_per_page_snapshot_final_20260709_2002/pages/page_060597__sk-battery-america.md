# SK Battery America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-09

**Source:** [EV Boom Hits Reality as Battery Plant Cuts Nearly 1,000 Jobs - AOL](https://www.aol.com/lifestyle/ev-boom-hits-reality-battery-214500357.html)  ·  `page_060597`

## Facts

- SK Battery America operates a manufacturing facility in Commerce, Georgia.
- The South Korean battery supplier laid off 958 employees at its Commerce plant.
- The layoffs represent about 37% of the workforce at the $2.6 billion plant that opened in 2022.
- The facility will continue operating with roughly 1,600 workers.
- The plant was a key supplier for the Ford F-150 Lightning.
- Ford canceled the fully electric version of the pickup in favor of an extended-range alternative.
- Ford CEO Jim Farley warned late last year that EV demand could slow due to the removal of EV credits and other factors.
- EV demand is growing but far more slowly than manufacturers once projected.
- Changing federal policy and the removal of consumer EV incentives have made the market environment less predictable for battery makers and automakers.
- Consumer enthusiasm for EVs is softening compared with aggressive expectations set earlier in the decade.
- Range anxiety, charging infrastructure limitations, and higher upfront costs remain key concerns for buyers.
- EVs accounted for roughly 8% of U.S. new vehicle sales in 2025, essentially unchanged from the previous year.
- Slower demand directly affects production volumes for suppliers like SK Battery America.
- Battery plants were built to support aggressive EV rollouts that many automakers have now delayed.
- Parts of the supply chain are temporarily oversized for current demand.
- Some manufacturers have reduced their raw material commitments amid revised EV production plans.
- Ford recently scaled back a lithium supply agreement.
- Several major automakers have collectively written off more than $55 billion after overestimating near-term EV demand.
- The write-downs reflect canceled projects, delayed factories, and investments that will take longer than expected to pay off.
- A number of planned EV models ranging from mainstream crossovers to luxury performance models have been scrapped entirely before reaching production.
- Companies are redirecting resources toward hybrids and plug-in hybrids as electric vehicles disappear from product roadmaps.
- Some analysts argue the industry is transitioning into a more realistic growth phase after years of overly optimistic projections.
- The market is adjusting to realities of consumer hesitation, infrastructure limitations, and financial challenges associated with scaling new technologies.
- Rising geopolitical tensions and the possibility of disruptions to global oil supply have raised concerns about future fuel prices.
- If gasoline costs spike again, electric vehicles could regain momentum faster than expected.
