# US9362546B1 - Thin film lithium conducting powder material deposition from flux

## Facts

- The invention is directed to battery technologies and processing techniques for solid-state lithium-ion batteries.
- All solid state lithium ion batteries have applications in electric cars and energy storage for solar cells.
- Ceramic electrolyte powder material is mixed with two or more flux materials at a temperature of less than 400° C. to form a fluxed powder material.
- The fluxed powder material is shaped and heated again at a temperature less than 1100° C. to form a dense lithium conducting material.
- The method deposits lithium conducting electrolyte materials with garnet, perovskite, NASICON, and LISICON structures.
- The ceramic powder material has a median particle size of about 100 nm to 10 um.
- The first flux material comprises lithium material and has a melting temperature of about 500-1000° C.
- A second flux material is mixed with the first to form a eutectic mixture with a melting point of less than 1000° C.
- The eutectic mixture is subjected to a temperature of about 100 to 1000° C.
- The resulting dense lithium conducting material has a second density of at least 80% of the theoretical crystalline density.
- The solid electrolyte can be deposited upon a substrate at relatively low temperatures, translating to low manufacturing cost and high efficiency.
- Solid electrolytes according to the invention have high stability to water, air, lithium metal anodes, and cathode potentials greater than 5V.
- Existing batteries with liquid electrolytes form SEI layers that limit cycle life and do not allow safe usage of Li metal anodes.
- Batteries with polymer electrolytes cannot be reliably deposited at less than 50 μm thickness and lack sufficient Li+ conductivity at <60° C.
- LiPON type batteries have low Li+ conductivity at reasonable temperatures and require expensive PVD processes.
- Sulfide solid electrolytes are unstable to air, water, and/or Li anodes and typically cannot be deposited as thin films.
- Many oxide solid electrolytes have low Li+ conductivity at reasonable temperatures.
- Lithium Garnet materials are a notable exception for stability and conductivity.
- Compositions similar to Li7La3Zr2O12 and doped compositions such as Li7-xLa3Zr2-xMxO12 (where M can be Nb or Ta) have high conductivity.
- The invention discloses a method of depositing a thin film of lithium conducting garnet LiALaBM′CM″DZrEOF, where 4<A<8.5, 1.5<B<4, 0<C<2, 0<D<2, 0<E<2, 10<F<14.
- M′ and M″ are selected from Al, Mo, W, Nb, Sb, Ca, Ba, Sr, Ce, Hf, Rb, and Ta.
- Traditional solid state reaction processes require repeated calcination at high reaction temperatures typically greater than 800° C. for >6 hours.
- Garnet calcined products usually require even higher temperatures (>1000° C.) for long times (>20 hrs) for sintering.
- The flux growth method prepares a slurry of electrolyte and flux powders.
- Viable powdered oxide components for garnet material include LiOH, La2O3, and ZrO2.
- Flux component salts may include LiOH, Li2CO3, Li3BO3, LiCl, LiBr, and/or LiI.
- In various embodiments, the flux comprises two types of flux powders: one from LiOH, LiCl, LiBr, LiNO3, LiSO4 and another from NaOH, NaCl, NaNO3, NaSO4, NaBr, Na2CO3.
- A third flux material comprising KOH, KCl, KNO3, KSO4, KBr, and/or K2CO can be used.
- Individual flux materials typically have a melting point of about 700° C.
- Mixing two flux materials with a eutectic melting point can achieve a lower melting temperature of about 200˜400° C.
- Example eutectic solvent mixtures include 0.3LiOH-0.7NaOH (m.p.=251° C.) and 0.29LiOH-0.71KOH (m.p.=227° C.).
- The ratio of flux powder to electrolyte powders can be about 2:1, 3:1, 6:1, or other ratios.
- Electrolyte and flux powders can be mixed using ball milling in an inert liquid such as ethanol, isopropanol, or toluene.
- The mixture is cast onto a carrier substrate which can serve as the anode current collector.
- The carrier substrate can be a copper, nickel, or stainless steel foil of <25 um thickness, or polymer material with nickel and/or copper coating.
- Flux materials are heated above their melting point to form a liquid phase that fills space between ceramic electrolyte powders.
- Dissolved ceramic electrolyte powers form a completely molten layer on the substrate.
- Nucleation of desired electrolyte phases occurs, initiated by gently cooling the system.
- Crystallites continue to grow and coalesce, forming a continuous layer while excess molten flux is pushed out.
- The system is quenched to room temperature and excess flux is washed away using solvents like water, ethanol, isopropanol, acetone, or acetonitrile.
- Flux may also be chemically etched by acids such as HCl or bases such as NH4OH.
- The resulting continuous dense polycrystalline ceramic layer has a thickness between about 100 nm and 100 um, preferably 1-10 um.
- The growth method may generate a structure with few grain boundaries in the plane of the film to avoid reducing total conductivity.
- For flux sintering, pre-synthesized electrolyte powders can be used instead of raw components.
- Pre-synthesized electrolyte ceramic powders can be prepared by conventional high temperature reaction followed by milling to reduce particle size to around 1 micron.
- Example preparation: 100 gms of pre-synthesized Li7La3Zr2O12 electrolyte powder is prepared by mixing 31.03 gms of Li2CO3, 58.65 gms of La2O3, and 29.57 gms of ZrO2 by ball milling in isopropanol for 24 hrs.
- The dried mixture is calcined at 900° C. for 12 hrs, and then 1100° C. for 12 hrs to form the garnet phase.
- The calcined powder is re-milled in isopropanol to reduce average particle size to 1 um.
- The synthesized electrolyte powder is mixed with flux components and liquid to form a slurry for film casting.
- The slurry is cast onto a substrate to form a film of thickness between 200 nm-100 um.
- The dried slurry can be heated at a rate of 1° C./min to a temperature of 400° C. and held for 6 hrs.
- Liquid phase sintering occurs where ceramic electrolyte particles dissolve at high energy contact points and re-precipitate at lower energy vertices, resulting in densification and coarsening.
- Densification can result in a reduction in volume of as much as 5-20%.
- Excess flux material is expelled to the surface of the film and can be washed away or trapped at triple points within the ceramic structure.
- A two-component battery structure has cathode particle elements coated on both sides of a cathode current collector.
- Cathode material may comprise a mixture of active cathode material LiFePO4, conductive additive materials like carbon black, and presynthesized Li7La3Zr2O12 material.
- Cathode layers thickness are between 1 um and 1000 um, preferably 100 um.
- Cathode current collector element thickness is preferably less than 20 um.
- Anode current collector is coated on both sides by electrolyte elements.
- Garnet precursor material, flux material, and optionally solvent, dispersant, and/or binder may be coated onto a substrate like nickel, copper, or stainless steel foil.
- Coating techniques include screen printing, slot-die, gravure, microgravure, doctor blade, knife-over-roll, comma coating, reverse comma coating.
- The coating is dried, calendered, and sintered to produce a dense garnet film on one or both sides of the substrate.
- A one-component battery structure positions the cathode current collector between cathode particle elements, with the cathode element interfacing with the electrolyte.
- Molten mixture of flux and electrolyte powder can be deposited onto substrates transported by rollers for fast manufacturing compatible with existing tools.
- Substrates can be dipped into a molten mixture to deposit material on both sides simultaneously.
- A slot-casting process provides an electrolyte mixture within a cavity, comprising dense electrolyte material optionally including polymer material.
