# Hyundai Motor Company

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-18

**Source:** [Hyundai announces new investment at Georgia plant, weeks after ICE raid | Georgia Public Broadcasting](https://www.gpb.org/news/2025/09/18/hyundai-announces-new-investment-at-georgia-plant-weeks-after-ice-raid)  ·  `page_009516`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Company is planning to add production capacity at its sprawling manufacturing site in Southeast Georgia.
- The company announced a $2.7 billion investment that promises to create 3,000 more jobs.
- The announcement was made on Thursday as part of what Hyundai called its "most ambitious growth strategy" ahead of an investor meeting in New York.
- Gov. Brian Kemp congratulated Hyundai on the announcement via social media, writing that it reinforces Hyundai’s "commitment to this project and our state."
- Savannah Mayor Van Johnson wrote on social media that the project "will bring new opportunities for our workforce, bolster our infrastructure, and strengthen Savannah’s position as a hub for advanced manufacturing."
- The investment announcement comes weeks after a massive immigration raid at the site that rounded up hundreds of workers.
- The immigration raid caused diplomatic tension between the U.S. and South Korea.
- The raid delayed completion of a Hyundai-LG Energy Solutions battery factory.
- Didi Caldwell, president and CEO of Global Location Strategies, discussed potential business fallout from the raid with GPB's Orlando Montoya before the announcement.
- South Korea's president stated last week that companies from his country might be "more hesitant" to invest in the U.S. if they fear immigration-related difficulties.
- Georgia has key economic ties to Korean firms including Hyundai, SK Battery, and Qcells.
- Didi Caldwell noted that investments are made after careful consideration and that the raid at the Hyundai plant outside Savannah undermines trust in the U.S. administration having companies' best interests at heart.
- The U.S. faces a shortage of skilled labor and a bureaucratic visa system, leading some overseas firms to send workers on short-term visas that often result in overstays and improperly documented workforces.
- Caldwell stated she is unaware of anyone trying to circumvent immigration laws but emphasized the necessity for specialized technicians and engineers to transfer institutional knowledge to the U.S. to replicate successful operations.
- Caldwell suggested it would be in everyone's best interest for the U.S. government and companies to collaborate on a path that allows knowledge transfer while employing people efficiently and law-abidingly.
- Labor availability and skills vary across Georgia, but the state generally fares well compared to the rest of the U.S., which faces shortages in construction and operations, particularly maintenance and mechatronics.
- Caldwell, who has 27 years of experience, noted that Southern labor rates have normalized as the region moves up the value chain.
- Georgia's advantages include being a right-to-work state with friendly labor management relations, a strong technical college system, and a state incentive program called Quick Start for recruiting and training workers.
- Advanced technology factories in Georgia require highly trained engineers, equipment installers, and construction workers skilled in concrete pouring, carpentry, and pipe fitting.
- Approximately 40% of U.S. construction workers are immigrants with varying documentation levels, and fear of ICE detention is exacerbating labor shortages by causing documented and undocumented workers to miss work or flee due to raid rumors.
- Companies prioritize certainty in investment and immigration policy, as current chaos surrounding immigration, tariffs, monetary policy, and fiscal policy forces increased scenario planning.
- The gap between best-case and worst-case business scenarios has grown by an order of magnitude due to this uncertainty.
