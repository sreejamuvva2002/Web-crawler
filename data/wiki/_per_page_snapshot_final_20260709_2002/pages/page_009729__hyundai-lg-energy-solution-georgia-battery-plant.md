# Hyundai-LG Energy Solution Georgia Battery Plant

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-11

**Source:** [Hyundai battery plant faces at least 2-3 month startup delay following raid, CEO says | CNN Business](https://edition.cnn.com/2025/09/11/business/hyundai-raid-plant-delay-ceo)  ·  `page_009729`

## Facts

- A battery plant co-owned by Hyundai is facing a minimum startup delay of two to three months following an immigration raid last week, according to Hyundai CEO Jose Munoz.
- The Georgia plant is operated through a joint venture between Hyundai and South Korea’s LG Energy Solution.
- The plant was at the center of the largest single-site enforcement operation in the U.S. Department of Homeland Security’s history last week.
- The plant is part of a $7.6 billion factory complex to make battery-powered models.
- The plant was slated to come online later this year (2025).
- Hyundai CEO Jose Munoz said he was surprised when he heard the news and immediately inquired if Hyundai workers were involved.
- The company discovered that the workers at the center of the raid were mainly employed by suppliers of LG.
- About 475 workers, mostly South Korean nationals, were arrested, according to U.S. immigration officials.
- It is typical for an automotive battery plant to employ these workers as it is getting off the ground, according to Munoz.
- For the construction phase of plants, specialized people are needed because there are a lot of skills and equipment that cannot be found in the United States, according to Munoz.
- Hyundai will source batteries from other plants while waiting for the LG plant to start up, including from a Georgia plant co-owned with Korean battery-maker SK On.
- Fallout from the raid has cascaded across the country, with Reuters reporting that workers at other LG plants, including those co-owned by GM, were asked to return home.
