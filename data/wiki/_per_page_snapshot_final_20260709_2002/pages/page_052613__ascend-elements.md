## Facts

- Poland's Ministry of Economic Development and Technology offered Ascend Elements up to 1.22 billion Polish zloty (approximately €290 million or $320 million) in grant funding.
- This represents one of the largest grants ever awarded by the Polish government.
- The financial incentive aims to support the construction of an advanced production facility for recycled cathode materials in Poland.
- The investment aligns with the European Union's broader goals to develop domestic battery supply chains and reduce dependency on imported materials, particularly from Asia.
- Poland's investment directly supports the EU Battery Regulation (2023/1542), which mandates that by 2030, new batteries must contain 12% recycled cobalt, 4% recycled lithium, and 4% recycled nickel.
- Pawel Pudłowski, Vice President of the Management Board at the Polish Investment and Trade Agency (PAIH), noted: "This project strengthens Poland's role in the European battery supply chain and supports regional economic development."
- According to the European Commission's Critical Raw Materials Act, the EU aims to source 10-40% of its raw materials from recycling by 2030.
- Poland currently accounts for approximately 3% of EU battery production (Eurostat, 2024).
- The country leverages EU funding mechanisms like the Just Transition Fund to build recycling infrastructure.
- Northvolt's €1 billion Swedish recycling plant became operational in 2023.
- The Bruegel Institute's 2024 analysis noted: "Poland's investment in battery recycling technology addresses a critical vulnerability in Europe's strategic autonomy plans."
- Ascend Elements is a U.S.-based company specializing in innovative battery recycling technologies that transform end-of-life lithium-ion batteries into high-value materials for new battery production.
- The company plans to implement its proprietary Hydro-to-Cathode™ technology in Poland—a direct synthesis process (patent US2022156789) that creates new precursor cathode active material (pCAM) from recycled lithium-ion cells.
- This breakthrough process recovers more than 95% of critical metals from spent batteries (Ascend Elements, 2024).
- The technology bypasses traditional intermediate processing steps, directly converting battery black mass to pCAM containing nickel, manganese, and cobalt.
- This streamlined approach reduces energy consumption and produces 70% lower CO₂ emissions per kilogram of NMC material compared to traditional mining operations (Circular Energy Storage, 2023).
- Ascend Elements has established operations in Poland through a joint venture with Polish company Elemental Strategic Metals.
- The partners currently operate a battery recycling facility in Zawiercie that processes over 10,000 tons of battery waste annually.
- Tomasz Poznar, Ph.D., Senior Vice President of Commercial at Ascend Elements, explained: "Our technology helps bridge the growing gap in cathode precursor supply and contributes to the EU's strategic net-zero goals."
- The new plant would be independently owned and operated by Ascend Elements, representing a significant expansion of their European presence beyond the Zawiercie joint venture.
- While specific job figures for the Polish facility haven't been announced, similar battery recycling operations in Europe typically create 300+ direct jobs.
- The project is expected to foster technology development through collaboration with local educational institutions, potentially supporting Poland's "Electromobility 2025" workforce development program.
- Linh Austin, President and CEO of Ascend Elements, stated: "We're committed to building a sustainable supply chain for battery materials on both sides of the Atlantic."
- The facility aims to address Europe's growing cathode precursor supply deficit, which according to Benchmark Mineral Intelligence (2024) will reach approximately 500,000 tons per year by 2030.
- Research from Fraunhofer ISI (2023) indicates that recycling lithium-ion batteries reduces CO₂ emissions by approximately 50% compared to using virgin mined materials.
- Umicore's Belgian recycling plant already supplies approximately 20% of the EU's cathode material needs.
- Ascend Elements currently operates a large battery recycling plant in Covington, Georgia, which processes approximately 30,000 metric tons of lithium-ion waste annually.
- Construction of its planned hydro-to-cathode production facility in Hopkinsville, Kentucky—designed for 60,000 tons per year capacity—has been temporarily paused, with operations now scheduled to begin in late 2026.
- In February 2025, Ascend Elements agreed with the U.S. Department of Energy to cancel a $164 million grant for cathode active material (CAM) production at its Kentucky plant.
- The Polish grant provides direct upfront funding, while the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act offers tax credits of up to $35 per kilowatt-hour for battery materials.
- The global battery recycling landscape is projected to reach $33.6 billion by 2030 (Grand View Research, 2024).
- Eric Gratz, co-founder and chief technical officer at Ascend Elements, noted that U.S. tariffs could benefit domestic battery recycling operations: "Unlike battery manufacturers relying on virgin materials that would be subject to import tariffs, recyclers using domestic materials would be less impacted."
- The U.S. has imposed 25% tariffs on Chinese lithium products (USTR, 2024).
