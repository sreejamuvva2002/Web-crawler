# Georgia Manufacturing Industry

**Type:** unknown  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** 2020-12-31

**Source:** [Georgia Rises as a Manufacturing Magnet - Georgia Trend Magazine](https://www.georgiatrend.com/2020/12/31/manufacturing-magnet/)  ·  `page_008248`

## Facts

- Manufacturing is hitting its stride in Georgia with nearly weekly announcements of new or expanding facilities from big names.
- Aerospace companies in Georgia include Gulfstream Aerospace Corp, Lockheed Martin Aeronautics, and Pratt & Whitney.
- A roster lists about 800 aerospace and manufacturing companies in the state.
- Floor covering heavyweights in Georgia include Shaw Industries Group, Mohawk Industries, and Milliken & Co.
- Automotive giants operating in Georgia include Kia Motors Manufacturing Georgia and Yamaha Motor Manufacturing Corp.
- Auto parts suppliers joining Georgia include newcomers SK Innovation and GEDIA Automotive Group, which will make electric vehicle components.
- Georgia is a national leader in the advanced manufacturing of machinery, electrical equipment, and fabricated metal products.
- Georgia outpaces overall U.S. manufacturing in 10-year gross domestic product growth.
- The state has more than 9,400 manufacturing operations accounting for a $61.1 billion pre-pandemic output, according to the Georgia Department of Economic Development (GDEcD).
- Georgia's manufacturing workforce grew to 270,000 before COVID-19 hit the state.
- Jim Reed, president of YKK Corp. of America, says Georgia is a fabulous place for manufacturers.
- YKK Corp. of America makes zippers, fasteners, and architectural products at several locations throughout Georgia.
- Jim Reed cites available workforce, strong logistical capabilities including highways, port, and airport, and friendly local/state government as unique benefits.
- Gunnar Kleveland is president and CEO of Textron Specialized Vehicles, makers of E-Z-GO golf cars in Augusta.
- Gunnar Kleveland notes the state brought big names like IKEA, Caterpillar, and Starbucks to Augusta.
- Gunnar Kleveland says companies like Textron have been in Georgia for many years, performing well, and will stay because it is a good place to do business.
- Hurst Boiler and Welding Co. builds and manufactures steam and hot water boiler systems in Thomas County.
- Jeff Hurst is vice president of marketing at Hurst Boiler and son of the company's founder.
- Hurst Boiler took advantage of Georgia job tax credits and Georgia port tax credits.
- Hurst Boiler retains tax credits by shipping as much as possible through Georgia ports.
- Hurst Boiler found a good workforce in Thomas, Colquitt, and Brooks counties.
- Sara Irvani is CEO of Buford-based shoe manufacturer Okabashi.
- Sara Irvani emphasizes that culture and ecosystem make the difference for manufacturers in Georgia.
- Regina Maddox is executive director of state trade association Next Generation Manufacturing.
- Regina Maddox notes Georgia has been No. 1 for business for seven years in a row, now eight.
- Access to a skilled workforce is among manufacturers' top priorities.
- The Technical College System of Georgia (TCSG) operates through 22 colleges and dual enrollment programs with high schools statewide.
- TCSG offers 600 programs, many geared toward manufacturing skills, with a 99% job placement rate.
- TCSG's Quick Start program delivers customized training free of charge to qualifying existing, expanding, and new businesses.
- Each TCSG college partners with local companies to prepare workers for current and future needs.
- Scott McMurray is GDEcD Deputy Commissioner of Global Commerce.
- Scott McMurray says jobs for school graduates represent hope and loves seeing schools partner with manufacturers in local communities.
- Companies often donate equipment to technical colleges so students can train on actual job tools.
- Gunnar Kleveland has run factories internationally and around the U.S., calling Georgia's workforce phenomenal.
- Textron Specialized Vehicles has a strong partnership with Augusta Technical College.
- Textron Specialized Vehicles employs more than 1,350 people in Richmond County.
- Textron Specialized Vehicles builds around 100,000 golf cars every year.
- Textron Specialized Vehicles benefited from Georgia Quick Start.
- Textron is excited about RPM (Reaching Potential Through Manufacturing) with Richmond County Schools.
- RPM brings students at risk of failing to earn a diploma into the program to finish high school while working on Textron products.
- More than 200 students have graduated from RPM, and 70 continue to work at Textron.
- Scott McMurray says companies need to see available talent and a growing talent pipeline as they expand.
- Many Georgia manufacturing businesses are focusing on sustainable operations and renewable energy solutions.
- This includes floor covering titans meeting sustainability goals, growing EV-component companies, and alternative energy market producers.
- Hurst Boiler has been manufacturing boilers for the energy generation process since 1967.
- Global customers for Hurst Boiler range from oil and gas power companies to distilleries using wood, biomass, solid waste, and hybrid fuel.
- Lumber mills in southern Georgia and wood pellet producers rely on Hurst Boiler's biomass unit to burn wood as an alternative to fossil fuels.
- Hurst says biomass is an upbeat trend to help customers reduce carbon, emissions, and operating and fuel costs.
- Hurst Boiler builds systems that burn agave waste generated by tequila distillers in Mexico.
- Tequila distillers use agave waste to burn as fuel in boiler systems because their electricity is intermittent.
- The boiler turns a turbine system to produce electricity for the distilleries, allowing them to keep electricity running continuously while distilling.
- Jim Reed says YKK finalized a 2050 plan calling for reducing carbon dioxide emissions by half in the next 10 years.
- YKK's 2050 plan requires its indirect supply chain to reduce CO2 emissions by 30%.
- By 2050, YKK will be carbon neutral.
- Jim Reed says the apparel industries are in a major shift toward the circular economy.
- This shift allows companies to rethink products, materials, lifecycle, and make them recyclable, repairable, and made of different materials.
- Okabashi's three brands of comfort shoes have been sustainably made since the business began in 1984.
- Okabashi's facility is 100,000 square feet.
- Okabashi employs 200 people.
- Okabashi produces just under 2 million pairs of shoes a year through a closed-loop manufacturing process.
- Okabashi shoes are made from recyclable material.
- Okabashi customers can return old shoes for a 15% discount on their next purchase.
- Sara Irvani says Okabashi is sustainably made in the USA and has seen an incredible resurgence in appreciation for U.S.-made products by customers.
- The pandemic caused many companies to consider advantages of U.S. manufacturing and supply chains.
- GDEcD's McMurray says more companies are looking to bring manufacturing back from overseas or expand existing U.S. operations, and look locally for suppliers to reduce long global supply chain risks.
- After the COVID-related statewide shutdown last spring, Georgia manufacturers learned to operate safely and ramped up production to pre-pandemic levels.
- Georgia had a great start to the fiscal year beginning on July 1 due to this ramp-up.
- Okabashi was back to full production by the fall.
- Sara Irvani says the pandemic highlighted local manufacturing importance, allowing partners to respond in days or weeks instead of months.
- Okabashi uses a local supplier base that was agile and worked in harmony during the pandemic without major supply chain shifts.
- YKK was considered essential and remained open despite the pandemic due to customers including the U.S. Department of Defense, Homeland Security, and healthcare industries.
- YKK provided zippers and fasteners for hazmat suits, trim for disposable protective gowns, and components for airtight, watertight, chemical-, and fire-resistant suits.
- YKK responded to high demand for these products.
- YKK's airtight, watertight zippers were critical for maintaining negative pressure in temporary isolation chambers for COVID-19 patients.
- YKK provided elastic to companies that pivoted to making face masks during a national shortage last year.
- YKK's rivets were used to repair about 1,400 N95 masks in short supply nationwide for the Lawrenceburg, Ky., Department of Health.
- The repaired masks were put to use by the health department.
- The pandemic created an unexpected boon for fitness and outdoor products, including golf-related items.
- Textron was surprised by increased interest in golf cars and recreational vehicles.
- Gunnar Kleveland says demand for golf did not subside because people can be outside and socially distance.
- Textron struggled to keep up with demand, went from shutdown to slow startup to full capacity, and still faces supply chain issues.
- Regina Maddox says the pandemic spurred innovation among manufacturers.
- Companies that innovated added new production lines, pivoted well, and will continue to do so.
- Before the coronavirus, GDEcD launched its Georgia Made initiative in February to recognize and support manufacturers across the state.
- Georgia Made is a certification program for products that are at least 50% manufactured in Georgia, excluding food and agricultural items.
- Similar to the state Department of Agriculture’s Georgia Grown program, Georgia Made aims to convey quality and pride and provide resources.
- Companies completing GDEcD's certification process can display the Georgia Made logo on products.
- Scott McMurray says a made-in-the-USA label is a real symbol of quality internationally, and they want the Georgia Made logo to have similar recognition in the U.S. and globally.
- The program will help participating companies connect with centers of innovation and educational institutions around the state.
- Educational institutions' cutting-edge research will help manufacturers solve production issues or find better building methods.
- GDEcD’s international trade division helps Georgia Made participants find export markets abroad, as it did for Hurst Boiler.
- McMurray describes the program as a full-spectrum resource to help companies grow and succeed.
- Along with Hurst Boiler, YKK was one of the first companies to sign up for the Georgia Made program.
- The program had about 20 participants at the end of 2020.
- Jim Reed says promoting Georgia as a manufacturing center benefits everyone, comparing it to Detroit's auto industry brand and Silicon Valley's tech brand.
- More successful manufacturing in Georgia will attract more people and benefit communities.
