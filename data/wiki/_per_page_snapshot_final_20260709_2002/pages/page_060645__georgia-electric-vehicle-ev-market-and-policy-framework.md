# Georgia Electric Vehicle (EV) Market and Policy Framework

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2015-02-11

**Source:** [#cleanair | Atlanta Electric Vehicle Development Coalition](https://atlantaevdc.com/category/cleanair/)  ·  `page_060645`

## Facts

- Union of Concerned Scientists released a statement about the benefits of driving on electricity to the State of Georgia.
- In 2014 alone, Georgia drivers saved 4.5 million gallons of gasoline thanks to electric vehicles.
- This savings provided an extra $10 million to put back into the local economy.
- A UCS analysis shows driving 100 miles in the average conventional vehicle costs a Georgia driver $13.57.
- Driving those same 100 miles in an electric vehicle costs $3.53 or less.
- The UCS analysis emphasizes the importance of forward-thinking policies to expand EV access in Georgia.
- Georgia is a national leader in electric vehicles due to strong state policies.
- Georgia has the second-highest number of electric vehicles on the road in the country.
- Atlanta passed Seattle to become the second-biggest metro area for electric vehicles.
- Joshua Goldman, lead policy analyst for UCS, states EVs are cheaper to fuel, cleaner to operate, and benefit Georgia drivers.
- Goldman notes Georgia has been a pioneer in putting electric cars on the road.
- Electricity rates in Georgia have been relatively stable for years while gas prices rise and fall.
- Electric vehicles protect drivers from future changes in gas prices.
- Anne Blair, Clean Fuels Director for Southern Alliance for Clean Energy, expresses encouragement over EV progress.
- Blair hopes Georgia leaders recognize the importance of EVs and what a standout Georgia is on adopting them.
- Georgia has thousands of electric vehicles on the road but still has room for growth.
- Policies encouraging consumers to choose EVs will continue to pay dividends for Georgians.
- The $5,000 ZEV Georgia State Income Tax Credit faced pressure to be killed, saved, or transformed.
- All three options were debated at the State Capitol during the 2015 Georgia State Legislative Session.
- HB 176 sponsored by Tommy Benton (R-Jefferson) seeks to broaden the tax credit to include gas-hybrids, high mileage cars, and future hydrogen Fuel Cell Vehicles.
- The annual cap for HB 176 is $10 million.
- A $2,000 credit only applies to Hydrogen Fuel Cell vehicles in 2017 and beyond under HB 176.
- The House Transportation Committee passed HB 170 bill which includes language from HB 122 to eliminate the EV Tax Credit as part of a transportation funding proposal.
- HB 170 also assesses EV drivers a $200.00/year road use fee.
- All three bills in the Ways and Means Committee were expected to be addressed next week.
- 2014 smashed the 100,000 annual unit sales mark for EVs in November.
- More new EV models launched in 2014 than in the prior 3 years combined.
- Electrified vehicles were expected to reach 1% of vehicles sold nationally in 2015.
- Projections estimated 165-170,000 electrified vehicles added to US roads in 2015 based on total industry sales of 16.5-17.0 million.
- Many states added electric vehicle or charging station incentives.
- Expanded charging infrastructure and high satisfaction rates among EV owners should propel EV sales growth close to 1%.
- Nissan LEAF approaches the 200,000 Federal Tax Credit phase out threshold.
- Nissan launched the all-electric LEAF in the US in 2011 and did the best job marketing it.
- By the end of 2015, cumulative LEAF sales were close to 170,000, just 30,000 units shy of phase out requirements.
- An all-new vehicle was slated to launch in the 2017 Model Year, on sale as early as January 2016.
- Carlos Ghosn had a plan for marketing the all-new LEAF without its $7,500 tax credit.
- Tesla was expected to FINALLY launch the Model X with Falcon Wing Doors in 2015.
- Elon Musk was expected to announce first deliveries of the 2015 Model X on the Tesla blog around Merry Christmas 2015.
- The Model X targets the high end 6-7 passenger SUV market and will begin to impact Tesla sales in 2016.
- The Model X attracts new buyers to the Tesla marque without hurting Model S sales, as most owners already traded up to the Model D.
- Savvy used car buyers were expected to snap up discarded Model S 1.0 offerings at reasonable ($50,000 – $60,000) prices.
- Elon Musk promised a future battery upgrade for the Model S.
- Software upgrades continue for the Model S.
- Chevrolet VOLT 2.0 was expected to be a lower cost version than VOLT 1.0.
- Former GM CEO Dan Akerson pushed to get $10,000 of cost out of the VOLT for the 2.0 version.
- The 2015 Chevrolet Cruze provided an idea of what the 2016 VOLT would look like: compact.
- Range may improve modestly to 45-50 electric miles.
- The vehicle was expected to have a $29,995 base price with useable gauges and a 3 person “Cruze Sized” rear seat.
- Unveiling was expected at the NAIAS the week of January 12, 2015.
- There was hope for using more than 60% of the 17.1kWh battery.
- Georgia and metro Atlanta garnered headlines in 2014 as the fastest growing EV market in the US.
- The handling of the current ZEV/LEV tax credit (currently $5,000/$2,500 with no sunset) was expected to become a national story.
- Nissan was expected to have its best sales month ever in December 2014 for the LEAF given metro Atlanta dealers were selling/leasing against fear that the $5,000 ZEV tax credit would disappear in 2015.
- Metro Atlanta/Georgia needs more EVs on roads to help get annual CO2 emissions well below the 150 million metric tons emitted in Georgia.
- "PV2EV" (tying EV charging to renewable power generation) was expected to have its day in the sun in 2015.
- Separate and uncoordinated incentives between solar power and EV charging station infrastructure kept these technologies apart for years.
- 2015 might see enough solar powered charging stations built to move the needle toward sustainable PV2EV deployment.
- Solar power costs were falling while the cost to retrofit parking lots and garages with EV charging stations remained expensive.
- Georgia was expected to become a leader in electric vehicle charging infrastructure.
- NRG pushed into the metro Atlanta market from its home base in Houston TX.
- Georgia Power announced construction of its own charging islands.
- Both firms offered DC Fast Charge and 240 V Level 2 charging.
- Metro Atlanta was expected to become a model for deployment of fast and convenient EV charging by the end of 2015.
- Metro Atlanta was expected to have at least 50 charging islands including installations in Athens and other outlying cities by the end of 2015.
- Public charging stations funded by GEFA were expected to improve EV charging station availability in the Atlanta suburbs.
- A legislator asked what Georgia received for the almost $15 million invested in electric cars via the tax credit.
- The response may determine whether Georgia’s $5000 ZEV income tax credit lives or dies.
- According to the Georgia Department of Economic Development, for every one percent of petroleum-based miles travelled in Georgia displaced by EVs, approximately $201 million dollars remains in the state annually.
- Each pure electric vehicle purchased keeps $2,242 annually in the state of Georgia by fueling with electricity rather than petroleum-based products.
- The tax credit received comes back as a refund after filing taxes and gets spent on clothes, appliances, and services in Georgia, creating a multiplier effect.
- Georgia Power set up a special tariff called Plug In Electric Vehicle Time of Use Rate Plan to encourage overnight charging when power is super cheap and plentiful.
- A study of 1000 Georgia electric car owners on the PEV rate plan showed they save $180 per year by shifting usage to the evening and overnight period.
- This helps utilize extra electric capacity available overnight.
- During the day, electric vehicles and equipment are quiet, clean, and efficient, offering users opportunities to save money on fuel and maintenance costs and reduce environmental impact.
- Atlanta was out of compliance with the EPA rule, and the metro area needed help to attain the standard and save everyone the cost of an emission sticker.
- Gasoline or diesel engines deteriorate over time, leading to higher emissions with vehicle age, whereas EVs potentially get cleaner over time as electricity generation gets cleaner.
- Electric cars send a message to young people about regional priorities.
- Mayor Reed stated the investment sent a strong message to millennials and makes Atlanta a more livable city drawing talented young people back to live and work.
- He likened it to the Beltline and other quality of life projects.
- Georgia is the 2nd largest market in the US for all EVs behind Nissan's success with the LEAF.
- BMW, Kia, and many other manufacturers are coming with electric cars.
- The recommended message to the legislature was to hold off for another year before taking action on the tax credit.
- If eliminating the credit, it should be done slowly and phased out over the next decade.
- Georgia's business climate is great in part because it avoids knee-jerk regulations causing uncertainty and confusion in the marketplace.
