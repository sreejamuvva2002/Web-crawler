# QCells Cartersville Facility

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [For years America built solar farms with foreign parts, but a giant Georgia megafactory just changed that calculation](https://energiesmedia.com/georgia-solar-cell-manufacturing-energies/)  ·  `page_001572`

## Facts

- The U.S. has installed more solar capacity than any other type of power generation for five years running.
- Solar cells, which convert sunlight into electricity, have been manufactured almost entirely overseas during the U.S. solar boom.
- American factories could assemble finished panels, but the critical cell component came from elsewhere.
- QCells operates a sprawling, L-shaped factory in Cartersville, Georgia.
- The facility is closing a long-standing gap in domestic solar cell manufacturing.
- By the time the Cartersville factory came online, U.S. panel assembly capacity could produce nearly 70 gigawatts of finished panels annually.
- This 70 GW panel assembly capacity far exceeds what the country installs in a single year, according to the Solar Energy Industries Association.
- Before QCells began production, only three U.S. companies made solar cells: Suniva in Georgia, and ES Foundry and Silfab in South Carolina.
- The combined operational capacity of those three pre-QCells U.S. cell makers was roughly 3 gigawatts.
- Cell manufacturing remained the weak link in an otherwise expanding domestic supply chain.
- The Biden administration recognized this gap and used the Inflation Reduction Act to introduce manufacturing tax credits for domestic cell producers.
- The Inflation Reduction Act also introduced domestic-content bonuses for developers who buy American-made components.
- These policies were explicitly designed to pull cell production back to the U.S. after ceding ground to overseas competitors, particularly China.
- QCells is a subsidiary of South Korean industrial giant Hanwha Group.
- QCells first announced the Cartersville project in early 2023.
- The company pledged to colocate all four major steps of the solar supply chain under one roof: silicon ingots, wafers, cells, and finished modules.
- The facility’s module lines went live in 2024.
- Cell production lines took longer to calibrate and have only recently begun commercial output.
- Full 3.3-gigawatt production is expected by the third quarter of this year.
- When full capacity is reached, QCells alone will more than double current U.S. operational solar cell capacity.
- Polysilicon enters at one end of the facility and moves sequentially through each production stage.
- Finished panels roll off the line at the other end of the facility.
- Combined with a module assembly plant roughly 30 miles north in Dalton, Georgia, the broader operation is expected to employ 3,800 people.
- These roles are described as high-tech, robot-assisted manufacturing positions.
- Cell manufacturing is more complex than panel assembly due to the use of potent chemicals like hydrofluoric acid and potassium hydroxide.
- Creating functional solar cells requires precise, multi-step industrial processes.
- An industry figure noted that leveraging complicated science to create a solar cell involves more intensive steps than panel assembly.
- In March, Silfab temporarily shut down its South Carolina cell factory after accidentally releasing both potassium hydroxide and hydrofluoric acid in rapid succession.
- State authorities found no impact to the surrounding community from the Silfab chemical release incident.
- The incident illustrated the risks when chemical processes go wrong in cell manufacturing.
- Another 22 gigawatts of solar cell capacity is currently under construction nationally, according to the Solar Energy Industries Association.
- Toyo recently announced a $357 million investment to add 1.5 gigawatts of cell production at its Houston facility.
- ES Foundry is working to expand its South Carolina factory to 3 gigawatts by year’s end.
- T1 Energy is building a cell fabrication plant outside Austin.
- The Trump administration phased out the installation tax credit for solar projects completed after July 4, 2025.
- A four-year safe harbor provision still applies to projects already in the pipeline.
- Once those projects are built or lapse, developers may lose their primary financial incentive to choose domestically produced cells over cheaper imports.
- Domestic production offers resilience against foreign supply chain schedule risks highlighted by Covid-era disruptions and rising shipping costs.
- American-made components delivered by truck from a nearby factory sidestep that uncertainty entirely.
- Electricity consumption in the U.S. is at its highest level in a generation.
- This demand is driven significantly by the rapid buildout of AI data centers.
- AI companies are focused on “speed to power,” the ability to bring new generation online quickly.
- Solar is well positioned to meet that need due to its relatively short installation timelines.
- Whether U.S. solar cell manufacturing can sustain momentum beyond the safe harbor window remains an open question.
- The convergence of industrial policy, AI-driven electricity demand, and supply-chain localization creates conditions that could support domestic production even as some incentives fade.
- The Cartersville factory is cited as the most visible sign yet that the U.S. is serious about owning more of this supply chain.
