# Hyundai Motor Group

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-05

**Source:** [Hyundai Metaplant Sets Benchmarks for Speed, Geotechnical Engineering - Terracon](https://www.terracon.com/2026/03/05/hyundai-metaplant-sets-benchmarks-for-speed-geotechnical-engineering/)  ·  `page_011903`

## Facts

- Hyundai Motor Group set out to build the largest electrified vehicle manufacturing plant in the United States.
- The facility is the only one to combine EV and battery production at the same campus.
- The project operated on an ambitious two-year schedule.
- The site is a 3,000-acre coastal location near Savannah, Georgia.
- Subsurface conditions included loose sands, soft clays, and a shallow groundwater table.
- Site conditions raised concerns about earthquake-related soil liquefaction, long-term settlement under heavy structural loads, and stability during deep excavations.
- The project scale involved fourteen buildings totaling 7.6 million square feet designed and contracted separately.
- Engineering teams worked across the U.S. and Korea.
- Every geotechnical recommendation had to balance safety, constructability, and cost control without slowing progress on one of the fastest large-scale industrial developments ever attempted.
- Terracon partnered closely with Hyundai, contractors, and design teams to reduce risk while keeping the project moving.
- The geotechnical approach emphasized rigorous site characterization, data-driven design, and close collaboration in the field.
- Comprehensive site characterization required a robust geotechnical investigation to develop accurate soil profiles, settlement predictions, and seismic site classifications.
- Investigation methods included seismic cone penetration testing (SCPT), multichannel analysis of surface waves (MASW), extensive lab testing, and 435 soil borings.
- Refined seismic analysis justified a lower liquefaction risk for the site.
- All buildings were supported on piles extending below liquefiable layers, eliminating the need for costly ground improvement programs or specialized structural designs and saving both time and money.
- Different structures required customized foundation solutions.
- Deep foundations, primarily auger-cast piles, were selected to control settlement and address liquefaction risks.
- An innovative hybrid foundation solution used displacement-type cast-in-place rigid inclusions (CGEs) in the upper sands combined with deeper auger-cast rigid inclusions for slab support to reduce pile lengths and optimize costs while maintaining performance.
- The team overcame significant challenges including site drainage limitations, groundwater control, and extreme weather events such as Hurricane Helene.
- Foundation strategies were adapted, such as using driven steel piles for excavation support, to maintain schedule and safety.
- Terracon’s proprietary Compass data-sharing platform kept stakeholders aligned, improved communication across international teams, and helped avoid costly delays.
- Less than two years after groundbreaking, the first vehicle rolled off the assembly line.
- The project was completed six months ahead of schedule.
- Terracon won state and national American Council of Engineering Companies (ACEC) awards for the project's innovation, collaboration, and engineering excellence.
- Key collaborators acknowledged included Hyundai Motor Group, Berkel & Company Contractors, Inc., Keller North America, structural and civil design teams, Georgia’s Department of Business Development, the Savannah Economic Development Authority, the Savannah Harbor-Interstate 16 Corridor Joint Development Authority, and Terracon management with dozens of engineers and technicians.
- Deep borings were performed using 10 drill rigs.
