# Community Benefits Agreement (CBA)

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Community benefits agreements can turn Southern manufacturing investments into good jobs and shared prosperity | Economic Policy Institute](https://www.epi.org/publication/community-benefits-agreements-can-turn-southern-manufacturing-investments-into-good-jobs-and-shared-prosperity/)  ·  `page_000772`

## Facts

- Major new public investments in Southern manufacturing continue to present opportunities to benefit local workers and communities.
- A long-standing Southern economic development model has historically prioritized corporate power and profits over workers and communities.
- This failed economic model is rooted in the legacies of slavery, anti-Black racism, and the suppression of worker organizing.
- The South lags all other U.S. regions on most indicators of economic health, including job growth and wages.
- Southern workers and their families experience significantly higher rates of poverty than in other parts of the country.
- Southern politicians have promoted a development model characterized by low wages, regressive taxation, lax environmental regulations, a weak social safety net, and opposition to unions to attract business.
- The automotive manufacturing industry has been a key recipient of public subsidies, receiving billions of dollars from Southern states in recent decades.
- This system of low taxation and corporate giveaways starves essential public goods like education and social safety net programs.
- Weak or nonexistent environmental regulations have contributed to toxic sites and resource degradation that disproportionately affect Black and brown families.
- Some announced manufacturing projects have been cancelled or reduced in size after the Trump administration’s slashing of federal supports for strategic industries.
- Many projects launched during the Biden administration continue to move forward despite federal support uncertainties.
- Manufacturing investments in traditional industries and nascent ones like electric vehicle (EV) and EV battery manufacturing are spurring significant job growth in some Southern communities.
- Community benefits agreements can be powerful vehicles for communities to secure lasting local economic benefits from major industrial development at new and existing facilities.
- A CBA is a legally enforceable contract between a private developer or company and a local coalition detailing how a project will benefit workers and the community.
- Benefits in a CBA can include commitments to strong labor standards, respect for workers’ rights to organize, equitable workforce recruitment, training, and hiring practices, affordable housing, environmental protections, child care, and public parks.
- CBAs mitigate risks for project developers by ensuring local project support and addressing important concerns early on.
- Failure to engage local communities can lead to strong opposition, interruption of development, obstacles to obtaining siting permits or rezoning approvals, or significant legal costs.
- In June 2024, developers shelved plans for a $1.3 billion data center in Indiana after facing significant local opposition over environmental concerns.
- Strong CBAs ensure that new industrial investments generate good manufacturing jobs that pay a living wage and expand pathways to unionization.
- The fights to secure CBA gains help forge strong, durable labor-community coalitions needed to reshape the political fabric of Southern communities.
- Between 2017 and 2023, manufacturing construction doubled in the East South Central Census division (Alabama, Kentucky, Tennessee, and Mississippi).
- The West South Central division (Arkansas, Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Texas) has the highest amount of manufacturing construction spending of any division in the U.S.
- Large federal investments through the Inflation Reduction Act, Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, and CHIPS and Science Act accelerated a long-term trend of manufacturing industries locating in the South.
- These federal investments included both direct public subsidies and tax credits to businesses investing in key clean energy manufacturing industries like batteries, electric vehicles, solar panels, and wind energy products.
- Some recent federal investments include incentives meant to encourage strong labor standards on projects receiving public funds.
- Since the third quarter of 2023, more than $125 billion worth of clean energy manufacturing investments were announced across Georgia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Kentucky, and Texas.
- Advancing even a portion of these announced projects would result in thousands of jobs for Southern workers.
- The South has retained the largest share of U.S. manufacturing employment of any region; in 2024, 35% of U.S. manufacturing employment was in the South.
- Since 2010, manufacturing employment in the South has grown by 17%, the quickest growth of any region.
- Manufacturing jobs became "good jobs" historically thanks to relatively high levels of unionization during the mid-20th century, which improved wages, benefits, and working conditions.
- Unionization in manufacturing has fallen in all regions since 1983, but the South has almost without exception had the lowest unionization rate of any region.
- Every Southern state except Maryland and Delaware has passed anti-union right-to-work laws that make it harder for workers to form, join, and sustain unions.
- Right-to-work laws suppress unionization rates and have driven down wages for both union and nonunion workers alike across the South.
- In 2025, Tennessee Republicans passed legislation prohibiting any company that enters into a CBA from receiving state economic development funds.
- This Tennessee legislation aims to discourage a coalition of West Tennessee residents and allied groups calling on Ford and SK Innovation to negotiate a CBA covering its massive BlueOval electric vehicle and battery manufacturing complex.
- Community Benefits Plans (CBPs) demonstrate how a company applying for public funds will ensure a proposed project provides benefits to workers and community members.
- Many federal agencies required companies to submit CBPs to receive certain grant funds designated by the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act or the Inflation Reduction Act.
- CBPs are not legally binding commitments but can lay important groundwork for a CBA and provide leverage for community benefits coalitions.
- Community benefits coalitions bring together multiple labor and community-based organizations representing interests of those most affected by a proposed new development.
- Project Labor Agreements (PLAs) are legally binding agreements in the construction industry that establish hiring procedures, help enforce prevailing wages, support dispute resolution, and can require contractors to hire through union hiring halls.
- Community Workforce Agreements (CWAs) are a type of PLA which include community-oriented commitments like equitable workforce development.
- Union Neutrality or Card Check agreements commit employers to remaining neutral regarding union organizing and respecting workers’ ability to use voluntary recognition options.
- Labor peace agreements involve unions agreeing not to engage in picketing or work stoppages during organizing in exchange for securing employer commitments to neutrality and card check.
- CBAs do not require government action and can shape outcomes of major projects even in contexts where state legislators have preempted local governments from establishing job quality or environmental standards.
- Cities like Detroit and Cleveland have ordinances requiring developers of projects using public resources to engage in a community benefits plan process.
- In 2005, Atlanta passed an ordinance specifying worker and community benefits for the Beltline redevelopment.
- A CBA can be negotiated alongside a PLA to ensure pathways to quality jobs for local residents during operational phases, including future expansions.
- To be successful, a CBA must include defined enforcement mechanisms that hold all parties accountable, clearly establish obligations, set metrics for measuring progress, and mandate ongoing monitoring of compliance.
- If a company or coalition fails to make good-faith efforts on commitments, an arbitration process is initiated.
- A newly established union at the project site is well-positioned to monitor CBA commitments and hold the company accountable over the long term.
- Building broad-based, durable coalitions and leverage necessary to compel private interests to engage in CBA negotiations is a challenging, long-term, resource-intensive organizing project.
- Vastly expanding the capacity of broad-based coalitions and grassroots organizations to gradually build community and worker power in Southern communities is the most essential ingredient for transforming existing power imbalances.
