# Aurubis AG

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-26

**Source:** [Germany’s Largest Investor in Georgia Gets Smelting  - Global Atlanta](https://www.globalatlanta.com/germanys-largest-investor-in-georgia-gets-smelting/)  ·  `page_017335`

## Facts

- Aurubis AG officially opened its $800 million smelting facility in Augusta, Georgia, a year ago.
- Georgia Governor Brian Kemp labeled the project “one of my favorite projects” for its strategic and economic value.
- The company held a “first melt” ceremony on Wednesday with legislators, industry leaders, local officials, and German Ambassador to the United States Jens Hanefeld.
- The plant processes scrap material ranging from copper cable to printed circuit boards using massive furnaces to produce blister copper and precious metals including gold, tin, zinc, and nickel.
- Aurubis CEO Toralf Haag described the operation as very complex with high temperatures, significant material flow, and a complex off-gas and water cooling system that had to be synchronized over the last month.
- The facility is called “the most technologically advanced multimetal recycling center” in the U.S.
- Site preparation took more than two years, starting with a groundbreaking in 2023 and leading to the ribbon-cutting ceremony last year.
- Aurubis has installed only half of its planned capacity in the first phase: 90,000 tons annually, with an expansion to 180,000 tons starting next year.
- The project has already created all 240 promised jobs after the company announced a doubling of the project’s size in 2022.
- It is unclear how tariffs on finished copper and other inputs might affect costs and profitability for Aurubis.
- Aurubis will sell its output at the same international market price as mined copper, framing it as a win for the earth and U.S. interests.
- Dr. Haag stated that while market dynamics are currently hard to predict, the U.S. is a huge and growing market where they concentrate production for local demand.
- According to Aurubis, the U.S. imports half of the 1.8 million tons of copper it consumes annually, with total consumption expected to rise 30 percent over the next five years.
- Dr. Haag added that U.S. demand is increasing mainly due to infrastructure, defense, AI, mobility, and transport spending, and global copper demand is expected to double by 2050.
- Aurubis has secured long-term agreements with domestic scrap aggregators for input materials and has similar supply arrangements with metals “off-takers.”
- The plant's circularity model turns waste into fuel for American factories and displaces imports, aligning with U.S. efforts to drive a manufacturing renaissance despite trade barriers.
- At the September 2024 groundbreaking, Aurubis highlighted its role in driving U.S. resiliency, featuring a large American flag on stage.
- Rep. Rick Allen (Republican, Georgia’s 12th district) stated that Aurubis exemplifies how international partnerships can solidify America’s industrial foundation and strengthen economic independence.
- The Augusta plant is named for the county where it sits and is called Aurubis Richmond.
- The facility aims to set a new standard for environmental responsibility by installing scrubbing and combustion technologies to remove pollutants from chimney emissions.
- Wastewater at the plant is recovered and reused, and the company has committed to discharging no effluent into surrounding waterways.
- Company officials noted that Aurubis's Hamburg facility is located in an urban area, contrasting with the Augusta site's environmental approach.
- Dr. Haag reported that the plant faced few hurdles during startup, comparable to similar plants in Germany and elsewhere.
- David Schultheis, president and managing director of Aurubis Richmond, thanked Georgia state leaders for strong support, smooth approval processes, and quick settlement enablement.
- Georgia was named the top state for business by Area Development magazine for the 12th consecutive year on the same day the plant opened.
- Aurubis operates other multimetal recycling sites in Spain, Belgium, and Germany, with smelters producing cathodes and rods in Bulgaria, Finland, Italy, and Germany.
- The company employs 7,100 people globally.
