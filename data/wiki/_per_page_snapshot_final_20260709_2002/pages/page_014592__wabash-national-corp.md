# Wabash National Corp.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** logistics  ·  **Published:** 2021-07-29

**Source:** [Wabash National Bets Big On Dry Vans, Increases Capacity By 20%](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/wabash-national-bets-big-dry-153215929.html)  ·  `page_014592`

## Facts

- Wabash National Corp. is ramping down traditional refrigerated capacity and converting floor space to add 20% more dry vans by 2023, counting on freight demand lasting well beyond the current peak.
- CEO Brent Yeagy stated on the company's Q2 call that demand for dry van production has exceeded physical capacity.
- Building dry vans to address demand spikes in 2018 and 2019 required a lot of overtime and weekend work, yet customers still wanted more than Wabash could manufacture.
- The capacity expansion adds 10,000 dry vans, representing a 5% increase in industry capacity but 20% more dry vans than Wabash currently produces.
- CEO Brent Yeagy noted that 10 years of continued growth in overall trailer demand makes it time for Wabash National to capitalize on this profitable opportunity.
- Wabash will use more lightweight molded structural composite (MSC) technology across its portfolio, including dry and refrigerated vans.
- Additional MSC for refrigerated van assembly capacity will follow in coming quarters, Yeagy said.
- Yeagy stated that conventional reefer design is the past and the company is all-in on commercializing the future, including working on zero-emission, solar-powered trailer refrigeration units.
- The company has about 100 zero-emission, solar-powered trailer refrigeration units in production with eNow.
- West Lafayette, Indiana-based Wabash is reorienting its business to focus on first-to-last-mile trailer products and exiting businesses that do not fit that plan.
- The company sold the Extract Technology business, part of its Diversified Products unit, to Dietrich Engineering Consultants at the end of the second quarter.
- Extract provides containment and aseptic systems for the pharmaceutical, health care, biotech, and chemical markets and was part of Wabash's 2012 acquisition of Walker Group Holdings.
- Extract accounted for $22 million in Wabash revenue in 2020.
- Yeagy stated that although Extract is an excellent business, the strategy is now squarely focused on the transportation, logistics and distribution industries.
- Wabash previously divested a fuel tank trailer business and sold its last remaining Wabash branch location.
- Wabash sold Garsite, an aviation refueling business, in 2019.
- CFO Michael Pettit stated that non-core asset sales have raised a total of approximately $40 million and structured the portfolio to align with the growth strategy.
- Higher retail sales and low business inventories prompted increased trailer production in Q2 when Wabash delivered 11,590 units.
- Rising commodity costs and supply chain disruptions drove higher costs.
- Yeagy said the company is having difficult but necessary conversations with customers about recovering cost increases throughout its backlog, which at $1.3 billion is 77% higher than a year ago.
- Pettit noted that a shortage of workers and unplanned downtime by chassis manufacturers because of a global shortage of semiconductors required adjusting capacity lower, a situation expected to last the rest of the year.
- Order books for 2021 have been closed for months and the 2022 order book is not yet fully open.
- Second-quarter revenue was $449 million for a gross margin of 12.4% of sales, Pettit said.
- Operating earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization was $35 million or 7.8% of sales, comparable to pre-pandemic margins.
- Net income was $4.3 million or 24 cents per diluted share.
- During the quarter, Wabash spent $30 million to reduce debt, $22 million to repurchase shares, $7 million for capital projects and $4 million to fund its quarterly dividend.
