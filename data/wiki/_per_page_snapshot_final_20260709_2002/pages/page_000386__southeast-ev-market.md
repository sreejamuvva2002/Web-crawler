# Southeast EV Market

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Hellbender Press - Southeastern electric vehicle sales and investments arc against headwinds](https://hellbenderpress.org/news/ev-sales-trend-upward-despite-headwinds)  ·  `page_000386`

## Facts

- Southeast light-duty EV sales grew by 38 percent from January 2024 through December 2024, outpacing the national year-over-year average growth rate for the first time.
- Florida is No. 1 in the region in EV sales growth, at 42 percent, and for EV market share, at 9 percent.
- The Sunshine State's EV market share is hovering just below the national EV market share average, which peaked above 10 percent in 2024, setting a market record.
- Georgia and North Carolina lead the region in EV investment and jobs.
- Tennessee and Alabama lag behind Florida, Georgia, and North Carolina in EV investment and jobs.
- The region is divided between two groups: Florida, Georgia, and North Carolina enjoying strong EV sales and market share growth; while Tennessee, South Carolina, and Alabama continue to lag far behind.
- EV growth in the region is self-reinforcing, as consumer familiarity from seeing EVs on the road and talking to owners or fleet operators increases purchase likelihood.
- Southeastern states, local governments, investor-owned utilities (IOUs), and EV charging companies are continuing to deploy public charging infrastructure.
- 80 percent of EV charging happens conveniently and affordably at home at night while EV drivers sleep.
- Public charging is critical for supporting long-distance travel, providing charging for EV-driving tourists, serving local drivers needing an extra boost, and supplementing the lack of residential charging at multi-family dwellings.
- Georgia is No. 1 in the region in terms of the number of public EV chargers per capita.
- Florida, Georgia, and North Carolina have historically topped the region in per-capita deployment for fast charging to support long-distance travel, on-route fleet vehicle charging, and EV rideshare drivers.
- In the second half of 2024, Alabama added 300 fast chargers to move ahead of North Carolina and above the national average for fast charging per capita.
- EV charging deployment and EV market growth go hand in hand, each benefiting the other.
- States like Georgia and Florida, which lead regional EV market share, also lead in charging infrastructure deployment.
- Alabama ranks last in EV market share but is a fast charging deployment surprise.
- Southeastern IOUs have invested $435.5 million in EV infrastructure through 2024.
- This utility investment represents approximately 6% of the nation’s IOU investments, despite the Southeast making up about 18% of the nation’s total population.
- Utilities are not proposing EV programs at scale, choosing instead to propose small pilot programs.
- When utilities propose more significant investments, regulators have often required them to scale back their efforts.
- On a per capita basis, all of the Southeast’s IOUs are below the national average of $38 per customer in approved EV investments.
- Duke Energy Florida and Florida Power and Light are close to the national average at $36 per customer, making Florida No. 1 in utility investment.
- In the Southeast, the overwhelming majority of public EV funding comes from the federal government, with states appropriating next to none of their own taxpayer dollars.
- South Carolina is No. 1 in public funding because it has drawn down $44.32 per capita in federal electric transportation funding, approximately 12 percent higher than the national average.
- Combined public funding from state and federal sources averages $38.23 per capita in the Southeast, which is way behind the national average of $74.01.
- Southeast states’ unwillingness to commit state-appropriate funding creates a reliance on the federal government’s support of the emerging EV market.
- Political uncertainty surrounds whether the federal government will honor already committed funds to states to electrify school and transit buses, deploy fast charging along highway corridors, allow government fleets to take advantage of vehicle and infrastructure tax credits, and reimburse already committed grants.
- This funding uncertainty will disproportionately impact Southeast states’ ability to support EV market growth.
- The degree of impact remains unclear, but this uncertainty is particularly problematic in the Southeast because investments and jobs depend on a strong EV market to succeed.
- Without strong demand for EVs, there is a risk that companies could shutter existing EV and battery plants and halt new plant construction, leaving workers without jobs.
- The EV market remains strong moving into 2025, with January EV sales up over 30% year over year after a record-setting December.
- January EV sales topped 100,000 units sold for the tenth consecutive month.
- The rapidly expanding used EV market grew over 30% year over year, with nearly 27,000 used vehicles sold in January.
- The used car market accounts for two-thirds of all vehicles consumers purchase.
- Used EVs are as affordable as their gas-car counterparts.
- Most new EVs purchased have been leased, which will result in over 1 million EVs coming off lease and into the used market over the next two years.
- These factors will enable a new and broader demographic of consumers to enter the EV market and experience low operating costs and high-performance benefits of EV ownership.
- Approximately 45% of car shoppers are considering buying an EV, with EV shopper demographics becoming more mainstream.
- 2025 is set to be the year consumer desire collides with political turbulence and rampant misinformation.
- Uncertainty looms regarding legal battles against the federal government’s attempt to claw back electric transportation and clean energy funding.
- Uncertainty exists about how tariffs and the threat of tariffs impact the auto sector, vehicle prices, and investments in EV manufacturing.
- Questions remain about whether new and used EV tax credits will remain available for consumers and commercial fleets.
- Uncertainty remains about whether growing consumer demand for EVs will cool.
- 2025 will be a telling year for the trajectory of the EV market.
