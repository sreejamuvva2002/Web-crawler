# US Executive Order on Unleashing American Energy and EV Policy

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2022-07-10

**Source:** [As Trump takes aim at EVs, how far will rollback go?](https://business.inquirer.net/502535/as-trump-takes-aim-at-evs-how-far-will-rollback-go)  ·  `page_000732`

## Facts

- US President Donald Trump took aim at electric vehicles as part of his flurry of first-day actions.
- Trump’s executive order on “Unleashing American Energy” included steps to ensure a level playing field for gasoline-powered motors.
- The executive order halted federal funding to build new EV charging stations.
- The executive order referenced the possible elimination of a federal tax credit for EV purchases.
- The executive order referenced the renouncement of a US waiver that allows California to set stricter requirements on cars.
- During his inaugural address, Trump said the moves would “end” the “Green New Deal.”
- Trump ridiculed Biden-backed incentives for EV sales during his inaugural address.
- Policy experts are skeptical Trump will junk all Biden-era EV programs because significant federal funding has gone toward projects in Republican congressional districts where thousands of jobs are expected to be created.
- Shares of EV makers like Rivian and EV charging companies such as EVgo fell sharply on Tuesday following the policy announcement.
- Tesla, which is led by close Trump ally Elon Musk, also saw its shares fall.
- Kathy Harris, director for clean vehicles of the Natural Resources Defense Council (NRDC), called Trump’s policy a sop for “fat-cat oil executives.”
- Harris noted that EVs are better for the environment and can save consumers money on gasoline.
- Many of Trump’s executive orders are expected to face legal challenges, including possible outcomes for the EV measures.
- Harris stated, “This is not the end of this story,” warning that if the administration tries to cut corners or ignore the law, they will end up in court.
- The Alliance for Automotive Innovation reiterated its criticism of California’s car regulations in a statement that did not address other elements in Trump’s executive order.
- John Bozzella, president of the Alliance for Automotive Innovation, said, “The country should have a single, national standard to reduce carbon in transportation,” and added, “We can’t have regulations that push the industry too far ahead of the customer.”
- Automakers are pausing some EV investments due to slowing growth, even as sales of emission-free vehicles climb to new levels in the United States.
- In 2024, EV sales in the country reached 1.3 million, up 7.3 percent from the prior year, according to Cox Automotive’s Kelley Blue Book.
- Kelley Blue Book pointed to a meaningful rise in EVs at different price levels.
- GM and Ford have scaled back some EV investments in recent months, pointing to slowing demand growth.
- A Ford executive warned in November that a glut of EVs across showrooms will lead to “incredible pressure” on prices in 2025.
- During the presidential campaign, Trump claimed Democrat Kamala Harris wanted to force EVs on consumers.
- Harris said she favored consumer choice.
- The Biden administration’s fuel economy rules required automakers to market fleets with sharply lower carbon dioxide emissions to address climate change.
- Laws such as the $400 billion Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 included a slew of lending and tax credit programs to boost EVs.
- Programs under the IRA and the 2021 infrastructure law are in various stages of implementation.
- Monday’s executive order directs officials implementing the IRA to undertake a 90-day review to ensure that spending does not unfairly favor EVs “by rendering other types of vehicles unaffordable.”
- Policy experts see no meaningful chance that the new administration will try to claw back US funds that have already been spent.
- It remains unclear whether Trump will seek to block other projects still moving through the pipeline.
- Nearly half of the $5 billion set aside for new EV chargers has been allocated to states under the infrastructure law, according to a memo from NRDC.
- The NRDC stated that the 2021 infrastructure law’s “embedded safeguards… should ensure continuity for infrastructure investments.”
- The NRDC noted that while the incoming administration could try drastic measures, those will face real-world and legal hurdles.
- In November, the Energy Department advanced projects to provide Rivian a $6.6 billion federal loan to build an EV manufacturing facility in Stanton Springs North, Georgia.
- Construction on the Georgia plant is expected to begin in 2026, according to Rivian.
- The Energy Department also advanced a $7.5 billion loan to StarPlus Energy to finance lithium-ion battery cell manufacturing plants in Kokomo, Indiana, under a Stellantis-Samsung joint venture.
- Neither Rivian nor Stellantis responded to AFP queries on the implications of the new Trump policy for their projects.
