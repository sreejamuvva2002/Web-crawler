# Ascend Elements

**Source:** [Facing headwinds, Ascend shifts plans for battery… | Canary Media](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/batteries/facing-headwinds-ascend-shifts-plans-for-battery-recycling-in-kentucky)  ·  `page_000700`

## Facts

- Ascend Elements, a Massachusetts-based advanced battery recycling company, opened a plant in Covington, Georgia in March 2023 that grinds used batteries into black mass powder.
- The company is currently building a plant in Hopkinsville, Kentucky called Apex 1 to refine black mass into battery materials.
- Ascend canceled plans to produce cathode active materials (CAM) at the Kentucky facility and agreed to cancel a $164 million grant for that portion won from the Department of Energy.
- The company intends to convert the CAM space into a lithium carbonate production line using proprietary technology rolled out at its Covington plant early this year.
- Apex 1 will still produce precursors to CAM known as pCAM, an effort aided by a separate $316 million grant from the DOE.
- Ascend plans to invest about $1 billion in the Kentucky project, according to spokesperson Thomas Frey.
- The company can sell its pCAM to specialized CAM manufacturers or to electric-vehicle and battery manufacturers who want their suppliers to use that particular material.
- Ascend will be the only large-scale manufacturer of pCAM in America, which spokesperson Thomas Frey noted makes the company appealing due to tariffs.
- The broader slowdown in U.S. EV manufacturing buildout and Detroit automakers pulling back on EV production enthusiasm pushed back timelines for the battery supply chain, including CAM and pCAM.
- Ascend extended its timeline for Apex 1 from the end of 2025 to the third quarter of 2026 to allow for a more cost-effective construction process.
- Commissioning is underway for the new lithium carbonate line at Ascend’s Covington factory, which should begin commercial production in the next few months.
- The Covington plant has struggled with used batteries catching fire during delivery and processing.
- Firefighters responded to a Feb. 20 conflagration in a tractor trailer delivering used batteries to the Covington site; the fire consumed the trailer but did not jump to the adjacent building.
- That incident was the 14th time Ascend’s Covington plant called in emergency teams, according to plant manager Andrew Gardner.
- The cause of the fires seems to have been batteries that were not properly packed or discharged prior to shipping.
- Ascend stopped operations for 10 days to work closely with the Fire Department and the mayor on safety and went on a blitz with customers to redo training on how to pack end-of-life batteries and scrap.
- Competitor Freyr Battery (now T1 Energy) had aspired to build a battery factory in Georgia but canceled plans amid the market slowdown.
