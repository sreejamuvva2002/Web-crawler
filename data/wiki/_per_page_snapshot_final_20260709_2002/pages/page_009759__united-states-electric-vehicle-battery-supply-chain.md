# United States Electric Vehicle Battery Supply Chain

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Trump could oversee an EV ‘battery boom’ — or bust - E&E News by POLITICO](https://www.eenews.net/articles/trump-could-oversee-an-ev-battery-boom-or-bust-2/)  ·  `page_009759`

## Facts

- President-elect Donald Trump is known for attacking electric vehicle mandates, but his impact on the EV supply chain from mining to recycling remains unclear.
- Trump is expected to speed up permitting for new mines.
- One analyst predicted a Trump battery boom for the industry.
- The battery supply chain is a continuum from mining, to making films and powders, to synthesizing battery cells and packs, to vehicle assembly.
- The U.S. has invested $200 billion in the effort to build the EV supply sector.
- Nearly every part of the EV battery manufacturing process is in its earliest stages and highly reliant on government tax credits, grants, and loans for survival, according to Nick Nigro, founder of Atlas Public Policy.
- Ongoing activity from mining to the construction and planning of EV battery plants is largely occurring in Republican congressional districts.
- Simon Moores, CEO of Benchmark Mineral Intelligence, stated that $110 billion has already been invested in EV supply chains, mostly downstream.
- About 60 percent of individual gigafactories and 80 percent of the investments are moving forward in red states.
- Jay Turner, a professor at Wellesley College, noted that of the 15 EV battery factories announced or expanded after the Inflation Reduction Act passed in 2022, eight are in planning and seven are under construction.
- Thirteen of those EV battery factories are located in Republican congressional districts.
- Demand for EV batteries could fall as much as 20 percent by 2030 compared to previous estimates if Trump follows through on campaign promises, according to Iola Hughes, head of research at Rho Motion.
- Trump is expected to alter the regulatory landscape for EVs, starting with EPA tailpipe emissions standards for cars built in model year 2027.
- The former president could impose tariffs on imported materials and target tax breaks for EVs and other Inflation Reduction Act provisions.
- Industry consensus suggests negative impacts on the EV battery supply chain likely would not be fully felt until 2027, even if Trump acts immediately upon taking office.
- Mining could be the part of the EV supply chain that gets the biggest boon from the change in presidential power.
- Peter Bryant, a strategist with Clareo, stated there will undoubtedly be a more pro-mining administration in the White House.
- Trump's actions on critical minerals date back to a 2017 executive order aimed at reducing the nation's reliance on other countries for them.
- Mining boosters are already in talks with Trump's transition team pushing for faster permitting, rollbacks of rules hindering mines, more access to public lands, and changes to the Inflation Reduction Act to ensure domestic mining companies benefit from production tax credits.
- Adam Webb, head of battery raw materials at Benchmark, said demand for raw materials used in lithium-ion batteries is expected to be lower in the U.S. than previously forecast due to expected lower EV sales under the incoming administration.
- Rho Motion's scenario analysis showed a 20 percent drop in demand for EV batteries by 2030 compared to the current outlook.
- The U.S. EV market currently accounts for about 8 percent of global lithium demand, 3 percent of global nickel demand, 7 percent of global cobalt demand, and 6 percent of natural graphite demand.
- Of the $83 billion the private sector has committed to the battery supply chain since the Inflation Reduction Act passed, $70 billion is going to Republican-led districts, with the remaining $13.1 billion going to Democratic-controlled areas.
- The Inflation Reduction Act's advanced manufacturing tax credit, known as 45X, will provide the biggest pot of money for battery companies.
- The $7,500 tax credit that car buyers can get for purchasing an EV is considered one of the most vulnerable incentives to elimination or rule changes.
- To qualify for the consumer EV tax credit, automakers must make battery cells in the U.S. and source an ever-escalating amount of crucial materials from the U.S. or its allies.
- Loren McDonald, chief analyst at Paren, noted that Musk has previously suggested ending the consumer tax credit would only minimally harm Tesla while sapping the EV efforts of traditional automakers.
