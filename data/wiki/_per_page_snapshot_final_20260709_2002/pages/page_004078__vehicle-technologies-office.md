# Vehicle Technologies Office

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-30

**Source:** [Incremental Purchase Cost Methodology and Results for Electric Vehicles | Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/vehicles/incremental-purchase-cost-methodology-and-results-electric-vehicles)  ·  `page_004078`

## Facts

- The U.S. Department of Energy’s Vehicle Technologies Office (VTO) leads and undertakes research and development to enable vehicle manufacturers to adopt new, efficient technologies.
- VTO works to reduce initial electric vehicle (EV) costs, defined as the cost of the vehicle for the buyer at the time of acquisition.
- Reducing initial vehicle cost supports the adoption of new, efficient, and clean mobility options that are affordable for all Americans.
- When combined with lower fueling and maintenance costs of an EV, reduced initial vehicle cost enables substantial overall cost savings for American consumers.
- DOE’s support of EV and battery research has helped drive the downward trajectory of EV and battery costs to date.
- VTO expects the costs of batteries to continue to decline.
- VTO has set research and development program targets of $75/kWh for battery packs by 2030.
- VTO expects initial cost parity of EVs with internal combustion engine vehicles (ICE) to be reached over the next 5 to 7 years.
- The webpage documents DOE’s approach for determining an incremental purchase cost for an EV compared with comparable ICE vehicles.
- The methodology covers plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEV), battery electric vehicles (BEV), and fuel cell electric vehicles (FCEV).
- The analysis uses current market costs rather than future costs based on lab scale R&D.
- DOE will continue periodic reviews of technology and the market to document changes in incremental purchase costs.
- As new reports are generated, they will be archived on the webpage.
