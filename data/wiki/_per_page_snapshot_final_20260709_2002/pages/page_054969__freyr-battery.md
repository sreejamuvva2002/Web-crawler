# Freyr Battery

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-02-15

**Source:** [A Battery Company CEO on the ‘Massive’ Effect of the Inflation Reduction Act - Inside Climate News](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/15022024/inside-clean-energy-battery-company-ceo-on-the-massive-effect-of-inflation-reduction-act/)  ·  `page_054969`

## Facts

- A Norwegian battery company has been working since 2022 to open a supersize factory outside of Atlanta that will bring more than 700 jobs to the region.
- Freyr Battery is developing a manufacturing process for lithium-ion batteries that it says will be less expensive and have less waste than the processes many competitors use.
- The batteries would be available for stationary energy storage and for use in large electric vehicles such as buses.
- The Georgia plant is one of the many projects whose existence is at least in part due to the Inflation Reduction Act, President Joe Biden’s 2022 climate law.
- The company said in November that it would not meet a target for one of its technical milestones by the end of 2023.
- It was delaying the opening of a large-scale plant in Norway.
- One upshot was that the plant in Coweta County, already in the works about 40 miles southwest of Atlanta with a projected opening of 2026, would go online before the one in Norway.
- Investors reacted negatively and the company’s share price dropped by about half.
- This all occurred in Birger Steen’s first few months as Freyr’s CEO.
- The company board appointed him to the role in August, and said the previous CEO, co-founder Tom Einar Jensen, would become executive chairman.
- Steen has about 30 years of experience in the technology sector, including stints leading Microsoft’s operations in Norway and Russia.
- He now faces some major challenges at the company’s technology and development center in Mo i Rana, a small city in northern Norway, where employees are taking a lithium-ion battery technology developed at MIT and trying to make it work in an automated manufacturing system.
- The development of the manufacturing process is teeing up the opening of the plant near Atlanta, a complex that will cost more than $2.5 billion to build and employ 723 people.
- Right now, the company has a head count of 213, most of them in Norway.
- The project would benefit from the 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit in the IRA, which is paid out based on the capacity, in megawatt-hours, of batteries made at the plant.
- The credit is much better than anything Norway can offer to Freyr, which is a disappointment to leaders in Norway, as the New York Times reports this week, but a boon to the United States.
- Birger Steen described the company's mission as decarbonizing transportation systems and storage systems by making the world’s most sustainable battery.
- The company aims to help build a critical component of the future energy system and produce it in the West, noting that 95 percent of LFP [lithium iron phosphate] production capacity right now is in China.
- Freyr successfully conducted automated casting trials of electrodes with active electrolyte slurry at its Norway facility.
- Full automation is a precondition for getting to multiple gigawatt-hour scale.
- The company recently did the first semi-automatic casting of electrodes, which took 30 hours instead of the scoped 30 days.
- Troubles during automation trials included a web not bending down as expected and wrinkling at the edges, which caused a lack of homogeneity in the process; this also took 30 days instead of the planned 30 hours.
- Freyr's original plan was to build a big plant in Norway due to 100 percent green energy from hydropower, local material supply prospects (lithium and graphite), proximity to a deepwater quay, good rail/road transport, and community support.
- The company also planned to build a facility in Georgia as part of a selection process.
- Freyr prioritized Georgia over other U.S. locations due to its central location, great airport nearby, railroads, access to port facilities by road or rail, strong local community partnership, and a strong base of resources with local skills ranging from trade school level to postdoc level.
- The original plan was to build in Norway first and then Georgia second.
- After the IRA was passed into law in August year before last (2022), Freyr concluded there would be no relevant policy response from European or Norwegian authorities, making it an easy choice to prioritize Georgia due to better project-level funding economics.
- Birger Steen stated that the IRA makes a 'massive' difference compared to traditional programs that focus on cost of capital, development funding, or cheap land at the beginning of a project.
- Unlike European policy initiatives, the IRA provides support based on production volume (paid out when producing gigawatt-hours), allowing the market to pick winners rather than the government.
- Low lithium prices make battery products more affordable and expand the total addressable market as demand picks up.
