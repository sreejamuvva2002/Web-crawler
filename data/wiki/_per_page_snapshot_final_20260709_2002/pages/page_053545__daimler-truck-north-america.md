# Daimler Truck North America

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-04-14

**Source:** [The South Stands Up! Solidarity with DTNA workers and auto workers on the move! - Southern Workers Assembly](https://southernworker.org/dtna-auto-solidarity/)  ·  `page_053545`

## Facts

- The Southern Workers Assembly expressed full solidarity with 7,000 Daimler Truck North America (DTNA) workers and UAW members in North Carolina, Tennessee, and Georgia engaged in a contract fight for record gains.
- The workers' current UAW master contract with DTNA expires at midnight on April 26.
- The UAW stated that if a new contract meeting workers' demands is not settled by the April 26 expiration, workers will go on strike.
- In March, UAW workers at DTNA voted by a margin of 96 percent to authorize a strike if necessary.
- Workers in the DTNA plants are prepared to take decisive action to win justice during the contract negotiations.
- UAW workers at DTNA manufacture Freightliner and Western Star heavy trucks and school buses for Thomas Built Buses.
- The DTNA contract fight is organized under the slogan “Record profits mean record contracts” and follows the historic “Stand Up” strike last fall by auto workers at plants owned by Ford, GM, and Stellantis.
- Daimler Truck, the parent company of DTNA, is the largest commercial vehicle manufacturer in the world.
- Since 2018, Daimler has seen a 90 percent increase in its profits.
- In 2023 alone, Daimler Truck’s profits increased by 39 percent, reaching nearly $6 billion.
- In March, CEO Martin Daum referred to company profits as “red hot” and asserted that “leftover money certainly belongs” to Daimler Truck shareholders.
- The company plans to increase stock dividends by 46 percent.
- Over the same period, the cost of basic living expenses such as groceries, gas, childcare, housing, and healthcare increased by upwards of 20 percent in some cases.
- Many DTNA workers have been forced to take second jobs to make ends meet despite the company's high profitability.
- Workers are demanding significant wage increases, cost of living adjustments, job security protections, improved retirement benefits, and an equalization of pay scales across all DTNA plants.
- Auto workers across the South are initiating an unprecedented, coordinated organizing drive at 13 nonunion auto manufacturers in the region.
- Workers at the Volkswagen plant in Chattanooga, Tennessee are set to vote to join the UAW on April 17-19.
- Workers at the Mercedes-Benz plant in Vance, Alabama have recently filed for a UAW election after reaching supermajority support.
- Organizing drives are going public at other auto plants across the Southeastern region.
- The UAW is building solidarity with DTNA workers in Mexico and Brazil to counter corporate strategies that threaten to move jobs to Mexico to suppress wage demands.
- UAW Region 9A Director Brandon Mancilla stated in a Labor Notes op-ed that the UAW is fully dedicated to building solidarity with workers in Mexico across auto, heavy truck manufacturing, and other sectors.
- UAW Region 9A Director Brandon Mancilla wrote in his op-ed that “The best resistance to Donald Trump is to unite our working-class struggles…The UAW, and the labor movement as a whole, face an existential question: are we going to consent to our decline, or do what it takes to end the international race to the bottom?”
- The auto industry is poised to transition production increasingly toward battery and electric vehicle (EV) manufacturing in the coming years, facilitated by billions in government subsidies.
- Nearly two-thirds of the new EV sector jobs are expected to come to the U.S. South, particularly Georgia, North Carolina, and South Carolina.
- Billions of dollars in capital investments across other manufacturing sectors are also flowing into the U.S. South.
- The United States is now the cheapest country globally to produce electric vehicles, with companies choosing the Southeastern region to leverage its historically anti-labor political environment and socioeconomic challenges.
- Workers across multiple sectors, including public schools, municipal services, and industry, are organizing to change the future of the Southeastern region.
- A decisive victory for DTNA workers is viewed as critical to building working-class power and turning the tide against corporate anti-labor strategies in the South.
