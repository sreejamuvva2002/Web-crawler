# Ascend Elements

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2022-02-11

**Source:** [Charged EVs | Ascend Elements to open battery recycling facility in Georgia - Charged EVs](https://chargedevs.com/newswire/ascend-elements-to-open-battery-recycling-facility-in-georgia/)  ·  `page_001464`

## Facts

- Battery recycling and manufacturing company Ascend Elements (formerly known as Battery Resourcers) is planning to open a battery recycling plant in Georgia by August of 2022.
- The facility will recycle lithium, cobalt, and nickel.
- The company plans to invest $43 million in the facility.
- The facility will span 154,000 square feet.
- The facility will be able to process 30,000 metric tons of discarded batteries and scrap per year.
- Ascend Elements selected the Covington, Georgia site due to its proximity to EV manufacturing hubs and battery factories.
- By 2023, Ascend Elements plans to open a facility to produce cathode active materials.
- The new facility will rely on the company’s patented Hydro-to-Cathode recycling technology.
- According to the company, Hydro-to-Cathode recycling technology is 93% cleaner compared to mining and the production of new materials.
- The Hydro-to-Cathode technology operates at a 59% lower cost compared to mining and new material production.
- Based on an article published in the journal Joule, the company states that Hydro-to-Cathode has a 53% longer life cycle than new cathode materials.
- Ascend Elements plans to open additional facilities in North America, Europe, and Asia.
- These additional facilities will process a combined total of up to 150,000 metric tons of lithium-ion battery materials per year.
- Ascend Elements CEO and Director Michael O’Kronley stated that the industry needs to match gigafactory capacity with “gigarecycling” facilities capable of recycling millions of batteries.
- The Covington facility will be the largest battery recycling facility in North America as of summer 2022.
- O’Kronley encouraged the development of recycling facilities even larger than the Covington site to prevent batteries from going to landfills.
