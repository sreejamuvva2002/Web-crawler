# Jack Lifton

**Type:** person  ·  **Role:** unknown  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-01

**Source:** [Why OEMs, Not Governments, Will Decide America's Critical Minerals Future - InvestorNews](https://investornews.com/market-opinion/why-oems-not-governments-will-decide-americas-critical-minerals-future/)  ·  `page_013604`

## Facts

- Jack Lifton, Co-Chair of the Critical Minerals Institute (CMI), states that governments can subsidize mines but OEMs decide where materials are sourced, which technologies are adopted, who earns long-term contracts, and ultimately which critical minerals projects survive.
- Successful OEMs—including automobile manufacturers, aerospace companies, defense contractors, and industrial motor producers—decide supply chain viability by asking who can reliably supply qualified materials at globally competitive prices year after year, rather than searching for rare earth mines.
- Federal agencies currently measure critical minerals success using political metrics such as counting permitted mines, announced processing plants, grants awarded, and billions of dollars committed.
- OEMs measure supply chain success by asking if suppliers can consistently meet specifications, deliver on schedule, expand production as demand grows, remain in business for ten years, and maintain globally competitive prices without permanent government support.
- The critical minerals supply chain must be built backward from the OEM rather than forward from the ore body, with customer specifications determining required alloys, metal purity, separation chemistry, concentrate requirements, and finally the type of economically viable ore body.
- The current investment community process reverses industrial planning by having junior explorers discover deposits, followed by investment banker presentations, government strategic importance announcements, and consultant market studies before asking if any OEM intends to purchase the products.
- The “mine-to-magnet” narrative is criticized for ignoring how OEM procurement actually works, as building every supply chain stage under one corporate roof does not align with actual purchasing decisions.
- OEMs rarely qualify suppliers based on mine ownership; instead, they qualify suppliers that consistently deliver products meeting demanding technical, commercial, and logistical requirements to reduce risk.
- An automotive company purchasing permanent magnets is buying manufacturing reliability rather than geology, while a defense contractor qualifying specialty alloys is purchasing confidence in consistent shipment performance.
- Investors should evaluate which companies understand OEM qualification procedures, which management teams have successfully supplied industrial manufacturers before, which design businesses around customer requirements rather than government funding opportunities, and which projects can survive after subsidies disappear.
- America needs more domestic customers willing to sign long-term purchase agreements based on confidence in supplier performance, as this confidence—not government grants or press releases—transforms mineral resources into industrial assets.
- Every successful supply chain begins with a purchase order rather than a drill hole.
