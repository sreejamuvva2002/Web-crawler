# State of Georgia

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2023-08-16

**Source:** [How GOP-dominated Georgia became a hub for clean energy jobs - Governors' Wind Energy Coalition](https://governorswindenergycoalition.org/how-gop-dominated-georgia-became-a-hub-for-clean-energy-jobs/)  ·  `page_000665`

## Facts

- A multibillion-dollar EV factory under construction west of Savannah, Georgia, spans 17 million square feet, which is about two and a half times larger than Disneyland, and will create more than 8,000 jobs.
- Near Atlanta, a $2.5 billion battery factory that will begin making batteries for renewable energy storage later this year will create hundreds more positions.
- In another Atlanta suburb, a solar panel factory under construction plans to hire 2,500 workers.
- In the year since the Inflation Reduction Act passed, clean energy companies have announced nearly 20 major new projects in Georgia totaling more than $12 billion, according to E2 (Environmental Entrepreneurs).
- Other projects already underway, like the massive electric vehicle plant near Savannah, have accelerated construction to begin production earlier.
- Hyundai, which is building the EV plant near Savannah, announced plans to build a $5 billion battery plant with SK On after the Inflation Reduction Act passed.
- Georgia Senator Jon Ossoff stated that Georgia will benefit more than just about any state in the country from these policies.
- Georgia’s governor is reluctant to acknowledge climate change, and none of the state’s Republican representatives in Congress voted for the Inflation Reduction Act.
- Other states with Republican leaders who voted against the IRA, including South Carolina and North Carolina, are also at the top of the list in new clean energy investments.
- In Georgia and South Carolina alone, 37 major new projects are already in development or have broken ground.
- Investment is moving more slowly in some states that have led on climate policy; California currently trails Oklahoma and Indiana.
- Georgia’s clean energy economy was growing even before the IRA as state and local leaders worked aggressively to attract solar, battery, and EV companies to build factories and create new jobs.
- Governor Brian Kemp has said he wants Georgia to be the “electric mobility capital of America” while courting new factories, even though he has done little to get EVs on roads.
- The IRA made investments surge and sped up some existing projects.
- Jeremy Bezdek, EVP of global corporate development and president of Freyr Battery U.S., stated that the company decided to move forward on a fast-track project to get production up to a year earlier because the math from the IRA motivated them to move faster.
- The Inflation Reduction Act offers clean energy tax credits that won’t expire for at least 10 years, unlike previous credits that were more uncertain because they had to be renewed repeatedly.
- Bezdek noted that if it was a 5-year program it would make pursuing large capital projects more challenging, but the fact that it’s a 10-year program helps them get comfortable with the CapEx spend required.
- Korean solar panel manufacturer Hanwha Qcells opened its first factory in Georgia in 2019.
- After the IRA passed, Hanwha Qcells decided to expand its facility and build a second large plant in Cartersville, Georgia, investing another $2.5 billion in the state.
- The company made the decision because the IRA included solar manufacturing tax credits that were drafted by Ossoff.
- Along with Georgia Senator Rafael Warnock, Ossoff pushed to ensure that EV and battery production were priorities in the bill.
- Ossoff has led trade delegations to South Korea and met individually with businesses there twice to recruit investment to Georgia.
- Aaron Brickman, a senior principal at the nonprofit RMI, stated that Georgia’s long track record of attracting new businesses put the state in a position to take advantage of the IRA quickly due to its economic development infrastructure.
- State and local government in Georgia supports training for workers and partnerships with schools.
- One program with the Technical College System of Georgia is designed to quickly train new employees while a factory is being built.
- The state and counties help companies find sites, guide them through permitting, and offer grants and other incentives.
- Freyr Battery U.S. considered 170 different industrial site locations in 34 states for its factory, looking at factors like the cost of power and the availability of skill development programs, before eventually choosing to work in Georgia.
- Some companies are choosing Georgia because of the concentration of existing clean energy companies; Ascend Elements, for example, with a massive new battery recycling facility, wanted to work near battery manufacturers.
- Brickman is working with seven Great Lakes states, from Minnesota to Pennsylvania, as the region aims to become more competitive for clean energy development.
- The opportunity for industrial transformation is huge, with an incredible amount of manufacturing jobs and commensurate CapEx in building out domestic manufacturing supply chains.
- For every mega project, there will be 10 or 20 smaller projects.
- The jobs are well-paid and offer opportunities to advance quickly, according to Bob Keefe, executive director of E2.
- Keefe recently visited Qcells’s first solar factory in Dalton, Georgia, where he spoke with a worker who had been in the carpet industry along with the rest of his family.
- The town calls itself the “carpet capital,” but lost thousands of jobs after the Great Recession as the industry contracted.
- The worker had never gotten a promotion in his carpet factory job, but has already been promoted three times over the past year at Qcells.
- Dalton used to be nothing but flooring and carpets, but now other companies are coming in that no one would have ever imagined could come to a place like that.
- Before the Inflation Reduction Act passed, U.S. leadership in clean energy had waned, but now only a year later, a lot of clean energy is looking at the U.S. as the place to go.
