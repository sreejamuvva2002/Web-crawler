# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America (HMGMA)

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-08-14

**Source:** [Worker injuries mount as giant Hyundai project nears completion - The Current](https://thecurrentga.org/2024/08/14/worker-injuries-mount-as-giant-hyundai-project-nears-completion/)  ·  `page_001032`

## Facts

- Construction of Hyundai’s newest electric vehicle manufacturing site began in January 2023 on 3,000 acres of clear-cut land just south of I-16 in Ellabell, Georgia.
- Hyundai officials aim to bring their first electric vehicle off the assembly line by the end of 2024 to make vehicles eligible for buyer incentives under the Biden Administration’s 2024 Inflation Reduction Act.
- HMGMA is a newly formed entity contracted with Georgia to receive $2.1 billion in tax deferments, subsidized construction costs, and other perks.
- HMGMA has promised to create 8,500 jobs together with other Hyundai affiliates on the site.
- Seven Hyundai factories are rising ahead of schedule from the Ellabell site.
- At least 20 traumatic injuries occurred at the site since construction began in January 2023 through May 2024, according to emergency services and fire department records.
- Thirteen of these injuries occurred in the first six months of 2024, including two falls, two head strikes from equipment, and four vehicle accidents.
- Many of the traumatic injuries required emergency airlifts off the site, and one resulted in a worker’s death.
- OSHA lists only 10 reports regarding accidents on the site between January 2023 and May 2024, creating a discrepancy with emergency services records.
- On April 29, 2023, construction worker Victor Javier Gamboa fell roughly 60 feet from a third-story steel beam at the HMGMA paintshop site.
- Gamboa was a subcontractor employed by Eastern Constructors who supported five children and a fiancee in Statesboro.
- His arrest lanyard was severed by a steel I-beam during the fall, and EMS was unsuccessful in saving him; he was pronounced dead 1 hour and 42 minutes after EMS arrived.
- OSHA ruled that Eastern Constructors committed both a serious and willful violation of labor law for failing to inspect lifesaving equipment and allowing use of a defective body harness.
- OSHA fined Eastern Constructors over $160,000 and added the firm to its Severe Violator Enforcement Program.
- Eastern Constructors contested the fine in November; the case remains open.
- HMGMA ended work with Eastern Constructors following the OSHA ruling and confirmed on Aug. 8 that the company was removed from the site.
- On Feb. 16, 2024, EMS airlifted a worker who fell 15 to 20 feet from a platform after experiencing significant delays in locating him on the sprawling site.
- The injured worker was found in a stupor-like state with blood leaking from his head and left ear, with no safety harness visible.
- Site workers encircled EMS personnel, shouting to hurry and trying to touch the patient, which EMS reported posed a significant delay in care.
- The worker was transferred via LifeStar helicopter to Memorial Health University Medical Center in Savannah.
- OSHA opened an inspection into a fall incident at a HMGMA assembly building on Feb. 21, but it remains unclear if it relates to the Feb. 16 incident.
- Two other workers fell and were injured on Sept. 23, 2023, and March 21, 2024; one fell from a crane and the other was flung from a boom lift 30 feet in the air.
- Neither of these fall incidents appears in OSHA inspection data.
- Five other traumatic injuries involved workers being struck by heavy objects, including a steel beam cutting a shin in October 2023, a mangled leg in December 2023, a metal pipe strike to the eyes in January, a forklift leg injury in December 2023, and a scissor lift foot crush in February 2024.
- On May 31, a 40-year-old Korean-speaking technician employed by SFA Engineering Corp. was trapped in a newly installed conveyor belt at the Hyundai GLOVIS logistics facility.
- The technician suffered uncontrollable bleeding from his left thigh, a deformed right hand with stripped skin, and a crushed chest possibly with a collapsed lung.
- First responders arrived 15 minutes after a manager called 911, placed two tourniquets, and performed a decompression needle procedure before airlifting him to Memorial Hospital via LifeStar helicopter.
- On June 3, HMGMA stated the man was treated for severe internal and external injuries and was in stable condition.
- OSHA classified the May 31 injury as an “amputation” and ended its onsite inspection on June 5, but had not issued violations or fines two months later.
- HMGMA confirmed the technician was still receiving medical treatment.
- Former and current site safety managers requested anonymity, stating workplace safety standards are among the worst they have experienced in their careers due to organizational chaos.
- A former safety manager witnessed workers not wearing basic safety equipment like hard hats or appropriate footwear on multiple days and noted safety orientation lasted only a few minutes.
- The pace of work created “an atmosphere of fear and frustration,” according to the former manager.
- HMGMA spokesperson Bianca Johnson stated that each of the seven Hyundai factories is independently responsible for reporting construction worker injuries to OSHA.
- Johnson wrote that HMGMA provides standard site orientation covering PPE and accident reporting, holds weekly safety team meetings with affiliates, and maintains an on-site Emergency Response Team trained in Medical, Fire, and Spill Response.
- OSHA levied fines against two subcontractors: Eastern Constructors Incorporated (over $160,000) and Sungwon Georgia Corp. ($22,000), both of which are contesting the fines.
- OSHA fined Sungwon Georgia Corp. $22,000 for exposing workers to 15-foot fall hazards without an anchored fall arrest system; Sungwon is appealing the fine.
- The Current reached out to all seven Hyundai companies regarding safety and only received a response from HMGMA.
- OSHA’s Savannah office did not return multiple phone calls seeking comment about safety standards or oversight of contractors at the site.
