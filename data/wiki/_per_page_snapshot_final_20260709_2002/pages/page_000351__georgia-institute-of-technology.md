# Georgia Institute of Technology

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2022-01-14

**Source:** [A Battery Breakthrough, Made from Rubber - Tech Briefs](https://www.techbriefs.com/component/content/article/40658-a-battery-breakthrough-made-with-rubber)  ·  `page_000351`

## Facts

- Mechanical engineering graduate researcher Michael Lee is part of a team at Georgia Institute of Technology rethinking an essential battery component by reshaping rubber.
- The polymer acts as a new structure for the battery transport solution known as the electrolyte.
- The Georgia Tech-developed electrolytes are synthesized from a liquid precursor solution consisting of plastic and salt monomers that bond to form a rubber polymer.
- Through heat, the rubber transforms into what the researchers refer to as a "3D interconnected plastic crystal phase."
- When the monomers turn to polymers, the plastic crystals "coarsen" within a rubber matrix, enabling the three-dimensional shape.
- Lee worked alongside Georgia Tech Professor Seung Woo Lee in the development effort.
- The rubber-based battery components include a lithium metal anode, rubber electrolyte, and a lithium nickel manganese cobalt oxide cathode.
- The rubber electrolyte is mainly composed of lithium salt, an elastomer known as poly (butyl acrylate), and a plastic crystal called succinonitrile.
- The unique structure has resulted in high ionic conductivity, improved mechanical properties, and electrochemical stability.
- These three traits support the advancement of the electric vehicle.
- Electric vehicles typically use lithium-ion batteries where ions are moved by a liquid electrolyte.
- When damaged, conventional flammable electrolytes present a fire hazard.
- Safety issues with lithium-ion batteries have led to alternative ideas like solid-state batteries that use rubber materials.
- Lee and the team's rubber electrolyte can be made at low-temperature conditions, generating robust and smooth interfaces on the surface of electrodes.
- The structure of the rubber electrolytes prevents damaging lithium dendrite growth and allows for faster moving ions.
- This enables reliable operation of solid-state batteries even at room temperature.
- Michael Lee stated that higher ionic conductivity means more ions can move at the same time.
- Lee noted that increasing specific energy and energy density of these batteries can increase the mileage of electric vehicles.
- Conventional solid-polymer electrolytes have been limited due to low ionic conductivity at room temperature and insufficient mechanical stability.
- Rubber has not previously been considered as an electrolyte for lithium batteries due to its low ionic conductivity.
- The team hypothesized that the 3D-structured rubber electrolyte simultaneously enabled the use of rubber as an electrolyte material and overcame challenges of conventional solid polymer electrolytes.
- Lee described the selection of conventional materials but noted it is an unconventional material choice for battery applications.
- The team believes the rubber is highly possible to be employed in current manufacturing processes.
- Solid electrolytes are unable to stretch and bend due to brittleness, whereas rubber has superior mechanical stability.
- Experimental data shows the rubber electrolyte can be extended up to almost 300% from its original state.
- This mechanical property is a key factor for operating next-generation lithium batteries, including lithium metal batteries that replace graphite anodes with lithium metal anodes to increase energy density.
- Conventional organic liquid electrolytes easily catch fire, leading to battery explosions.
- Rubber electrolytes are safer due to their flame retardancy.
- The rubber electrolyte-based lithium metal batteries can exhibit high energy density exceeding 410 Watt-hours per kilogram (Wh/kg electrode+electrolyte).
- The batteries maintained a high energy density of 235 Wh/kg at a power density of 184 Wh/kg.
- If commercialized, EVs with rubber electrolyte-based Li batteries could travel over 490 miles on a single charge compared to 310 miles for EVs with conventional Li-ion batteries.
- Large-scale production of rubber electrolytes needs to be investigated at a manufacturing level to become a mainstream part of battery technology.
