# Die-Tech Industries Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2008-07-29

**Source:** [Die shop transitions to production stamper](https://www.thefabricator.com/thefabricator/article/bending/die-shop-transitions-to-production-stamper)  ·  `page_053866`

## Facts

- Die-Tech Industries Inc. was started in Carrollton, Ga. in 1987 by third-generation tool- and diemakers Tom and Jerry Wysoczynski.
- The company began accepting production stamping jobs for its presses in 1999 to fill idle time between testing and offset fixed costs.
- President and CEO Tom Wysoczynski stated that global competition hit tool and die shops hard, causing margins to shrink to the point where awarded contracts raised questions about quoting mistakes.
- Die-Tech purchased servo feeds, coil handling equipment, and straightening systems from Dallas Industries in Troy, Mich., to automate feed presses for production pace.
- The company determined it needed accurate, flexible servo feeds to remain competitive in quality and productivity.
- Servo feeds allowed fast and simple changeovers in the job shop environment.
- Dallas' feed equipment handles tough applications, such as stamping an automotive fan hub that must be held flat to 0.005-inch total indicated runout (TIR).
- Die-Tech acquired a Dallas Space-Saver™ servo feed that reduced required floor area by up to half compared to conventional feed system layouts.
- The space-saving equipment eliminated the cost and need for material looping pits and saved additional floor manufacturing space.
- The production line comprises a powered reel and load car mechanism, a combination coil restrictor/peeler/threader/debender, and a high-powered single unit combining servo roll feed and pull-through straightener.
- The line is attached to a 500-ton press with a 132-inch-long bed and is used primarily for progressive dies with multiple in-die nut and stud staking applications.
- The system handles coils weighing up to 30,000 pounds and material widths from 12 inches to 36 inches, with diameters up to 72 inches.
- Large coil capacity allows the stamper to make fewer coil changes, resulting in more uptime and less start-up scrap.
- Die-Tech achieves approximate feed and press speeds of up to 83 strokes per minute (SPM) at a 6-inch feed length and 240-degree feed cycle.
- The company implemented automatic, self-centering capabilities to reduce die mis-hits and quality defects caused by improper setup.
- The powered reel is self-centering with a variable-speed AC drive, dual-pressure adjustable air brake, coil width scaling arms, hydraulic-powered mandrel expansion, push-button support arm expansion, and a fail-safe braking system.
- A combination coil restrictor prevents coils from springing when restraining bands are cut, while the peeler lifts the leading edge and the threader guides material edges into the straightener.
- The debender removes leading and trailing edge bends from coil material to prevent mis-hits, breakage, and unexpected repairs.
- Tom Wysoczynski emphasized that accuracy is critical for staking operations, noting a mis-hit could cause up to $1,500 in damage to replace a staking head.
- For rear hatch mounting brackets with six staking heads, an inaccurate feed could cost up to $9,000 to repair the die, plus downtime and labor.
- The straightener unit includes a piloting head that pivots upward to release pressure on material as die pilot pins precisely locate the stock.
- A full-function control system with a touchscreen interactive programming unit slashed setup times by guiding operators through sequential steps.
- Subsequent setups for repeat jobs are completed quickly by entering an identifying code or part number, setting all line systems to predetermined parameters in less time than die setup.
- The company typically performs three to four setups on the line and press in a 24-hour period.
- Die-Tech developed its own performance-based software package to track operations because the stamping business grew too fast for existing service capabilities.
- The software tracks die and press maintenance schedules, setup performance, material requirements planning (MRP) status, routing and logistics, downtime, repair mean times, projected run times, and scrap rates.
- Operators cannot clock onto a job without receiving proper training, and all quality check sheets are printed electronically to ensure team members have the latest copies.
- The software system controls scrap, sorting results, daily quality bin inventory, rework hours, and training hours.
- Dock audits and employee training audits are conducted electronically using wireless scanners, with results emailed immediately to supervisors, human resources, and quality control managers.
- If a defective product occurs, the company can identify the date, shift, employee, and steel heat within 10 minutes for immediate investigation.
- Die maintenance and machine maintenance are tracked on a predictive database using data generated directly from parts produced or time used.
- Schedules are created based on manufacturing use of tools or machines to minimize maintenance costs and create a predictive maintenance environment.
- The company's main business now comes from production stamping and assembly, while upholding die manufacturing standards.
- Die manufacturing continues to be a good income generator for the company.
- The company currently runs approximately 40 presses ranging from 45 to 800 tons around the clock.
- Die-Tech has more than 550 customer dies and tool sets on hand to run on a contract basis.
- Stamping income has soared to levels nearly 10 times that of 10 years ago.
