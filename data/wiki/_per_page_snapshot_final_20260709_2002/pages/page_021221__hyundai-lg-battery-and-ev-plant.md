## Facts

- US Department of Homeland Security officials detained hundreds of people, including alleged undocumented South Korean workers, during a raid on a Hyundai-LG battery plant being built in Georgia.
- 475 people were detained in the raid, with the majority from South Korea.
- The immigration component of the raid came as part of a larger, months-long investigation into labour practices at the site.
- South Korea’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs demanded the rights of its investors and citizens to be respected following the raid.
- Foreign Ministry spokesperson Lee Jaewoong stated: “The business activities of our investors and the rights of our nationals must not be unjustly infringed in the process of US law enforcement.”
- The episode highlights the disruptive effect President Donald Trump’s immigration crackdown is having on his efforts to attract foreign investment.
- The Hyundai-LG plant represents the biggest foreign investment in the state of Georgia.
- Since Trump returned to power in January, US Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) has been bolstered by record funding and new latitude to conduct raids.
- President Trump wants to deport “the worst of the worst” criminals, but ICE figures show a rise in the detainment of non-criminals.
- Trump shifted his administration’s approach to workplace raids, which business leaders warn could undermine industries relying heavily on undocumented and seasonal workers not easily replaced with domestic labour.
- Pew Research Center cited preliminary Census Bureau data saying the US labour force lost more than 1.2 million immigrants from January through July of this year.
- White House spokeswoman Abigail Jackson framed the raid as part of a wider enforcement campaign against undocumented foreign workers, stating: “Any foreign workers brought in for specific projects must enter the United States legally and with proper work authorisations.”
- Jackson added that President Trump will continue enforcing federal immigration laws while delivering on his promise to make the US the best place to do business.
- Steven Schrank, special agent in charge of Homeland Security investigations in Atlanta, said the operation “underscores our commitment to jobs for Georgians and Americans.”
- Schrank described the raid as the “largest single-site enforcement operation” in the department’s history.
- Georgia Governor Brian Kemp touted the manufacturing site as the largest economic development project in the state’s history.
- Hyundai Motor Group, South Korea’s biggest automaker, began manufacturing electric vehicles (EVs) a year ago at the $7.6bn plant.
- The plant currently employs about 1,200 people.
- Hyundai partnered with LG Energy Solution to build an adjacent battery plant, which is slated to open next year.
- It remained unclear how many detainees were held for lacking proper documentation versus arrested for other violations.
- Al Jazeera reporter Mike Hanna noted there was no clarity on what exactly would happen to those arrested during the operation.
- A South Korean government official told Reuters that many of the detainees were being held at an ICE detention facility.
- South Korea’s top diplomat Lee announced plans to dispatch diplomats from its embassy in Washington and consulate in Atlanta to the site, and to form an on-site response team centred on the local mission.
- In July, Seoul pledged $350bn in US investment to ease tariff threats from Trump.
- The US president hosted South Korean President Lee Jae Myung at the White House in August.
