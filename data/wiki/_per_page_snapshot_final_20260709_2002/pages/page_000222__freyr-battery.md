# Freyr Battery

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-01-13

**Source:** [Georgia Offered $358M in Incentives for EV Battery Factory Bid](https://www.govtech.com/policy/georgia-offered-358m-in-incentives-for-ev-battery-factory-bid)  ·  `page_000222`

## Facts

- A Norwegian battery upstart could receive more than $358 million in grants, tax breaks and other inducements from state and local governments to build its planned Coweta County factory where it will build giant energy storage systems for utilities and other clients.
- The state and local incentives offered to Freyr Battery are detailed in documents obtained by The Atlanta Journal-Constitution through the Georgia Open Records Act.
- Freyr announced plans in November for a $2.57 billion factory in Coweta where it employ 724 workers making energy storage systems and lithium-ion batteries.
- Freyr ranks among the legion of companies moving to Georgia to produce electrified products, be it electric vehicles or batteries, a priority among state leaders.
- Freyr told the AJC that the state’s education system and ability to recruit workers capable of thriving in the burgeoning clean-tech sector were the deciding factors when choosing Georgia over more than two dozen states for its first North American factory.
- The package of incentives offered by Georgia and Coweta leaders helped tip the scales for Freyr's location decision.
- Much of the global supply chain for batteries and EV components is based in China.
- The Biden administration has pumped billions of dollars into grants, tax credits and other federal incentives to entice companies into establishing battery and EV manufacturing in the U.S. as part of ambitious goals to fight climate change, create jobs and reduce the nation's dependency on its geopolitical rival.
- Local and state governments have clamored to win these corporate investments, dangling billions more in property tax breaks and other freebies.
- State and local incentives are a common though controversial currency in the war for jobs, with critics saying the inducements come at the expense of existing residents and businesses who have to shoulder expenses from supporting these new industries.
- Greg LeRoy, executive director of left-leaning incentives watchdog Good Jobs First, said he worries Coweta County residents will have to cover local government costs incurred by Freyr’s new factory until the tax abatement ends.
- Greg LeRoy stated: “You’re going to have to hire more teachers, pick up more trash, widen more lanes and answer more 911 calls,” adding that if the company isn’t paying for a share of those induced costs, then everybody else gets stuck with the bill.
- Pat Wilson, commissioner of the Georgia Department of Economic Development, said Freyr represents a new niche within the electric battery and vehicle ecosystem and helps diversify the state’s fast-growing sector.
- Pat Wilson stated: “You have a great company with a technology that is vital to this transition to electrification,” noting it is a battery sector that Georgia and the U.S. as a whole currently do not have.
- Georgia’s increasing talent pool cemented Coweta County as Freyr’s final choice, according to Freyr CEO Tom Einar Jensen.
- Freyr CEO Tom Einar Jensen stated: “The most important factor for selecting our first US plant location was talent,” and that the total package of financial incentives for Giga America played an important role in the decision.
- The single largest component of the Freyr inducements were about $228 million in local property tax breaks that will stretch over 20 years.
- Coweta County offered Freyr a $20 million grant.
- The state agreed to provide nearly $90 million in sales tax exemptions for qualified machinery to be installed in the factory and construction materials used in building the plant.
- Nearly $11 million in tax savings will come from state tax credits granted for new jobs created.
- A $7 million state grant will offset land costs.
- The Freyr incentive package could grow in value over time, with no value assigned to other potential tax breaks for clean room equipment, energy used in manufacturing and a handful of other incentives based on future expenditures.
- The sprawling Freyr factory, which will be called Giga America, will rise near Amazon and Shopify distribution centers of I-85 near Newnan.
- Sarah Jacobs, president of the Coweta County Development Authority, said Freyr will help add some industrial diversity to an area flush with logistics and shipping companies.
- Within the incentive package offer, Freyr will need to employ 724 workers at the new factory with an average salary of more than $60,000.
- Sarah Jacobs noted that the average salary is 127% of the average wage in Coweta.
- Sarah Jacobs stated: “Those are exactly the kind of jobs we want to attract to the county.”
- Coweta County and Arizona were the final two options Freyr considered for its factory location.
- The Freyr incentives are large but pale by comparison to inducements offered for EV factories like Hyundai Motor Group ($1.8 billion) and Rivian ($1.5 billion).
- Greg LeRoy questioned why Georgia is willing to financially incentivize more job creation when unemployment remains near record-lows.
- Greg LeRoy stated: “The vast majority of job takers will be people coming from elsewhere, even people moving into the labor market.”
- Greg LeRoy said it’ll be nearly impossible for each new Freyr employee to generate enough local spending and tax revenue to justify the incentives averaging roughly $490,000 per job.
- Freyr was founded in 2018 and is also building a plant in Norway.
- Freyr’s energy storage systems use lithium-ion batteries to store electricity from renewable forms of energy, such as wind and solar.
- Freyr, publicly traded since last year, reported a $93.9 million net loss in the third quarter.
- The company and project are supported by Koch Industries, a company better known for fossil fuels and leaders who have funded groups that questioned climate change.
- Koch has emerged as a major player in battery technology, and the project will benefit from incentives in President Joe Biden’s signature climate bill, the Inflation Reduction Act.
- The 870,000-square-foot facility is expected to open in 2026, according to the incentives package.
- Freyr’s first phase will focus on initial battery cell production, and a second phase of cell production is expected to be ready by 2029.
- Pat Wilson said Freyr will attract companies that produce battery parts such as cathodes and anodes.
- Pat Wilson stated: “This gives us a great opportunity to go recruit those pieces as well.”
- On Wednesday relative to the publication date, Qcells confirmed a $2.5 billion expansion of Georgia production in what officials called the largest ever investment in clean energy manufacturing in U.S. history.
- The total value of Freyr's incentives is $358.5 million, including approximately $228 million in local property tax breaks over 20 years, nearly $90 million in sales tax exemptions on machinery and construction materials, a $20 million local quality job creation grant, $11 million in state quality job creation credits, a $7 million Regional Economic Business Assistance (REBA) grant, and $3.3 million in Georgia Quick Start funding.
- The total incentive value does not include certain incentives based on future potential expenditures.
