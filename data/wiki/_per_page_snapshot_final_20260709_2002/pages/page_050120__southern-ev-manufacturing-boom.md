# Southern EV Manufacturing Boom

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [New ‘Battery Belt’ Opens Organizing Front In The South - PopularResistance.Org](https://popularresistance.org/new-battery-belt-opens-organizing-front-in-the-south/)  ·  `page_050120`

## Facts

- Hyundai’s first U.S. plant for manufacturing electric vehicles is under construction in Ellabell, Georgia, near Savannah.
- Construction is actively underway at the Toyota Battery mega-site in Liberty, North Carolina.
- Production at the Toyota Battery site is slated to begin in 2025.
- By 2030, when the 7 million-square-foot complex is fully operational, it will have 14 production lines.
- The Toyota Battery complex will feature 10 production lines dedicated to batteries for electric vehicles and plug-in hybrid electrics.
- The Toyota Battery complex will feature four production lines for hybrid electric vehicles.
- The Toyota Battery complex will be operated by 5,100 workers.
- The total population of Liberty, North Carolina is 2,655.
- The Toyota Battery mega-site carries a staggering $13.9 billion price tag.
- Federal and state governments are driving a manufacturing boom across nearly every aspect of the EV supply and production chain in the South.
- In Kings Mountain, North Carolina, two companies backed by a $90 million grant from the Department of Defense plan to drain a former open-pit lithium mine lake and resume mining.
- The Kings Mountain lithium mining project expects to yield enough lithium for 1.2 million electric vehicles per year.
- This lithium will be refined at a soon-to-open $1.3 billion facility in nearby Chester County, South Carolina.
- Much of the dramatic expansion in investment is attributed to the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) passed in 2022.
- The IRA provided tremendous financial incentives, largely through grants and tax credits, to encourage cleaner energy production.
- State governments are offering record-breaking incentive deals to EV manufacturers including tax breaks, infrastructure development, and workforce development funding.
- Georgia will provide Hyundai up to $2.1 billion in tax breaks and other state assistance for its $7.9 billion capital investment in an EV and battery production complex outside Savannah.
- The Hyundai complex in Georgia is forecast to employ up to 8,500 workers.
- The IRA aims to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and slow climate change while competing with China, which currently produces the most EVs at the lowest prices.
- Stephen Roach, former chair of Morgan Stanley Asia, stated in July that the U.S. wants to contain China even if it comes at the expense of addressing climate change.
- The Biden administration recently imposed massive tariffs on imports from China related to various aspects of EV production.
- These policies aim to create an entirely domestic supply and production chain for electric vehicles.
- Since the passage of the IRA, companies have announced $110 billion in capital investments in new EV manufacturing.
- Roughly half of that money and the corresponding jobs are destined for the U.S. South according to the EV Jobs Hub.
- Eighty-five percent of the investments are concentrated in right-to-work states.
- S&P Global Market Intelligence estimates that as many as two-thirds of EV jobs may eventually be located in the South.
- Michigan tops the list in EV capital investments, followed closely by Georgia, North Carolina, and Tennessee with $23 billion, $20 billion, and $17 billion respectively.
- In Tennessee, a joint venture between Ford and SK On is investing $5.6 billion to develop BlueOval City outside of Memphis.
- BlueOval City is a six-square-mile complex where 6,000 workers will produce EVs and batteries.
- The company boasts that BlueOval City will be among the largest auto manufacturing facilities in U.S. history.
- The site will receive batteries from a BlueOval SK Battery Park in Kentucky.
- The BlueOval SK Battery Park in Kentucky is a $5.8 billion project that will employ another 5,000 people.
- Outside of Columbia, South Carolina, Scout Motors (owned and operated by Volkswagen) recently broke ground on a site it has invested $2 billion into.
- The Scout Motors site will be operational in 2026.
- Once operational, the Scout Motors site will employ 4,000 workers and produce 200,000 vehicles a year.
- South Carolina is the country’s number one exporter of tires and home to BMW’s massive 11,000-worker plant in Spartanburg.
- The BMW Spartanburg plant will soon begin receiving batteries from a nearby facility operated by AESC.
- In April, AESC announced an expansion adding a second battery production facility for a total investment of $3.1 billion.
- Auto and other industries have been steadily increasing their footprint in Southern states for several decades due to the region's pro-business climate, lowest union density, lowest wages, and stringent anti-labor laws.
- Georgia Economic Development Commissioner Pat Wilson told the Financial Times last October that massive labor stoppages in traditional union states will benefit Southern states as companies look to grow.
- In March, Georgia passed a law forbidding companies that receive state incentives from recognizing a union by any method other than secret ballot elections.
- South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster pledged in his State of the State address this year to fight unions "to the gates of hell."
- In a joint statement meant to deter Chattanooga Volkswagen workers from organizing, McMaster and five other Southern governors contended that the UAW drive threatens their values and would put their states' jobs in jeopardy.
- Nearly every new foreign-owned auto plant that has opened in the U.S. since the 1990s has chosen the South for its base of operations.
- The Midwest's share of automotive jobs has fallen from around 60 percent to around 45 percent, while the South’s share grew from 15 percent in 1990 to 30 percent today.
- The South is home to 60 percent of Black workers and a growing population of immigrant workers.
- Nearly two-thirds of plant construction investments in Southern states are for manufacturing in general, not just EVs.
- The UAW has committed $40 million over the next two years to organize 13 non-union automakers largely concentrated in the South.
- Workers at Ultium Cells in Lordstown, Ohio, which manufactures batteries for General Motors, recently won a strong agreement providing the UAW’s initial step into the EV industry.
- Chattanooga Volkswagen workers won their union vote overwhelmingly in April.
- Several weeks later, Daimler Truck North America workers in North Carolina, Georgia, and Tennessee won a record contract.
- Workers at Mercedes in Alabama came up short in their May vote on unionizing.
- Last year, workers in Georgia at Blue Bird, the country’s second-largest bus manufacturer which includes electric buses among its vehicles, won a victory to join the Steel Workers (USW).
- Workers at New Flyer in Alabama, another manufacturer of electrified buses, voted yes this year for the Industrial Division of the Communications Workers (IUE-CWA).
- The Southern Workers Assembly, a cross-sector rank-and-file network, has recently launched a program aimed at getting workers jobs in strategic sectors to organize.
