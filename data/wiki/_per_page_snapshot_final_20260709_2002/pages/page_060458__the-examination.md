# The Examination

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_recycling  ·  **Published:** 2026-07-07

**Source:** [Tracing Lead Pollution in Nigeria to the Global Auto Supply Chain – Global Investigative Journalism Network](https://gijn.org/stories/tracing-lead-pollution-nigeria-global-car-supply-chain/)  ·  `page_060458`

## Facts

- Vernon, Southern California had high lead levels principally caused by a major battery recycling plant decommissioned in 2015.
- Soil samples at Vernon’s preschool taken in 2014 contained lead levels of 95 parts per million.
- Cleanup efforts around Vernon are still ongoing more than 10 years after the contamination was discovered.
- Lead pollution measured in Ogijo, Nigeria reached 1,900 parts per million, which is 20 times the amount recorded in Vernon’s soil.
- Ogijo has more lead recycling factories than anywhere else in Africa.
- Lead recycled in Ogijo from used car batteries is shipped to supply companies including Tesla, Ford, and General Motors.
- More than half of the children tested in Ogijo registered blood lead levels that could cause lifelong brain damage.
- All recycling factory workers in Ogijo had blood lead levels exceeding the World Health Organization’s threshold of five micrograms per deciliter for serious health risks.
- The investigation was published in November 2025 as a series of articles, videos, and multimedia visuals.
- Will Fitzgibbon served as the main reporter for The Examination on this investigation.
- Initial data for the topic came from seven university academics throughout Africa who published a paper in 2017.
- In September 2023, Fitzgibbon published a piece reviewing international studies and surveys warning of prevalent worldwide lead contamination.
- In December 2023, Fitzgibbon published a story researched with The Museba Project, Ghana Business News, and Grist focusing on lead pollution caused by battery recycling in Ghana, Cameroon, and the Democratic Republic of Congo.
- The team collaborated with The New York Times and local African outlets including Premium Times, Joy FM, Pambazuko, and Truth Reporting Post.
- US manufacturers have known about lead pollution problems for years but have avoided commitments to using lead certified as safely produced.
- Automakers’ environmental policies aimed at reducing emissions do not include reducing pollution from lead.
- Environmentally responsible battery recyclers in Africa can be pushed out of the business by less scrupulous and cheaper competitors.
- The team used financial resources from The Examination to access trade databases tracing exports from Nigeria, Togo, South Korea, Pennsylvania, and New Jersey.
- Trade data showed a single lead smelter in Ogijo received discarded batteries from abroad, extracted the lead, and shipped it through Trafigura to the port of Baltimore.
- From Baltimore, the recycled metal traveled to East Penn Manufacturing in Lyon Station, Pennsylvania.
- East Penn Manufacturing supplies batteries for carmakers including Tesla, General Motors, and Ford.
- East Penn Manufacturing is one of the world’s largest battery producers.
- After the investigation, East Penn Manufacturing stated it was unaware of lead pollution from its Nigerian suppliers and ended all imports from them.
- The Examination contracted SRADev (Sustainable Research and Action for Environmental Development), a Nigerian nonprofit research group recommended by experts.
- Through SRADev-hosted events, 70 people in the Ogijo community volunteered to have their blood tested.
- SRADev distributed nasal filters to detect lead in the air released by the lead recycler’s smelting process.
- The University of Ibadan collected and analyzed soil samples near factories.
- Olamide Agunbiade is a regional health official quoted by The Examination regarding the study's necessity.
- Impact producer Funmibi Ogunlesi and impact editor Miriam Wells created a WhatsApp group to keep Ogijo community members informed of developments.
- Steve Myers served as the story’s editor for The Examination.
- Nigerian authorities started cataloging the health and environmental damage caused by the factories and promised to close some down.
