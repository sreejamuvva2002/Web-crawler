## Facts

- Yazaki North America is part of the Japanese-based Yazaki Group.
- Yazaki created its Monozukuri Research Laboratory in Canton in 2014 to increase innovation and build customer trust.
- Monozukuri is a Japanese term indicating a way of manufacturing that encompasses a synthesis of technological prowess, know-how and spirit of Japan’s manufacturing practices.
- Lab Director Doug Neely stated the lab researches Industry 4.0 technology for products including wire harnesses, power distribution and control products, connectors, driver information systems, sensors and hybrid and electric vehicle products.
- Industry 4.0 technologies include Internet of Things, Big Data and Analytics, Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning, Cloud computer, Cyber-Physical Systems, Augmented Reality and Virtual Reality, 3D Printing, Robotics and Automation, Automation for supply chain verification, Digital Twin, 5G Technology, and Edge Computing.
- Doug Neely said reducing cost is the No. 1 reason for implementing smart manufacturing tools, with quality being important too.
- Neely stated his department's mission is to research technologies and test them to determine if they reduce costs and improve efficiency in factories.
- The goal of digitization is to bring more confidence and control during product launches.
- Neely noted the team is just a ways off from modeling everything effectively.
- Neely said there is no end in sight to his team’s work because new technologies with smart factory applications are constantly getting invented.
- Neely stated, “How do we know we have arrived at Industry 4.0 compliance? We will never arrive because when we do, we’ll be in developing I5.0.”
- General Motors CEO Mark Reuss suggested capturing knowledge as it happens using video technologies that could include smart phones.
- Reuss’ suggestion was captured in an interview by David E. Cole, founder of the Center for Automotive Research in Ann Arbor and former University of Michigan engineering professor.
- David E. Cole said Mark told him he wanted to capture the innovation of his young engineers who are very tech savvy, even though they may not have a lot of real-world auto manufacturing experience.
- Hitachi Astemo Americas created a Smart Factory Center of Excellence.
- Maurice Henson is Senior Director of Operations – Information Technology at Hitachi Astemo Indiana.
- Henson said the center is composed of cross functional teams where experts specializing in essential areas for Hitachi regularly meet for developing products.
- The Smart Factory Center consolidates various hubs for gathering and spreading knowledge primarily for training purposes, augmented by video training and knowledge capture.
- Henson noted global and local groups have created many Smart Factory-focused videos posted on SharePoint to highlight how Hitachi Astemo is actively pushing forward with Industry 4.0.
- Hitachi Astemo’s Smart Factory initiative uses Amazon Web Services to aggregate data and make it easily available to all employees throughout the global corporation.
- An additional connected factory improvement is currently in development approval to create the Hitachi Astemo Standard to accelerate making data available to connected workers.
- Henson noted Hitachi Astemo is committed to embracing knowledge management as an integral part of its internal processes in response to the demand of I4.0.
- Henson said the company has recognized the significance of data and insights on developing these smart-factory technologies.
- Henson stated, “We’re dedicated to efficiently capturing, sharing and utilizing knowledge to enhance our operations and stay competitive in the ever-evolving manufacturing landscape.”
- Henson added, “We recognize the importance of focusing not only on the tool, but the people and processes needed to manage as an entrepreneur-knowledge portfolio.”
- Sudhanshu Gaur is vice president, R&D at Hitachi America Ltd and chief architect of Smart Manufacturing at Hitachi Astemo.
- Gaur said Hitachi Astemo captures knowledge from its employees using cloud-based platforms and Artificial Intelligence, with insights shared across the organization to enable informed decision making.
- Generative AI enhances information retrieval within this initiative.
- Gaur stated their vision is to provide timely and efficient access to designed information, tailoring the retrieval process to the specific needs and contexts of different teams within their global network.
- Their online Communities of Practice comprises various manufacturing sites that are actively adopting Digital Transformation initiatives, where practitioners from different backgrounds and locations collaborate, exchange ideas, discuss challenges and share success stories.
- Gaur said Hitachi Astemo’s Knowledge Management strategy creates video snapshots of newly developed applications deployed in production operations to demonstrate new ideas and advancements.
- Another example of video use is recording interviews with staff members describing their experiences, insights, and best practices, ranging from engineers discussing complex problem-solving to operators sharing daily routines.
- Gaur noted these videos provide a more personal connection and allow team members across the globe to learn from each other’s unique perspectives.
- Gaur said they encourage teams to create short video snippets when facing specific challenges or issues, which are then shared with relevant experts within the organization to facilitate efficient troubleshooting.
- They are also using AI-driven insights from capture videos to improve production efficiency, automate quality inspections, and augment operators’ daily work routines.
- A Hitachi Astemo spokeswoman said her company is now looking into how to capture knowledge as it happens using video technologies that also could include smart phones.
- Henson believes most Michigan manufacturers will adopt I4.0 technologies.
- Henson said businesses are recognizing the benefits of embracing i4.0 principles to enhance operations, improve efficiency and stay competitive in the global market.
- Michigan is largely known as the home of the US auto industry where recognizing this principle may mean the difference in how companies survive for the next 25 years.
- Henson noted it likely will be necessary for Automakers like General Motors and Ford Motor Company to demand I4.0 compliance by their respective supply chains.
- Gaur echoed that a mandate by OEMs, together with MEDC incentives, can certainly drive compliance and create a standardized approach to adopting I4.0 practices.
- Gaur stated I4.0’s focus on data-driven decision-making means seamless data exchange between manufacturers and OEMs can enhance the entire product lifecycle and potentially unlock new business opportunities.
- Gaur summarized that a healthy business ecosystem is needed that can help set Industry 4.0 implementation goals, provide the right mentorship and training incentives to help realize those goals.
