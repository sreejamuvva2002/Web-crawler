# Superior Essex Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Superior Essex Inc. | Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/books/politics-and-business-magazines/superior-essex-inc)  ·  `page_010435`

## Facts

- Headquartered at 150 Interstate North Parkway, Atlanta, Georgia 30339, U.S.A.
- Public company incorporated in 1930 with 4,100 employees and $1.8 billion in sales (2005).
- Stock trades on NASDAQ under ticker symbol SPSX.
- Classified under NAIC code 331528 Other Nonferrous Foundries.
- Leading manufacturer of cable and wire among the largest industry players in North America with a significant global presence.
- Operates 23 production facilities in the United States, Mexico, and Europe with an established global distribution network.
- Customer base includes cable television and telephone companies, as well as distributors and manufacturers in a variety of industries.
- Offers more than 500 magnet wire and winding wire products marketed through Essex Group Inc. in North America and China.
- Serves the European market through its EssexNexans joint venture.
- Magnet wire products are critical components in millions of items, including automobiles, home appliances, generators, motors, transformers, and commercial machinery.
- World's largest producer of outside plant copper communications cables via Superior Essex Communications LP.
- Serves original equipment manufacturers and the motor repair market via its Essex Brownell, Essex Express, and Active Industries units, which offer various electrical insulation products.
- Formed in 1998 from the combination of Essex Group Inc. and Superior TeleCom.
- Deepest roots trace to Detroit, Michigan, where Addison E. Holton established Essex Wire Corp. in 1930.
- During its formative years, produced battery cables, wiring harnesses, and other wire items for the Ford Model A from leased space in Ford Motor Company's Highland Park plant.
- Acquired Logansport, Indiana-based RBM Manufacturing Company, a producer of electrical switches and automotive components, during the 1930s.
- During the 1940s produced enough magnet wire to build millions of transformers used by America and its allies during World War II.
- Essex plants also produced thousands of miles of field telephone wire for the Army Signal Corps, as well as wiring harnesses for B-24 bombers.
- Awarded the highly prestigious 'E' Award by the Secretary of War.
- Relocated headquarters to Fort Wayne, Indiana in 1954.
- Walter F. Probst succeeded Addison Holton as president in 1958; Holton retired in 1962, at which time Probst assumed board chairman.
- By 1965 sales totaled $355 million with seven divisions, 41 plants throughout North America, and 26 warehouses.
- First listed on the New York Stock Exchange in 1965 with 5 million shares.
- Unveiled an $8 million expansion initiative in 1965, including $3.2 million for its Essex Wire and Cable division, $2.5 million for its Essex Automotive division, and $2.3 million for the acquisition, expansion, and improvement of other facilities.
- Paul W. O'Malley named company president in early 1966 after being with Essex since 1952; Probst continued as chairman and CEO.
- By 1968 in the process of building a new 30,000-square-foot facility in Fort Wayne for its Metal Products division, bringing total plants to 67 in 30 states and Canada.
- Acquired Transport Motor Express Inc., one of the nation's largest trucking companies, in a stock deal valued at $12 million in 1968.
- Essex Wire Corporation operated under the name Essex International Inc. by 1970 with an employee base of 25,700.
- Sales reached approximately $845 million in 1973 while operating about 100 plants.
- Merged with East Hartford, Connecticut-based United Aircraft Corporation (United Technologies Corporation) in 1974, creating the nation's 30th largest company with combined 1973 sales of $3.13 billion.
- Walter Probst retired as chairman and CEO in 1974, succeeded by Paul O'Malley; O'Malley was elected Essex group vice president and director until retiring in 1981.
- James A. O'Connor, a 31-year Essex veteran, succeeded O'Malley in 1981.
- During the late 1970s faced criticism from labor leaders for being one of the most anti-labor companies in the industrial north.
- AFL-CIO criticized the company for encouraging multiple unions to keep labor decentralized and weak, scattering production to diffuse strikes, paying low wages, and providing poor benefits packages.
- In September 1978 the U.S. Justice Department charged Essex and five other wire manufacturers with price fixing.
- Company denied wrongdoing while the agency charged that Essex and its counterparts exchanged information about prices and conspired to restrain trade.
- Founder Addison Holton died at age 83 in 1980.
- In 1982 announced plans to invest $3 million in its Fort Wayne facility, which would become the company's world headquarters, scheduled for completion in 1984.
- By 1983 employed 8,982 workers worldwide with an annual payroll of $26.8 million.
- Became a privately owned firm in 1988 when MS/Essex Holdings bought the company from United Technologies; John M. Bruce was president and CEO at the time.
- Purchased by Bessemer Holdings in 1992 but remained privately owned until becoming publicly traded again in 1997.
- Acquired magnet wire and electrical motor distributor Brownell in 1995, merging it into Essex's distribution arm as Essex Brownell.
- Acquired Active Industries, a converter/fabricator of electrical insulation products, in 1998.
- In September 1998 announced being acquired by Superior Telecom Inc. in a deal valued at $1.4 billion in cash, stock, and debt.
- Acquisition finalized in 1999, making Essex a subsidiary of Superior and creating North America's largest cable and wire manufacturer.
- Essex Chairman, President, and CEO Steven Abbott became Superior's president and chief operating officer following the deal.
- Superior Telecom started in 1954 as Superior Cable Corporation in Hickory, North Carolina, producing plastic insulated, twisted pair cable and wire.
- Production took place in a new 32,000-square-foot plant authorized by Hickory Development Corp. at a cost of $160,000.
- Manufacturing equipment installed in May, with first reel delivered to Hickory Telephone Company in August.
- Attorney Walker Geitner was Superior's first president; named chairman in 1959.
- James L. Robb succeeded Geitner as president after being with the firm since its inception.
- Established a second manufacturing plant in Rocky Mount, North Carolina in 1960.
- By 1962 expanded headquarters plant three times to 150,000 square feet, adding production for coaxial cables and wire for missile, aircraft, and industrial applications.
- In 1963 played a pioneering role in the telephone sector with the roll-out of the industry's first all-plastic drop wire.
- Erected a new cable plant in Brownwood, Texas in 1964 to serve the southwestern United States.
- By 1965 home plant reached 125,000 square feet of manufacturing space, about 35,000 square feet of office space, and a 35,000-square-foot building for warehousing and shipping; annual sales reached nearly $20 million.
- Plans included constructing a separate facility devoted to research and development.
- Acquired by Continental Telephone in 1967 and renamed Superior Continental Corp.
- Annual sales reached $90 million in 1969 and approximately $125 million in 1971 under new ownership.
- Announced plans to build an eight-story corporate office facility in Hickory for $3 million in 1971.
- J. L. Robb became president of manufacturing for Continental Telephone, and Warner T. Smith served as Superior Continental's president.
- Hickory manufacturing plant grew to 600,000 square feet.
- Divested by Continental Telephone in 1976; Robert C. Pittman and A. L. Viles purchased it for $17.1 million in a cash and stock deal.
- Reincorporated as parent company of Superior Cable facilities in Hickory and Rocky Mount, North Carolina, plus Brownwood, Texas plant.
- Deal included Hickory Brand Telephone Cords, Keller, Texas-based Communication Apparatus Company, and a non-operating plant in Angier, North Carolina.
- Acquired by optical fiber and cable manufacturer Siecor Corporation in 1980; Siecor relocated from Horseheads, New York to Hickory in 1981.
- Divested by Siecor five years later (1985) as an independent firm marketing its own line of fiber optic cable.
- In 1987 Siecor convinced Japan to purchase some of its fiber optical cable after five years of negotiating.
- Alpine Group Inc. acquired Superior in 1993, adding premises data cables to its product lineup and acquiring other firms.
- Superior TeleCom Inc. went public in 1996 with a listing on the New York Stock Exchange; annual sales reached $500 million in 1997.
- Held about 30 percent of the market for copper building wire by August 1999.
- Announced plans to close three wire manufacturing plants and lay off 469 employees in order to optimize financial returns in a highly competitive market suffering from oversupply.
- Closed plants in Glendale, Arizona; Pauline, Kansas; and Tiffin, Ohio while continuing production at sites in Sikeston, Missouri; Florence, Alabama; Anaheim, California; Lafayette, Indiana; and Columbia City, Indiana.
- In 2000 awarded a three-year contract to supply cable and wire to Alltel Communications Products Inc., which served 8.5 million customers in 25 states with both wireless and wired communication services.
- Deal involved Superior Essex supplying Alltel with all of its cable and wire.
- Launched an e-commerce Web site in late 2000 to boost sales, allowing order entry, shipment tracking, estimated arrival times, and industry news feeds.
- By December 2001 occupied a 228,000-square-foot warehousing and distribution facility in northeast Indiana previously occupied by defense giant Raytheon.
- Maintained a workforce of 5,000 in 2003, giving employees better benefits without increasing out-of-pocket contributions despite national healthcare cost trends.
- Streamlined benefit packages and renegotiated contracts with healthcare providers to achieve this.
- Stephen M. Carter was CEO as of early 2004.
- In April 2004 announced plans to acquire the North American outside plant communications cable and wire operations of Belden Inc. for approximately $95 million.
- Purchase freed Belden from a market where it had not been able to turn a profit for several years.
- Carter stated the acquisition would align capacity in the outside plant copper communications cable industry with demand, which declined 50 percent in North America since 2000.
- Consolidated manufacturing facilities and reduced capacity but needed to look beyond internal solutions to serve customers efficiently.
- Confident they could smoothly transition Belden's customer base, which last year generated approximately $200 million in revenues, without disruption.
- In February 2006 announced revenues reached $1.8 billion in 2005, up from $1.4 billion the previous year.
- Carter attributed increased revenues to consolidating acquisitions and pointed to increased earnings despite skyrocketing energy costs.
- Principal subsidiaries include Superior Essex Communications LP and Essex Group, Inc.
- Principal competitors include Alcatel, APA Cables and Networks Inc., and General Cable Corporation.
