# Georgia State and Federal Incentive Programs for EV Manufacturing

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Georgia Grants in 2026: JDIG Southern Cousin, GDEcD Incentives, and the Billion-Dollar EV Belt | Funding Landscape](https://fundinglandscape.com/answers/georgia-grants-funding-guide-2026)  ·  `page_008277`

## Facts

- Georgia historically kept state grant programs lean and relied primarily on tax incentives, workforce training, and direct capital projects to attract business.
- The state does not have a GoldenLEAF equivalent or a large discretionary development foundation.
- Georgia's funding architecture includes the Quality Jobs Tax Credit (QJTC), Qualified Opportunity Zone tax credits, OneGeorgia Authority, and Georgia Department of Community Affairs (GDCA).
- The arrival of Rivian, Hyundai Metaplant, SK Innovation battery plants in Commerce, Jackson, and Covington, and a dozen Korean and German automotive suppliers changed the game for Georgia's rural counties.
- Federal co-investment including the CHIPS Act, IRA manufacturing credits, and DOE loan programs are all active in Georgia.
- Bryan County went from rural coastal county to one of the largest EV manufacturing hubs in the Western Hemisphere in 18 months.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development (GDEcD) is the primary state agency for business attraction and retention incentives.
- Georgia's main incentive mechanism is the Quality Jobs Tax Credit (QJTC), not a cash grant.
- The QJTC provides a per-employee credit against Georgia income tax for companies creating at least 50 jobs paying 10% above the county average wage.
- QJTC credits range from $1,250 to $4,000 per job per year for 5 years, depending on county tier.
- In distressed Tier 1 counties, a company creating 100 jobs at $60,000 average salary could earn $400,000 per year in tax credits for five years, or $2 million total.
- The QJTC functions as a grant equivalent by reducing state tax liability rather than providing cash-in-hand.
- OneGeorgia Authority is the primary vehicle for cash grants and was created to bridge development gaps between metro Atlanta and rural Georgia.
- OneGeorgia operates Equity Fund grants that go to local governments for infrastructure supporting job-creating projects.
- Water, sewer, broadband, rail spurs, and road improvements qualify for OneGeorgia Equity Fund grants.
- OneGeorgia Equity Fund awards typically range from $500,000 to $3 million and require a matched private investment commitment.
- Applications for OneGeorgia Equity Fund go through regional development centers (RDCs), not directly to the state.
- OneGeorgia Frontier Fund grants address the most severely distressed counties with fewer than 50,000 residents.
- The 50 most distressed Georgia counties are eligible for OneGeorgia Frontier Fund assistance.
- Frontier Fund can provide slightly more flexible assistance including direct support for industrial building construction.
- GDEcD administers the Competitive Grant Program for Rural Counties through the OneGeorgia framework as a closing fund for rural projects.
- QuickStart is Georgia's employer-funded workforce training program administered by the Technical College System of Georgia (TCSG).
- QuickStart provides custom job training at no cost to qualifying new or expanding businesses and has been running since 1967.
- Qualification for QuickStart requires creating a minimum number of new jobs, paying wages above a county baseline, and operating in an eligible sector like manufacturing, distribution, IT, or headquarters operations.
- Retail, food service, and hospitality do not qualify for QuickStart.
- Businesses using QuickStart receive custom curriculum developed by TCSG instructors, training facilities (including on-site), instructors and equipment, and sometimes paid trainees during the training period.
- For rural counties, QuickStart training combined with OneGeorgia infrastructure grants creates a package that lowers startup costs substantially.
- The Hyundai Metaplant in Bryan County is receiving QuickStart training for its 8,500-job workforce.
- Porsche's US headquarters training and Yamaha's manufacturing training have previously run through QuickStart.
- Georgia is one of the top-five IRA beneficiary states because of its advanced manufacturing base.
- The IRA's Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit applies to clean energy components made in the US.
- SK Innovation's battery plants in Commerce, Jackson, and Covington qualify for IRA Section 45X credits.
- Rivian's plant in Statesboro and Hyundai Metaplant qualify for related IRA credits.
- These IRA credits are worth hundreds of millions of dollars annually for manufacturers operating at scale.
- The DOE Loan Programs Office (LPO) Title 17 Clean Energy Financing Program provides loans at below-market rates for clean energy manufacturing projects in Georgia.
- The minimum project size for DOE LPO loans is $5 million.
- Georgia-based EV component suppliers, battery material processors, and clean energy equipment manufacturers are eligible for DOE LPO programs.
- The EPA's Clean Heavy-Duty Vehicles program under the IRA has made awards in Georgia for fleet electrification targeting school districts and port authorities with diesel fleets.
- Georgia Ports Authority, which operates Savannah the second-largest container port in the US, received infrastructure funding related to electrification under this program.
- SBIR programs at DOD, DOE, and NIH are available to Georgia companies with no special state registration required.
- Georgia Tech's Enterprise Innovation Institute (EI2) provides specific support for Georgia small businesses pursuing federal SBIR awards, including proposal assistance and match funding.
- EI2 is part of Georgia Tech's MEP affiliate and provides fee-based consulting that DOE MEP partially subsidizes.
- The CHIPS and Science Act Section 9003 provided $500 million for semiconductor workforce training nationally.
- Georgia Tech received funding for its semiconductor workforce programs through the CHIPS Act.
- Wolfspeed has explored Georgia expansions for its semiconductor facility.
- The Georgia Department of Community Affairs (GDCA) administers federally funded community development programs across the state as pass-through federal programs.
- Community Development Block Grants (CDBG) in Georgia flow to non-entitlement communities: cities under 50,000 population and counties that are not Fulton, DeKalb, Gwinnett, or Cobb.
- GDCA runs competitive CDBG rounds for water and sewer infrastructure, economic development, housing rehabilitation, and community facilities.
- A typical rural Georgia county can receive $500,000 to $1.5 million per CDBG award.
- CDBG applications open annually, usually in spring.
- HOME Investment Partnerships Program funds flow through GDCA to local governments and Community Housing Development Organizations (CHDOs) for affordable housing construction and rehabilitation.
- HOME awards typically range from $250,000 to $750,000 per project.
- Atlanta, Augusta, Savannah, and Macon apply for HOME funds directly to HUD rather than through GDCA.
- The State Small Business Credit Initiative (SSBCI) allocated $112 million to Georgia.
- Georgia's SSBCI program operates through the Georgia Loans for Underserved Business (GLUE) network, providing access to capital for minority-owned and socioeconomically disadvantaged businesses.
- SSBCI in Georgia is administered through approved financial institutions rather than directly by the state.
- GDCA runs the Broadband Deployment Initiative for rural Georgia communities as a planning grant and funding guidance program to help counties access USDA ReConnect and FCC Rural Digital Opportunity Fund money.
- Applications for most GDCA programs go through Georgia's Grant Management System.
- Invest Atlanta is Atlanta's primary economic development authority managing Tax Allocation Districts (TADs), business retention incentives, and small business financing.
- The Westside TAD covers English Avenue, Vine City, and neighborhoods adjacent to Mercedes-Benz Stadium.
- The Westside TAD captures increment property tax revenue and reinvests it in the district.
- Businesses locating or expanding in the Westside TAD can access facade improvement grants typically ranging from $5,000 to $25,000.
- Tenant improvement grants for commercial build-outs are available in the Westside TAD.
- Invest Atlanta also manages the Atlanta Affordable Housing Fund, a revolving loan fund that provides below-market financing for affordable housing development.
- The Atlanta Affordable Housing Fund targets projects serving households at 60% AMI or below.
- The City of Atlanta's Office of Small Business and Entrepreneurship provides grants through the Small Business COVID Recovery and Resiliency Grant Program which is active in limited form in 2026.
- APD Urban LLC is a public-private partnership focused on Pittsburgh neighborhood redevelopment administering grants for small business tenants in the Pittsburgh Yards development.
- Pittsburgh Yards is a 31-acre mixed-use site in southwest Atlanta.
- APD Urban LLC grants range from $5,000 to $50,000 for qualifying businesses in creative industries, food and beverage, and light manufacturing.
- Georgia's 159 counties include dozens that are persistently poor and receive disproportionate federal rural development funding.
- The Appalachian Regional Commission covers 35 Georgia counties in the northern mountains.
- ARC grants in Georgia typically go to workforce training programs, broadband infrastructure, and healthcare access in mountain counties like Rabun, Towns, Gilmer, and Pickens.
- ARC allocated approximately $12 million to Georgia projects in FY2024.
- USDA Rural Development has a Georgia state office in Athens with active programs including Community Facilities Direct Loans and Grants for hospitals, libraries, schools, and emergency services.
- USDA Rural Development offers Water and Environmental Program grants for water and sewer infrastructure in rural communities.
- The Rural Energy for America Program (REAP) provides grants for agricultural producers and rural small businesses making energy improvements.
- REAP grants cover 25% of the cost of qualifying renewable energy systems or energy efficiency improvements.
- A rural Georgia agricultural processor installing solar panels on a 5,000 sq ft facility might receive a $15,000-$40,000 REAP grant alongside a REAP loan covering another 50% of the cost.
- Applications for REAP open annually and deadlines vary by fiscal year.
- The Community Foundation for Greater Atlanta makes grants through several funds to nonprofits in a 23-county metro area.
- The Goizueta Foundation focuses on Hispanic/Latino communities in metro Atlanta and provides grants from $25,000 to $250,000 to nonprofits serving those communities.
- The Woodruff Foundation is the largest private grant maker in Georgia with roughly $100 million in annual grants for education, health, and community development.
- The Woodruff Foundation is invitation-only and does not accept unsolicited applications.
- South Arts is headquartered in Atlanta and administers Jazz Road Creative Residencies, Jazz Road Tours, Southern Circuit filmmaker grants, and the Southern Prize and State Fellowships across the 9-state South Arts region.
- Current open deadlines include June 30, 2026 for Jazz Road Creative Residencies and the Southern Circuit call for screening partners runs through April 2027.
