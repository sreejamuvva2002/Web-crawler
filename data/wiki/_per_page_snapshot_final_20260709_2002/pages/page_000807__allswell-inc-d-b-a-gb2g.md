# Allswell, Inc. d/b/a GB2G

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** workforce_training  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Engineers from Mexico say they were misled into manual labor in Georgia factories](https://www.ajc.com/news/georgia-news/a-total-lie-mexican-engineers-say-they-were-misled-into-manual-labor-in-ga-factories/CB44GCXWARFL7FLICASGYRAS24/)  ·  `page_000807`

## Facts

- Six Mexican nationals were brought to the U.S. under false pretenses, with engineering job offers from a staffing agency serving as smoke screens for low-paid assembly-line work in Georgia factories.
- The visa program used was the Trade NAFTA (TN) visa, intended for high-skilled jobs but lacking oversight and linked to Southeast auto sector worker exploitation.
- The company that hired the Mexican nationals and supported their TN visa applications is a Newnan staffing agency, GB2G, Inc., also known as Allswell.
- Workers say Allswell hired them to work for several different auto parts manufacturers in the West Point area that supply the Kia auto plant there.
- Allswell has not been charged with any wrongdoing and claims it provides engineering staffing solutions while being in full compliance with all laws, citing federal on-site inspections that found no violations.
- Only one of the six interviewed Mexican professionals filed a formal complaint against the company; others preferred to move on rather than initiate lengthy legal processes due to uncertain immigration status.
- During his first night in Georgia, worker Luis was promised housing but instead shared a house floor with seven other TN workers.
- The six Mexican nationals arrived between December 2020 and May 2021, during a pandemic-driven labor shortage that made low-wage jobs harder to fill with U.S. workers.
- Shelly Anand, executive director of the Sur Legal Collaborative, noted employers are seeking more compliant labor forces in high-turnover industries during this economic moment.
- Ben Botts, legal director at Centro de los Derechos del Migrante (CDM), highlighted that TN visa fraud allegations in the West Point auto sector are notable given the industry's visibility as a model for rural economic development.
- In April, a class action lawsuit filed in Atlanta federal court claimed a Gwinnett-based labor recruiter used the TN visa program to hire dozens of Mexican engineers who performed manual labor in Luverne, Alabama.
- The lead plaintiff in the Alabama case is Jaime Obregon Acosta, a Mexican national with a bachelor’s in mechanical engineering and a master’s in business administration, alleging horrendously long hours and hourly wages a fraction of U.S. citizens'.
- Over 40 workers hired through the visa program have joined the Alabama class action lawsuit.
- Civil rights litigator Daniel Werner stated he frequently encounters visa misclassification cases aimed at lowering labor costs.
- Mexican engineers interviewed by the AJC provided documentation showing they worked at three parts makers supplying automaker Kia in West Point: Hyundai Mobis, Hyundai Glovis, and Mando.
- Allswell's website lists Hyundai Mobis, Hyundai Glovis, and Mando as part of its client roster; all are South Korean auto parts manufacturers with facilities in the West Point area.
- Hyundai Mobis and Mando did not respond to requests for comment.
- The head of Glovis Georgia’s HR department stated her company does not work with Allswell, suggesting another subsidiary, Glovis America, may have received the workers.
- Kia Georgia stated it is committed to equal employment opportunities and expects the same principles from its business partners.
- TN visa status is only for Canadian or Mexican nationals intending temporary U.S. work, requiring a job offer under 60 NAFTA professions that typically require at least a bachelor’s degree.
- In fiscal year 2021, the U.S. issued 24,904 TN visas, up from 16,119 four years prior, with Mexico accounting for 98.8% of total visas issued to TN workers and dependents that year.
- The State Department operates the TN program for Mexican applicants and claims to work aggressively to ensure visa adjudication integrity through extensive vetting.
- Unlike the H-2A farmworker program, which has labor protections and investigations by the U.S. Labor Department’s Wage and Hour division, TN workers lack equivalent labor protections and reporting mechanisms.
- Because a TN visa is tied to the sponsoring employer, quitting for a better job in the U.S. can be difficult, leaving workers stuck.
- In fall 2020, Heber Zapata saw a LinkedIn posting from Georgia labor recruitment agency SPJ Connect for U.S.-based engineering roles and moved nearly 2,000 miles to take it.
- Zapata was offered a $44,000 salary as a "production engineer" with benefits exceeding his Mexican earnings, supported by an Allswell letter describing the job as professional and specialized.
- The AJC reviewed six TN visa support letters showing five men were hired at Hyundai Mobis and one at Mando, with offered salaries ranging from $40,000 to $44,000 a year.
- Zapata worked overnight shifts (7 p.m. to 7 a.m.) on the assembly line at Hyundai Mobis, earning roughly $580 a week (just over $30,000 annually), compared to American workers performing similar work who earned higher hourly wages.
- Zapata filed reports with LaGrange Police Department (July 24, 2021 incident report), the National Human Trafficking Hotline, and U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) but received no follow-up action.
- Eight months after arriving in West Point, Zapata left Allswell in August 2021 and applied for a visa granting legal status to human trafficking victims; his allegations remain under investigation.
- Labor recruiter SPJ Connect continues advertising vacancies via social media videos pitching TN visa benefits to Mexican university students.
- During an April 8 visit to Universidad Tecnológica de Huejotzingo, SPJ Connect executive Yadira Leos told students the company has roughly 500 monthly vacancies and emphasized professional growth in the U.S.
- SPJ Connect colleague Elizabeth Cantú stated in a deleted video that all recruited jobs are in the U.S. automotive industry and "Everyone goes to the production line the first year" regardless of job title.
- Allswell is registered in Coweta County and provides workers to Georgia-based automotive manufacturers; it should not be confused with All’s Well, a healthcare staffing firm in California, South Carolina, Nevada, Florida, and Pennsylvania.
- SPJ Connect is based in Hogansville with offices in Mexico and served as the first point of contact for the interviewed workers.
- Hyundai Mobis in West Point builds chassis, bumpers, and cockpit modules for nearby Kia; a 2018 Select Georgia report identified it as a top auto employer with nearly 850 workers.
- SPJ Connect and Allswell present as separate entities but share the same registered agent address (124 Big Horn Drive in Newnan) per Georgia Secretary of State records, with Youngjin Lee listed as CEO of SPJ Connect and secretary of Allswell.
- LaGrange city officials boosted recruitment efforts; in July 2021, two Economic Development Office officers participated in a SPJ Connect recruiting trip to a Mexican university.
- Three months prior, a consortium of Mexican technical colleges signed a collaboration agreement with SPJ Connect for graduate placement, hosted by the LaGrange Economic Development Office.
- LaGrange Mayor James Thornton stated industries must recruit internationally when local hiring falls short but emphasized creating jobs for residents as a primary economic development goal.
- Tension exists on assembly lines regarding foreign workers; Augusto noted that plants with Mexican workers know locals earn much more, and some locals told the migrants to "Get out of here."
- Jose, an industrial engineering graduate, worked as a production supervisor in Tijuana earning roughly $850 per week before accepting a 2020 production engineer position at Hyundai Mobis on a TN visa.
- The AJC story originated from April 2022 reporting on the Alabama class action suit, which prompted six former Allswell employees to contact the newspaper with similar experiences and documentation.
