# Allswell (GB2G)

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** workforce_training  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [‘A total lie’: Mexican engineers say they were misled into manual labor in Ga. factories - Human Trafficking Search](https://humantraffickingsearch.org/resource/a-total-lie-mexican-engineers-say-they-were-misled-into-manual-labor-in-ga-factories/)  ·  `page_001221`

## Facts

- Georgia-based companies recruited Mexican college graduates for engineering work but allegedly had them perform assembly line work instead.
- Mechanical engineer Luis accepted a quality engineering role in West Point, Georgia, and arrived at Hartsfield-Jackson International Airport in December 2020.
- Upon arrival, Luis was told by his new employer that the engineering job he uprooted his life to take did not exist.
- For the next 11 months, Luis worked 12-hour shifts on a Georgia auto parts manufacturer factory floor, carrying heavy loads over 100 pounds.
- Six Mexican nationals told The Atlanta Journal-Constitution they were brought to the U.S. under false pretenses with engineering job offers from a staffing agency as smoke screens for low-paid assembly-line work.
- The visa used by the workers is the Trade NAFTA (TN) visa, which is meant for high-skilled jobs and does not cover assembly line work.
- The company that hired the Mexican nationals and supported their TN visa applications is Newnan staffing agency GB2G, Inc., also known as Allswell.
- Workers say Allswell hired them to work for several different auto parts manufacturers in the West Point area that supply the Kia auto plant there.
- Allswell has not been charged with wrongdoing and claims full compliance with federal, state, and local laws, stating federal agents found no violations during on-site inspections.
- Only one Mexican professional filed a formal complaint against Allswell; others preferred to move on due to lengthy legal processes and immigration status risks.
- Luis was promised housing but instead shared a house floor with seven other TN workers upon arrival.
- The six Mexican nationals arrived between December 2020 and May 2021 during a pandemic-induced labor shortage that made low-wage jobs harder to fill with U.S. workers.
- Shelly Anand, executive director of Sur Legal Collaborative, noted employers are seeking more compliant labor forces due to high turnover in certain industries.
- Ben Botts, legal director at Centro de los Derechos del Migrante (CDM), noted allegations of TN visa fraud in the West Point auto sector are not unusual and highlighted concerns about sourcing parts from producers engaging in unlawful practices.
- In April, a class action lawsuit filed in Atlanta federal court claimed a Gwinnett-based labor recruiter used the TN visa program to hire dozens of Mexican engineers for manual labor in Luverne, Alabama.
- Lead plaintiff Jaime Obregon Acosta holds a bachelor's in mechanical engineering and master's in business administration; over 40 hired through the visa joined the suit.
- Civil rights litigator Daniel Werner cited cases of visa misclassification aimed at lowering labor costs.
- Workers hung on for months due to pandemic difficulties finding jobs in Mexico and needing money, with some feeling ashamed to return home.
- Luis said workers were treated like animals and pack mules, while Augusto called the job offer a total lie claiming companies deceive the U.S. government about bringing qualified personnel.
- Immigration attorney Charles Kuck stated that if true, the experience could amount to human trafficking; one engineer applied for protection under these laws.
- Auto parts manufacturers might not be responsible for TN visa violations since they used a third-party staffing firm to shield themselves from liability and compliance costs.
- Charlotte Alexander, associate professor at Georgia State University’s College of Law, noted employers use third-party agencies to avoid compliance costs and liability.
- Documentation showed Mexican professionals worked at three parts makers supplying Kia in West Point: Hyundai Mobis, Hyundai Glovis, and Mando.
- Allswell's website lists Hyundai Mobis, Hyundai Glovis, and Mando as clients; they are South Korean auto parts manufacturers with facilities in West Point.
- Mobis and Mando did not respond to comment requests.
- Head of Glovis Georgia HR denied working with Allswell, suggesting Glovis America might have received the workers.
- Kia Georgia stated it is committed to equal employment opportunities and expects the same from business partners.
- Allswell should not be confused with All’s Well, a healthcare staffing firm with branches in California, South Carolina, Nevada, Florida, and Pennsylvania.
- TN visa status is for Canadian or Mexican nationals working temporarily in the U.S. under 60 NAFTA professions requiring at least a bachelor's degree.
- In fiscal year 2021, the U.S. issued 24,904 TN visas, up from 16,119 four years prior; Mexico accounted for 98.8% of total TN visas and dependents in 2021.
- The State Department operates the TN program for Mexican applicants and claims aggressive vetting, but it does not collect or publish data on TN visas by destination state or employer.
- In fall 2020, Heber Zapata saw a LinkedIn posting from Georgia labor recruitment agency SPJ Connect for U.S.-based engineering roles.
- Zapata was offered a $44,000 salary as a production engineer with benefits exceeding his Mexican earnings.
- Allswell sent a visa support letter stating the job was professional and specialized, requiring complex engineering knowledge.
- Charles Kuck noted the $44,000 offer was a red flag; average annual salary for mechanical or industrial engineers in West Point is roughly $80,000.
- AJC reviewed six TN visa support letters showing five men were hired at Hyundai Mobis and one at Mando, with salaries ranging from $40,000 to $44,000.
- Zapata worked overnight shifts (7 p.m. to 7 a.m.) on the assembly line at the Hyundai Mobis plant.
- Zapata earned roughly $580 per week (~$30,000/year), while American workers at the same plant performed similar work for higher hourly wages.
- Zapata filed reports with LaGrange Police Department on July 24, 2021, the National Human Trafficking Hotline, and ICE; no action was noted by police.
- Eight months after arriving in West Point, Zapata left Allswell in August 2021 and applied for a visa granting legal status to human trafficking victims.
- His trafficking allegations are still under investigation.
- SPJ Connect continues advertising vacancies via social media videos pitching TN visa benefits to Mexican university students, with executive Yadira Leos noting roughly 500 monthly vacancies.
- On April 8, Leos addressed Universidad Tecnológica de Huejotzingo, emphasizing personal and professional growth for workers.
- Colleague Elizabeth Cantú stated in a deleted video that everyone goes to the production line the first year regardless of job title.
- Allswell is registered in Coweta County; SPJ Connect is based in Hogansville with offices in Mexico.
- SPJ Connect and Allswell share the same registered agent address: 124 Big Horn Drive in Newnan, with Youngjin Lee listed as CEO of SPJ Connect and secretary of Allswell.
- Georgia's auto industry boom transformed rural areas, but migrant workers might mean Georgians don't reap full economic benefits from locally located jobs.
- Leos stated employers prefer TN employees over locals once familiar with Mexican work culture, repeating this message on June 13 Facebook Live.
- LaGrange city officials boosted recruitment efforts; two Economic Development Office officers participated in a July 2021 recruiting trip to Mexico.
- A consortium of Mexican technical colleges signed a collaboration agreement with SPJ Connect three months prior to the July 2021 event.
- LaGrange Mayor James Thornton stated industries must recruit internationally when local jobs cannot be filled locally.
- Tension exists on assembly lines; Augusto noted comments about Mexican workers taking jobs and earning more than locals.
- Jose, an industrial engineer from Sinaloa, worked as a production supervisor in Tijuana earning ~$850/week before accepting a 2020 position at Hyundai Mobis.
- The AJC withheld Jose's last name due to immigration status uncertainty after he left Hyundai Mobis but remained in the U.S.
- The story originated from an April 2022 AJC publication about a class action suit, leading six former Allswell employees to contact the newspaper with similar experiences.
