# Georgia Electric Car Charging Network

**Type:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Role:** charging_infrastructure  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Georgia Electric Car Charging Network Guide 2025–2026 | Recharged](https://recharged.com/articles/georgia-electric-car-charging-network/)  ·  `page_060643`

## Facts

- Georgia is turning into a manufacturing and logistics hub for electric mobility, with major investments by automakers and battery suppliers.
- The state is rapidly stitching together a robust web of Level 2 and DC fast chargers between Georgia Power, federal NEVI funding, and private networks.
- Georgia Power operates community Level 2 and DC fast chargers across the state with kWh-based pricing.
- Under the Georgia Power Make Ready Infrastructure Program, for business, municipal, and multifamily sites installing six or more ports or at least one DC fast charger, Georgia Power will design, build, and own the electrical infrastructure up to (but not including) the chargers themselves.
- The Georgia Power Make Ready program covers up to hundreds of thousands of dollars in make-ready costs and runs through at least 2028, while funding lasts.
- Georgia Power offers separate rebates and managed charging pilots that can offset Level 2 hardware for businesses and pay residential customers to charge off-peak.
- Under the Federal NEVI Formula Program, Georgia is slated to receive roughly $135 million over five years to build fast-charging hubs every ~50 miles along Alternative Fuel Corridors.
- The state has already awarded funds for dozens of sites under the NEVI program, each with at least four DC fast chargers that must operate 24/7.
- Under the Federal 30C Alternative Fuel Infrastructure Tax Credit, businesses, nonprofits, and municipalities that install charging in eligible census tracts can get a federal tax credit of up to 30% of project costs, capped at $100,000 per charger, through 2032.
- The 30C tax credit is stackable with other programs when structured correctly.
- Electrify America lists 30+ DC fast-charging stations in Georgia.
- EVgo has sites in and around Atlanta and along key corridors.
- Tesla’s Supercharger network blankets Metro Atlanta and major interstates, with more sites now including CCS compatibility or NACS access for non-Teslas.
- I-75 serves as a corridor between Atlanta and Florida/Tennessee with strong DC fast coverage at Pilot/Flying J, Electrify America sites, and Tesla Superchargers.
- GM-EVgo-Pilot truck-stop hubs are expanding along the I-75 route.
- I-85 is already designated an EV corridor between Atlanta and Greenville/Charlotte, with a goal of fast chargers roughly every 50 miles and multiple private-network sites.
- New NEVI-backed hubs are closing remaining gaps on I-85 between Atlanta and the South Carolina line.
- I-20 serves as a corridor between Atlanta and Birmingham/Augusta with a growing mix of Electrify America, EVgo, utility-enabled sites, and Tesla coverage.
- Coverage thins out on I-20 between major cities.
- I-16 serves as a corridor between Macon and Savannah that is historically sparse but targeted in Georgia’s NEVI plan and recent funding rounds focused on rural gaps.
- New four-stall DC hubs are expected at travel centers and highway exits along I-16.
- Select towns and travel centers on US Highways like US 82 and US 441 will receive NEVI-funded hubs operated by companies like Pilot Travel Centers, Love’s, and regional providers.
- Some NEVI projects funded in late 2025 will still be under construction through 2026.
- Georgia Power community Level 2 chargers cost around $0.28/kWh.
- Georgia Power community DC fast chargers cost around $0.48/kWh.
- A 60 kWh session on Georgia Power DC fast charging (roughly 200 miles in many EVs) would be about $29 before any idle fees.
- Third-party networks like Electrify America, EVgo, and Tesla use dynamic pricing that often ranges higher than utility-run stations, especially for pay-as-you-go users.
- Membership plans or automaker partnerships can significantly lower per-kWh rates on third-party networks.
- Metro Atlanta has a dense mix of Level 2 and fast charging around downtown, Midtown, Buckhead, and major job centers.
- A growing presence of chargers exists in county buildings, libraries, and parking decks after local initiatives added public EV stalls.
- Smaller cities and rural Georgia have coverage clustered at interstate exits, travel centers, and a few downtown Level 2 sites.
- Long stretches of US highways in rural areas have no DC fast charging yet, with Level 2 potentially being the only fallback in some towns.
- Many NEVI-funded sites in rural areas are in planning or construction phases through 2026.
- Big brands like Pilot, Love’s, Waffle House, and Walmart are adding 400 kW-class chargers at highway-adjacent locations, often with both CCS and NACS connectors.
- Automakers are rolling out more NACS-equipped EVs and adapters, making Tesla Superchargers a practical option for many non-Tesla drivers.
- Between 2027 and 2030, there will be a noticeable shift from destination chargers to ambient charging, with chargers appearing at grocery stores, gyms, and apartment complexes.
- Regulators and drivers are pushing networks to improve uptime, payment simplicity, and station maintenance.
- More dynamic pricing tied to grid conditions and time-of-day is expected, especially as data centers and industrial loads grow in Georgia.
- There will be a heavier focus on fleets (delivery, ride-hail, municipal) with dedicated depots that may reduce competition at public stations.
- Level 2 chargers use 240V and typically add 25-50 miles of range per hour depending on power level and the EV's onboard charger.
- New NEVI-funded DC fast charging sites are required to provide at least four DC fast chargers that can run simultaneously, often at 150-350 kW, which can add 150+ miles of range in 20-30 minutes for many EVs.
