# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-08-26

**Source:** [U.S. agency to reexamine permit for Hyundai's $7.6 billion electric vehicle plant in Georgia - The Sumter Item](https://www.theitem.com/stories/us-agency-to-reexamine-permit-for-hyundais-76-billion-electric-vehicle-plant-in-georgia,419250)  ·  `page_021216`

## Facts

- Hyundai is building a $7.6 billion electric vehicle plant in Georgia.
- A federal agency plans to reassess its environmental permit for the plant after a conservation group complained that regulators failed to properly examine potential impacts on the area's water supply.
- State and local economic development agencies that applied for the project's 2022 permit never mentioned Hyundai wanted to withdraw up to 6.6 million gallons (25 million liters) per day from the underground aquifer that is a major regional source of drinking water.
- Details about the plant's water needs emerged earlier this year as the Georgia Environmental Protection Division considered a proposal for four new wells to supply water to the auto factory.
- The Army Corps will revisit its prior finding that the project would have "negligible impacts."
- The Ogeechee Riverkeeper conservation group gave notice in June that it planned to sue if the agency refused to revisit the permit issued for the Hyundai project in October 2022.
- Ben Kirsch, legal director of the riverkeeper group, stated that the concentration of pumping will have local impacts on domestic and agricultural wells and raised questions about impacts on natural springs, wetlands, tributaries, and streams.
- The Army Corps' reassessment letters do not order any delays or disruptions to construction at the plant site.
- Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America stated it will assist as needed to ensure the Army Corps gets the information it needs.
- Hyundai stated in a company statement that it has worked tirelessly with relevant authorities to ensure they are good neighbors and that operations do not negatively impact the community's water resources.
- Sara Lips, spokesperson for the state Environmental Protection Division, said the extra scrutiny by the federal government is "unlikely to impact or delay" a final decision by Georgia regulators on whether to permit wells for the Hyundai project.
- The Army Corps permit obtained by state and local economic developers authorized the filling or dredging of 221 acres (89 hectares) of wetlands at the plant site just a few months after Hyundai announced plans to build its EV factory in May 2022.
- The Army Corps concluded at the time that the project would have "negligible impacts on municipal and private water supplies" and relied on information provided by economic developers.
- Trip Tollison, president and CEO of the Savannah Area Economic Development Authority, stated, "We never purposefully withheld anything," noting he worked with state officials to bring Hyundai to Georgia.
- Tollison expects the updated information requested by the Army Corps to be submitted within 10 days and noted the federal agency would typically complete its review within a month.
- Tollison expressed confidence that the reevaluation won't hold up the project, stating, "There's enough water for everyone," and "We feel really good about where we are."
- Georgia's environmental agency issued draft permits in July for the four wells to supply Hyundai and is now evaluating public comments before reaching a final decision.
- The wells would be drilled in neighboring Bulloch County, where some farmers and rural residents have expressed worry that the auto plant will siphon water away from their crops and homes.
- State regulators concluded that water withdrawals by the Hyundai plant would lower water levels in the aquifer up to 19 feet (5.8 meters) for private wells within 5 miles (8 kilometers).
- State regulators said most wells won't see any impacts because they reach deeper into the ground.
- The state agency also stated that nearby rivers and streams won't be affected because dense layers of rock seal off the aquifer from surface water.
- Ben Kirsch with Ogeechee Riverkeeper hopes a second look by the Army Corps will provide more details on how state regulators reached their conclusions.
- Kirsch stated, "We definitely want to see the Corps independently evaluate this," and added, "This should have been all considered before the wetlands were filled and buildings went up."
