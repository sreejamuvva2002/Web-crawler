# Battery Belt

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-07

**Source:** [New ‘Battery Belt’ Opens Organizing Front in the South | Labor Notes](https://labornotes.org/2024/07/new-battery-belt-changing-south)  ·  `page_000418`

## Facts

- Construction is underway at the Toyota Battery mega-site in Liberty, North Carolina.
- Production at the Toyota Battery site is slated to begin in 2025.
- By 2030, the Toyota Battery complex will be fully operational as a 7 million-square-foot facility.
- The Toyota Battery complex will feature 14 production lines: 10 dedicated to batteries for electric vehicles and plug-in hybrid electrics, and four for hybrid electric vehicles.
- The Toyota Battery complex will be operated by 5,100 workers.
- The total population of Liberty, North Carolina is 2,655.
- The Toyota Battery mega-site carries a $13.9 billion price tag.
- Federal and state governments are dubbing the region the “Battery Belt” to describe a manufacturing boom across nearly every aspect of the EV supply and production chain.
- In Kings Mountain, North Carolina, two companies backed by a $90 million Department of Defense grant plan to drain a former open-pit lithium mine lake and resume mining.
- The Kings Mountain mining project is expected to yield enough lithium for 1.2 million electric vehicles per year.
- This lithium will be refined at a soon-to-open $1.3 billion facility in Chester County, South Carolina.
- Much of the dramatic expansion in EV investment is a result of the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) passed in 2022.
- The IRA included tremendous financial incentives, largely grants and tax credits, to encourage cleaner energy production.
- State governments are adding record-breaking incentive deals on EV manufacturers in the form of tax breaks, infrastructure development, and workforce development funding.
- Georgia will give Hyundai up to $2.1 billion in tax breaks and other state assistance for its $7.9 billion capital investment in an EV and battery production complex outside Savannah.
- The Hyundai complex in Georgia is forecast to employ up to 8,500 workers.
- Alongside reducing greenhouse gas emissions and slowing climate change, the IRA is part of a U.S. effort to compete with China, which currently produces the most EVs at the lowest prices.
- Stephen Roach, former chair of Morgan Stanley Asia, told Axios in July: “We want to contain China, even if it comes at the expense of addressing the world’s climate change crisis.”
- The Biden administration recently imposed massive tariffs on imports from China related to various aspects of EV production.
- These policies aim to create an entirely domestic supply and production chain for electric vehicles.
- Since the IRA passage, companies have announced $110 billion in capital investments in new EV manufacturing.
- Roughly half of that money and corresponding jobs are destined for the U.S. South, according to the EV Jobs Hub.
- Eighty-five percent of the investments are concentrated in right-to-work states.
- S&P Global Market Intelligence estimates that as many as two-thirds of EV jobs may eventually be located in the South.
- Michigan tops the list in EV capital investments, followed closely by Georgia ($23 billion), North Carolina ($20 billion), and Tennessee ($17 billion).
- In Tennessee, a joint venture between Ford and SK On is investing $5.6 billion to develop BlueOval City outside of Memphis.
- BlueOval City is a six-square-mile complex where 6,000 workers will produce EVs and batteries.
- The company boasts BlueOval City will be “among the largest auto manufacturing facilities in U.S. history.”
- The BlueOval City site will receive batteries from a BlueOval SK Battery Park in Kentucky, a $5.8 billion project that will employ another 5,000 people.
- Outside of Columbia, South Carolina, Scout Motors (owned and operated by Volkswagen) broke ground on a $2 billion site.
- The Scout Motors site is expected to be operational in 2026, employ 4,000 workers, and produce 200,000 vehicles a year.
- South Carolina is the country’s number one exporter of tires and home to BMW’s massive 11,000-worker plant in Spartanburg.
- The BMW Spartanburg plant will soon begin receiving batteries from a nearby facility operated by AESC.
- In April, AESC announced an expansion adding a second battery production facility for a total investment of $3.1 billion.
- Auto and other industries have been steadily increasing their footprint in Southern states for several decades, drawn to a reactionary, pro-business climate with the lowest union density, lowest wages, and most stringent anti-labor laws.
- Georgia Economic Development Commissioner Pat Wilson told the Financial Times last October: “The fact that you have these massive labor stoppages in the traditional union states definitely will benefit Southern states as companies are looking to grow.”
- In March, Georgia passed a law forbidding companies that receive state incentives from recognizing a union by any method other than secret ballot elections.
- South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster pledged in his State of the State address to “fight [unions] to the gates of hell.”
- McMaster and five other Southern governors issued a joint statement contending that the UAW drive “threatens the values we live by” and would “certainly put our states’ jobs in jeopardy.”
- Nearly every new foreign-owned auto plant opened in the U.S. since the 1990s has chosen the South for its base of operations.
- The Midwest's share of automotive jobs has fallen from around 60 percent to around 45 percent, while the South’s share grew from 15 percent in 1990 to 30 percent today.
- The South is the region that 60 percent of Black workers call home, along with a growing population of immigrant workers.
- Nearly two-thirds of plant construction investments in Southern states are for manufacturing beyond EVs.
- The UAW is committing $40 million over the next two years to organize 13 non-union automakers, largely concentrated in the South.
- Workers at Ultium Cells in Lordstown, Ohio, which manufactures batteries for General Motors, recently won a strong agreement providing the UAW’s initial step into the EV industry.
- Chattanooga Volkswagen workers won their union vote overwhelmingly in April.
- Several weeks later, Daimler Truck North America workers in North Carolina, Georgia, and Tennessee won a record contract.
- Workers at Mercedes in Alabama came up short in their May vote on unionizing, but the union continues organizing to build worker committees and shop floor militancy.
- Last year, workers in Georgia at Blue Bird, the country’s second-largest bus manufacturer which produces electric buses, won a victory to join the Steel Workers (USW).
- Workers at New Flyer in Alabama, another manufacturer of electrified buses, voted yes this year for the Industrial Division of the Communications Workers (IUE-CWA).
- The rise of the EV industry presents organizing challenges due to huge plant sizes and looming anti-union Supreme Court decisions.
- Localized supply chains and vertically integrated operations where battery, parts, and whole car are made at one site may open opportunities for workers.
- Workers can identify key chokepoints in the production process where a small group taking action has outsized power to disrupt operations.
- The Southern Workers Assembly, a cross-sector rank-and-file network, recently launched a program aimed at getting workers jobs in strategic sectors to organize.
- Ben Carroll lives in Durham, North Carolina and is the organizing coordinator for the Southern Workers Assembly.
