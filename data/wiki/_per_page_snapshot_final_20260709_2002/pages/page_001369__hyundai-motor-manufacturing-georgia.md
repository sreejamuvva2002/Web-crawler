# Hyundai Motor Manufacturing Georgia

**Type:** workforce_training  ·  **Role:** workforce_training  ·  **Published:** 2023-08-02

**Source:** [How to build a robust labor pipeline? Hyundai partners with K-12 schools, colleges and state - AOL](https://www.aol.com/sports/build-robust-labor-pipeline-hyundai-090122044.html)  ·  `page_001369`

## Facts

- Hyundai's 8,100-employee automobile assembly plant and EV battery factory is set to open soon in the Savannah region.
- The Savannah region's training centers and school systems are creating workforce pipelines for Hyundai and its suppliers.
- Talent needs to be flowing through labor conduits early, according to Hyundai officials and school administrators.
- Workforce programs emphasize skills ranging from technical certificates to higher-level engineering and computer science knowledge to boost the local workforce pool.
- School administrators from Chatham, Effingham, Bulloch, and Bryan counties are part of the Savannah Joint Development Authority that brokered the Hyundai deal.
- These counties utilize CTAE (Career Technical, Agricultural Education) career pathway programs designed to prepare students for the workforce through targeted classes, certificates, or articulated college credit.
- Each county's school district provides different CTAE pathway offerings across their K-12 schools.
- The Savannah-Chatham County Public School System offers 17 different subjects or clusters for students, including collision repair, computer science, engineering and technology, and manufacturing.
- These pathways are geared toward the EV manufacturing industry, according to Angie Lewis, executive director of the district's Office of College and Career Readiness.
- Computer science programs are offered at each high school in the Savannah-Chatham County Public School System.
- Computer science pathways can focus on general computer science, cybersecurity, computer networking, or gaming to prepare students for the Hyundai plant.
- Chatham County public school students can apply for a choice school to attend programs outside their attendance zone.
- Savannah elementary, middle, and high schools have programs centered around STEM education.
- Jenkins High School offers an engineering program that provides dual college credit and real-world experience for students pursuing degrees or certificates in the field.
- Administrators have a strong partnership with technical colleges and dual enrollment offerings at Armstrong State University and Georgia Southern University.
- Administrators plan to meet with Hyundai and workforce development officials to create more specific pathways serving the Hyundai plant.
- Georgia Speaker of the House Jon Burns stated that the General Assembly has a study committee actively looking into aligning workforce needs with school curriculums.
- Aligning workforce needs could mean creating a new pathway specifically geared toward EVs, according to some school officials.
- Todd Wall, director of CTAE at the Effingham County School District and CEO of the Effingham College and Career Academy, said discussions are already taking place on creating a dual enrollment program between high schools and Savannah Tech's Electric Vehicle Professional certificate.
- The potential partnership is hoped to be rolled out this fall, allowing high school seniors to graduate with the certificate in hand.
- Savannah Tech serves Chatham, Effingham, Bryan, and Liberty schools.
- Ogeechee Tech is Bulloch County's regional technical college.
- Hyundai plans to expand EV certificate programs to technical colleges across south Georgia.
- Effingham County's College and Career Academy opened in 2010 and offers programs in logistics, engineering, automotive, and computer science among other subjects specifically prepared to serve Hyundai's needs.
- The Bulloch County School District has a Career-Workforce Steering Committee working on a three-year career workforce development plan.
- The plan aims to increase employability, enlistment, or enrollment by bolstering CTAE pathways, dual enrollment opportunities, and work-based learning.
- Charles Wilson, Bulloch County Schools' superintendent, wrote that leadership has been collaboratively developing a workforce development plan for the past 10 years to meet industry expansion needs in the region.
- The steering committee hopes to engage with Hyundai and other industry representatives as the plant ramps up towards opening.
- Hyundai representatives are focused on creating strategies to grow long-term talent in technical skills, high-level engineering, and computing fields.
- Savannah Tech announced an Electric Vehicle Professional certificate program and a training partnership with Hyundai supplier Sewon America.
- Savannah Tech also announced a certificate for EV repair technicians.
- Brent Stubbs is the senior manager of learning and development for the Hyundai Metaplant.
- More partnerships with area technical colleges are in the works, according to Stubbs.
- Hyundai's most immediate priority is filling gaps in the local high-level engineering pool.
- The automaker is in discussions with Savannah State, Georgia Tech, and Georgia Southern about adding an EV component to their curriculum.
- Stubbs described the facility as a first-in-the-world smart factory with automation, requiring computer scientists and software engineers.
- Hyundai is looking at building relationships for that talent pool.
- Georgia Southern University currently offers six different types of undergraduate engineering degrees and six graduate-level engineering degrees.
- Statesboro-based Georgia Southern will add two engineering doctorates this fall: a Ph.D. in applied computing and a Ph.D. in engineering with concentrations in civil, electrical, advanced manufacturing, and mechanical engineering.
- Mohammad Davoud is the dean of GSU's Allen E. Paulson College of Engineering and Computing.
- Davoud said the university has been planning these programs for about 10 years in anticipation of Georgia's growth.
- STEM programs such as engineering and applied computing are extremely attractive to industries, directly providing an employee pool of highly trained engineers, computing professionals, and problem solvers with B.S., M.S., and Ph.D. degrees, according to Davoud.
- Adding advanced degrees will result in high-quality post-secondary educators to support the next generation, bolstering the ecosystem of engineers and computer scientists, according to Davoud.
- Stubbs described a symbiotic relationship between the metaplant and area universities, noting that previously young talent left for college at UGA or Georgia Tech and then moved away, but now they can stay for great jobs at a company that shares their values.
- Quick Start is a division of the Technical College System of Georgia that provides customized workforce training for companies.
- To support the Hyundai workforce, the Technical College System is building a $62.5 million Quick Start Training Center on the metaplant site.
- The center will prepare Hyundai employees as well as those of relevant suppliers.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development will request more than $10 million in funds from the Georgia Legislature to expand the existing Quick Start Advanced Manufacturing Center in Pooler.
- Stubbs described Quick Start's two major goals: training new employees (called "metapros" by Hyundai) to be ready on Day One, and building community relationships by providing training for incoming technical college programs and building interest in EV manufacturing.
- The training center will feature a scaled-down version of everything experienced in the plant, including welding, stamp, paint, general assembly, and quality control, with some proprietary elements.
- Quick Start is working with Hyundai's subject matter experts to customize the curriculum.
- The training focuses on teaching about automotive electric vehicles and safety rather than how to build the car, which will be done after hiring.
- Hyundai aims to create a new talent pipeline excited about electric cars.
- Hyundai plans to hire 500 employees this year and another 700 by the end of 2024, with the majority coming from the local labor pool.
- The pipelines being created will feed a gradual workforce buildup, with Hyundai projecting the full workforce of 8,100 to be on the job by 2031.
- Nancy Guan is the general assignment reporter covering Chatham County municipalities for the Savannah Morning News.
- Latrice Williams co-authored the series examining the local workforce landscape from labor pools and training programs perspectives.
- The five-part series explores how Savannah's economy is evolving, where officials expect newcomers to move from, and the scale of workforce development programs being put in place.
