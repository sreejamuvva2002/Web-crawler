# Georgia EV Incentives

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-11-21

**Source:** [EV Incentives in Georgia | ZV Charging](https://zvcharging.com/incentives/ga/)  ·  `page_017705`

## Facts

- Average residential electricity rate in Georgia is 15.540 ¢/kWh.
- Program data was last updated on November 21, 2025.
- There are 51 nationwide federal incentives that typically apply in all states.
- Georgia has created 2 state-level EV incentive programs.
- There is 1 utility rebate or program offer from electric utilities serving Georgia.
- The U.S. Department of Transportation Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) Zero Emissions Airport Vehicle and Infrastructure Pilot Program provides funding to airports for up to 50% of the cost to acquire ZEVs and install or modify supporting infrastructure.
- FAA pilot program grant funding must be used for airport-owned, on-road vehicles used exclusively for airport purposes.
- Vehicles and infrastructure under the FAA pilot program must meet the Federal Aviation Administration's Airport Improvement Program requirements, including Buy American requirements.
- The FAA pilot program gives priority to applicants located in nonattainment areas defined by the Clean Air Act and projects that achieve the greatest air quality benefits per dollar of funds spent.
- Georgia offers an Alternative Fuel Vehicle (AFV) Conversion Tax Credit providing an income tax credit for 10% of the cost to convert a vehicle to natural gas, electricity, propane, and hydrogen, up to $2,500 per vehicle.
- Converted vehicles under the AFV tax credit must be fueled solely by an alternative fuel and meet emissions standards for low-emission or zero emission vehicles defined by the Board of Natural Resources.
- Unused portions of the Georgia AFV tax credit may be carried forward for up to five years, but beginning January 1, 2025, the carryforward period is reduced to up to three years.
- Any motor vehicle equipped with idle reduction technology in Georgia may exceed state gross, axle, and tandem weight limits by up to 550 pounds.
- To qualify for the idle reduction weight exemption, operators must present written certification of the technology's weight and demonstrate or certify that it is fully functional at all times.
- Residential customers of Georgia Power may receive up to a $200 rebate for purchasing and installing a 208/240-volt Level 2 charger in a single-family home or townhome.
- The Georgia Power residential charger rebate requires a dedicated circuit for the charger, application within 6 months of installation, and excludes third-party vendors.
- The U.S. Department of Energy provides grants of up to $200,000 for research and development of commercial innovations related to EV chargers, EV batteries, and biodiesel, hydrogen, and fuel cell vehicle technologies.
- Domestic small businesses are eligible applicants for the DOE AFV R&D grants.
- The U.S. Department of Transportation's Federal Transit Administration offers grants through the Buses and Bus Facilities Program to replace, rehabilitate, and purchase buses, vans, and related equipment, and construct associated bus facilities including low or zero emission vehicles or facilities.
- FTA Buses and Bus Facilities Program funding may be requested for workforce development training or training at the National Transit Institute.
- Eligible applicants for the FTA program include state, local, and tribal governments that allocate funds to or operate fixed-route bus services, with private nonprofit organizations engaged in public transportation eligible as subrecipients.
- The U.S. Department of Transportation established a carbon reduction formula program for states to reduce transportation emissions through activities like truck stop electrification, diesel engine retrofits, vehicle-to-infrastructure communications equipment, public transportation, port electrification, and deployment of alternative fuel vehicles including charging or fueling infrastructure.
- At the request of a state, DOT must provide technical assistance in developing a carbon reduction strategy that must be updated at least once every four years.
- State projects under the DOT carbon reduction formula program are treated as Federal-aid Highway Program projects.
- The U.S. Department of Transportation's Federal Highway Administration Charging and Fueling Infrastructure Discretionary Grant Program offers funding to deploy publicly accessible EV charging and alternative fueling infrastructure in urban and rural communities and along Alternative Fuel Corridors.
- The CFI Program offers two funding types: Community Charging and Fueling Grants and Alternative Fuel Corridor Grants, with varying award amounts and cost share requirements.
- Community Program infrastructure deployments must be located on public roads or publicly accessible locations and prioritize low-income, underserved, rural, and high-density communities.
- Corridor Program grants are available for infrastructure deployments along designated AFCs and for educational and community engagement activities.
- Eligible applicants for the CFI Program include metropolitan planning organizations, U.S. territories, special purpose districts and public authorities, and state, local, and tribal governments.
- Clean Construction and Clean Agriculture are voluntary programs promoting reduction of diesel exhaust emissions from construction and agricultural equipment by encouraging proper operations, maintenance, use of emissions-reducing technologies, and cleaner fuels.
- Clean Construction and Clean Agriculture are part of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency's Diesel Emissions Reduction Act Program, which offers funding for clean diesel construction and agricultural equipment projects.
- The U.S. EPA Clean School Bus program provides funding to replace existing school buses with clean, alternative fuel, or zero emission school buses, potentially awarding up to 100% of the cost for the bus, charging equipment, or fueling infrastructure.
- Eligible alternative fuels under the EPA Clean School Bus program include electricity, natural gas, hydrogen, and propane.
- Eligible applicants for the EPA Clean School Bus program include school districts, state and local government programs, federally recognized Indian tribes, nonprofit organizations, and eligible contractors.
- The EPA prioritizes funding for high-need local education agencies, low income, rural and tribal schools, and applications that cost share through public-private partnerships, grants, or school bonds.
- Beginning January 1, 2023, a federal tax credit is available to businesses and tax-exempt organizations for purchasing new EVs and FCEVs.
- New EVs/FCEVs with a GVWR below 14,000 lbs must have a battery capacity of at least 7 kWh, while those above 14,000 lbs must have at least 15 kWh.
- The federal Commercial EV and FCEV Tax Credit amount equals the lesser of 15% of purchase price for PHEVs, 30% for EVs/FCEVs, or the incremental cost compared to an equivalent ICE vehicle.
- Maximum federal tax credits are capped at $7,500 for vehicles under 14,000 lbs and $40,000 for vehicles above 14,000 lbs.
- Businesses cannot combine the Commercial EV/FCEV Tax Credit with the Clean Vehicle Tax Credit.
- The U.S. DOE's National Renewable Energy Laboratory supports communities through the Energy to Communities Program by offering in-depth partnerships, peer-learning cohorts, and one-on-one matches with experts.
- Eligible communities for the NREL E2C Program include local governments, tribal governments, metropolitan and regional planning authorities, utilities, community-based organizations, transit agencies, school districts, housing authorities, and universities.
- The U.S. DOT and DOE Joint Office of Energy and Transportation offers grants of up to $4 million for projects expanding community e-mobility access and providing clean reliable energy.
- Joint Office grant topics include solving no-home charging, electrifying light- and medium-duty fleets, and developing managed charging programs, with a minimum 50% cost share requirement for demonstration and deployment projects.
- Eligible applicants for Joint Office grants include universities, businesses, nonprofit organizations, and state, local, and tribal governments.
- The Congestion Mitigation and Air Quality Improvement Program provides funding to state DOTs, local governments, and transit agencies to meet Clean Air Act requirements by reducing mobile source emissions and regional congestion.
- CMAQ eligible activities include transit improvements, travel demand management, congestion relief, diesel retrofit projects, alternative fuel vehicles and infrastructure, and medium- or heavy-duty ZEVs with related charging equipment.
- CMAQ projects must demonstrate emissions reductions, be located in or benefit an EPA-designated nonattainment or maintenance area, and qualify as transportation projects.
- The U.S. DOE provides grants for transportation decarbonization research with priority given to cost-effective EV charging deployment for those without home access, innovative mobility solutions for underserved communities, and R&D to reduce EV battery size, cost, range limitations, and emissions.
- DOE EV charger grant priorities also include electrification of off-road and non-road vehicles, materials technologies to improve EV efficiency and affordability, use of alternative fuels in commercial off-road technologies, and planning for medium- and heavy-duty EV charging and hydrogen fueling corridors.
- Applicants for DOE EV charger grants must demonstrate how proposed projects will benefit underserved communities lacking access to clean transportation options.
- The U.S. DOT FHWA EV Charger Reliability and Accessibility Accelerator Program offers funding for repairing and replacing existing non-operational publicly accessible Level 2 and DC fast chargers, covering up to 80% of eligible project costs.
- Eligible applicants for the FHWA EV Charger Accelerator include state departments of transportation and local governments.
- The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development offers funding for utility connections supporting EV charging infrastructure through the HOME Investment Partnerships Program, available to state and local governments as grants, direct loans, loan guarantees, credit enhancements, rental assistance, or security deposits.
- The U.S. DOE offers free EV deployment technical assistance to local governments through the Energy Ready Charging Smart Program, which provides roadmaps for EV deployment and helps lower infrastructure deployment costs.
- The U.S. USDA Rural Energy for America Program provides loan guarantees and grants to agricultural producers and rural small businesses for renewable energy systems or energy efficiency improvements, including EV charging stations.
- Eligible EV charging stations under the USDA REAP program may only be used for private fleet purposes and cannot dispense electricity for retail use, with a maximum grant award of 25% of project costs subject to congressional appropriations.
- The U.S. DOE Office of Manufacturing and Energy Supply Chains established the ITAC Program to provide clean energy resources for small- to medium-sized manufacturers, universities, career institutions, and individuals seeking hiring and training opportunities.
- DOE offers grants of up to $300,000 per project for ITACs to develop EV conversion playbooks for small- and medium-sized manufacturers, with eligible applicants including universities, community colleges, trade schools, union training programs, and public sector-supported applicants.
- The U.S. DOE Communities Local Energy Action Program Pilot facilitates sustained economic and environmental benefits through clean energy deployment work, offering technical assistance to up to 36 low-income, energy-burdened communities experiencing direct environmental justice or economic impacts from shifting away from fossil fuels.
- The U.S. DOT FHWA Federal Lands Access Program provides funding for transportation facility improvements on or adjacent to Federal lands, with eligible electric mobility projects including light-duty EV charging, public transportation charging, commercial charging infrastructure planning, workforce development, and vehicle acquisition.
- Eligible applicants for the FHWA FLAP program include state DOTs, local governments, and tribal governments, and projects must meet the FHWA EV Charging Minimum Standards Rule.
- The U.S. DOT INFRA grant program provides federal financial assistance to eligible transportation infrastructure projects addressing climate change and environmental justice impacts, including supporting modal shifts to reduce vehicle miles traveled, developing ZEV infrastructure, using demand management strategies, and installing EV chargers along the National Highways System.
- Eligible applicants for DOT INFRA grants include states, metropolitan planning organizations serving urbanized areas with populations over 200,000, local governments, political subdivisions, port authorities, and tribal governments.
- The U.S. EPA offers grants for heavy-duty ZEVs and associated infrastructure that may cover up to 100% of total project costs, including incremental replacement costs for Class 6 or 7 vehicles, capital/installation/operation/maintenance costs for charging/refueling infrastructure, workforce development training, and planning activities.
- Eligible applicants for EPA heavy-duty ZEV grants include state governments, municipalities, public school districts, tribal governments, and nonprofit school transportation associations, with additional funding available for projects in nonattainment communities.
- The American-Made Challenges are prize competitions partnered with the National Renewable Energy Laboratory designed to incentivize entrepreneurs to spur manufacturing, develop innovative solutions, and create domestic jobs through public-private partnerships.
- The U.S. DOE offers grants of up to $2 million for large-scale renewable energy planning and siting projects, including EV chargers, through the R-STEP Program, with eligible applicants including state-based collaboratives led by state energy offices and universities.
- The U.S. DOT FTA Low or No Emission Grant Program offers grants to local and state government entities for purchasing or leasing low or zero emission transit buses and supporting facilities, with funding available for workforce development training at the National Transit Institute.
- Applicants for the FTA Low No Program using ZEVs must submit a long-term fleet management plan detailing how funds will be used for resources and acquisitions.
