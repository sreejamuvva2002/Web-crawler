# Hyundai Motor Group

## Facts

- A raid on Thursday at a battery factory under construction at Hyundai’s sprawling auto plant west of Savannah resulted in the detainment of 475 workers, more than 300 of whom were South Koreans.
- Many detained South Korean nationals were engineers and equipment installers brought to Georgia for the highly specialized work of getting an electric battery plant online.
- Some detained workers were involved in after-sales service and installation work.
- Attorney Charles Kuck stated that many of the detained workers were doing work authorized under the B-1 business visitor visa program.
- The detained South Korean workers had planned to stay in the U.S. for just a couple of weeks and never longer than 75 days.
- Video released by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement showed some detained workers shackled with chains around their hands, ankles, and waists.
- Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem stated that the Hyundai plant raid sends a clear signal.
- President Donald Trump said the workers were here illegally and that the U.S. needs to arrange with other countries to have experts train U.S. citizens for specialized work such as battery and computer manufacturing.
- Attorney Charles Kuck said no company in the U.S. makes the machines used in the Georgia battery plant, so they had to come from abroad to install or repair equipment on-site.
- Kuck stated that training someone in the U.S. to perform this specialized equipment installation work would take about three to five years.
- Rosemary Coates, executive director of the Reshoring Institute, said it is not unusual for foreign companies to send workers from abroad to set up U.S. factories and then train U.S. workers.
- Coates noted that the same practice occurred in the 1980s with Japanese carmakers setting up U.S. factories and in the 1990s with German carmakers.
- A B-1 visitor for business visa allows foreign workers to stay for up to six months while getting reimbursed for expenses and collecting a paycheck back home.
- Under B-1 rules, foreign workers can supervise construction projects but cannot build anything themselves unless equipment installation is spelled out in a contract, according to Los Angeles immigration lawyer Angelo Paparelli.
- South Korea is one of 41 countries whose citizens can use the U.S. Electronic System for Travel Authorization (ESTA), which provides a visa waiver for up to 90 days if they provide a legitimate reason for their visit, effectively giving them B-1 visa status, according to immigration attorney Rita Sostrin.
- Advocates called for the detained workers to be released during a news conference Monday at a church in Savannah, about 25 miles east of the site where Hyundai began producing electric vehicles a year ago.
- Sarah Park, president of the Korean American Coalition of Atlanta, stated that many of the detained South Korean workers had special skills needed to get the battery plant running.
- Daniela Rodriguez, executive director of Migrant Equity Southeast, said immigrants from Mexico, Guatemala, Colombia, Chile, Ecuador, and Venezuela were also detained in the raid.
- Rodriguez reported that the group's Savannah office was flooded with calls from family members of workers who could not be reached and were presumed detained.
- Workers described seeing armed agents and military-style vehicles during the raid, while drones and helicopters hovered overhead.
- One woman with a work permit who was not detained told Rodriguez she felt like she was being followed as an animal while they were hunting for her.
- Even some workers who were not detained feel unsafe about returning to their jobs at the site.
- Savannah labor union leader Christi Hulme, president of the Savannah Regional Central Labor Council, said local unions complained that Hyundai and its contractors improperly used South Korean workers for basic construction falling outside visa waiver rules.
- Hulme stated that Korean workers were pouring cement, erecting steel, performing carpentry, and fitting pipes.
- Hulme claimed that basically local labor was being given to illegal immigrants.
- Spokespersons for Hyundai’s Georgia EV factory and the adjacent battery plant did not immediately reply to an email message seeking comment.
- South Korea’s foreign minister Cho Hyun called the raid a very serious matter during a legislative hearing before departing for the U.S. on Monday to secure his citizens’ return on a charter flight.
- Lawmaker Cho Jeongsik from the liberal governing Democratic Party questioned how South Korean companies could continue investing in the U.S. if U.S. authorities detain hundreds of Koreans almost like a military operation.
- Some South Korean lawmakers called for retaliatory investigations of Americans who allegedly work illegally in South Korea.
- Experts say the raid is unlikely to prompt major tit-for-tat measures given South Korea's dependence on the U.S. for security and business ties.
- The Homeland Security agency’s largest workplace raid yet targeted Georgia, a symbol of bilateral cooperation where many large South Korean businesses operate and plan future investments.
- Only weeks ago, South Korea promised hundreds of billions in U.S. investments to reach a tariff deal.
- President Trump and South Korean President Lee Jae Myung held their first summit in Washington on Aug. 25.
- Kim Taewoo, former head of Seoul’s Korea Institute for National Unification, criticized the way Trump is pressuring the Korean government as very rough and unilateral, stating it won't be good for U.S. national interests in the long term.
