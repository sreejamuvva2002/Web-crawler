# State of Georgia EV/Battery Industry

**Type:** government_program  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [China curbs on graphite could roil EV supply chain in the U.S.](https://www.ajc.com/news/china-curbs-on-graphite-could-roil-ev-supply-chain-in-the-us/VNGMMSM3KFDALGRTNNNP57MWLI/)  ·  `page_050061`

## Facts

- China's move to curtail graphite exports could disrupt the supply chains of U.S. electric vehicle and battery makers that rely on highly processed graphite for plug-in automobiles.
- China's graphite export restrictions follow U.S. export controls on semiconductors sold to China, which the U.S. cites as being driven by national security concerns.
- The trade tit-for-tat demonstrates China's willingness to leverage its outsize influence over key minerals vital to battery production within the EV supply chain.
- Starting in December, China will require export permits for specialized forms of synthetic graphite and natural flake graphite.
- China dominates the global market for graphite, which is used in the anode of nearly every EV battery.
- In 2021, the U.S. sourced roughly one-third of its graphite supply from China.
- China is also a major player in other rare earth minerals used in EV production, including lithium.
- A Congressional Resource Service report noted last year that the U.S does not have any active graphite mines and has few prospects to develop domestic reserves.
- Kevin Ketels, an assistant professor of global supply chain management at Wayne State University, stated there is no easy way for the U.S. to decouple from China regarding graphite supplies.
- Stephanie Valdez Streaty, director of industry insight at Cox Automotive, noted that while the immediate impact of China's actions is unclear, it highlights the importance of a robust and sustainable U.S. supply chain.
- China's graphite restrictions have the potential to increase EV prices or place additional cost pressures on automakers, according to Valdez Streaty.
- More than 870,000 EVs were sold in the U.S. in the first nine months of the year, with the industry on pace to surpass 1 million units annually for the first time in 2023.
- U.S. companies and the federal government have prioritized researching new battery chemistries and finding alternative raw material sources to reduce reliance on China and build a domestic supply chain.
- Since 2018, Georgia has recruited more than $25 billion in EV-related investments and commitments for over 30,000 jobs, according to Governor Brian Kemp's office.
- Electric vehicles, which produce no tailpipe greenhouse gas emissions, are central to President Joe Biden's climate and economic agenda.
- The Biden administration has passed sweeping legislation encouraging automakers and battery companies to manufacture in the U.S., aiming to create end-to-end domestic supply chains and tens of thousands of jobs.
- American and foreign brands have responded by announcing new multi-billion-dollar EV and battery plants across the U.S.
- SK Battery America built a massive factory in Jackson County, northeast of Atlanta, to supply Volkswagen and Ford.
- Synthetic graphite maker Anovion has announced plans for a plant in Bainbridge.
- Copper and mineral recycler Aurubis operates a factory in Augusta.
- Ascend Elements recycles lithium-ion batteries at a facility in Covington.
- Hyundai is constructing a $7.6 billion EV and battery assembly plant in Bryan County near the coast in partnership with LG Energy Solution.
- Hyundai is also building a battery plant with an SK subsidiary in Bartow County.
- Rivian expects to break ground early next year on its $5 billion factory located an hour east of Atlanta.
- Cox Enterprises, which owns the publishing company behind this article, holds approximately a 4% stake in Rivian.
- SK stated it does not expect China's graphite restrictions to immediately affect its supplies but is closely monitoring the long-term impact with partners.
- Georgia Commissioner of Economic Development Pat Wilson stated that Governor Kemp tasked his agency with recruiting "the jobs of tomorrow," which has diversified the U.S. supply chain and benefited residents statewide.
- Critical and rare earth minerals essential for supporting these jobs are controlled by a few countries with unstable, corrupt governments or those unfriendly to U.S. interests, according to Wilson.
- When one country controls 90% of the processing of a key mineral like graphite, international recognition demands supply diversification and technology investment to alleviate potential system pinch points.
- New battery technology development aimed at reducing reliance on imported minerals is occurring in Georgia.
- Matthew McDowell, co-director of Georgia Tech’s Advanced Battery Center, identified silicon as a promising alternative to graphite that is cheaper, more abundant, and stores more energy.
- Silicon is already used in smaller applications, with major automakers like GM, Mercedes-Benz, and Porsche banking on it for next-generation batteries.
- Sila Nanotechnologies, co-founded by Georgia Tech professor Gleb Yushin, holds a $3.3 billion valuation and has a deal with Mercedes to produce silicon anodes for G-Class vehicles.
- The silicon alternative technology still needs to mature, and in the interim, the U.S. must source graphite from more reliable trade partners.
- There is currently no solution to fully replace graphite, despite rapid development efforts.
