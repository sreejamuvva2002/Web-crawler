# Volvo Group North America

**Source:** [Volvo Group North America: Giving Energy Projects a Boost with New Investment Guidelines | Better Buildings & Better Plants Initiative](https://betterbuildingssolutioncenter.energy.gov/implementation-models/volvo-group-north-america-giving-energy-projects-boost-new-investment)  ·  `page_004184`

## Facts

- Volvo Group North America evaluates proposed capital projects using standardized methods including Net Present Value (NPV), Internal Rate of Return (IRR), Modified IRR (MIRR), and Simple Payback.
- Profitability calculations such as NPV, IRR, and MIRR are used for larger investments, while Simple Paybacks are used for minor investments.
- Previously, some energy efficiency projects faced resistance due to long payback periods and the challenge of estimating energy costs over time.
- Implementing the Volvo Group Guideline for Energy-Efficiency Investment Calculations addresses these barriers by providing clear guidance on factoring in the risk of sharp rises in energy costs.
- The new guideline levels the playing field for energy efficiency projects and helps prioritize Volvo Group’s vision and environmental responsibility, one of the company’s core values.
- Volvo Group’s vision is to become the world leader in sustainable transport solutions.
- Reducing energy consumption at manufacturing sites is a key method by which the company is working towards this vision.
- Objectives for U.S. manufacturing sites include reducing energy intensity by 25% by 2020 from a 2009 baseline.
- Volvo Group sites are striving towards CO2 neutrality.
- Objectives include reducing idling losses (system losses during non-production hours) by 50%.
- Reducing system losses when a system is idle has become an important consideration.
- The new guideline was developed in 2013 by Volvo’s Environmental Director and members of the Executive Management, Environmental Council, and Corporate Standards teams.
- This guideline is an update of the Guidelines for Energy Saving Investments guideline developed in 2006.
- The Guideline for Energy-Efficiency Investment Calculations was disseminated throughout Volvo Group by the Corporate Standards team after its development.
- Energy cost projections used in the guideline were developed in collaboration between the Volvo Environmental Council and Executive Management team and are reviewed annually.
- Maintenance and Energy staff have been essential in executing the guideline throughout the new project approval process.
- All PPE investments are reviewed according to the Volvo Group Investment Policy.
- Once approved, environmental and energy professionals are involved in collecting, reporting, and analyzing data on the implementation and impact of each energy savings project.
- There are two metrics for measuring the impact of the new guideline: the number of new energy savings projects and a reduction in company energy intensity.
- Volvo Group Trucks Operations tracks proposed and implemented energy savings projects.
- Energy consumption data is tracked at the corporate level.
- Both sets of data are featured in the annual Volvo Group Environmental Data Report.
- Since the guideline was launched, numerous energy savings projects have been approved that may not have received capital funding otherwise.
- The guideline provides a powerful tool for the company’s energy, maintenance, and environmental managers to obtain funding for strategic energy projects.
- This has helped Volvo make significant strides in energy efficiency at its U.S. plants.
- The company met its initial 25% energy intensity reduction target in 2014.
- The company followed up by setting a new 25% target.
- The New River Valley truck plant in Dublin, Virginia used the guideline to gain approval for an energy-efficient lighting project.
- Volvo’s New River Valley plant uses the Group guideline in all energy project financial calculations.
- A case study shows the use of the Group guideline in replacing 400w High Intensity Discharge (HID) metal halide bulbs with energy-efficient Light-Emitting Diode (LED) lights with occupancy sensors at the New River Valley plant.
- Traditionally, HID lights are used for high bay plant lighting, parking lot lights, wall packs, and outdoor flood lights.
- LED bulbs use less power relative to the amount of light generated and help reduce greenhouse gas emissions by saving energy.
- The comparison shows 400w HID metal halide bulbs use 465 input watts versus 215 for LED bulbs with occupancy sensors.
- HID bulbs operate 21 hours/day while LED bulbs with occupancy sensors operate 20 hours/day due to dimming during lunch and breaks.
- The yearly cost for HID bulbs is $213.95 compared to $21.67 for LED bulbs, based on a $/KWH rate of $0.07.
- Fixture costs are $250.00 for HID bulbs and $1,043.00 for LED bulbs.
- Maintenance costs are $30.00 for HID bulbs and $0.00 for LED bulbs.
- The simple payback period for the HID setup is 4.01 years.
- Total fixture costs for the plant project are $72,250.00 for HID and $260,750.00 for LEDs.
- The project yields yearly cost savings of $65,084.63.
- The project yields yearly energy savings of 805,923 KWH (or 806 MWH).
- The project yields yearly CO2 savings of 620 tons.
- Graphs 2 and 3 show a hypothetical comparison of ROI results using the Guideline versus not using it.
- The comparison is based on an actual project proposal at New River Valley to replace HID bulbs with LEDs with occupancy sensors.
- The Guideline allows for a 10% projection in electricity prices in Year 1 to 3, while the second calculation assumes a 5% increase in Y1 to Y3.
- The comparison shows a payback period of four to five years for the LEDs with or without the Guideline.
- The return on investment (ROI) is much higher in year 4 and year 5 respectively when the 5%/10%/15% increase specified in the Guideline is used.
- Using the Guideline, a 5%/10%/15% increase in electricity price cuts a year off of the projected payback period.
