## Facts

- Volkswagen AG called itself an "unintended victim" in a battle between two battery suppliers and urged the U.S. government to extend a reprieve to buy batteries key to its planned U.S. electric vehicle production.
- The U.S. International Trade Commission (ITC) sided with LG Chem in a trade secrets case but permitted SK Innovation to import components for domestic production of lithium ion batteries for Ford Motor Co's EV F-150 program for four years, and for Volkswagen of America’s electric vehicle line for two years.
- VW requested its carve out be extended to at least four years to give an adequate transition period.
- VW expressed hope that the two suppliers will settle the dispute outside of the courtroom.
- The German automaker has invested $800 million to build electric vehicles in Tennessee and is hiring hundreds of employees.
- Volkswagen stated it will take all necessary steps to fulfill its commitment to provide skilled jobs to proud Tennessee workers.
- Ford Chief Executive Jim Farley publicly encouraged LG Chem and SK Innovation to reach a settlement on Thursday.
- VW and Ford previously warned the dispute could disrupt supplies of key EV parts and cost U.S. jobs during the COVID-19 pandemic.
- Georgia Governor Brian Kemp called on President Joe Biden to overturn the ITC ruling, warning the long-term prospects for the $2.6 billion SK Innovation battery plant in Jackson County, Georgia, would be "harmed significantly."
- The SK Innovation plant will eventually build batteries for Volkswagen and Ford.
- LG Energy Solution counsel Dave Callahan said the company remains committed to negotiating a fair settlement for the theft of its trade secrets and stated the Georgia plant will be able to operate.
- Dave Callahan added that the only obstacle to resolving the matter and securing the plant's long-term future is SK Innovation's refusal to acknowledge its wrongdoing and make amends.
