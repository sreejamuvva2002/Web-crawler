# SK Innovation

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [The Georgia GOP Is Begging Biden To Act On Battery Plant, While Dems Stay Silent | HuffPost Latest News](https://www.huffpost.com/entry/georgia-skinnovation_n_605a0947c5b6cebf58d2123a?_hsenc=p2ANqtz-8XDETryfNcHoMWh7kZirFZ3QbneWSZHYpXx1kep95-t7j-oHTgv2O0QV0wCqA77h1KYID7)  ·  `page_050034`

## Facts

- President Joe Biden campaigned on the promise of electric vehicles and green jobs, and Georgia voters responded by electing him to the White House and handing the state’s two Senate seats and control of Congress to Democrats.
- A U.S. International Trade Commission (ITC) ruling threatens to shut down a giant electric vehicle battery factory set to employ upward of 2,600 workers in the suburbs north of Athens, Georgia.
- Georgia’s Republican lawmakers have emerged as the project’s most vocal defenders in an unusual partisan twist.
- President Biden has just 19 days left to veto trade authorities’ ruling.
- The ITC ruled last month in favor of South Korean battery giant LG Chem, finding rival SK Innovation guilty in a trade secrets case.
- The ITC banned SK Innovation from importing what it needs to run its planned Georgia factory for the next decade.
- There has been no sign from the White House that it will block the decision so far.
- SK Innovation has hired consultants to draft a plan to shut down its $2.6 billion plant in Georgia.
- The move sent Peach State officials scrambling to save one of the largest economic development projects in the state’s history.
- Pat Wilson, GOP commissioner of Georgia’s Department of Economic Development, said: “There’s nothing partisan about the jobs of the future.”
- Georgia’s Republican leaders have been very public in their call for Biden to act to save the Commerce, Georgia, plant.
- None of the six Democrats representing Georgia in the House of Representatives responded to repeated requests for comment.
- Five emails to Democratic Sen. Jon Ossoff’s office went unanswered, and former gubernatorial candidate Stacey Abrams declined an interview request.
- Sen. Raphael Warnock (D-Ga.) declined an interview but his spokesman provided a link to a March 4 confirmation hearing video where Warnock pressed Polly Trottenberg, Biden’s pick for the No. 2 job at the Department of Transportation, on whether the agency would “provide to President Biden an analysis of” the ITC ruling’s effect on U.S. battery supplies.
- Warnock said during the hearing: “It’s not a good position for U.S. automakers if they’re only relying on almost one cell supplier.”
- Warnock stated the adverse ITC ruling “threatens seriously the future of that project, whether it’ll happen at all,” calling it “one of the largest economic investments in Georgia’s history and a severe punch in the gut... for President Biden’s own goals.”
- Conservative Atlanta-based pundit Erick Erickson called the ITC’s decision “a bad day for all of us here in Georgia” and compared potential job losses to Keystone XL oil pipeline construction worker layoffs.
- Erickson wrote that if Warnock and Ossoff lack political clout to reverse the decision, they will “help contribute to the loss of over 2,000 jobs in Georgia,” questioning if Warnock wants that going into 2022.
- Carol Browner, former Clinton-era EPA administrator, served as an expert witness for SK Innovation, testifying before the ITC that eliminating its Georgia plant put the U.S. electric vehicle supply chain at risk.
- Sally Yates, former Obama-era deputy attorney general, told The New York Times’ Dealbook the case could “seriously undermine critical progress on the climate crisis” and risked “killing jobs and setting back the Georgia economy.”
- Andrew Young, civil rights leader and former UN ambassador during the Carter administration, warned in an opinion piece that the ITC ruling “imperils thousands of direct and indirect Georgia jobs and postpones the future of the new era auto industry” over a technicality.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp (R) repeatedly urged Biden to “support thousands of hardworking Georgians and their communities.”
- On March 12, Kemp sent a letter to Biden asking him to “exercise the authority granted to you under law to disapprove the ITC ruling on grounds that it is contrary to the public interest and will seriously jeopardize your administration’s environmental and economic goals.”
- Kemp wrote: “Simply put: the livelihoods of thousands of Georgians are now in your hands,” and “This critical opportunity requires us to work together for the good of my state and our country.”
- GOP Rep. Buddy Carter hoped Biden was helping broker an agreement between LG Chem and SK Innovation to allow manufacturing plans to proceed.
- Carter said: “We don’t want to lose those jobs in Georgia and we don’t want to lose those jobs in the electric vehicle industry,” and expressed hope that presidential staff are engaged in brokering a deal.
- White House U.S. Trade Representative spokesman Adam Hodge stated the administration is “currently reviewing the policy implications of the ITC determination” and will complete the review within the 60-day federal law deadline.
- The dispute began in 2017 when SK Innovation poached engineers in Seoul, prompting LG to file lawsuits accusing them of violating noncompete contracts.
- In 2019, LG’s battery subsidiary, LG Energy Solutions, lodged a complaint with the ITC.
- Around the time SK Innovation announced plans to open its massive Georgia factory to supply Ford Motor Company and Volkswagen, LG turned to the ITC.
- LG filed a separate lawsuit in Delaware federal courts for monetary damages, pending conclusion of the ITC case.
- On Feb. 10, the ITC found SK Innovation guilty of destroying documents allegedly detailing what it learned from its LG hires.
- SK Innovation argued that South Korean laws do not require companies to retain the same documents as U.S. statutes.
- The commission ruled unanimously to ban SK Innovation from importing raw materials for the next 10 years, which LG said would take to develop batteries independently.
- The ITC granted a carve-out allowing SK Innovation to build batteries for four years for Ford’s electric F-150 pickup truck and two years for Volkswagen’s Crossover series.
- The commission determined the carve-outs should give automakers time to find alternative sources or for SK Innovation to reach a settlement with LG.
- Tesla is the largest battery maker in the U.S., but its Nevada Gigafactory only produces packs for its own vehicles.
- LG operates Midwest projects that will produce roughly 30 gigawatt-hours per year when completed, providing batteries to General Motors and other automakers.
- SK Innovation’s Georgia plant would add another 22 gigawatt-hours of annual capacity.
- The rest of the battery market is made up of much smaller companies.
- LG sent a letter on March 10 to Warnock vowing to “do whatever we can to help the people and workers of Georgia,” hinting it was prepared to make investments in the state to offset the closure, according to The Atlanta Journal-Constitution.
- David Callahan, lawyer for LG, said the company was preparing to invest $4.5 billion in the U.S., resulting in at least two more factories, one of which could be in Georgia.
- SK Innovation stated LG is “using the ITC to try to muscle SK out of the US EV battery market, period.”
- A SK Innovation spokesperson said: “SK has always been open to good-faith settlement talks with LG,” but criticized LG as “inflexible and unreasonable” and noted “LG doesn’t want competition.”
- James Frith, analyst at BloombergNEF, said allowing LG to dominate poses risks to producing EVs at the speed and affordability the Biden administration wants.
- Frith stated: “Competition within the battery industry has been one of the key factors that helped to drive down cell and pack prices over the past decade,” and warned it’s not a good position for U.S. automakers relying on almost one cell supplier.
- Frith noted other manufacturers could fill SK Innovation’s void, including Japan’s Panasonic Corporation, South Korea’s Samsung, and China’s CATL or BYD.
- Callahan denied LG was seeking a monopoly and accused SK Innovation of bluffing about shuttering the plant to stave off a costly settlement.
- Callahan said: “If SK Innovation intends to be in this hugely growing market in the long haul... it would be suicide to leave Ford and Volkswagen in the lurch,” and added, “They’re threatening Georgia. If I were Georgia, I would be mad.”
- SK Innovation already started hiring hundreds of workers, and it could take years for another company to replace the plant or increase supply to electrify the U.S. auto fleet.
- Wilson said: “We need batteries in order to keep up with the demand, this was not only an investment into Georgia but an investment into electric vehicles in the United States and a diversification of the supply chain outside of Asia,” adding, “We saw this as a win-win for the state and the country.”
