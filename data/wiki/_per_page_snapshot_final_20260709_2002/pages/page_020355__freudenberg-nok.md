# Freudenberg-NOK

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2004-06-08

**Source:** [Best Practices: Freudenberg-NOK/Cleveland, GA: Continuous Kaizens | Manufacturing.net](https://www.manufacturing.net/home/article/13217605/best-practices-freudenbergnokcleveland-ga-continuous-kaizens)  ·  `page_020355`

## Facts

- Freudenberg-NOK is a Plymouth, MI-based manufacturer of seals and gaskets, custom rubber products, PTFE components and other products for the automotive industry.
- The company operates 17 U.S. manufacturing facilities as part of a global network of 47 plants operated by Germany-based parent, the Freudenberg and NOK group.
- Freudenberg-NOK's 17 U.S. facilities generated $1 billion in sales in 2003 and contributed to global sales of $7.5 billion.
- The company underwent a company-wide shift toward lean systems in the 1990s, reaching an industry milestone of 17,000 completed kaizens by 2002 as chairman and CEO Joseph Day retired.
- Kaizen initiatives helped boost productivity, reduce defects, and slash work-in-process inventory by 80%.
- Freudenberg-NOK's Cleveland, GA plant makes valve-stem seals, radial shaft seals and crank shaft seals used in automobile engines, small-equipment engines, outboard motors and similar powered equipment.
- The Cleveland facility is a 15-year-old, 125,000-sq.-ft. site located 65 miles north of Atlanta.
- The Cleveland plant won the 2001 Shingo Prize for Excellence in Manufacturing and is certified in ISO 14000, 9001, and QS 9000.
- The plant will turn out approximately 200 million oil and valve-stem seals in 2004 in its role as a company "lead" center specializing in specific product types for customers.
- The Cleveland facility is vertically integrated from "cradle to grave," making its own synthetic rubber on-site to quickly adapt to design changes and experiments.
- Gary VanWambeke, general manager of Freudenberg-NOK's radial shaft seal division (with the company since 1997), oversees major kaizens that are typically five-day events using a cross-functional team, with 12 to 30 conducted per year.
- The plant also conducts truncated or "minor" kaizens designed for daily problem-solving by small work groups, performing over 300 of these per year.
- The Cleveland plant employs 420 workers and utilizes a multi-faceted improvement program created in 1992 called Growtth (Get Rid of Waste Through Team Harmony) that combines kaizen activity with Six Sigma and lean principles based on the Toyota Production System.
- Worker certification at the Cleveland plant progresses from running a single cell devoted to one seal product, to cross-cell training, to certification in lean systems (green- and black-belt levels), and Six Sigma (introduced at Cleveland in 2000, also with green- and black-belt levels).
- To date, the Cleveland plant has produced nine black belts (four lean, five Six Sigma) and 52 green belts (12 lean, 40 Six Sigma).
- Employees must demonstrate a basic understanding and support of Six Sigma and lean to be promoted from a set-up to a lead position, shown through certification progress, number of minor kaizens completed, and participation in major events.
- Emily Bailey, the plant's materials manager, is a Six Sigma black belt pursuing her lean-systems black belt.
- Donna Unger, VanWambeke's executive assistant, holds a lean-systems and Six-Sigma green belt and has participated in Growtth activity, kaizens, and a Six Sigma project.
- The plant dedicates cells to a single customer's orders, typically a major automaker, ensuring that customer always has a direct contact at Freudenberg-NOK familiar with their seal usage.
- Ford Motor Co. recently sent a letter congratulating the Cleveland plant for shipping zero defectives last year.
- Defects at the plant dropped from 650 parts per million in 1997 to 9 ppm by 2003.
- Sales per associate at the Cleveland facility jumped 57% during the same period defects were reduced.
- The plant's safety improved as OSHA reportables dropped 40% and lost work days dropped 80%.
- The plant's total 2003 savings from lean and Six Sigma activities was $1.2 million.
- VanWambeke is also in charge of five other facilities (one each in Brazil and Mexico and three in New Hampshire) and plans to drive standardization across the division so perfected processes spread to others.
- The company's philosophy avoids adding brick and mortar; recent kaizens freed up seven presses worth of equipment that are being sent to open floor space in Mexico and Brazil to give greater delivery flexibility for customers operating in both countries.
- Workers receive cash incentives for their participation in successful improvement projects.
- A suggestion system called the "Make My Job Easier" box evolved into an implementation system where Growtth managers help workers form small work groups to fix problems, eventually leading to a natural trickle of ideas as employees learned to self-facilitate.
- Green- and black-belt certification requires significant time spent outside the plant for extensive reading and completing projects at other Freudenberg-NOK facilities, with workers making their own arrangements alongside daily responsibilities.
- Doug Hints, the plant's valve-stem seal operations manager, notes that green- and black-belt applicants must become their own lean systems managers due to the self-directed nature of the certification.
- Management uses a unique hierarchy with two plant managers and single supervisors for all three shifts to ensure accountability across shifts.
- Business-cell leaders are empowered to adjust their schedules, typically coming in early to catch the end of third shift at 7 a.m., staying through first shift, and catching the beginning of second shift.
- Cleveland won the 2001 Shingo award in the Large Business category after pursuing it with help from previous Shingo winners within the company and receiving an audit on its first application.
- The plant is placing new emphasis on improving its 5S strategy and implementing a behavior-based safety program to further reduce OSHA reportables.
- The company encourages suppliers to adopt lean principles, sending experts from the Cleveland plant to supplier facilities to help them start the process.
- Operations manager Doug Hints believes the auto industry will become more global but also more centralized with fewer suppliers and higher requirements, stating that suppliers not heavily into lean will not survive.
- President & CEO Dr. Mohsen Sohi, a Six-Sigma black belt, continues building on lean and Six Sigma principles and requires all plant managers to be similarly certified.
