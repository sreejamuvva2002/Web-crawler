# Georgia

**Type:** investment_announcement  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-16

**Source:** [Foreign firms' share of Georgia investment falls in record year overall - Global Atlanta](https://www.globalatlanta.com/foreign-firms-share-of-georgia-investment-falls-in-record-year-overall/)  ·  `page_009211`

## Facts

- Georgia marked a record year with $26.3 billion in pledged investment, while the share of that total coming from international companies decreased compared to recent years.
- Of the 23,200 new private-sector jobs announced by companies in the state in the year ended June 30, 6,500 (about 28 percent) came from companies headquartered in Canada, South Korea, and Japan, which were the top three.
- This represents a far lower share than two years ago, when foreign investors accounted for nearly half of new jobs announced and two-thirds of foreign investment in capital terms.
- Prior years appear to have been FDI outliers due to massive investments from Hyundai Motor Group in fiscal 2022 and related suppliers for its Metaplant continuing into 2023, which contributed to South Korea emerging as the top investor nation.
- Without Hyundai, fiscal 2022 would have seen only $3 billion in announced foreign investment, driven by projects like Korean solar panel maker Qcells, Norway’s Freyr Battery, and Finland’s ADMARES.
- That $3 billion total is roughly the same as the amount of foreign investment the state saw announced in fiscal 2025.
- The Georgia Department of Economic Development notes that the number of international projects is up.
- More than a quarter of the 423 projects the state worked on this year involved international companies, totaling 115, which is up from 114 last year and 76 in 2022.
- Most international projects were expansions, with about two-thirds of new international investment (about $3 billion) coming from existing investors like Canada’s Irving Tissue, which committed another $600 million to its Macon facility and added 100 new jobs.
- In metro Atlanta, Germany’s Mercedes-Benz announced plans to expand its Sandy Springs headquarters, hire 500 additional people, and develop a separate R&D center elsewhere in the region.
- Pat Wilson, commissioner of the Georgia Department of Economic Development, stated that the state's stable and reliable business climate gives companies confidence to plan for the long term.
- Pat Wilson added that whether a company is new to Georgia or already there, domestic or international, the question is no longer if they will expand in the state, but when.
- Expansions made up three-quarters of the 423 projects and perennially account for most new jobs.
- The companies that expanded already accounted for 50,000 jobs in the state, which is a key reason the state aggressively pursues major projects.
- New greenfield projects included Greenbox Systems’ $144 million automated warehouse in Butts County backed by Japan’s SoftBank, and beauty company Kiss USA’s fulfillment center in Bryan County.
- The Greenbox Systems and Kiss USA projects have promised 300 and 395 jobs, respectively.
- The state’s Global Commerce figures carry a caveat that while many announced expansions are underway, most are multi-year projects that do not commence hiring and production until years later.
- As market conditions and company plans change, the actual number of jobs created and capital deployed may not match initial pledges.
- High-profile examples of stalled or altered projects include Freyr pulling the plug on its $2.6 billion plant in Newnan, Sungeel Hi-Tech reconsidering a Toccoa recycling facility, and Aspen Aerogels moving to source from China instead of building a new $325 million plant in Georgia.
- All three stalled projects faced difficulty as the EV market tightened, threatening Gov. Brian Kemp's signature EV strategy amid the Trump administration's pullback on EV incentives.
- The state does not announce when prior investment pledges fall through, but it has recourse to recoup taxpayer funding spent on wooing those projects.
- While money for site preparation may already be spent, states and communities can use “clawback” provisions to recoup some tax incentives calculated on baseline job and capital investment levels.
- Gov. Brian Kemp’s office issued the news release detailing the state's investment and job figures.
