# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2024-11-02

**Source:** [Appeal among 'potential avenues' in challenging Georgia permits for Hyundai wells](https://www.yahoo.com/news/appeal-among-potential-avenues-challenging-090838140.html)  ·  `page_021208`

## Facts

- State environmental permits were issued for four Bulloch County wells expected to supply up to 6.6 million gallons of water for Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America and related development.
- The Georgia Environmental Protection Division (EPD) approved the state environmental permits on Oct. 7.
- Opponents have 30 days to file an administrative appeal against the state permits, with a deadline of Wednesday, Nov. 6.
- The Ogeechee Riverkeeper organization (ORK) has threatened legal action over a separate federal environmental permit for the 2,500-acre electric vehicle and battery manufacturing site along Interstate 16 in Bryan County.
- ORK stated it is "working on a number of potential avenues" related to the state's approval of the wells.
- ORK spokeswoman Meaghan Gerard stated that the organization is actively evaluating the details of the permits and EPD's comment responses while considering next steps.
- ORK claims that EPD and the State of Georgia cannot continue to allow industry to monopolize limited, pristine groundwater.
- Attorney Don Stark stated that the ORK Board has authorized exploring all legal avenues for a state permit appeal, while acknowledging the associated time restrictions.
- Georgia EPD issued the well permits one week after production commenced at the 16-million-square-foot facility.
- Hyundai states the facility will eventually use up to 4 million gallons of water piped across the county line from the wells when manufacturing hits its peak of 300,000 vehicles per year.
- Bryan and Bulloch counties were each granted two permits to withdraw water from the Floridan Aquifer, which covers 100,000 square miles beneath Florida and parts of Georgia, Alabama, and South Carolina.
- The wells are located in Bulloch County because Bryan County has state restrictions on pumping to prevent saltwater intrusion into the aquifer near Hilton Head Island.
- EPD states there is no risk of saltwater intrusion into the Floridan Aquifer in Bulloch County.
- Professor Ryan Rowberry of Georgia State University College of Law stated that an administrative appeal of the state permits serves as a precursor to potential legal challenges.
- Rowberry explained that the appeal process aims to determine if opponents and the agency can reach an agreement to avoid litigation.
- The administrative appeal process is designed as a negotiation-style exercise to find common ground between opponents and the agency.
- Rowberry noted that the administrative appeal process is typically faster, more flexible, and less costly than litigation.
- EPD could decline to modify the permits, which might lead to a court challenge.
- To avoid litigation, EPD could potentially agree to reduce the legal water withdrawal limits and the number of wells, or add conditions to the existing permits.
- Experts state that the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) could set its own extraction limits while revisiting a federal environmental permit for the $7.6 billion Hyundai project.
- USACE announced in August that it is launching a reevaluation of a key federal permit in response to ORK's threatened legal challenge.
- USACE noted that state and local development officials omitted the site's projected water use from their federal Clean Water Act permit application.
- USACE later learned of the Hyundai complex's water demands through EPD's draft permits for the four wells.
- ORK informed USACE in June that it intended to challenge the October 2022 approval of a permit that facilitated the project.
- ORK accused USACE of not completing required steps and overlooking water supply concerns during the site's permitting process.
- The Clean Water Act requires USACE to collect pertinent information and analyze permit requests impacting or disturbing "waters of the U.S.," including wetlands.
- ORK challenged USACE's finding that it was reasonable to assume the Bryan County water supply was adequate and would not require water withdrawals or an EPD permit.
- Applicants Savannah Harbor-Interstate 16 Corridor Joint Development Authority Secretary Trip Tollison and Georgia Department of Economic Development Commissioner Pat Wilson knew the project's water needs months before the permit approval.
- The Savannah Harbor-Interstate 16 Corridor Joint Development Authority stated it has provided USACE with details on projected well withdrawals and their impact on the aquifer.
- As a condition for the state permits, EPD requires Bryan and Bulloch counties to create a fund to address potential significant impacts to private wells within a 5-mile radius of the new withdrawal sites near the Interstate 16-Georgia Highway 119 interchange.
- EPD estimates the Floridan Aquifer depth will drop by up to 19 feet near the new wells, with private wells potentially declining by up to 15 feet within the withdrawal's "cone of influence."
- EPD identified 13 drinking-water wells and three agricultural wells drawing from the aquifer within the mitigation zone, where the aquifer level is projected to drop between 8 and 13 feet.
- Professor Rowberry noted that mitigation funds mandated by agencies like EPD are typically well-intentioned but often poorly administered.
- Rowberry stated, "After we've mucked something up, we usually don't know how to mitigate it very well."
- Stricter limits on well withdrawals are significant because no short-term alternative water supply is currently available for the facility.
- EPD's well permits include a provision requiring Bryan and Bulloch counties to develop plans to replace aquifer withdrawals with alternative water sources within 25 years.
- The alternative water supply plans are due within six months of EPD's permit approval.
- ORK criticizes EPD for not mandating a faster transition away from aquifer withdrawals, noting that Savannah is already decreasing its aquifer reliance in favor of the Savannah River.
- ORK spokeswoman Meaghan Gerard stated that area residents are being forced to rely more on treated surface water for drinking, while agricultural producers face water supply uncertainties and higher production costs.
- Gerard criticized the 25-year timeline provided by EPD to find an alternative water supply as inadequate and insufficient.
- State and local economic development officials negotiating Hyundai's Bryan County agreement are considering Savannah's Industrial and Domestic Water Treatment Plant as a future alternative water source.
- A consultant informed the SEDA Board of Directors that connecting to the alternative water source would likely cost around $1 billion.
- Trip Tollison, president and CEO of SEDA and secretary of the Joint Development Authority, stated that securing funding, acquiring rights-of-way, and designing/installing the alternative water system will take up to 25 years.
