# Rivian Automotive, Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-05

**Source:** [Rivian Boosts GA Plant Capacity To 300,000 Units, Reveals R2 Costs 50% Less to Build Than R1 - EVinfo.net](https://evinfo.net/2026/05/rivian-boosts-ga-plant-capacity-to-300000-units-reveals-r2-costs-50-less-to-build-than-r1/)  ·  `page_008188`

## Facts

- Rivian has increased the planned output of its upcoming Georgia manufacturing facility.
- The company said its second U.S. plant will begin with an annual capacity of 300,000 units.
- This represents a notable increase from the previously announced 200,000-unit capacity.
- The update was shared alongside Rivian’s first-quarter earnings report.
- The automaker revised its financing strategy to expect $4.5 billion in loans from the U.S. Department of Energy to support construction.
- This loan expectation is down from an earlier estimate of $6.6 billion.
- Rivian plans to begin drawing on those DOE funds sooner, starting in 2027.
- Construction timelines remain unchanged, with production in Georgia still scheduled to begin by the end of 2028.
- The facility will manufacture vehicles built on Rivian’s midsize platform.
- Production will include the R2 crossover and a robotaxi variant tied to a 50,000-unit partnership with Uber.
- Production of the R2 has already started at Rivian’s expanded plant in Normal, Illinois.
- Customer deliveries from the Illinois facility are expected later this spring.
- The Illinois facility currently has the capacity to produce up to 155,000 R2 vehicles annually.
- With the addition of the Georgia plant, Rivian’s total production capacity is projected to reach 515,000 vehicles per year.
- According to Chief Financial Officer Claire McDonough, that scale is expected to support the company’s path toward positive free cash flow.
- In the first quarter, Rivian reported $1.38 billion in revenue and a net loss of $416 million.
- The company is counting on the more affordable R2 lineup to deliver economies of scale that its higher-priced R1T pickup and R1S SUV have not yet achieved.
- Looking ahead, Rivian expects continued growth driven by the R2, the upcoming R3, and future models.
- Although the initial Georgia plan included two construction phases of 200,000 units each, the revised approach increases early capacity while still preserving room for future expansion at the site.
- Rivian approached development of the R2 crossover with a clear objective to retain the appeal of the premium R1 lineup while significantly lowering both production costs and the eventual purchase price.
- Based on early impressions including prototype evaluations, the company appears to have executed on that strategy.
- According to Rivian, input costs for the R2 have been reduced by roughly 50% compared with the R1T and R1S.
- That cost compression is expected to support a starting price around $45,000 over time.
- The higher-spec R2 Performance variant will launch first at approximately $58,000 when deliveries begin in the near term.
- Rivian provided deeper insight into the engineering decisions behind those savings in its latest earnings update.
- A major example of cost reduction is the redesigned electrical architecture.
- By adopting a more consolidated zonal computing system, the company reduced total wiring harness length by 2.3 miles and cut connector usage by 60%.
- High-voltage cabling was also reduced by 70% after integrating multiple power conversion components into a single unit.
- The new Maximus drive unit features 41% fewer parts than the Enduro units used in the R1 platform.
- Rivian integrated the inverter directly into the drive unit, improving packaging and thermal management while reducing component count.
- Additional cost reductions came from replacing ultrasonic sensors with corner radar systems, lowering sensing costs by about half.
- Mechanical simplification played a major role in cost savings.
- The front suspension switches from a double wishbone setup to a MacPherson strut design, delivering significant savings.
- Large-scale die castings reduce underbody complexity by 90% in terms of part count.
- Rear door assemblies have been simplified by cutting roughly 65% of components.
- CEO RJ Scaringe said these changes reflect a broader focus on design for manufacturing and scaling efficiencies, enabling Rivian to deliver the R2 profitably at a more accessible price point.
- The company also benefits from improved supplier leverage as it grows, reducing component costs such as the windshield, which is now roughly half the cost of the R1 equivalent.
- Combined with its smaller size and lower material requirements, the R2 represents Rivian’s push toward true mass-market scale.
- The remaining question for the project is execution as the vehicle moves into full production.
