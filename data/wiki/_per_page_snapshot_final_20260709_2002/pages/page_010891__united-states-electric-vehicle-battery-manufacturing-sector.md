# United States Electric Vehicle Battery Manufacturing Sector

**Type:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-02-20

**Source:** [EV Battery Manufacturing Capacity Will Rise When 10 New Plants Come Online This Year. But Can They Thrive in Chaos? - Inside Climate News](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/20022025/inside-clean-energy-ev-battery-manufacturing-capacity/)  ·  `page_010891`

## Facts

- Ten new electric vehicle battery factories are on track to go online in the United States this year.
- The new plants include facilities from global battery giants Panasonic, Samsung, and SK On, as well as automakers Ford, Honda, Hyundai, Stellantis, and Toyota.
- If all ten plants open in 2025, U.S. EV battery manufacturing capacity is poised to grow to 421.5 gigawatt-hours per year.
- This represents a 90 percent increase from the end of 2024, according to Benchmark Mineral Intelligence.
- The industry is facing uncertainty as the Trump administration takes steps that would reduce demand for electric vehicles.
- Battery startups Kore Power and Freyr recently canceled plans for new U.S. factories.
- Evan Hartley, a battery industry analyst for Benchmark, stated that even if tax credits for battery manufacturing are undone, the plants are too far along in development to be canceled.
- Most of the new plants are located in Republican states, making it difficult to take away thousands of jobs promised to key voter bases.
- The Trump administration is poised to preside over a banner year for the United States as a major player in EV batteries, largely due to policies from the Biden administration.
- LG and Honda have teamed up on a joint venture factory in Jeffersonville, Ohio.
- The LG-Honda plant will have capacity to build 40 gigawatt-hours of batteries per year and support 2,200 jobs.
- The projected investment for the LG-Honda plant is $4.4 billion.
- Construction on the LG-Honda plant is in its final stages, with production expected to begin late this year.
- Kore Power, an Idaho-based startup, scrapped plans to build a $1.2 billion battery factory in Buckeye, Arizona.
- Kore Power cited failure to finalize a crucial federal loan before Trump took office and freeze new grants and loans as a reason for cancellation.
- Kore Power is shifting its focus to finding an existing building to retrofit for battery production.
- Freyr, which has roots in Norway, canceled plans to build a $2.6 billion battery plant in Georgia.
- Freyr is instead focusing on a solar panel factory in Texas that it acquired last year.
- Freyr named Austin, Texas, as its new corporate headquarters.
- Analysts note that startup challenges are largely related to technical activity, manufacturing scale and precision, and the difficulty of consistently making high-quality products.
- Evelina Stoikou, head of battery technology and supply chain research at BloombergNEF, identified two main unanswered questions for the U.S. market: how potential federal policy changes will affect automakers, battery makers, and consumers, and what the utilization rate of these plants will be after production starts.
- Joint ventures between battery manufacturers and automakers are likely to face more certainty around expected demand due to integrated supply chains.
- All new plants will be influenced by policy decisions, consumer trends, and economic factors.
- Some analysts raised concerns that the building boom could lead to a battery supply exceeding demand for a few years.
- The Trump administration could further harm demand by revising or eliminating tax credits for consumers buying EVs.
- Jay Turner, a Wellesley College environmental studies professor, expressed uncertainty regarding multibillion-dollar decisions for projects in planning or under construction.
- Trump’s tariff policies are a major source of uncertainty due to their effect on prices for essential materials like graphite, which is mainly produced in China.
- Trump’s actions could reduce the profitability of new battery plants, potentially allowing China to dominate the near-future battery and EV industries.
- The Trump administration has frozen funding for climate, energy, and environmental justice programs through executive orders.
- Funding for the National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure program was canceled as part of Trump’s executive orders, presenting legal and practical concerns.
- Nikola Corp filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection after failing to find a buyer or raise enough money to meet commitments.
- Nikola is a Phoenix-based company whose founder and CEO Trevor Milton was convicted in 2022 of wire fraud and securities fraud.
