# NextStar

**Type:** joint_venture  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-11-24

**Source:** [Plan to use 900 foreign workers at Windsor EV battery plant called an 'insult' to Canadian labourers | CBC News](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/windsor/canada-trades-union-foreign-workers-insult-ev-battery-plant-1.7039366)  ·  `page_050043`

## Facts

- NextStar plans to use up to 900 foreign workers to install equipment at its electric-vehicle battery plant in Windsor, Ontario.
- Canada's Building Trades Unions (CBTU) describes NextStar's plan to hire as many as 900 foreign workers as an "insult" to Canada's tradespeople.
- Sean Strickland is executive director of CBTU, which represents more than 500,000 skilled trades workers in 14 unions.
- Strickland stated the installation work should go to trained Canadians, specifically workers in southwestern Ontario.
- Strickland called the plan "absolutely unconscionable" and urged resolving the situation to maximize Canadian jobs on the project.
- CBTU noted its members have performed similar state-of-the-art technology installations in the automotive sector for 100 years, including battery plants across North America.
- NextStar's requirement of 900 foreign workers with original equipment manufacturer (OEM) experience is beyond industry standards seen by CBTU.
- NextStar confirmed it will hire about 1,600 technicians from outside suppliers to assemble, install and test equipment.
- The hiring includes up to 900 "temporary specialized global supplier staff," mainly from South Korea.
- NextStar stated these workers have specific knowledge of the equipment from building and disassembling it for shipping and will see the installation through.
- The Windsor plant is a partnership between Stellantis and South Korean company LG Energy Solution.
- The facility is slated to open in 2025 and received $15 billion in federal and provincial subsidies.
- Strickland noted it is too difficult to determine an acceptable number of foreign workers until the trades union secures a meeting with the companies.
- Construction at the plant includes a mostly completed building base, ongoing concrete pouring, and expected equipment installation in the coming weeks.
- The Construction Employers Coordinating Council of Ontario and Provincial Building and Construction Trades Council of Ontario spoke out against NextStar's plans.
- These two groups stated their workers have been involved in auto, EV and battery sector construction over the last decade and called the need for foreign permits a "false narrative."
- They asserted there isn't a project in Ontario their contractors and skilled trades workforce cannot build and urged dialogue with industry.
- Vic Fedeli, Ontario's minister of economic, job development and trade, spoke with NextStar CEO Danies Lee about workers coming to Windsor.
- Fedeli stated the temporary workers will assist with training 700 Ontario workers to equip them with net new skills for future projects as the industry transitions to building cars of the future.
- Fedeli clarified these temporary workers will perform highly specialized work, upskill Ontario workers, and then return home.
- François-Philippe Champagne, federal minister of industry, said he spoke to building trade unions Friday and they are "very much on the same page."
- Champagne stated he wants to sit down with the company and unions to determine the minimum required amount of people to transfer technology so Canadians can operate and build the plant.
- Industry experts noted bringing foreign workers to set up new projects is expected, especially in partnerships with foreign entities in the electrification space.
- Joe McCabe, president of Auto Forecast Solutions, stated foreign representation at project kickoffs is standard and must be accepted as a short-term necessity.
- NextStar said temporary staff will work on site anywhere from three to 18 months before returning home.
- A NextStar spokesperson clarified these are not permanent full-time jobs.
