# Mercedes-Benz USA

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [PolitiFact | New Jersey's taxes, cost of living paid off for Georgia with Mercedes](https://www.politifact.com/factchecks/2015/jan/14/mark-drewniak/new-jerseys-taxes-cost-living-paid-georgia/)  ·  `page_046397`

## Facts

- Mercedes-Benz announced it will relocate its corporate offices to metro Atlanta.
- New Jersey served as the headquarters for Mercedes-Benz's U.S. operations for over 50 years.
- New Jersey lawmakers made a last-minute incentive effort to retain Mercedes-Benz.
- Governor Chris Christie's spokesman stated New Jersey could not effectively compete with Georgia for the relocation.
- Mercedes-Benz USA cited the high cost of doing business and tax environment as the primary reason for leaving New Jersey.
- Mercedes-Benz pledged $93 million in capital costs to build a new headquarters in Georgia.
- The nonpartisan Tax Foundation rated New Jersey as having the worst overall business tax climate, literally 50th out of 50 states.
- Georgia ranked 36th overall in the Tax Foundation's state business climate index, besting New Jersey in every category except unemployment insurance.
- Georgia's corporate tax rate is 6 percent, ranking 8th nationally, while New Jersey's corporate tax rate is 9 percent, ranking 41st.
- The borough of Montvale charges Mercedes-Benz $21.81 per $1,000 of assessed value for local, county and school taxes.
- Sandy Springs, expected to be Mercedes' temporary or permanent home, would charge a property tax rate of $35.38 per thousand of assessed value.
- Mercedes-Benz pays property taxes on full assessed value in New Jersey, with last year's taxes on its 37-acre campus valued at about $37 million totaling $916,700.
- In Georgia, residential and commercial property is assessed at only 40 percent of its value rather than the full value.
- For every $1 million in property value, Mercedes-Benz pays $21,810 in taxes in New Jersey but would pay $14,154 in Georgia.
- Expected exemptions from the Fulton County Development Authority would cut Mercedes' Georgia tax bill in half for the first year and increase it just 5 percent for a decade.
- Fulton County Tax Administrator Matt Buff stated that what looks like a higher rate in Georgia is actually 60 percent off initially, with even more savings after exemptions.
- Site selection consultant John Boyd of the Boyd Company Inc. predicted the relocation to Fulton County would reduce Mercedes-Benz's costs, including labor and property taxes, by about 20 percent.
- Mercedes-Benz corporate communications manager Donna Boland stated that company research shows a cost of living differential from the move in the 20 percent to 30 percent range.
- Mercedes-Benz CEO Steve Cannon downplayed the role of incentives in the relocation decision.
- Neither state has made public what it offered, but Georgia's incentive package could be valued at $23 million, not including local incentives.
- Mercedes-Benz expects additional savings from labor costs in a right-to-work state like Georgia.
- The relocation targets lower housing costs in the Southeast and a push to find employees and buyers among millennials.
- Kelley Blue Book senior analyst Karl Brauer noted that auto executives see millennials as key to avoiding the appearance of being outdated.
- Karl Brauer indicated a likely new manufacturing plant for Mercedes-Benz in the near future, located somewhere in the Southeast.
- The planned manufacturing plant would follow Kia, Toyota and other automakers to the region, benefiting from lower business costs.
- Brauer speculated that Mercedes has mapped out expansion options 50, 100, or 250 miles from an Atlanta headquarters.
- John Boyd described the Southeast as becoming the "center of gravity" for the auto industry.
- Rutgers University professor Joe Seneca observed that both Georgia and New Jersey have had a tepid post-recession job recovery.
- Seneca characterized the state competition for investment as "intense economic development warfare" indicating a "Darwinian nature" where more is to be expected.
- PolitiFact concluded that the move will save Mercedes-Benz on its tax bill and expert analysis shows cost savings of about 20 percent on factors such as labor expenses.
