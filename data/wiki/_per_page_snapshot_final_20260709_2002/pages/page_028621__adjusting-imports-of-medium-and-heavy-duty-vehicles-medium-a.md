# Adjusting Imports Of Medium- And Heavy-Duty Vehicles, Medium- And Heavy-Duty Vehicle Parts, And Buses Into The United States

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-10-17

**Source:** [Adjusting Imports Of Medium- And Heavy-Duty Vehicles, Medium- And Heavy-Duty Vehicle Parts, And Buses Into The United States – The White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/10/adjusting-imports-of-medium-and-heavy-duty-vehicles-medium-and-heavy-duty-vehicle-parts-and-buses-into-the-united-states/)  ·  `page_028621`

## Facts

- The Secretary of Commerce transmitted a report to the President finding that imports of medium- and heavy-duty vehicles (MHDVs), MHDV parts (MHDVPs), and buses threaten to impair U.S. national security under section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962.
- The Secretary found that MHDVs are essential to U.S. national security by enabling the Department of War to maintain military readiness, transport personnel, weapons systems, ground defense vehicles, and critical supplies.
- MHDVs support emergency response across medical, law enforcement, and disaster relief efforts, and move over 70 percent of the Nation’s freight by weight, including food, fuel, and medical supplies.
- Import penetration from foreign assemblers of MHDVs is substantial and growing, reaching 43 percent for Class 4 through 8 MHDVs sold in the U.S. market.
- The United States suffers a 50 percent import penetration rate in Class 8 MHDVs, which are the heaviest-duty trucks.
- American companies continue to shift MHDV production outside the United States due to favorable dynamics for offshoring.
- The U.S. relies on foreign suppliers for critical MHDVPs including engines, batteries, transmission shafts, castings, and forgings, creating national security vulnerabilities.
- The supply chain for MHDVPs is much less diversified than for domestic passenger vehicles and light trucks, with significantly fewer U.S. producers.
- There is significant overlap in components, materials, and manufacturing capacity between the MHDV industry and the bus industry.
- Buses are vital to national security for military troop movements, government disaster response, evacuations, and operations in critical infrastructure sectors like energy, healthcare, and emergency services.
- The U.S. trade balance in buses has worsened in recent years, risking over-dependence on foreign supply chains.
- The President concurred with the Secretary's findings and determined it necessary to impose a tariff system to adjust imports of MHDVs, certain MHDVPs, and buses.
- A 25 percent ad valorem duty rate is imposed on all imports of MHDVs and specified MHDVPs.
- A 10 percent ad valorem duty rate is imposed on buses and other vehicles classified in HTSUS heading 8702.
- The tariffs apply to goods entered for consumption or withdrawn from warehouse on or after 12:01 a.m. eastern daylight time on November 1, 2025.
- The goal of the MHDV import adjustment is long-term stabilization of U.S.-produced MHDVs' market share at approximately 80 percent.
- The actions aim to strengthen supply chains, bolster industrial resilience, create high-quality jobs, expand the skilled workforce, increase domestic capacity utilization, and encourage capital investment and innovation in U.S. industries for MHDVs, MHDVPs, and buses.
- An import adjustment offset program is established for MHDV manufacturers assembled in the United States from November 1, 2025, through October 31, 2030.
- Eligible MHDV manufacturers may apply for an import adjustment offset amount equal to 3.75 percent of the aggregate value of all MHDVs assembled in the United States by that manufacturer.
- An equivalent import adjustment offset process is established for MHDV engine manufacturers based on the aggregate value of engines assembled in the United States.
- The offset percentage rate reflects the total duty owed when a 25 percent duty is applied to parts accounting for 15 percent of an MHDV’s value.
- Only MHDVs that undergo final assembly in the United States are eligible for the import adjustment offset calculation.
- A manufacturer's import adjustment offset amount may only be used by authorized importers of record to offset tariff liability related to that manufacturer's MHDVP tariff liability.
- If the Secretary finds an offset program inconsistent with addressing national security threats, they may prospectively prohibit manufacturers from using offsets for specific products.
- Imports of MHDV knock-down kits or equivalent parts compilations are not eligible for import adjustment offsets.
- For MHDVs qualifying for USMCA preferential tariff treatment, importers may submit documentation to identify U.S. content, and the 25 percent duty will apply exclusively to the value of non-U.S. content.
- Imports of MHDV knock-down kits or equivalent parts compilations under USMCA remain subject to the additional ad valorem duty rate until a process is established to apply tariffs exclusively to non-U.S. content.
- The Secretary is authorized to reduce tariffs owed under Proclamation 9704 (aluminum) and Proclamation 9705 (steel) by up to half the applicable rate for steel or aluminum producers operating in Canada or Mexico that supply U.S. automobile or MHDV manufacturers.
- Steel and aluminum tariff adjustments are limited to quantities equal to newly committed U.S. production capacity and cannot result in an adjusted rate lower than 25 percent.
- Rate adjustments for steel and aluminum are limited to imports qualifying for USMCA preferential treatment that were smelted and cast or melted and poured in Canada or Mexico.
- The Secretary will continue to monitor imports of MHDVs, MHDVPs, and buses and review their status with respect to national security, informing the President as necessary.
- Only manufacturing drawback claims made under subsections (a) and (b) of section 313 of the Tariff Act of 1930 remain available for duties imposed on MHDVPs and automobile parts; the Secretary may adjust offset accrual amounts to avoid excessive benefits for exported production receiving drawback.
- The Secretary, in consultation with the Chair of the U.S. International Trade Commission and the Commissioner of CBP, will determine modifications necessary to the HTSUS to effectuate the proclamation.
- The Secretary and Secretary of Homeland Security are directed to take all appropriate actions to implement the proclamation, including issuing regulations, rules, guidance, and procedures.
- Donald J. Trump is identified as the President of the United States who issued the proclamation on October 17, 2025.
