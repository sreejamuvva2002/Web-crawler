# Honda

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-05-19

**Source:** [Honda postpones $11B EV production investment in Canada | ESG Dive](https://www.esgdive.com/news/honda-postpones-ontario-canada-investment-ev-batteries-11-billion-tariffs/748458/)  ·  `page_009069`

## Facts

- Honda is postponing its planned investments to build an electric vehicle supply chain in Canada by two years amid tariffs and slower than expected electric vehicle demand in North America, CEO Toshihiro Mibe said during the company’s earnings call on May 13.
- Honda last April announced plans to invest up to $11 billion to establish an EV production hub in Canada.
- The Canadian EV production hub included a factory with an annual output of 240,000 EVs a year.
- The hub included a 36 gigawatt hour battery factory in Alliston, Ontario, developed with joint venture partner Posco Future M Co.
- The original plans for Honda’s Canada EV hub included all aspects of manufacturing and the procurement of raw materials for battery production.
- The hub plans included a cathode active material and precursor processing and separator plant with joint venture partner Asahi Kasei.
- Honda is consulting with the Canadian government and Ontario Province regarding the postponement, which has received their consent.
- CEO Toshihiro Mibe stated during the earnings call: “We have to observe what is happening and ultimately make the decision.”
- The revised plans come as Honda seeks to shift more vehicle production to the U.S. to avoid new U.S. duties on imported vehicles.
- New U.S. duties are expected to significantly impact Honda's profits in the upcoming fiscal year.
- Honda’s FY 2026 forecast called for its operating profit to fall by nearly 59%, or over 700 billion yen ($4.8 billion).
- Honda's margins are expected to decline by over half.
- CEO Toshihiro Mibe stated: “In the mid-term, if the tariff measures are to be in place for a long time, then we will have to increase our production capacity in [the] United States.”
- Honda has already begun studies on increasing U.S. production capacity.
- Honda will reevaluate its Canada investment plans based on market conditions as they unfold.
- CEO Toshihiro Mibe stated: “If the conditions were to change, at that point in time we have to revisit this.”
- The postponed Canadian investment would have been Honda's largest ever investment in the country.
- Among tariff mitigation plans, Honda is moving production of the Honda Civic hybrid from Japan to its U.S. plant in Indiana.
- Production of the CR-V compact SUV will be moved to Ohio or Indiana due to tariffs.
- The CR-V was originally planned to continue production in Ontario.
- As of 2023, the Canada factory was the global lead plant for manufacturing the CR-V and North America lead plant for the CR-V hybrid, according to Honda.
- Honda did not specify which U.S. plant will assemble the CR-V, as plans are still being finalized.
- Honda’s postponed investments in Canada have drawn criticism from Ontario Premier Doug Ford, who last month expressed optimism that Honda would maintain production in the country.
- Doug Ford said a media report that Honda planned to shift more vehicle production to the U.S. were “not accurate at all.”
- Doug Ford stated: “We’re going to keep Honda here, and I’ll do everything I can to protect the people and their jobs.”
- In a press conference on May 13 after the plans were made public, Doug Ford said he would hold Honda and other automakers “accountable” for pulling some vehicle production out of Canada.
- Before tariffs were levied, Honda planned to ramp up EV production in the U.S.
- Over the past year, Honda has been retooling three facilities in Ohio to create an EV production hub.
- Honda stated that each of its North America production facilities will have a “critical role” in its electrified future.
- Honda revised its EV production plans in Ohio since last year.
- In February, Honda announced plans to produce a mix of ICE, hybrid and fully electric models on the same production lines.
- The mixed production lines allow for enough flexibility to adapt to changes in market demand in North America.
