# Electric Vehicle Market & Incentive Policy

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Electric Cars Are the Future? Not So Fast - AutoSpies Auto News](https://www.autospies.com/news/Electric-Cars-Are-the-Future-Not-So-Fast-93527/)  ·  `page_060648`

## Facts

- Tesla recently launched production of a mass-market vehicle.
- Volvo promised to phase out gasoline-only engines by 2019.
- Federal government offers a tax credit of up to $7,500 each for the first 200,000 electric or plug-in hybrid cars a manufacturer sells.
- State tax credits, subsidies for recharging infrastructure, relief from gasoline taxes, preferential lanes and parking spots, and government fleet purchases help taxpayers pay for every electric car on the road.
- In Georgia, electric vehicle sales plummeted 80% the month after a $5,000 tax credit was repealed.
- When Hong Kong slashed a tax break worth roughly $55,000 for a Tesla in April, its sales ground to a halt.
- From 2005 to 2010, some hybrid buyers enjoyed a $3,500 tax credit.
- Hybrid sales peaked at 487,000, or 3.1% of total vehicles, in 2013, according to Edmunds.com, when gasoline averaged $3.51 a gallon.
- A surge in oil supply from fracking caused gasoline prices to plummet to $2.36 a gallon, and hybrids’ market share dropped to just 2.1%.
- Bloomberg predicted electric cars would become “price competitive” with ICE cars in eight years without subsidies.
- Economists Thomas Covert, Michael Greenstone, and Christopher Knittel estimate that at the current battery cost of $270 per kwh, oil would have to cost more than $300 a barrel (in 2020 dollars) to make electric and gasoline equally attractive.
- If battery costs fall to $100, as Tesla Founder Elon Musk has targeted, oil would have to average $90 for EVs to be equally attractive.
- Kevin Book of ClearView Energy Partners notes that optimists “overlook the compensating effect of incumbent technology,” citing the decline in natural gas prices from hydraulic fracking and internal combustion engine efficiency rising 2% a year.
- ClearView says that in an optimistic scenario where battery costs fall 10% a year starting now and gasoline begins at $5 a gallon, electric vehicles will be competitive in five years.
- If battery costs fall just 5% a year and gasoline starts at $2.25, it will take more than 20 years for electric vehicles to become competitive.
