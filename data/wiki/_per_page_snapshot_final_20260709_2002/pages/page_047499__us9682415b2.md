# US9682415B2

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2014-03-26

**Source:** [US9682415B2 - De-stacking process for the separation of lubricated aluminum sheets 
        - Google Patents](https://patents.google.com/patent/US9682415B2/en)  ·  `page_047499`

## Facts

- The process separates individual lubricated aluminum sheets from a stack as they enter a stamping press for fabrication.
- The process has application in the automotive industry.
- Body parts for many vehicles are fabricated from several body sheets, with a recent trend replacing heavier steel sheets with lighter aluminum sheets.
- Aluminum sheets are normally delivered to the stamping line in stacks and need to be separated before proceeding into the stamping press.
- Lubricant combined with lightweight and relatively thin gauge aluminum promotes viscous hydrodynamic sticking of the sheets.
- The process breaks the viscous layer to more easily separate the sheets for fabrication at high speeds.
- The process involves placing a lift underneath a stack of aluminum sheets imparting clearance of the center of the stack upwards.
- Clearance causes the edges of the stack to bend downwards, creating a concave bending curvature facing the lift.
- Induced curvature provides inter-sheet shear necessary to break adhesion caused by lubricant.
- The lift may be placed at varying positions under the stack to cause a desired curvature depending on sheet shape and size.
- Compressed air or air knives may be blown into sheet edges to provide additional separation.
- Other gases and fluids such as water may be used alone or in combination with compressed air for more separation.
- Air knives applied to the axis of the curved edge are optimum for separation.
- The stack may be exposed to shaking or vibrating motion in vertical, horizontal, or diagonal directions to help separate the top sheet.
- Amount of clearance imparted by the lift may be up to about 12 inches.
- Clearance examples include increments from about 1 inch to about 12 inches.
- Process is not limited to any particular aluminum alloy sheet and can utilize AA5XXX series aluminum alloy sheets.
- Aluminum sheet thickness ranges from about 100 microns to about 4000 microns.
- Core layer thickness examples range from about 150 microns to about 3500 microns, down to about 550 microns to about 700 microns.
- Stack of aluminum sheets may comprise up to about 500 sheets.
- Area of each aluminum sheet may be up to about 100 inches by about 88 inches.
- Aluminum sheets may be shaped as rectangular, Chevron, semi-developed, or fully developed sheets.
- Any lubricant may be used, including petroleum-based, oil-in-water emulsions, oil-based, water-based, and dry-film lubricants.
- Lubricants are optionally petroleum-based.
- Amount of lubricant used may be up to about 300 mg/ft2.
- Lubricant amount examples range from about 25 mg/ft2 to about 275 mg/ft2.
- Process applications extend beyond automotive to marine, aeronautic, and railway industries.
- Example 1 experiment used a stack of 300 lubricated aluminum sheets (approximately 0.0315 inches by 72 inches by 60 inches) lubed with 50 mg/ft2 of MP-404 petroleum-based lubricant on a steel pin pallet.
- A 2x4 wooden block produced no noticeable separation.
- A single 4x4 wooden block (approximately 3 inches clearance) produced some separation but sheets were difficult to separate.
- Two 4x4 wooden blocks (approximately 5 inches clearance) produced well separated sheets de-stackable by hand.
- Compressed air treatment on the two 4x4 block configuration worked well, causing multiple sheets to separate.
- Example 2 added an additional 3 inches of clearance to the blocks from Example 1.
- Results showed aluminum sheets separated better with more clearance and compressed air produced much better separation.
- Compressed air blown along the axis of the bend worked best, while perpendicular application was not effective.
