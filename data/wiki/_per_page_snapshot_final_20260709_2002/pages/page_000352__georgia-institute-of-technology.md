# Georgia Institute of Technology

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2025-06-05

**Source:** [A New Metal Design for Solid-State Batteries | Research](https://research.gatech.edu/new-metal-design-solid-state-batteries)  ·  `page_000352`

## Facts

- Solid-state batteries are less flammable and can hold more energy than lithium-ion batteries but often require intense pressure to function.
- Georgia Tech researchers have designed a new metal for solid-state batteries that enables operation at lower pressures.
- Matthew McDowell is a professor and Carter N. Paden Jr. Distinguished Chair in the George W. Woodruff School of Mechanical Engineering and the School of Materials Science and Engineering at Georgia Tech.
- Combining lithium with softer sodium metal results in improved performance and novel behavior for solid-state batteries.
- Research findings were published in the paper “Interface Morphogenesis with a Deformable Secondary Phase in Solid-State Lithium Batteries” in Science on June 5, 2025.
- Lithium-ion batteries use a flammable liquid electrolyte, while solid-state batteries utilize a less flammable solid material.
- During operation, lithium metal changes shape and can lose contact with the solid electrolyte, which degrades battery performance.
- High pressure applied via metal plates is commonly used to maintain contact but makes batteries heavy and bulky, with plates sometimes larger than the battery itself.
- Georgia Tech research scientist Sun Geun Yoon led the team that developed a solution requiring less operating pressure.
- Pairing lithium with sodium reduces required pressure because sodium is very soft.
- In a controlled environment, sodium metal is soft enough that a gloved finger can leave an imprint in it.
- Researchers applied the biological concept of morphogenesis to understand how the lithium and sodium combination behaves under low pressures.
- McDowell’s research group has been applying morphogenesis to battery materials as part of a project funded by the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) in collaboration with several other universities.
- The battery represents among the first viable demonstrations of using morphogenesis, with sodium deforming readily at the low pressures needed for solid-state batteries to function.
- Potential applications include longer-lasting phone batteries and electric vehicles capable of driving 500 miles between charges.
- McDowell and his team have filed for a patent for this battery system.
- Solid-state batteries still require further development before commercial use but could eventually compete with lithium-ion technology.
- McDowell’s lab continues to experiment with other materials to further improve solid-state battery performance.
- The research project received funding from the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA).
- The publication is dated June 5, 2025, and originates from Atlanta, GA.
