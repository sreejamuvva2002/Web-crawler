# Volvo

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-17

**Source:** [Volvo kills the EX30 electric SUV continuing EV reversal](https://www.usatoday.com/story/cars/news/2026/03/17/volvo-kills-ex30-electric-suv/89195278007/)  ·  `page_014134`

## Facts

- Volvo is discontinuing its EX30 electric subcompact SUV in America after just two model years.
- The decision comes as automakers adapt to tariffs, production costs and shifting EV policies.
- Many car brands are focusing on more profitable gas and hybrid models as consumer interest in those vehicles grows.
- Volvo will continue to sell the EX30 globally but has decided to discontinue the EV after the 2026 model year.
- The EX30 joins a slew of electric vehicles sentenced to death as tariffs and production costs force automakers to adapt production strategies to a new automotive landscape.
- Between the current administration's stance on electric vehicles, a lack of federal incentives and tariffs that have devastated some of the world's largest automakers, many EVs won't survive 2026.
- Electric vehicles may have made an impact on the overall American market in recent years, but not all EVs are equal.
- Some giants like the Tesla Model Y and 3 have become household names and daily drivers for thousands of Americans.
- Other electric vehicle nameplates that haven't seen the same level of success are dead weight to many automakers as they struggle to survive an ultra-competitive market.
- Volvo sold around 5,400 EX30 EVs in 2025.
- Hyundai sold over 47,000 Ioniq 5 SUVs in 2025.
- Tesla sold over 192,000 Model 3 electric sedan units in 2025, eclipsing the sales of competitors by thousands of units.
- The 2026 Volvo EX30 starts at $40,345.
- It produces 268 horsepower and 253 pound-feet of torque.
- Volvo's electric SUV has up to 261 miles of standard driving range.
- Volvo offers a Twin Motor Performance trim for $46,345 that produces 422 horsepower and 400 pound-feet of torque.
- The Twin Motor Performance trim has 253 miles of driving range.
- Volvo's EX30 is pricier than some larger electric SUVs.
- Several more affordable models offer better standard driving range.
- The EX30 may appeal to a small niche of EV fans, but it doesn't make enough impact to be worthwhile for the brand to continue producing.
- Volvo follows several brands including Ford in discontinuing an electric vehicle nameplate that had promise, but ultimately was not sustainable to produce.
- Many car brands are giving up on electric vehicle production because President Donald Trump has rolled back Biden-era EV mandates.
- Since brands aren't required to produce electric vehicles, they can turn their efforts to more profitable and sustainable gas and hybrid nameplates that can be more cost-effective to produce and more appealing to American buyers.
- Toyota recently made two of its best-selling models, the Camry and RAV4 standard hybrid models.
- The Japanese automaker's decision to completely eliminate gas-only Camry midsize sedans and RAV4 small SUVs echoes the buying trends of consumers.
- American car buyers are purchasing affordable fuel-efficient vehicles.
- Tesla's sales numbers are an anomaly compared to EV sales from other brands.
- Volvo's EX30 is unlikely to be the last casualty of a rapidly evolving market.
