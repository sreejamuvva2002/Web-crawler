# Georgia Institute of Technology

**Type:** research_center  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** 2024-09-22

**Source:** [New Battery Cathode Material Could Revolutionize EV Market and Energy Storage | News Center](https://news.gatech.edu/news/2024/09/22/new-battery-cathode-material-could-revolutionize-ev-market-and-energy-storage)  ·  `page_000333`

## Facts

- A multi-institutional research team led by Georgia Tech’s Hailong Chen has developed a new, low-cost cathode material for lithium-ion batteries.
- The revolutionary cathode material is iron chloride (FeCl3).
- FeCl3 costs a mere 1-2% of typical cathode materials while storing the same amount of electricity.
- Cathode materials play a major role in a battery’s capacity, energy, efficiency, lifespan, and affordability.
- The team published their findings on the new cathode in Nature Sustainability.
- “For a long time, people have been looking for a lower-cost, more sustainable alternative to existing cathode materials. I think we’ve got one,” said Chen.
- Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) were first commercialized by Sony in the early 1990s.
- Batteries are currently responsible for about 50% of an EV’s total cost.
- Chen’s FeCl3 cathode would be the fifth type successfully commercialized for LIBs.
- The development represents a significant step forward toward all-solid-state lithium-ion batteries.
- Conventional LIBs use liquid electrolytes that have hard limits on energy storage and can leak or catch fire.
- All-solid-state LIBs use solid electrolytes to dramatically boost efficiency, reliability, safety, and energy capacity.
- With the FeCl3 cathode, a solid electrolyte, and a lithium metal anode, the cost of the whole battery system is 30-40% of current LIBs.
- “Our cathode can be a game-changer,” said Chen.
- The technology may be less than five years from commercial viability in EVs.
- Currently popular EV cathodes are oxides that require a gigantic amount of costly nickel and cobalt.
- FeCl3 contains only iron (Fe) and chlorine (Cl), which are abundant, affordable elements found in steel and table salt.
- In initial tests, FeCl3 performed as well as or better than other, much more expensive cathodes.
- FeCl3 has a higher operational voltage than the popularly used LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) cathode.
- “This could not only make EVs much cheaper than internal combustion cars, but it provides a new and promising form of large-scale energy storage, enhancing the resilience of the electrical grid,” said Chen.
- The research was led by Chen and postdoc Zhantao Liu, who is the lead author of the study.
- Collaborators included Ting Zhu and Yuanzhi Tang from Georgia Tech, Jue Liu from Oak Ridge National Laboratory, and Shuo Chen from the University of Houston.
- Funding for the project came from the National Science Foundation under Grant Nos. 1706723 and 2108688.
- Chen holds appointments in Georgia Tech’s George W. Woodruff School of Mechanical Engineering and the School of Materials Science and Engineering.
- The team's lab began researching solid electrolyte materials in 2019.
- Initial attempts starting in 2019 to make solid-state batteries using chloride-based solid electrolytes with traditional oxide-based cathodes failed because the materials did not get along.
- Researchers hypothesized that a chloride-based cathode would provide a better pairing with the chloride electrolyte for improved battery performance.
- The team is open to opportunities to scale up the technology and push it toward commercial applications.
