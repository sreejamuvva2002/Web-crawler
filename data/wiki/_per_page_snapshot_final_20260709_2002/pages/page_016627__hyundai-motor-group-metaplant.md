# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-05-28

**Source:** [Hyundai’s Metaplant in Search of Diligent Robots – GretAi News](https://news.gretai.com/hyundais-metaplant-in-search-of-diligent-robots/)  ·  `page_016627`

## Facts

- The Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant was built on bare fields in Ellabell, Georgia, less than three years ago.
- The facility spans 697,000 square meters (7.5 million square feet) across 11 buildings and is set to become America’s largest dedicated plant for EVs and hybrids.
- The Metaplant has a production capacity of 500,000 Hyundai, Kia, and Genesis models per year.
- The U.S. $7.6 billion investment in the Metaplant produced its inaugural 2025 Ioniq5 vehicle in October.
- Ioniq models assembled at the plant are eligible for $7,500 consumer tax credits for U.S.-built EVs with North American batteries, although the credits face potential elimination under the Trump administration.
- The factory provides Hyundai with protection against potential tariffs and positions the automaker ahead of many rivals.
- Hyundai executives describe the Metaplant as North America’s most heavily automated factory, serving as a showcase for AI and robotic technology.
- Workers, referred to as “Meta Pros,” can park in nearly 1,900 spaces beneath solar roofs that provide up to five percent of the plant’s electricity.
- Hyundai targets obtaining 100 percent of its energy from renewable sources.
- The plant utilizes hydrogen trucks from Hyundai-owned Xcient, the world’s first commercialized hydrogen fuel-cell semis, with a fleet of 21 trucks hauling parts from area suppliers using 400-kilometer ranges and zero tailpipe emissions.
- Finished vehicles are primarily shipped by rail to reduce fossil-fuel emissions and the company's carbon footprint.
- Approximately 850 robots at the plant unload parts from hydrogen trucks at the docks.
- About 300 automated guided vehicles (AGVs) navigate the factory without tracks, avoiding human workers and automatically ferrying parts to workstations for just-in-time delivery via an AI-based procurement system.
- Jerry Roach, senior manager of general assembly, states that the AGV system eliminates reliance on human decision-making for part delivery timing and locations.
- A fleet of sled-like AGVs autonomously slide under rolling electric Hyundais to grab wheels and ferry finished vehicles to parking areas without human drivers.
- The welding shop houses 475 industrial robots that assemble “bodies in white” chassis components with high speed and precision.
- Robotic quadrupeds designed by Hyundai-owned Boston Dynamics, called Spots, use 360-degree vision to inspect car welds for potential defects.
- Hyundai plans to deploy Atlas humanoid robots from Boston Dynamics across its global factories to sense environments, avoid collisions, and manipulate parts in complex sequences.
- Hyundai executives state that while automation will inevitably replace some jobs, it is primarily used to free humans from hazardous and repetitive tasks rather than replace autoworkers entirely.
- Collaborative robots are used to install bulky doors on the assembly line without physical safety separation, ensuring perfect placement and preventing paint damage.
- The plant features an onsite steel stamping facility where robots stamp roofs, fenders, and hoods in a quiet environment requiring no ear protection, with parts automatically stored in overhead racks.
- The Metaplant was designed from the ground up as an electrified car plant, avoiding the need to retrofit existing internal-combustion factories like those used by Tesla and Rivian.
- The facility currently employs 1,340 “Meta Pros” with an annual average pay of $58,100, which is 25 percent higher than the Bryan County, Georgia average.
- Hyundai’s annual local payroll at the plant has already reached $497 million.
- The company projects a total of 8,500 direct jobs on site and 7,000 indirect jobs for local suppliers and businesses in the future.
- Hyundai currently sources battery cells from Georgia and SK On, with some Ioniq5 batteries imported from Hungary.
- The Metaplant campus includes HL-GA, a $4 billion joint venture with LG Energy Solutions that plans to produce nickel-cobalt-magnesium cells starting next year, with packs assembled on site by Hyundai’s Mobis subsidiary.
- Hyundai is on track to open a second $5 billion battery plant in Georgia as a joint operation with SK On.
- These projects are part of Hyundai’s planned $21 billion investment in the U.S. between now and 2028, exceeding its previous $20 billion investment since entering the market in 1986.
