# T1 Energy

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_materials  ·  **Published:** 2025-03-22

**Source:** [Solar Manufacturing Accelerates In Texas, Trump Or No Trump](https://cleantechnica.com/2025/03/22/solar-manufacturing-accelerates-in-texas-with-freyr-battery-in-the-mix/)  ·  `page_055005`

## Facts

- Freyr Battery previously planned a $2.6 billion battery factory in Georgia but ditched the plans last year.
- The Norwegian firm began laying plans to acquire a forthcoming 5-gigawatt solar manufacturing facility in Texas under Trina Solar's wing in 2024.
- Trina Solar turned the Texas project over to Freyr after changing its heart.
- FREYR is still targeting a start of construction in Q2 2025 with anticipated first solar cell production in H2 2026.
- The creation of a U.S.-owned and operated company that can provide a turnkey solar technology solution is expected to solve a bottleneck for developers, create up to 1,800 direct jobs, satisfy local content requirements for U.S. solar projects, and competitively differentiate FREYR.
- Freyr rebranded itself into “T1 Energy” this year and moved its headquarters to Austin, Texas.
- As T1 Energy, the company reportedly closed the Trina Solar deal last week to the tune of $850 million.
- The new factory will be located in Rockdale, Texas.
- Mission Solar Energy announced a new investment of $265 million on March 20 to bump up the size of its existing campus in San Antonio.
- The Mission Solar expansion will add another 2 gigawatts to the facility’s production capacity once completed in 2026.
- The expansion will enable Mission Solar to combine both solar cell and solar module fabrication in the same space.
- Mission Solar launched its solar manufacturing venture at just 300 megawatts, later bumping its solar output up to 1 gigawatt with an assist from the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act.
- Producing solar cells in-house increases domestic content in Mission Solar Energy’s products, providing customers with reliable, American-made solar solutions.
- The company will source polysilicon from another branch of its parent company, OCI TerraSus, which deploys hydropower to raise the green profile of its production facility in Malaysia.
- Texas continues to top all other states for installed wind capacity, a position it has held since early 2000’s.
- Texas is also challenging California for installed solar capacity now.
- The state only had 14 or so manufacturers to its credit as recently as 2023, mostly clustered in the racking business and other associated hardware.
- Mission Solar held the title of Texas’s lone solar module maker until 2023, when the leading Indian firm Waaree Energies announced that its new 5-gigawatt solar shop will be located in Brookshire, Texas.
- In 2023, the Texas-based firm SEG Solar also announced its intention to expand its footprint with an assist from the Inflation Reduction Act.
- Last August, SEG Solar commenced operations at its 2-gigawatt solar module plant in Houston.
- On January 14, Standard Chartered joined with Apollo Clean Transition Capital to set up a new energy transition fund of up to $3 billion.
- The partners will deploy the Apterra global infrastructure financing platform, which is under the wing of ACT Capital’s parent company Apollo Asset Management.
- As part of the arrangement, Standard Chartered has also acquired a minority stake in Apterra.
- Apollo Asset Management has already racked up more than $40 billion in energy transition investments over the past five years.
- Apollo Asset Management Co-President Jim Zelter emphasized that further scale-up is coming due to unprecedented capital demands across next-gen infrastructure, sustainable power and other transition assets.
- Standard Chartered Group Chief Executive Bill Winters added that their complementary origination and distribution capabilities increase the scale of financing they can jointly deploy and the size of projects in which they can participate.
- The Apterra platform has also been busy since its launch just two years ago, already having more than $4.8 billion in transactions under its belt.
- On March 21, the current White House administration pulled federal support for solar modules through the Defense Production Act.
- By the time former President Joe Biden peacefully turned over the reins of federal power to somebody else on January 20, the U.S. was already surpassing Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam, and other leading nations in solar panel production.
