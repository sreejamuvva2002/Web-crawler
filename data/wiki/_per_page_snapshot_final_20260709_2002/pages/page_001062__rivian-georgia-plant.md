## Facts

- The Rivian Georgia plant is a large-scale electric vehicle manufacturing campus selected to expand Rivian’s North American production capacity.
- The plant aims to create thousands of jobs and position the company closer to southeastern logistics and supplier networks.
- Rivian settled on a multi-thousand-acre site in the greater Atlanta region.
- The site was sited to give easy access to the I-75 and I-85 corridors and freight routes that feed both the Atlanta metro and statewide distribution chains.
- The site sits within driving distance of Atlanta’s international airport and key rail corridors.
- This location reduces inland transit time for high-value parts and finished vehicles destined for ports or regional dealers.
- State and local incentives played a major role in the location decision.
- Georgia and partner counties offered performance-based tax credits, infrastructure support and workforce training commitments aimed at lowering the plant’s operating cost during the first decade of operations.
- Rivian prioritized a location with an available workforce pool skilled in manufacturing trades and automotive assembly.
- The workforce includes technicians, electricians and welders supplied by regional community colleges and trade programs.
- Land acquisition and pre-construction work included phased purchases, environmental assessments, and site grading.
- Early site prep focused on utility extensions, stormwater controls and access roads to meet permitting deadlines.
- The Georgia plant links directly to the Port of Savannah via major interstate and rail links.
- This gives Rivian one of the shortest overland routes to a high-capacity container port on the U.S. East Coast.
- Intermodal rail and dedicated trucking corridors were part of the site’s selection criteria.
- These corridors enable inbound shipments of stamped bodies, electronics and battery cells and outbound transport of complete vehicles to regional dealers and export facilities.
- Plans include rail spurs or nearby interchange options to connect the campus to mainline freight carriers.
- The plans also include designated truck staging zones and route improvements to limit plant-related traffic through residential areas.
