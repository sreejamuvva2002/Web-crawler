# Bosch Mobility

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Measures that aim to close the cost gap at Bosch Mobility – further job cuts unavoidable - Bosch Media Service](https://www.bosch-presse.de/pressportal/de/en/current-press-statement-on-recent-changes-at-bosch-mobility-279232.html)  ·  `page_018047`

## Facts

- The economic environment and market conditions for Bosch Mobility have recently become more challenging.
- The global vehicle market continues to see subdued development.
- A lack of a regulatory framework is making it difficult to establish new technologies, such as hydrogen.
- Market penetration of emerging technologies such as electromobility and automated driving is experiencing considerable delays.
- Demand in Bosch’s sales markets is shifting extensively to regions outside Europe.
- Ongoing structural changes and very high price and competitive pressures exist in the global automotive industry.
- Bosch Mobility faces an annual cost gap of around 2.5 billion euros worldwide in its Mobility business.
- Bosch intends to use all levers to reduce costs at all levels quickly to close the cost gap.
- The company sees potential to reduce costs through productivity gains from artificial intelligence in manufacturing and engineering.
- Cost reduction measures include reducing material and equipment costs, reducing capital expenditure, and making logistics and global supply chains more efficient.
- Structural and personnel adjustment measures are unavoidable.
- Bosch anticipates a further reduction of around 13,000 jobs, particularly at its Mobility locations in Germany.
- Time frames for necessary adjustments vary and extend until the end of 2030.
- A sharp decline in demand has caused significant overcapacity in administration and sales as well as in development and production.
- Stefan Grosch is a member of the Bosch board of management and director of industrial relations.
- In Europe and Germany, Bosch’s strong presence makes it impossible to maintain its current high headcount.
- Power Solutions and Electrified Motion divisions are particularly affected by the cuts.
- Affected locations include Feuerbach, Schwieberdingen, and Waiblingen in the greater Stuttgart area, as well as Bühl and Homburg.
- Jobs are also to be cut in Bosch’s corporate functions, administration, sales, and subsidiaries.
- Bosch remains committed to Germany as an industrial location.
- Stefan Grosch states that Germany is and will remain central to Bosch regarding the number of associates, but positioning must be more efficient to hold own against tough global competition.
- Increased competitiveness is a prerequisite for securing orders in Germany, and thus headcount in the country.
- At Feuerbach, development, sales, administration, and the Power Solutions plant for powertrain components are affected.
- A stagnating automotive market and worldwide decline in diesel proportion significantly impact the Feuerbach plant, which currently produces mostly diesel products.
- Demand for hydrogen technology products commercialized recently is too low due to considerable delays to the ramp-up of the hydrogen market in Europe.
- Underutilization of production capacity and overall workforce overcapacity exist at the Feuerbach site.
- Some 3,500 jobs are to be cut at Feuerbach by the end of 2030, including around 1,500 at the plant.
- At Schwieberdingen, Power Solutions, Electrified Motion, and Mobility Electronics divisions will cut around 1,750 jobs in sales, purchasing, administration, and development by the end of 2030.
- A negative order situation and slow ramp-up of emerging technologies increase pressure to adjust costs and structures at Schwieberdingen.
- At Waiblingen, Bosch plans to phase out production for connector technology by the end of 2028, currently employing around 560 people.
- The Waiblingen plant mainly produces connectors based on thermoplastic and silicone rubber for the global automotive industry.
- Production volumes and headcount at Waiblingen have been declining for many years, making the plant uncompetitive.
- Subsidiaries Bosch Healthcare Solutions GmbH and Bosch Industrial Additive Manufacturing at Waiblingen are not affected by the planned measures.
- At Bühl/Bühlertal location, where Bosch develops, commercializes, and manufactures electric drives for low voltage applications for European automakers, around 1,550 jobs will be cut by the end of 2030.
- Job cuts at Bühl affect sales, purchasing, administrative functions, development, and manufacturing.
- At Homburg location, Bosch plans to cut around 1,250 jobs by the end of 2030 and largely consolidate operational activities of the Power Solutions division in the eastern part of the plant.
- Consolidation at Homburg aims to leverage synergies and reduce structures.
- A stagnating automotive market, worldwide decline in diesel vehicles (including dominant commercial vehicle business), and delay in hydrogen market ramp-up significantly impact Homburg.
- Bosch has presented necessary measures and personnel adjustments to employee representatives and informed associates.
- Stefan Grosch states that despite urgent need for action, they stand by agreements with employee representatives and will quickly discuss necessary measures at individual locations for socially acceptable solutions.
- The company plans to begin discussions with employee representatives responsible at individual locations immediately.
- Markus Heyn is a member of the board of management of Robert Bosch GmbH and chairman of the Mobility business sector.
- Markus Heyn states that geopolitical developments and trade barriers such as tariffs lead to considerable uncertainty and more intense competition.
- Bosch aims to seize growth opportunities wherever possible and ensure Mobility locations worldwide remain viable.
- Markus Heyn is convinced that Bosch Mobility can prevail in the highly competitive global market but must pave the way now using its own resources to secure competitiveness as time is pressing.
