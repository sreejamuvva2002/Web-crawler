# SK Battery America Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2026-03-06

**Source:** [SK lays off nearly 1,000 workers at Commerce plant amid cooling automaker EV plans](https://www.atlantanewsfirst.com/2026/03/06/sk-lays-off-nearly-1000-workers-commerce-plant-amid-cooling-automaker-ev-plans/)  ·  `page_000548`

## Facts

- SK Battery America Inc. laid off nearly 1,000 workers at a manufacturing plant northeast of Atlanta on Friday.
- The layoffs occurred amid automakers’ changing electrification plans and uncertain consumer demand for EVs.
- Friday marked the last working day for 958 plant employees, representing about 37% of its workforce.
- The reduction was reported via a Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) notice filed by human resources chief Chuck Moore.
- Impacted workers will be paid through May 6.
- The Commerce plant will continue to employ approximately 1,600 workers.
- SK opened the $2.6 billion battery plant in Commerce, Georgia, in January 2022.
- The facility notably supplied batteries for the Ford F-150 Lightning electric pickup truck.
- Ford announced plans to cancel the fully electric version of the F-150 Lightning in December.
- The U.S. electric vehicle market is currently at a standstill due to the Trump administration steering federal support away from electrification.
- Federal policy is shifting toward more lax automotive emissions rules and broader support for oil and gas industries.
- SK Americas spokesperson Joe Guy Collier stated the workforce reduction aligns operations with current market conditions.
- Collier affirmed SK Battery America remains committed to Georgia and building a robust U.S. supply chain for advanced battery manufacturing.
- SK is actively pursuing future customers, including opportunities in the Battery Energy Storage System sector.
- The City of Commerce and Jackson County commission chair did not immediately respond to requests for comment.
- Ford scrapped the fully-electric F-150 Lightning in December in favor of an extended-range version.
- A Ford spokesperson declined to comment on supplier personnel actions.
- SK and Ford previously invested a combined $11.4 billion in joint battery plants across the U.S.
- SK ended its joint venture with Ford in December.
- SK also supplies batteries to Volkswagen.
- Georgia U.S. Sen. Jon Ossoff stated that the layoffs represent lost battery manufacturing jobs and blamed Trump’s policies for hurting Georgia’s economy.
- SK has invested significantly in Jackson County, Georgia, as automakers developed EV plans supported by the Biden administration's domestic supply chain efforts.
- In June 2020, SK announced plans to invest $940 million to expand its battery manufacturing presence in Atlanta.
- Gov. Brian Kemp’s office stated the 2020 expansion would create 600 jobs.
- SK and Hyundai are jointly building a $5 billion battery factory near Cartersville, northwest of Atlanta.
- Georgia has attracted other major EV manufacturing investments, including Rivian’s $5 billion factory and Hyundai’s $7.6 billion factory complex.
- Few states benefited more than Georgia from Biden’s Inflation Reduction Act, which accelerated green energy project development.
- Georgia announced 33 additional green energy projects by the end of 2024, the most nationwide according to environmental group E2.
- Total investments in Georgia's green energy projects topped $20 billion, pledging more than 25,000 jobs.
- Qcells, a unit of South Korea’s Hanwha Solutions, resumed normal production on Friday after temporarily reducing hours and pay due to U.S. customs detaining imported solar panel components.
- A spokesperson for Gov. Kemp’s office expressed gratitude for the remaining workforce at SK’s factory.
- Georgia has announced over 219,700 new jobs since 2019, according to a state spokesperson.
- EV demand is still growing but has not met automakers’ recent ambitious expectations.
- EVs accounted for about 8% of new vehicle sales in the U.S. in 2025, matching the previous year's rate.
- Automakers are reevaluating multibillion-dollar electrification plans as financial losses mount and consumer demand shifts.
- Manufacturers including Ford, General Motors, Stellantis, and others have canceled factory and investment plans, laid off workers, and pivoted to hybrid and plug-in hybrid vehicles.
- Consumers are increasingly favoring hybrids and efficient gasoline vehicles due to concerns over EV driving range and charging infrastructure availability.
- Under President Donald Trump, Congress eliminated tax credits of up to $7,500 for consumers purchasing new or used EVs.
- The administration announced plans to weaken fuel economy and greenhouse gas emissions rules for automakers, removing federal incentives for cleaner vehicle fleets.
