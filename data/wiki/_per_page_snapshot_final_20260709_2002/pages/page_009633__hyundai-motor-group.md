# Hyundai Motor Group

**Source:** [South Korean workers return to Georgia after Hyundai immigration raid | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/immigration-raid-hyundai-korea-ice-georgia-eae8c87ed6c6c4f0c5679f7f8fe59f54)  ·  `page_009633`

## Facts

- Two months after 300 South Korean nationals were detained and left the U.S. following an immigration raid at Hyundai’s electric vehicle manufacturing site in Georgia, some of those workers have returned to America to resume their jobs.
- The September raid shut down work on a battery plant under construction at the sprawling site near Savannah where Hyundai Motor Group began producing electric vehicles last year.
- Most of the 475 workers detained were South Korean nationals.
- U.S. immigration officials said the detained workers entered the U.S. with expired visas or visa waivers that prohibited them from working.
- The battery plant’s operator, HL-GA Battery Co., stated that construction has resumed with a mix of new and returning workers.
- HL-GA Battery Co. thanked the U.S. and South Korean governments, as well as Georgia officials, for their collaboration in supporting a smooth and timely return.
- HL-GA Battery Co. stated it remains on track to start production in the first half of next year and continues to actively hire local positions to operate the facility.
- HL-GA Battery spokesperson Mary Beth Kennedy confirmed that some returning workers were among the South Korean nationals detained in September but did not disclose how many have returned.
- More than 300 South Korean workers were detained in an immigration raid on Sept. 4 at the sprawling site where Hyundai Motor Group produces electric vehicles near Savannah.
- The detained workers spent a week at a Georgia detention center before the South Korean government negotiated their release and flew them home.
- The roundup was conducted by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement, which released video showing some detained workers shackled in chains.
- The raid sparked outrage and feelings of betrayal in South Korea, a key U.S. ally that has pledged hundreds of billions of dollars in American investments.
- The Georgia raid targeted one of the state’s largest and most high-profile manufacturing sites, where Hyundai produces electric vehicles at a $7.6 billion plant.
- All 475 people detained worked at the battery plant, which will produce batteries to power Hyundai EVs.
- The battery plant is operated by HL-GA Battery, a joint venture between Hyundai and LG Energy Solution.
- The detained South Korean nationals were largely engineers and other highly skilled workers who came to the U.S. temporarily to install equipment and perform specialized work to get the battery plant up and running.
- It remains unclear how many formerly detained workers are returning.
- Metro Atlanta attorney Jongwon Lee stated he has firsthand knowledge of two Korean nationals planning to return after the U.S. State Department confirmed their B-1 business visitor visas were still valid.
- Kihwan Kim, president of the Federation of Korean Associations for the Southeast U.S., stated that some workers detained in the raid are hesitant to return to the U.S. due to the humiliation of the event.
- Kim noted that the affected workers can seek employment opportunities in other countries instead of returning to the United States.
- The South Korean government demanded improvements to the U.S. visa system for skilled Korean workers following the raid.
- Last month, the South Korean Foreign Ministry announced that U.S. officials agreed to allow South Korean workers on short-term visas or a visa waiver program to help build industrial sites in America.
- U.S. President Donald Trump initially defended the immigration raid in September by stating the detained workers “were here illegally.”
- During an October visit to Seoul, Trump stated he was opposed to getting the detained workers out of the country.
