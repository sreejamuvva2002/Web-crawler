# Murata Electronics North America Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** power_electronics  ·  **Published:** ?

## Facts

- Murata Electronics North America Inc. is one of 47 wholly owned subsidiaries of Murata Manufacturing Co. Ltd. of Kyoto, Japan.
- Murata Manufacturing Co. Ltd. reported $3 billion in worldwide revenue for 1998, with $425 million derived from its North American operations.
- Murata Electronics estimates its 1999 sales will increase to about $515 million.
- The company controls approximately 30% of the North American market for ceramic chip capacitors.
- Ceramic capacitors accounted for 49% of Murata Electronics' 1998 sales, with microwave products accounting for a distant second at 18%.
- Murata Electronics pursues a vertical integration strategy, acquiring and processing raw materials, designing manufacturing equipment and software, and manufacturing all its products.
- The company's last notable acquisition was in 1981.
- Murata developed the Dry Phantom test equipment, which mimics electromagnetic-wave reflection, absorption, and dissipation characteristics of the human head, upper torso, and arm for testing handheld wireless devices like cell phones.
- The Dry Phantom design uses dielectric ceramic powder, polymer, and carbon powders, developed in conjunction with NTT of Japan.
- Murata is developing other products for the wireless-telecom industry, including the monolithic, surface-mountable LDA82 Chip Multilayer Antenna based on low-temperature co-fired ceramics technology.
- Murata Electronics announced a laser-tuned High Frequency Active Filter with high-speed op amps and advanced thick-film technology for cable-modem routers and HDTV set-top boxes.
- The company plans to evolve from a materials company and commodity supplier to an application-solutions and high-end design partner, customizing chip antennas to specific customer design features rather than producing standard products.
- Price erosion has been significant in ceramic caps, prompting Murata's shift to higher-margin, application-specific parts.
- Capacitor prices have stabilized due to short supply, with lead times at Murata Electronics increasing from eight to 10 weeks to an average of 14 to 18 weeks.
- Products like SAW filters, oscillators, and capacitors are heavily booked with none in oversupply.
- Murata's plant in State College, Pennsylvania produces about 1.1 billion chip capacitors per month.
- The company has seen approximately a 50% increase in capital investment this year.
- Murata Electronics is phasing out its reliance on expensive palladium, with a complete transition to nickel expected by the end of next year.
- North American sales to contract electronics manufacturers (CEMs) have risen from 8% to 20% of total sales over the past three years.
- Murata is setting up a full-time business unit to handle major CEM contractors such as Flextronics, Jabil, SCI, and Solectron.
- Another 20% of Murata Electronics' North American sales goes through distributors including Arrow, Avnet, Future, Pioneer-Standard, TTI, Capsco, and Tonar.
- Major OEMs are increasingly partnering with big distributors, forcing suppliers to win business through third-party distributors who may carry competing lines.
