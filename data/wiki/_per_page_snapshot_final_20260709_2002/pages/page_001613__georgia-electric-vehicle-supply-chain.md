# Georgia Electric Vehicle Supply Chain

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2025-08-19

**Source:** [Georgia grapples with loss of federal funding for its EV industry](https://www.savannahnow.com/story/business/energy-resource/2025/08/19/taking-off-the-training-wheels-will-force-georgia-ev-industry-to-ride-its-own-bike-and-build-it-too/85709811007/)  ·  `page_001613`

## Facts

- The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) ends federal tax credits for electric vehicles (EVs) and related battery manufacturing, impacting the growing EV market.
- The OBBBA's removal of incentives is expected to slow EV sales growth and increase reliance on manufacturer discounts and deals.
- Building a domestic battery supply chain and expanding charging infrastructure are key challenges for the EV industry's future.
- President Donald Trump bought an $80,000 red Tesla from Elon Musk at the White House lawn on March 11, stating he would pay full price.
- The OBBBA was enacted July 4 and ends federal tax credits for electric vehicles by the end of September and tax breaks for American battery manufacturers in 2030.
- More clean energy projects were cancelled in the first quarter of 2025 than the past two years combined, according to research group Atlas Public Policy.
- Since January, cancellations and downsized projects have amounted to roughly $14 billion in abandoned investments.
- The abandoned investments include a $1 billion factory planned to produce thermal barriers for batteries in Statesboro.
- Georgia rolled out billions of dollars in tax breaks for EV manufacturing plants with the goal of becoming the 'electric vehicle capital of America' by the end of Gov. Brian Kemp's second term.
- Industry leaders are reluctant to show signs of defeat and boast that the industry is growing towards an independent supply chain that will rely less on federal support.
- Georgia Public Service Commissioner Tim Echols stated that tax credits played a purpose but that purpose may be over, suggesting the state may not need subsidies in the future.
- Electrification Coalition Policy Manager Skye Golann emphasized the need to develop an American critical mineral supply chain during the opening speech of an Aug. 7 press briefing titled 'How EV Investments Are Powering Prosperity in Georgia.'
- The Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), passed in August 2022, provided tax credits, subsidies, and incentives for purchasing and manufacturing EVs and their charging facilities.
- A 2024 report by the Rhodium Group and MIT found that investments in batteries, fueling equipment, and critical minerals refining and processing have each grown at least three-fold since the IRA.
- In 2022, the U.S. Department of Energy granted $178 million for the construction of Solvay Specialty Polymers' battery-grade polyvinylidene fluoride facility in Augusta.
- Last year, the DOE awarded $80 million to Fort Valley-based Blue Bird for its electric school bus manufacturing and workforce development.
- Cox Automotive Director of Industry Insights Stephanie Streaty noted that EV purchases ticked up in the U.S., spurred by an IRA credit offering a $7,500 discount for a new EV and $4,000 or 30% for a used EV.
- Last year was a record year of 1.3 million EVs sold, according to Streaty.
- The Georgia Clean Energy Roadshow hosted by Echols at Savannah Technical College on Aug. 12 featured over 60 vehicles, including one of the City of Savannah's Nissan Leafs starting at about $30,000.
- When Echols first began the roadshow 15 years ago, only the Tesla Roadster was on display, worth more than $100,000 at the time.
- The average EV transaction this year is $10,000 more expensive compared to a gas vehicle, according to Cox Automotive data.
- Before the passing of the OBBBA, Cox Automotive predicted that EVs would make up 10% of all cars sold for 2025, up 2% from last year.
- Cox adjusted its EV sales prediction to 8.5% due to tariffs, interest rates, and other headwinds, according to Streaty.
- Skye Golann stated that taking away federal tax credits will dampen some growth and called the OBBBA a setback for EVs.
- Lithium-ion batteries could make up 40% of the cost of a car, according to Streaty.
- China still dominates the battery's mineral processing, admitted Golann.
- The U.S. is home to just 1% of the world’s lithium reserves, according to the World Economic Forum.
- On Aug. 6, GM announced plans to co-manufacture six EV models with Hyundai, including an electric van for the North American market.
- A day later, GM inked a deal to import Chinese batteries that will function as a stopgap until it can build its own lithium batteries at lower cost in the U.S., according to the Detroit Free Press.
- Kia's Head of Department Corporate Services and Information Technology Ted Arnold stated that about half of Kia’s manufacturing for EV and gas vehicles remains overseas.
- Hyundai, a parent company of Kia, is in the process of bringing suppliers locally so they don't have to import, according to Arnold.
- The OBBBA will end the 45X tax credit for U.S. battery manufacturers in five years, which will cede growth to other countries such as China, Mexico, Canada, and members of the European Union, according to think tank Energy Innovation.
- In January, Norway-based Freyr Battery called off a battery manufacturing plant in Newnan, stating so in a letter to Coweta County.
- In February, Aspen Aerogels told investors it halted construction of its second plant manufacturing thermal barriers for EV batteries in Statesboro and will instead increase production in China, according to Latitude Media.
- In March, Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America President José Muñoz announced an expansion focused on hybrid vehicles at its grand opening, marking a diversion from its expected all-electric operations.
- Streaty noted that the IRA 45X reduction credit doesn't sunset until 2030, calling it a glimmer of light for manufacturers to take advantage of tax breaks before they end.
- Dealers are getting aggressive with rebates, deals, and discounts now that the IRA is going away, according to Streaty.
- Manufacturers have already spent money to build assembly lines and factories and will want to put incentives on those cars, said Echols.
- EVs eventually need to be able to sell on their own without constant incentives, according to Streaty.
