# Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [US agency to reexamine permit for Hyundai's $7,6 billion electric vehicle plant in Georgia - Batteries News](https://batteriesnews.com/us-agency-to-reexamine-permit-for-hyundais-76-billion-electric-vehicle-plant-in-georgia/)  ·  `page_021215`

## Facts

- A federal agency plans to reassess its environmental permit for Hyundai’s $7.6 billion electric vehicle plant in Georgia after a conservation group complained that regulators failed to properly examine the sprawling factory’s potential impacts on the area’s water supply.
- The Army Corps of Engineers said state and local economic development agencies that applied for the project’s 2022 permit never mentioned Hyundai wanted to withdraw up to 6.6 million gallons (25 million liters) per day from the underground aquifer that’s a major regional source of drinking water.
- Details of the plant’s needs came out earlier this year as the Georgia Environmental Protection Division considered a proposal for four new wells to supply water to the auto factory.
- As a result, the Army Corps said it will revisit its finding that the project would have “negligible impacts.”
- The Army Corps sent a similar letter to the Ogeechee Riverkeeper conservation group, which gave notice in June that it planned to sue if the agency refused to revisit the permit issued for the Hyundai project in October 2022.
- Ben Kirsch, the riverkeeper group’s legal director, stated that the concentration of pumping will have local impacts on domestic and agricultural wells, and raised questions about impacts on natural springs, wetlands, tributaries, and streams.
- The Army Corps’ decision comes as Hyundai pushes to start production before the end of the year at its 2,900-acre (1,170-hectare) plant in Bryan County west of Savannah.
- The site will produce EVs and the batteries that power them.
- The South Korean automaker plans to employ 8,000 workers at the plant, making it the largest economic development project Georgia has ever tackled.
- The Army Corps ordered no delays or disruptions to construction at the plant site as a result of its permit reassessment.
- Cheri Dragos-Pritchard, a spokesperson for the Army Corps’ Savannah District, stated that the permit is still valid and work has not been requested to stop, though it is unknown how long the additional review might take.
- Hyundai Motor Group Metaplant America said it will assist as needed to ensure the Army Corps gets the information it needs.
- The company stated it has worked tirelessly with relevant authorities to ensure they are good neighbors and that operations do not negatively impact the community’s water resources.
- Sara Lips, a spokesperson for the state Environmental Protection Division, said the extra scrutiny by the federal government is “unlikely to impact or delay” a final decision by Georgia regulators on whether to permit wells for the Hyundai project.
- The Army Corps permit obtained by state and local economic developers authorized the filling or dredging of 221 acres (89 hectares) of wetlands at the plant site just a few months after Hyundai announced plans to build its EV factory in May 2022.
- The Army Corps concluded then that the project would have “negligible impacts on municipal and private water supplies,” relying on information provided by economic developers.
- Trip Tollison, president and CEO of the Savannah Area Economic Development Authority, stated they never purposefully withheld anything.
- Tollison said he expected the updated information requested by the Army Corps to be submitted within 10 days.
- He noted the federal agency would typically complete its review within a month and expressed confidence that the reevaluation won’t hold up the project.
- Tollison said, “There’s enough water for everyone,” and added, “We feel really good about where we are.”
- Georgia’s environmental agency issued draft permits in July for the four wells to supply Hyundai.
- The state agency is now evaluating public comments before reaching a final decision on the wells.
- The wells would be drilled in neighboring Bulloch County, where some farmers and rural residents have said they worry the auto plant will siphon water away from their crops and homes.
- State regulators concluded that water withdrawals by the Hyundai plant would lower water levels in the aquifer up to 19 feet (5.8 meters) for private wells within 5 miles (8 kilometers).
- Regulators said most wells won’t see any impacts because they reach deeper into the ground.
- The state agency also said that nearby rivers and streams won’t be affected because dense layers of rock seal off the aquifer from water at the surface.
- Kirsch with the Ogeechee Riverkeeper said he hopes a second look by the Army Corps will provide more details on how state regulators reached those conclusions.
- He stated, “We definitely want to see the Corps independently evaluate this,” and added, “This should have been all considered before the wetlands were filled and buildings went up.”
