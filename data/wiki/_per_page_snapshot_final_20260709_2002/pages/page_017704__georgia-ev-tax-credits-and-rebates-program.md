# Georgia EV Tax Credits and Rebates Program

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Georgia EV Tax Credits and Rebates (2025) | EnergySage](https://www.energysage.com/ev-charging/georgia-ev-incentives/)  ·  `page_017704`

## Facts

- Georgia ended its EV tax credit program in 2016 but built a comprehensive network of utility rebates, charging incentives, and infrastructure programs.
- Carroll Electric Membership Cooperative offers a Drive Free for a Year program providing a $30 monthly energy credit during the first year of EV ownership, totaling $360 in savings.
- Snapping Shoals EMC offers a similar EV incentive for new purchases and leases with a $30 monthly credit intended to cover charging costs during the first year.
- Tri-County EMC residential customers qualify for the EV365 program, providing a $30 monthly bill credit during the first year of EV ownership.
- Georgia EV owners can use high occupancy vehicle (HOV) lanes on highways regardless of passenger count.
- With a PeachPass, EV owners can access high occupancy toll (HOT) lanes at no additional cost.
- Public charging in Georgia averages $0.41 per kWh.
- Home charging in Georgia typically runs $0.16 per kWh.
- Twelve utilities across Georgia offer Level 2 EV charger rebates ranging from $50 to $250.
- A utility offers a $250 rebate to help reduce the Level 2 EV charger installation cost, requiring enrollment in the Drive Free for a Year program to qualify and save a total of $610 with monthly billing credits.
- Customers can qualify for a $250 rebate on a new Energy Star-certified Level 2 charger with connected features, automatically enrolling them in the utility's EV charging program.
- A utility offers $100 toward a Level 2 EV charger for at-home charging.
- A utility provides a $50 rebate per NEMA 14-30 or 14-50 outlet for new residential construction projects to build EV-ready homes.
- A utility supports electrification with a program offering a $250 Georgia EV charger rebate for installing a Level 2 charger at home.
- A utility provides a $250 rebate toward a residential Level 2 charger upon completing the application form.
- Residential customers receive a $200 rebate for Level 2 chargers, requiring sign-up for the utility's plug-in vehicle rate for at least a year.
- A utility offers a $250 rebate on Level 2 chargers.
- Customers can qualify for a $250 Level 2 charger rebate to save on the upfront cost of charging their EV at home.
- A utility offers a rebate program where customers can save $250 on a Level 2 EV charger.
- MEAG is currently the only public power community in Georgia offering a Georgia EV incentive, providing a $250 rebate toward a residential Level 2 charger.
- The Electric Vehicle Charging Station Tax Credit for Businesses allows commercial entities to claim a state tax credit of up to $2,500 (up to 10%) to cover a portion of EV charger installation costs, applying to Level 2 and DC Fast stations.
- Georgia Power Company uses a tiered structure for business rebates: up to $2,000 total for projects with five or fewer Level 2 chargers ($50 per kWh limit); $250 per kWh rebate for six or more Level 2 chargers (limits of $20,000 per project and $40,000 per year); DC Fast chargers qualify for $100 per kWh rebates (up to $20,000 per project and $40,000 annually).
- Cobb EMC offers grants ranging from $500 to $5,000 for businesses based on project size and available funds, requiring publicly available charging projects.
- Sumter EMC commercial customers can qualify for a $500 rebate per charger for Level 2 and DC Fast charging.
- Many Georgia homes built before 1990 need electrical panel upgrades to safely support Level 2 EV charging, as older 100-amp panels often lack capacity for the required 240-volt, 40-amp circuits.
- The federal Energy Efficient Home Improvement Tax Credit covers up to 30% of electrical panel upgrade costs (capped at $600) when supporting EV charging, applicable only to work completed before December 31, 2025.
- Georgia Power offers a time-of-use plan charging $0.137 per kWh during on-peak hours (2 to 7 PM weekdays) or $0.0146 per kWh off-peak, with demand charges up to $11.70 per kWh.
- Georgia Power's Overnight Advantage plan waves demand charges for a higher per-kWh rate: $0.286 per kWh on-peak, $0.098 per kWh off-peak, and $0.021/kWh during super-off-peak hours (11 PM to 7 AM).
- Coastal Electric Cooperative offers rates from $0.20 per kWh during on-peak hours to $0.05 per kWh during super off-peak hours (10 PM to 6 AM), providing up to 75% savings for overnight charging.
- Cobb EMC residential customers can join the NiteFlex program for up to 400 kWh of free energy each month between midnight and 6 AM, with additional usage at $0.05 per kWh.
- Flint Energies provides a super-off-peak rate of $0.05 per kWh for charging between midnight and 6 AM, plus a $35 monthly basic charge and a $25 bill credit during the first year.
- Georgia has approximately 2,241 public charging stations statewide as of September 2025, including 6,304 public charging ports.
- Of the publicly available chargers, 4,515 are Level 2 stations and 1,789 are DC fast chargers.
- Most public charging options in Georgia are located in Atlanta, followed by Alpharetta and then Savannah.
- Public charging costs in Georgia average $0.41 per kWh, with many networks using variable pricing based on location, time of day, and power delivery.
- Continued investment in EV infrastructure and supportive clean energy policies is planned through 2030.
