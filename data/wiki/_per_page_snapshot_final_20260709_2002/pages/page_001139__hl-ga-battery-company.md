# HL-GA Battery Company

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-09-09

**Source:** [Georgia battery-factory raid reveals new Trump target](https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/2025/09/09/hyundai-battery-factory-raid-trump-employment-enforcement/86043936007/)  ·  `page_001139`

## Facts

- The federal E-Verify system is supposed to help employers determine whether their workers are legal, but its use isn't always required.
- Since 1986, employers have been required to verify workers' eligibility to work, and can be fined or jailed if caught knowingly breaking the law.
- Enforcement of employer hiring laws has typically focused on the workers rather than those who hire them.
- Trump officials claim some employers exploit desperate workers through lower pay and dangerous working conditions to pocket savings.
- Some employers argue there aren't enough willing American workers to do the toughest jobs at the wages they're offering.
- Since January, the Trump administration has targeted workers at raids of California car washes and marijuana farms, a Louisiana racetrack, a Florida construction site and a Nebraska meatpacking plant.
- Those past enforcement actions have not yet translated into criminal cases or fines against the employers, but significantly disrupted business operations in some cases.
- The administration has fined two Colorado cleaning companies millions of dollars for hiring dozens of undocumented workers and won criminal indictments in two other schemes to employ illegal labor in Arizona and Ohio.
- Officials declined to comment on an active criminal investigation at the Georgia plant, sparked in part by local labor unions' complaints that foreign workers were filling jobs.
- Federal officials on Sept. 4 raided the HL-GA Battery Company project about 30 miles northwest of Savannah, detaining nearly 500 workers.
- The majority of the detained employees were South Koreans who lacked permission to work in the United States.
- Special Agent in Charge Steven Schrank of Homeland Security Investigations in Georgia and Alabama stated that companies must bring workers in legally if needed for building or other projects.
- Schrank added that the operation sends a clear message that those who exploit the system and undermine the workforce will be held accountable.
- So far, only the detained workers are facing punishment, as they were taken to immigration detention sites for deportation.
- South Korean officials are working with the U.S. State Department to voluntarily repatriate approximately 300 detained South Korean citizens.
- South Korean media reported the detained workers could fly home via charter flight as soon as Sept. 10.
- The treatment of South Korean detainees contrasts with tens of thousands of Mexicans, Venezuelans and Guatemalans who face months in jail-like detention centers before forced deportation.
- Worksite enforcement actions have been rare in recent years, with HSI records showing investigations into employers can take years to bring to trial.
- The Trump administration has recently injected new urgency into employer cases by issuing millions of dollars in fines and bringing criminal cases across the country.
- Over the past decade, there have been fewer than 200 federal prosecutions of employers for hiring illegal labor, according to Brian Owsley, associate professor of law at the University of North Texas-Dallas College of Law.
- The Biden administration largely halted raids on employers, according to Owsley.
- Manufacturing experts say the U.S. workforce lacks the technical expertise that South Korean and other international workers have, whose specialized knowledge is required to ramp up production in the country.
- The battery plant will serve the adjacent Hyundai Metaplant car factory.
- The projects received millions in state and local tax incentives after promising to create thousands of new jobs.
- Federal investigators say the raid resulted from a monthslong investigation into employment practices, but court documents show no evidence they knew large numbers of South Koreans were working there.
- The only four people listed as specific targets in the search warrant have Hispanic names.
- The Department of Homeland Security and ICE did not immediately respond to requests for comment regarding the search warrant or the four target persons.
- In a Sept. 7 social media statement, Trump acknowledged that his immigration crackdown complicated his goals of bringing back manufacturing.
- Since January, the White House has touted hundreds of billions of dollars in commitments from foreign companies like Hyundai, which co-owns the plant with LG.
- Trump called on all foreign companies investing in the U.S. to respect immigration laws and encouraged them to legally bring technical talent while hiring and training American workers.
- Border czar Tom Homan warned employers that federal officials are closely watching workplaces and stated they will do more worksite enforcement operations.
- Homan told CNN that employers hire illegal aliens because they can work them harder, pay them less, and undercut competition that hires U.S. citizen employees.
- The American Immigration Council estimates at least 6% of manufacturing workers nationwide are undocumented, based on U.S. census data analysis.
- Undocumented workers represent 7% of the manufacturing workforce in Georgia.
- Federal law provides for $3,000 fines and up to six months in jail for employers, but it is only enforced when officials decide an employer committed a "pattern or practice" of routinely hiring unauthorized employees.
- While federal law mandates E-Verify to help ascertain work authorization, it is up to states to determine whether to require its use.
- Fewer than half of states mandate the use of E-Verify for public and some private employers, according to a World Population Review analysis.
- Foreign workers can easily produce high-quality fake documents, making E-Verify not foolproof.
- A police department in Maine recently tangled with the White House over hiring an officer cleared by E-Verify who was later found to be in the country illegally.
- Assistant Secretary of Homeland Security Tricia McLaughlin stated that using E-Verify does not absolve employers of their failure to conduct basic background checks to verify legal status.
- Federal prosecutors have rarely gone after big-name firms for hiring undocumented workers, according to Owsley.
- In April, the Trump administration fined two Denver-based cleaning companies $6.2 million and $1 million respectively for hiring dozens of unauthorized workers.
- That same month, prosecutors ordered the multimillion-dollar seizure of 14 properties, seven bank accounts and 15 vehicles from a Chinese staffing company accused of laundering money and supplying foreign workers to an Ohio factory.
- Owsley noted that workplace enforcement has historically been used against smaller employers rather than major corporations like Hyundai or Tyson.
- During a Sept. 5 news conference, Schrank said investigators are still trying to determine who hired the illegal workers at the Georgia construction site.
- Schrank explained there was a network of subcontractors and subcontractors for subcontractors on site, with employees working for various companies beyond just the parent company.
- Investigators continue to work on determining exactly which workers worked for what companies.
