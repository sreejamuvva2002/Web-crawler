# Rivian Automotive

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Rivian breaks ground on $5 billion Georgia electric vehicle plant | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/rivian-electric-vehicles-georgia-plant-tesla-a4678f82d9ed276af0a4b0c1050de573)  ·  `page_001052`

## Facts

- Rivian broke ground Tuesday on a $5 billion electric vehicle facility in Georgia.
- The groundbreaking occurred east of Atlanta.
- The project starts despite President Donald Trump’s successful push to roll back electric vehicle tax credits.
- Starting Sept. 30, buyers will no longer qualify for savings of up to $7,500 per car.
- CEO RJ Scaringe said Rivian will sell its R2 sport utility vehicles because it is a superior vehicle, not for environmental or tax incentive reasons.
- Scaringe described the R2 as an incredible five-passenger offroad machine that happens to be electric.
- The Georgia plant was first announced in 2021 and is key to Rivian’s profitability.
- Rivian currently makes the high-end R1T pickup truck and R1S SUV in Normal, Illinois, as well as delivery vans for Amazon and others.
- Rivian’s truck prices start at $71,000.
- The Illinois plant will begin making the smaller R2 next year with prices starting at $45,000.
- An expanded Illinois plant will be able to assemble 215,000 vehicles yearly.
- If the R2 is a hit and Rivian successfully produces an even smaller R3, it will need more capacity.
- The Georgia operation will be able to make 200,000 vehicles yearly starting in 2028.
- Scaringe called the Georgia operation the foundation for growth.
- Rivian plans another 200,000 in capacity in phase two of the Georgia plant.
- Rivian expects to deliver 40,000 to 46,000 vehicles this year, down from 52,000 last year.
- The company is limiting production in part to launch 2026 models.
- Alex Oyler, North American director of auto research firm SBD Automotive, said it’s do-or-die time for Rivian and that scale is key to profitability.
- EV sales growth in the U.S. rose only 1.5% in the first half of 2025, according to Cox Automotive.
- Tesla accounted for almost 45% of U.S. electric vehicle sales in that period, according to Cox.
- General Motors’ slice of American EV sales climbed to 13%.
- Rivian had a 3% share in the first half of the year, behind Tesla and six traditional automakers.
- Excluding Tesla, Rivian is the most successful startup automaker.
- Rivian initially targeted demand for electric pickups and SUVs.
- Competition now includes Ford’s F-150 Lightning and the electric Chevrolet Silverado.
- After an initial public offering in 2021, Rivian shares have fallen by more than 80%.
- Rivian lost $1.66 billion in the first half of 2025.
- Stellantis canceled Ram’s electric truck program last week.
- Ford delayed production at a new Tennessee plant.
- General Motors abandoned plans to build electric vehicles in suburban Detroit.
- Sam Fiorani, vice president at AutoForecast Solutions, noted slowing EV growth doesn’t play in Rivian’s favor but said there is still an EV market.
- Georgia pledged $1.5 billion in incentives to Rivian for 7,500 company jobs paying at least $56,000 a year on average.
- Rivian can only benefit from most incentives if it meets employment goals.
- The state is already spending $175 million to buy and grade land and improve roads.
- Georgia Gov. Brian Kemp wants to make Georgia the electric mobility capital of America.
- Kemp acknowledged the road has not been smooth but voiced confidence Rivian can deliver an innovation revolution benefiting Georgians.
- Rivian was supposed to be making trucks by now at the 2,000-acre (800-hectare) site near Social Circle, about 45 miles (70 kilometers) east of Atlanta.
- The company paused construction in 2024 as it burned through cash.
- German automaker Volkswagen agreed to invest $5.8 billion in Rivian for software and electrical technology.
- Then-President Joe Biden’s administration agreed to loan Rivian $6.6 billion to build the Georgia plant.
- Scaringe said Rivian has built a very close relationship with the U.S. Department of Energy and that goals align with Trump’s focus on U.S. manufacturing and technology.
- Kemp urged Energy Secretary Chris Wright to back Rivian.
- Some residents oppose the plant, citing it as an inappropriate neighbor to farms and potential groundwater pollution.
- Eddie Clay, a resident living less than a mile away, said his well water turned mud-choked after excavation at the site.
- Tariffs are costing Rivian $2,000 per vehicle.
- The Trump administration ending a tax-credit program will cost the company $140 million in revenue this year.
- Long-term threats include low-priced, cutting-edge Chinese EVs.
- Scaringe said starting construction shows Rivian is working through its challenges and that they have clear sight to finish and launch.
