# Richard Franza

**Type:** person  ·  **Role:** research_and_development  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Augusta University expert weighs in on emerging electric vehicle market - The Augusta Press](https://theaugustapress.com/augusta-university-expert-weighs-in-on-emerging-electric-vehicle-market/)  ·  `page_000609`

## Facts

- Electric vehicles are steadily making their way toward prevalence on the road and in the market, particularly in Georgia and other parts of the Southeast.
- Over the next three years, automakers and equipment producers will have factories in development in and around Georgia.
- Richard Franza, Ph.D, professor of management at the Hull College of Business at Augusta University, stated it is not a question of if EV manufacturers are coming to Georgia, but how fast they will arrive.
- The Georgia Supreme Court recently rejected an appeal challenging bond agreements between the state and the Joint Development Authority of Jasper, Morgan, Newton and Walton Counties (JDA) for the $5 billion Rivian EV manufacturing plant west of Atlanta.
- Construction of Hyundai Motor Group’s EV and battery manufacturing facilities, representing another investment exceeding $5 billion, is still underway west of Savannah.
- Kia Corporation, owned by Hyundai, announced an investment of $200 million to build its electric SUV factory in West Point, Georgia.
- Augusta Technical College is building an automotive training center along Laney Walker.
- Augusta Technical College is also constructing an advanced manufacturing school off Interstate 20.
- Copper foil plants by companies Aurubis and Denkai America have put the CSRA on the EV manufacturing map.
- Richard Franza noted that building a manufacturing or assembly facility automatically attracts suppliers around it, citing major EV investments by companies like Amazon.
- Franza expects logistics companies FedEx and UPS to switch to electric fleets, which will help establish charging networks.
- Franza stated that Georgia already had a battery manufacturer before getting vehicle manufacturers, giving the state a good existing network for the automobile industry.
- The pace at which EV infrastructure is built is a key factor in determining when EVs may become commonplace.
- Franza estimates more charging stations will appear on highways before seeing a significant increase in electric cars on those roads.
- In March, the Georgia House of Representatives passed Bill 406, which would charge EV drivers by the kilowatt-hour for electricity to qualify the state for $135 million in federal funding toward public charging stations.
- In April, Governor Brian Kemp and the Georgia Department of Natural Resources announced an initiative with Georgia Power to install charging stations at Cloudland Canyon, Fort Yargo State Park, High Falls State Park, Skidaway Island, and Wormsloe Historic Site.
- Franza observed that charging stations are already starting to spring up in apartment complexes, gas stations, and other retail locations, providing at least a two-year head start on big manufacturers in Georgia.
- The wider public's adaptation to electric vehicles over fossil fuels will largely be determined by how fast the federal government moves on emissions requirements.
- Currently, little lobbying is needed for EV transition because the Biden administration is already pushing hard for an expeditious shift.
- President Joe Biden’s Federal Sustainability Plan, enacted via Executive Order 14057 in late 2021, commits the federal fleet to transition light-duty vehicles completely to zero-emission vehicles by 2027 and heavy-duty vehicles by 2035.
- Franza warned that if administrations change after the 2024 election, more lobbying may be needed to maintain EV transition momentum.
- Georgia has not yet manufactured any actual electric vehicles.
- The state’s charging infrastructure to support Tesla and other currently traveling EVs remains limited.
- While the EV industry taking root in Georgia would not likely speed up manufacturing or use in the state immediately, the combination of automakers and suppliers building a supply chain base helps protect against significant delays in the industry's growth.
- Electric vehicles currently account for less than 1% of vehicles on the road.
- The strongest indicators of the growing shift to EVs will be the ratio of gas stations versus charging stations, and the production of combustion engines versus electric cars.
- Franza advised looking for when these ratios start to change, noting it is not a black-and-white issue.
- The main issue in the early years will be how much consumers want EVs compared to traditional combustion engine vehicles.
