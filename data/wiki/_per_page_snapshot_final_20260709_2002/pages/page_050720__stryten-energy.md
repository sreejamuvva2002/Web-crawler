# Stryten Energy

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2023-08-04

**Source:** [The Flow Battery Code Is Starting To Crack: Red State Edition - CleanTechnica](https://cleantechnica.com/2023/08/04/the-flow-battery-code-is-starting-to-crack-red-state-edition/)  ·  `page_050720`

## Facts

- Flow batteries leverage two specialized liquids to generate electricity when they meet each other, separated only by a thin membrane.
- The basic flow battery technology dates back to the 1970s, but earlier iterations were bedeviled by corrosion among other issues.
- Flow batteries emerged as an application for wind and solar industries in the early 2000s to provide long duration storage solutions for intermittent energy resources.
- Georgia is now home to its first ever utility-scale flow battery, which will serve as a model for similar facilities across the country.
- Georgia Governor Brian Kemp is one of 19 Republican governors who signed their states up to the “Anti-ESG Alliance.”
- Snapping Shoals EMC hosts Georgia’s first-ever flow battery and provides electric service to approximately 100,000 homes and businesses in an eight-county area.
- The eight-county service area includes large portions of Newton, Henry, and Rockdale counties, as well as parts of DeKalb, Butts, Walton, Jasper, and Morgan counties.
- Snapping Shoals EMC belongs to a sprawling network of member-owned utilities established under the Rural Electric Cooperative umbrella during the Great Depression for federally funded electrification opportunities.
- Electric cooperatives have a community benefit mission in their charters that enables them to explore new clean power and energy storage technologies.
- Stryten Energy, a Georgia-based company, supplied the new battery for the Snapping Shoals project.
- Stryten Energy’s partnership with Snapping Shoals will focus on demonstrating the VRFB (Vanadium Redox Flow Battery) energy storage capabilities and evaluating additional use cases for the long-duration energy storage technology.
- The Snapping Shoals project represents the first VRFB energy storage system manufactured and installed in the state of Georgia.
- Shaun Mock, CEO and President of Snapping Shoals, stated that the cooperative sees Stryten Energy as a natural partner in harnessing the power of VRFB for long-duration energy storage.
- Mock added that they are confident VRFB will be a game-changer in how they reliably and affordably store clean energy for their 100,000+ residential and business customers.
- Stryten CEO and President Mike Judd explained that long-duration energy storage technologies are needed to support widescale power generation from renewable energy sources and that VRFB is ideally suited for this application.
- The Snapping Shoals project will demonstrate the ability of flow battery technology to manage key grid tasks, including cost control, shaving peak demand, and avoiding curtailments.
- Stryten noted that Georgia has more than 3.6 gigawatts (GW) of solar, wind, and storage capacity according to the Department of Energy.
- There is almost 1.1 GW of additional planned clean energy capacity in the works in Georgia, which will power more than 155,000 homes.
- Small-scale flow batteries for home energy storage are already inching into the residential market.
- Researchers at Georgia Tech have developed a new space-saving design for flow batteries that could help accelerate the trend toward residential adoption.
- Vanadium, a hard silvery-gray transition metal, lends itself to simplicity in flow battery design because it can exist in four different states of oxidation.
- Stryten’s vanadium flow battery can operate for more than 20 years without losing storage capacity.
- The U.S. Department of Energy’s Pacific Northwest National Laboratory is researching a “sugar battery” that uses a simple sugar derived from starch to enhance flow battery solutions.
- In June, Energy Secretary Jennifer Granholm visited Georgia and stated that since the start of the Biden Administration, DOE has tracked more than $100 billion in new battery supply chain investments.
- These DOE-tracked battery supply chain investments include a whopping 19 facilities in Georgia, adding up to over $26 billion and over 23,000 jobs.
- In the same period, DOE has tracked more than $5 billion in new solar manufacturing investments, including over $2.8 billion in Georgia.
- These new solar manufacturing facilities will add over 4,100 new jobs to the state of Georgia.
