# Rivian Electric Vehicle Manufacturing Plant

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2022-03-31

**Source:** [How an Electric Truck Factory Became a Lightning Rod in Georgia — New York Progressive Action Network](https://nypan.org/about/news-and-updates/2022/3/31/how-an-electric-truck-factory-became-a-lightning-rod-in-georgia)  ·  `page_020330`

## Facts

- The project is billed as the largest economic development project in the history of Georgia.
- It is an electric vehicle factory to be built by upstart automaker Rivian.
- The factory could grow to be five times as large as the Pentagon.
- It is expected to produce as many as 400,000 emissions-free trucks a year.
- The project represents a transformational $5 billion investment.
- It will create 7,500 new green jobs.
- The initiative unites California-based Rivian and Republican Governor Brian Kemp to bring EV production to an area dominated by gas-guzzling pickups.
- Opponents have held rallies, organized online, spread conspiracy theories, and threatened local officials.
- The debate reflects broader environmental movement tensions between building new emissions-free infrastructure and preserving unspoiled lands.
- Michael Burger, executive director of the Sabin Center for Climate Change Law at Columbia University, noted the trade-off is always case-by-case and NIMBYism sometimes wins out regarding solar, wind, and transmission projects nationwide.
- Opponents fear the factory will contaminate groundwater.
- Many disapprove of the lucrative public incentives offered to Rivian.
- Residents worry the huge facility will change the area's bucolic character, increase light pollution, snarl traffic, and spur more development.
- The opposition movement has spilled into the Georgia governor's race.
- Former Senator David Perdue is challenging Governor Kemp in the Republican primary and has targeted him over the factory.
- On March 1, Perdue held a rally in Rutledge, about 50 miles east of Atlanta near the planned factory site.
- Perdue was introduced by opposition group leaders and argued the factory is a bad community fit and that Kemp sold out to special interests.
- Perdue invoked George Soros, noting his hedge fund owns $2 billion in Rivian stock and he is a frequent conservative target.
- Perdue stated: “We can grow the economy without selling out and giving our tax dollars to people like George Soros... We can invest in rural Georgia without kicking our communities to the curb.”
- Representatives for Kemp and Rivian said they are sensitive to community concerns and that site selection and incentive packages were done properly.
- Bert Brantley, deputy chief of staff to Governor Kemp and an official who helped lure Rivian, acknowledged the real impact on residents and stated they deserve answers to their questions.
- James Chen, vice president of public policy for Rivian, said environmental concerns are misplaced and the community should celebrate clean economy jobs.
- Chen described Rivian as an American company leading in technology and innovation that wants to operate in a green way.
- JoEllen Artz, a 74-year-old retiree near the site and factory opponent, believes the project will wreck the local ecosystem and contaminate the aquifer in a well-water community.
- Artz, a Republican supporting Perdue, said: “This company that makes green products is going to destroy what Mother Nature spent million of years putting together.”
- The roughly 2,000-acre site is largely undeveloped and contains a 200-year-old house the company may help relocate.
- Residents in the area hunt in the woods surrounding the site.
- Jeanne Dufort, a local real estate broker supporting Stacey Abrams, attended the opposition rally wearing a supportive T-shirt.
- Dufort, who has lived in the area for 21 years, is concerned the factory will irrevocably transform the community's small-town feel.
- Dufort stated: “We’re not red or blue... We have carefully defined what we want this community to be, and this is not it.”
- Some residents worry Rivian may fail and leave a vacant industrial site, citing its November public offering valuation of nearly $70 billion followed by a stock tumble.
- Rivian reported revenue of $55 million and a net loss of $4.7 billion for the previous year, causing its stock to drop sharply.
- Governor Kemp allocated $125 million in his proposed budget for land and training costs associated with the factory.
- State and local municipalities are expected to provide hundreds of millions of dollars in tax breaks to Rivian over the years ahead.
- Chas Moore, an auto repair shop partner, criticized the $125 million allocation to a California corporation while small-business owners lack similar options, stating government should not pick winners and losers.
- Ray Austin, a landscaping business owner who attended the rally, expressed skepticism about EV viability for hauling heavy gear.
- Austin stated: “I’ll never go electric on a vehicle because I can’t.”
- Dena Astin, a kindergarten teacher, expressed concern about potential pollutants in lithium batteries used for EVs.
- Astin noted: “There are problems with electric cars, just like there are problems with gasoline fueled cars.”
- Bruce LeVell, a Georgia businessman and Trump adviser who introduced Perdue, cast the project as a Democratic effort to sway votes in an overwhelmingly Republican county.
- LeVell claimed Soros has tremendous money backing the project and stated: “We don’t need George Soros involved in anything dealing with Georgia.”
- Protests have grown vitriolic, including an incident where Edwin Snell excoriated officials at a public meeting and kicked his hat at them, calling the area rural rather than an industrial waste dump.
- A Facebook group with over 3,000 members serves as a clearinghouse for negative articles about Rivian and Kemp, with commenters launching personal attacks on local project officials.
- Shane Short, executive director of the Development Authority of Walton County, and his family faced online threats, prompting him to skip a planned public meeting.
- Short acknowledged people say things they don't mean when angry or anxious but does not believe the factory will change the community's character.
- A recent Georgia Chamber poll found that two out of three aware residents in the area support the project.
- Short anticipates many local residents will go to work for Rivian and views the factory as a local economic boon.
- Chen stated Rivian plans to mitigate environmental impact by using recycled water instead of well water, minimizing light pollution, and blending buildings into the landscape.
- Chen asserted: “We believe we will have zero impact on the drinking water aquifers.”
- Opponents used GoFundMe to raise nearly $20,000 for their campaign and legal help.
- Governor Kemp's budget is expected to be approved despite opposition.
- Construction on the site was set to begin in the summer following the March 2022 article.
- Rivian expects to begin producing trucks at the facility in 2024.
- Dufort stated that stopping the project would require political pressure on the governor, shifts in public opinion, or Rivian pulling out.
