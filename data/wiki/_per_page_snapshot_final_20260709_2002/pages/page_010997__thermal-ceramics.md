# Thermal Ceramics

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** thermal_management  ·  **Published:** ?

**Source:** [Insulation Manufacturer Improves Business with Georgia Tech Assistance - Enterprise Innovation Institute](https://innovate.gatech.edu/insulation-manufacturer-improves-business-with-georgia-tech-assistance/)  ·  `page_010997`

## Facts

- Thermal Ceramics is an Augusta-based insulation manufacturer that is a world leader in the production of ceramic fiber products, insulating firebricks, and specialty insulation products.
- The company has more than 3,000 employees in more than 30 locations worldwide.
- Thermal Ceramics supplies customers in the petrochemical, chemical, automotive, and power generation industries.
- In the early 1990s, the Augusta, Ga. facility became ISO certified to be in conformance with quality management standards maintained by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO).
- By 2005, the facility's ISO system had begun to lose its relevance and was considered burdensome.
- Sherri Pettigrew serves as the environmental health and safety/quality assurance manager for Thermal Ceramics.
- Georgia Tech quality specialists Elliot Price, Deann Desai, Don Pital, Holly Grell-Lawe, and Dennis Kelly assisted Thermal Ceramics with re-tooling its quality management system.
- The company implemented ISO 9001, an international quality management system standard that presents fundamental management and quality assurance practices applicable to any organization.
- Thermal Ceramics decided to become ISO 14001 certified, the international specification for an environmental management system, so Georgia Tech built that system from scratch for the company.
- The Georgia Tech team helped Thermal Ceramics integrate the ISO 9001 and ISO 14001 standards into one system and served as the company’s internal auditors.
- Thermal Ceramics transitioned its ISO system from a burdensome paper system to something that is online and totally electronic.
- As a result of becoming ISO 9001 and 14001 certified, Thermal Ceramics was able to increase its sales by $6 million while saving $2 million in costs.
- The Augusta facility employs a total of 450 people and was able to add seven employees following the quality and environmental standards success.
- Thermal Ceramics sought assistance from the Enterprise Innovation Institute in the area of lean management principles, a set of tools that helps organizations identify and steadily eliminate waste from their operations.
- Georgia Tech lean specialist Paul Todd led an executive introduction to lean, a 5S overview and planning session, and 5S training and implementation.
- The company formed four teams that represented each of the three manufacturing areas plus a warehouse/shipping group for aggressive lean implementation.
- Lean implementation activities included painting lines for hoppers and pallets, getting rid of clutter, implementing visual cues, and labeling.
- Thermal Ceramics is currently conducting lean projects in the areas of energy reduction, downtime reduction, safety, increasing melting efficiency, and shipping error reduction.
- Weekly 5S meetings are held at the facility to maintain momentum on lean initiatives.
- In December 2007, the Augusta operation sought Georgia Tech’s assistance in the high-impact area of energy management as part of the U.S. Department of Energy’s Industrial Assessment Center program.
- The Augusta facility spans 58 acres and 10 buildings.
- Georgia Tech energy specialists Mike Brown and Pierpaolo Baldisserotto, along with several Georgia Tech co-op students, visited the plant to evaluate challenges, studied energy consumption history, and measured major energy-consuming equipment.
- A report produced by the team included recommendations such as recovering fiber waste, recovering heat from the kiln exhaust, repairing air leaks, and replacing desiccant air dryers with refrigerated dryers.
- The report estimates that Thermal Ceramics could save more than $1.8 million, which is 22 percent of the facility’s annual energy costs, by implementing the recommendations.
