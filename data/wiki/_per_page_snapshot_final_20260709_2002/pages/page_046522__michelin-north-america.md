# Michelin North America

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** automotive_components  ·  **Published:** 2026-06-25

**Source:** [Behind the Numbers: Michelin's Tuscaloosa Closure is a Sobering Bellwether - BriefGlance.com](https://briefglance.com/articles/behind-the-numbers-michelins-tuscaloosa-closure-is-a-sobering-bellwether)  ·  `page_046522`

## Facts

- Michelin North America announced that its BFGoodrich Tires plant in Tuscaloosa, Alabama will be wound down by the end of 2028.
- Production from the Tuscaloosa facility will be consolidated into a more capable plant in Fort Wayne, Indiana.
- The closure requires a one-time provision of around €220 million to cover severance and decommissioning costs.
- Phased layoffs in Tuscaloosa will result in the loss of 1,200 jobs over a two-year drawdown period ending in 2028.
- Michelin has committed to providing severance packages and support for displaced workers, adhering to its stated value of “Respect for People.”
- The Tuscaloosa plant began manufacturing tires in 1946 and was acquired by Michelin in 1990.
- Over its operational lifespan, the Tuscaloosa facility produced nearly half a billion tires.
- The plant historically contributed over $100,000 annually to local charities in Tuscaloosa.
- Michelin cited that its U.S. BFGoodrich sites were running “well below their designed capacities,” creating structural inefficiencies that cannot be sustained.
- Intensifying competition in the recreation and off-road tire market is driving Michelin's need for agility, cost-efficiency, and modern production lines.
- The Fort Wayne, Indiana facility possesses superior size, footprint, and infrastructure to become the BFGoodrich brand's North American hub.
- Michelin has been investing in the Fort Wayne facility for years, including a $50 million upgrade completed over a decade ago.
- This consolidation follows a nearly identical playbook to Michelin's 2023 announcement to close its Ardmore, Oklahoma plant by 2025, which impacted 1,400 workers.
- The Ardmore closure was driven by the facility's inability to pivot cost-effectively toward manufacturing tires for electric vehicles (EVs) and incorporating sustainable materials.
- Michelin is strategically shedding legacy assets that are too expensive or inflexible to retool for the next generation of automotive technology.
- The rise of EVs is fundamentally changing tire demand, requiring different compounds, tread patterns, and load-bearing capabilities.
- Modernizing a World War II-era plant to meet modern EV tire specifications is often less economical than consolidating production in newer facilities.
- Michelin is simultaneously pouring over $200 million into its Canadian facilities in Nova Scotia to produce advanced EV tires.
- The tire industry is being squeezed by a combination of electrification, automation, and sustainability demands.
- Industry 4.0 technologies, including AI, robotics, and digital twins, are becoming competitive necessities for faster operation, higher precision, waste reduction, and supply chain resilience.
- Industrial investment is shifting toward locations with superior infrastructure, skilled workforces, and modernization potential rather than solely the lowest costs.
- Tuscaloosa's mayor pledged to collaborate with state and local partners to support displaced families and court new investment during the transition.
- A former auto executive noted that while severance packages may be fair, they cannot replace the economic stability and community identity provided by a long-standing industrial anchor.
- The €220 million provision covers direct closure costs but does not account for ripple effects on local suppliers, small businesses, or the city's tax base.
