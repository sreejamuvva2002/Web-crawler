# Panasonic

**Type:** facility  ·  **Role:** battery_cell_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-01-24

**Source:** [How much is the government investing in Panasonic? Explaining tax incentives](https://www.kshb.com/news/local-news/powering-change-panasonic-and-de-soto/state-local-and-federal-governments-cut-panasonic-huge-deals-explaining-all-tax-incentives)  ·  `page_048028`

## Facts

- Panasonic's multi-billion, roughly five million-square-foot facility is just months away from opening in De Soto, Kansas.
- Kansas Gov. Laura Kelly has lauded the project as a first-of-its-kind investment and the largest economic development project in the state's 161-year history.
- The plant, with an estimated April 2025 opening date, is expected to be the largest battery factory in the world.
- The project started with Senate Bill 347, the Attracting Powerful Economic Expansion Act (APEX) in 2022.
- The goal of the now-expired APEX legislation was to create tools for success in Kansas when competing for large economic development projects.
- According to the bill, the APEX funding mechanism provides tax incentives for firms that agree to invest at least $1 billion in Kansas within a five-year period.
- The legislation allowed the state to enter into one agreement in 2022 and 2023, with all APEX incentives utilized for Panasonic.
- Expert researchers from the economic development nonprofit Good Jobs First said data from the IRS shows local tax incentives are only a small portion of the average major company in America's total cost structure.
- Good Jobs First researchers believe local incentives don't usually determine where companies go but governments overspend in mega deals like Panasonic to be competitive.
- Greg LeRoy, executive director for Good Jobs First, stated that individual battery plants will get more than $1 billion per year, per factory from the federal government for several years early in their production cycles, which alone could pay for the factories and payrolls before revenue from selling batteries.
- Panasonic's capital investment into the De Soto facility totals $4 billion.
- The plant is receiving far more than its initial investment from state, local and federal governments, a rare occurrence according to Good Jobs First.
- Greg LeRoy noted that Uncle Sam is already paying these companies a great deal of money for every battery they take off the loading dock.
- Panasonic could earn $6.8 billion in federal tax incentives from the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA).
- The IRA's Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit, the 45X credit, is expected to pay over $1 billion per year to Panasonic and seven other factory deals.
- Kansas' total APEX investment is $829.2 million, indicating a huge stake in the success of the plant.
- The incentives are not guaranteed but are provided on a reimbursement basis after Panasonic hires workers and makes its own investments.
- Kansas Department of Commerce breakdown includes: Investment Tax Credit of $500 million (12.5%) over 5 years; Payroll Rebate of $234 million (10%) over 10 years; Training and Education of $5 million per year over 5 years; Relocation Investment of $1 million per year over 10 years; Sales Tax Exemption of $60.2 million over 5 years.
- The Department of Commerce believes every $1 of Kansas APEX incentives will generate $26.06 of private investment.
- The De Soto City Council unanimously approved a development agreement with Panasonic in October 2023.
- The city offered a 25% reduction in Panasonic's water and sewer utilities since the company will be the highest water purchaser.
- The city offered a 50% reduction in building permit fees.
- The city offered a 20-year Tax Increment Financing incentive.
- The city's TIF incentive for the company is in exchange for more than $200 million in public infrastructure projects.
- TIF financing uses property tax collected from the increased value of the development to reimburse the developer for costs of infrastructure improvements needed to make the development possible.
- De Soto Mayor Rick Walker stated that through the TIF, they are forgoing roughly $40 million in property tax over 20 years and will receive $248 million worth of infrastructure all at once upon completion.
- Mayor Walker explained the company will still pay taxes every year but will be reimbursed because of the initial investment, noting they have paid roughly $200 million on city projects to collect back over 20 years.
- City projects being supported by Panasonic include the redevelopment of the former Sunflower Army Ammunition Plant and significant infrastructure and services to support the new facility and future growth.
- Supported project breakdown includes: $40 million sewer treatment plant expansion; $28 million sewer line extension; $47 million water treatment plant expansion; $19 million new fire station and truck; $84.2 million road improvements; $11 million new water tower.
- According to the city's development agreement, Panasonic must remit annual payments of $800,000 to fund operation of a new fire station, police services and road maintenance via a community improvement district.
- Twenty-eight mills of property tax for education are protected per Kansas law, with 20 mills going to the state of Kansas and eight mills to USD 232.
- The city's latest estimate is a tax payment of approximately $14 million to USD 232 and an estimated $35 million to the state of Kansas for education over the 20-year life of the TIF.
- Greg LeRoy suggested local neighbors ask who the building crews are, if construction companies are local or union, if they have apprenticeship programs for women and people of color, or if it is mostly itinerant workers sending money back to Texas and Tennessee.
- Mayor Walker said Panasonic is already making a tangible difference and return on investment, as the city was able to cut six mills out of the 2025 budget, reducing city property taxes by roughly a third for taxpayers.
- Mayor Walker added that quality of life enhancements will be seen while maintaining a low property tax burden on citizens of De Soto.
- Panasonic is required to make a minimum $1 billion investment in the project and employ at least 2,500 full-time employees to maintain the incentive level.
- The incentive agreement will be measured every year, with Panasonic required to provide documentation showing the commitment is being met annually.
- Researchers with Good Jobs First have been tracking economic development deals like Panasonic for 27 years.
- Good Jobs First stated that when a package crosses the $1 billion mark, financial risks are much higher for states and communities.
- Good Jobs First believes local governments take on the biggest risk in these deals since they are obligating property taxes over a long period of time.
- Greg LeRoy emphasized it is up to residents to remain educated and officials to ensure company obligations are met, stating accountability falls on the mayor, county executive, Congress member, and senators regarding who gets jobs, local hiring, and job quality.
