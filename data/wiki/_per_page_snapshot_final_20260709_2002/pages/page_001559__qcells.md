# Qcells

## Facts

- South Korea's Hanwha is one of the world's largest and most diversified manufacturing companies.
- Hanwha made its name in the 1950s as an explosives manufacturer before expanding into sectors including aerospace, shipbuilding, machinery, and energy.
- Clean energy is now one of Hanwha's biggest business focuses.
- Qcells is a subsidiary of Hanwha and a globally renowned manufacturer of solar panels.
- Qcells products are ranked as the most popular solar panels in the US by Wood Mackenzie.
- Prior to the new facility, Qcells had no operational base in the US and shipped products from the Korean Peninsula.
- Qcells' newly opened production plant in Cartersville, Georgia, marks the company's first physical footprint in America.
- Energy thinktank Ember reported on 10 June 2026 that solar surpassed coal in US power generation in May, marking the first time this occurred in a monthly reading.
- Solar accounted for a record 12.8% of US electricity output in May 2026.
- Coal's share slipped to 12.2% in May 2026, marking its fourth-lowest monthly share on record.
- This shift occurred despite the Trump administration's active promotion of fossil fuel energy sources.
- Qcells has been owned by South Korean conglomerate Hanwha since 2012.
- The start of solar cell production at the Cartersville, Georgia facility marks a major step toward completing what Qcells describes as the only vertically integrated solar manufacturing plant in the US.
- Hanwha is targeting rapid scale-up at the Cartersville site.
- By Q3 2026, the Cartersville site is expected to produce 3.3GW each of ingots, wafers, and cells annually.
- By Q3 2026, the Cartersville site is expected to produce 3.5GW of modules annually.
- These production figures would make the Cartersville site the largest solar cell factory in US history.
- Module assembly at the Cartersville site is already running at full capacity, producing 16,700 panels each day.
- Andy Park, Global CEO of Qcells, stated that producing the first solar cells at Cartersville is a milestone for Qcells and American manufacturing.
- Andy Park noted that as ingot, wafer, and cell lines reach full capacity, major solar panel components will be made in Georgia.
- Andy Park emphasized that a dependable domestic supply chain creates thousands of good-paying jobs and gives customers greater certainty on price, supply, tariffs, and product trust.
- Qcells has maintained a manufacturing presence in Georgia since opening its site in Dalton in 2019.
- The Dalton campus tripled its module capacity to 5.1GW in late 2023.
- Combined with the Cartersville plant, total module capacity across Qcells' Georgia operations is expected to reach 8.6GW per year.
- This combined capacity is equivalent to 47,000 panels per day.
- The combined output is sufficient to power approximately 1.3 million US homes annually.
- The two Georgia sites are expected to support nearly 4,000 jobs total.
- An estimated 3,800 of these roles will be direct positions.
- The energy policy environment adds complexity to Qcells' position due to President Trump's open criticism of solar power and efforts to dismantle clean energy tax credits.
- The Trump administration issued an executive order titled 'Ending Market Distorting Subsidies for Unreliable, Foreign Controlled Energy Sources'.
- The executive order stated that the federal government has forced taxpayers to subsidize expensive and unreliable energy sources like wind and solar.
- The executive order claimed reliance on green subsidies threatens national security by making the US dependent on supply chains controlled by foreign adversaries.
- Qcells appears to have avoided the worst impacts of the Trump administration's subsidy reforms due to its fully domestic manufacturing footprint.
- The company qualifies to claim the Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Tax Credit at every stage of production, from ingots and wafers through to cells and modules.
- This qualification position is currently unreplicated by any other US solar manufacturer.
- Qcells states its integrated domestic model offers customers insulation from tariff volatility and international supply disruption by keeping major solar supply chain components within US borders.
