# Archer Aviation Inc.

**Type:** company  ·  **Role:** vehicle_manufacturing  ·  **Published:** 2025-11-15

**Source:** [iSpyVNY - Archer Aviation Takes Control of Hawthorne Airport: Buzz, Backlash & Battle Ahead](https://www.ispyvny.com/news/archer-aviation-hawthorne-airport-acquisition-buzz-backlash-battle)  ·  `page_017029`

## Facts

- Hawthorne City Council voted 5–0 on Nov. 12 to approve consent on the master lease assignment for Hawthorne Municipal Airport, clearing the way for Archer Aviation Inc. to take operational control.
- The $126 million agreement is a pivotal step in Archer's plans to establish an electric air taxi network across Los Angeles and the surrounding region.
- Planned routes will connect key destinations including LAX, VNY, BUR airports, Hollywood, Orange County, Long Beach, SoFi Stadium, Rams (Woodland Hills), and Santa Monica.
- The 80-acre city-owned airport, known as Jack Northrop Field, will support Archer’s role as the official air taxi provider for the 2028 Olympics announced by LA28 in May.
- By assuming the remaining 30 years of a master lease held by Hawthorne Airport LLC (HAL), along with its subleases, Archer will control nearly all non-runway land through 2055.
- The lease setup previously prompted monopoly-related litigation addressed by the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals in an Aug. 4, 2022 memorandum, which dismissed the case on procedural grounds for being filed after the statute of limitations.
- SEC filings show Archer has signed agreements with HAL, 395 Park Place LLC (“395”), and Advanced Air LLC, linking the transaction to new hangar construction already under environmental review.
- A Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND) prepared by consultant Watearth lists 395 as developer of 13 hangars in a document dated Sept. 19, 2025 describing the Hawthorne Airport Hangars Project.
- The city posted its public notice for the hangar project on Nov. 5, setting a Dec. 9 hearing date for possible MND adoption without identifying the applicant.
- Local community group Hawthorne Quiet Skies expressed frustration with the lack of transparency, stating the acquisition was never mentioned at recent Airport Communities Network meetings.
- City officials did not respond to multiple phone and email requests for comment, and Archer also did not reply before publication.
- The proposed project represents only the first phase of airport work, separate from a larger redevelopment described in Archer’s Q3 2025 shareholder letter.
- CEQA documents note geotechnical investigations by Bay City Geology in August 2024 for the narrow-scope 13-hangar project.
- Hawthorne Quiet Skies warned of potential environmental impacts from what it called the “first ever eVTOL hub,” stating residents are entering this new era unprepared with no clear pathway to success.
- Concerns were raised during LAX/Community Noise Roundtable's Nov. 12 meeting, where Chair Denny Schneider suggested gathering more information for a future agenda on shared South Bay airspace.
- In April, Watearth initiated consultation with relevant tribes under Assembly Bill 52 as part of the CEQA process for the proposed hangars.
- Hereditary Chief Andrew Salas of the Gabrieleño Band of Mission Indians – Kizh Nation responded in letters dated July 8 and July 17, 2025, warning of a high likelihood that prehistoric cultural resources, including burials, remain beneath portions of the airport.
- Salas stated that any excavation, grading, or trenching near the airport carries a high likelihood of intersecting unrecorded tribal cultural resources protected under CEQA.
- The MND incorporates several of the tribe’s recommendations, including a Kizh Nation approved monitor during ground-disturbing work and protocols for handling inadvertent discoveries.
- Kizh leaders disputed the city's characterization of the consultation in the weeks leading up to the December vote, with Salas claiming key recommendations were brushed aside and interaction treated as a formality.
- Archer must navigate federal aviation regulations and certification requirements beyond environmental and cultural reviews.
- The deal positions Hawthorne to become a proving ground for electric air taxis.
- Archer’s CEO Adam Goldstein told investors during the company’s Q3 2025 call that proving electric air taxis work in Los Angeles would allow scaling the product across the U.S. and the world.
- A Georgia Institute of Technology press release on Oct. 17, 2025, cited hurdles in certification, air-traffic integration, and public acceptance for scaling air mobility.
- Dr. Laurie Garrow, a Georgia Tech professor who co-directs the Center for Urban and Regional Air Mobility, stated that safe corridors need to be defined for aircraft to operate alongside traditional air traffic and that aviation certification is a rigorous process requiring time to earn public trust.
- On Oct. 23, the National Transportation Safety Board posted on X that an unmanned Air Vev Inc. Zero eVTOL crashed in a field and was consumed by fire, raising safety concerns for nearby residential areas.
- Archer’s shareholder letter explicitly stated plans for the airport to serve as a testbed for AI-powered aviation.
- At the Nov. 15 meeting, Archer representative Eric Lentell clarified that FAA restrictions limit test flight locations and that testing is conducted at a remote site in Salinas, California, not Hawthorne.
- Hawthorne Quiet Skies expressed support for California innovators but emphasized the need for collaboration and proposed establishing a new Hawthorne Airport noise working group led by Archer.
- Under the agreement terms, Archer and the sellers can walk away if closing is not finalized by December 30, 2025.
