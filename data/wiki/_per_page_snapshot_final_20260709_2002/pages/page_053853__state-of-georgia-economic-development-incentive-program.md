# State of Georgia Economic Development Incentive Program

**Type:** policy_or_incentive  ·  **Role:** government_support  ·  **Published:** 2026-05-25

**Source:** [Georgia eliminates key economic development incentives and tax credits - SaportaReport](https://saportareport.com/georgia-eliminates-key-economic-development-incentives-and-tax-credits/columnists/mariasmetro/maria_saporta/)  ·  `page_053853`

## Facts

- Area Development Magazine has ranked Georgia the No. 1 state for business for the past 12 years.
- The Georgia General Assembly eliminated about a dozen tax incentives and tax credits used to attract investment to the state.
- Eliminated incentives included those for corporate headquarters and expansions.
- Eliminated incentives included tax credits for alternative fuel, low-emission and zero-emission vehicles.
- Eliminated incentives included tax credits for medical equipment and supplies.
- Eliminated incentives included sales tax exemptions for machinery, equipment or materials in the construction and operation of a clean room (most often used for pharmaceutical research and development).
- Eliminated incentives included sales tax exemptions for machinery and equipment used to reduce or eliminate air or water pollution.
- Other cut incentives included money for reforestation, land conservation, historic building restoration, the arts and live performances and short-line railroads.
- Gov. Brian Kemp approved the elimination of those incentives to fill a $1 billion gap in the budget created by the reduction in the state income tax rate from 5.19 percent to 4.99 percent.
- The state income tax rate will be reduced by .125 each year until it reaches 3.99 percent.
- Grant Cagle, president and CEO of the Georgia Economic Developers Association, stated that Georgia is competing with states that have much more aggressive incentive packages and expressed concern about taking away tools in an already limited tool chest.
- The legislation eliminated tax credits and incentives retroactively to Jan. 1, 2026.
- Companies may have made investment decisions based on getting those eliminated tax breaks.
- Chris Clark, president and CEO of the Georgia Chamber of Commerce, shared concerns about the elimination during the May 20 quarterly board meeting of the Georgia Research Alliance.
- The General Assembly eliminated a dozen tax incentives in the final hours of the legislative session.
- Some eliminated credits had outlived their usefulness, but others remain active and important tools in Georgia’s economic development strategy.
- Affected tools include tax incentives and credits tied to clean technology, pharmaceuticals, corporate headquarters and port activity.
- These incentives are part of real recruitment and retention conversations happening every day with companies considering where to invest, expand and create jobs.
- The overnight elimination of incentives was criticized for sending a negative signal to business prospects due to lack of consistency and predictability in tax and economic policy.
- Major investment decisions are often made years in advance and modeled around existing incentives and long-term financial assumptions.
- When the state abruptly changes course or removes tools without a broader strategic framework, it creates uncertainty and sends the wrong message to employers and investors.
- The 2026 legislative session occurred in the middle of a pivotal election year, with reducing taxes on Georgians becoming a rallying cry among key legislators.
- Blake Tillery, chairman of the Georgia Senate Appropriations Committee, initially proposed legislation in January to eliminate 29 tax incentives.
- Blake Tillery announced last October that he was running to be the Republican nominee for Lt. Governor and came in third in the May 19th primary.
- Tillery stated businesses have had their decade of tax breaks and it is time for people to have theirs.
- Tillery argued that reducing the state’s income tax would reduce taxes for many small businesses, which create the most jobs.
- Tillery acknowledged Georgia may land one less Fortune 500 company but prioritized reducing taxes on small businesses and families over corporate breaks.
- In 2010, the Special Council on Tax Reform and Fairness for Georgians proposed an overhaul of Georgia’s tax code.
- The late A.D. Frazier chaired the special council in 2010.
- A.D. Frazier told the Buckhead Rotary Club in April 2011 that lobbyists killed the “revenue-neutral” recommendations in a 34-page report calling for income tax reductions by increasing state sales taxes.
- Tax credits and incentives to build data centers survived scrutiny, despite being highly controversial due to high energy and water usage.
- Data center incentives are worth $1.5 billion in state funds and $1.3 billion from local governments.
- Tillery cited that corporate tax credits alone are not the reason Georgia has been number one to do business for 12 years, but is okay with losing that title if everyday Georgians pay less income tax.
- States with no or reduced income taxes usually have to pay higher property and sales taxes, which often places a heavier burden on their lower-income residents.
- Democratic gubernatorial candidate Michael Thurmond proposed cutting the state sales tax in half, calling it the most regressive tax in the state.
- Michael Thurmond came in third in the primary, which Keisha Lance Bottoms won outright to become the Democratic nominee for governor.
- Chris Clark has served as president of the Georgia Chamber since 2010.
- Clark emphasized that Georgia has built its reputation on stability, competitiveness and a pro-growth business climate, and preserving that reputation matters just as much as any individual incentive itself.
