# U.S. Electric Vehicle Supply Chain & Policy Landscape

**Source:** [The US has big EV goals. Can the domestic EV industry… | Canary Media](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/electric-vehicles/the-us-has-big-ev-goals-can-the-domestic-ev-industry-meet-them)  ·  `page_052386`

## Facts

- Jay Turner, a professor of environmental studies at Wellesley College and author of Charged, tallied 48 new EV supply chain projects representing a combined $50.3 billion in investment as of late May.
- The tally increased by at least $3.8 billion over the previous 30 days.
- General Motors and Samsung SDI announced plans to invest more than $3 billion in a battery factory since late April.
- Anovion, a maker of graphite for lithium-ion batteries, unveiled plans for an $800 million production facility in Georgia.
- Tesla began construction of a $375 million lithium refining plant in Texas expected to supply enough material for the lithium-ion batteries in about 1 million EVs per year by 2025.
- The U.S. has seen 331 gigawatt-hours of new planned battery production capacity announced since the Inflation Reduction Act became law.
- The U.S. is on track to produce 910 gigawatt-hours’ worth of batteries each year by 2030.
- This capacity is projected to supply 5.3 million EVs per year in the U.S. by 2030, up from 1.6 million per year currently.
- Jay Turner described the investments as "enormous, unprecedented" and noted the U.S. has never seen this scale of investment before.
- The Biden administration has set a goal of EVs making up half of all new light-duty vehicle sales by 2030.
- The Inflation Reduction Act boosted ambitions with tax credits and incentives for domestic manufacturing of EVs, batteries, critical materials, and for consumers/companies buying them.
- Automakers including BMW, Ford, General Motors, Hyundai, Mercedes, Nissan, Stellantis, Volkswagen, and Chinese companies plan to invest a collective $1.2 trillion through 2030 in EVs and batteries globally, according to an October 2022 Reuters analysis.
- Atlas Public Policy forecasted $860 billion invested by 2030, with nearly a quarter ($210 billion) going to the U.S., more than any other country.
- China overwhelmingly dominates much of the EV supply chain, from mineral refining to cathode/anode production, battery cells, and EV manufacturing.
- U.S. EV and battery capacity may expand roughly twentyfold over the next decade but starts from a minuscule share of global supply.
- The debate centers on the Inflation Reduction Act’s 30D tax credits of up to $7,500 for purchasing plug-in or fuel-cell EVs.
- Eligibility depends on complex rules regarding battery and material sourcing from the U.S. or free-trade partner countries.
- A future implementation stage could begin as early as next year, making tax credits unavailable for EVs containing materials/products from "foreign entities of concern," including China.
- U.S. Senator Joe Manchin played a pivotal role in crafting the tax credit and enabling the Inflation Reduction Act's passage.
- Manchin wants strict limits on foreign battery components to push manufacturers to set up shop in the U.S.
- Manchin threatened to sue the federal government over the Treasury Department's April rollout of relaxed guidance on domestic and free-trade-partner content requirements.
- Rules require at least half of the value of battery components to be produced or assembled in North America to qualify for $3,750 of the credit.
- 40 percent of the value of critical minerals must be sourced from the U.S. or a free-trade-agreement country to qualify for the other half.
- These requirements will rise by 10 percentage points per year.
- Rules effective since the start of the year require EVs to undergo final assembly in North America to qualify for the tax credit.
- The final assembly rule disqualifies EVs from Hyundai and Kia, which are assembled in South Korea, prompting them to plan moving some production to the U.S.
- Domestic-content rules disqualify EV models from Audi, BMW, and Nissan, and limit others like the Ford Mustang Mach-E to partial credits of $3,750.
- The full $7,500 credit is available for the Tesla Model Y and about a dozen other models including the Cadillac Lyriq, Ford F-150 Lightning, Volkswagen ID.4, Chevy Bolt, Blazer, Equinox, and Silverado.
- Eligible models currently make up more than 90 percent of the U.S. EV market, according to Albert Gore, executive director of the Zero Emission Transportation Association (ZETA).
- ZETA represents automakers, battery manufacturers, mining companies, charging equipment makers, utilities, and other companies invested in a growing domestic EV market.
- Albert Gore noted it will take time for North America’s EV supply chains to develop sufficient capacity to serve increasingly stringent sourcing requirements.
- Gore stated the Inflation Reduction Act tries to accomplish three overlapping goals: making EVs plentiful/affordable, securing the supply chain by onshoring capacity from vehicles to batteries and minerals, and moving the supply chain away from China toward free-trade agreement countries.
- The Biden administration announced a late March trade deal with Japan that could allow Japanese EV battery minerals to qualify under 30D tax-credit rules.
- Industry observers expect similar efforts with the European Union and other allies in the coming months.
- The pot of money for manufacturers via tax credits is many times larger than the $7,500 consumer credit.
- Gore identified the 45X production tax credits as more powerful for encouraging domestic production of cathodes and battery components.
- 45X credits include up to $45 per kilowatt-hour for domestically produced batteries and 10 percent of production cost for cathode/anode materials and critical minerals.
- These credits were designed to close the cost gap between U.S.-made batteries and Chinese batteries, and help achieve EV cost parity with internal-combustion vehicles.
- Atlas Public Policy tallied at least $83 billion in loans, grants, and tax credits from the IRA and 2021 infrastructure law for the domestic EV industry.
- This includes $8.1 billion of grants and at least $25 billion of loans specifically for low- or zero-emissions vehicle production.
- The U.S. Department of Energy has $7 billion in grant money from the infrastructure law to directly fund battery and critical-mineral supply chains.
- The first round of DOE grants in October awarded $2.8 billion to 20 companies, expected to leverage more than $9 billion in private investment.
- Some U.S. EV supply chain companies fear lax rules may open loopholes for imported products that will stifle U.S. competitiveness.
- 6K, a U.S.-based company, won a $107 million DOE grant in October to develop plasma-based technology for refining minerals into cathode-grade materials.
- Thanh Nguyen, 6K’s senior vice president of deployment, stated they must leapfrog Chinese technology because copying China is impossible on cost.
- Current Treasury rules allow 30D tax credits for EVs with batteries containing cathodes made in China or non-free-trade partners, as long as at least 50 percent of the completed product's value comes from U.S. or free-trade partner minerals/materials.
- Mary Cronin, 6K’s senior vice president of government affairs, warned this may undermine efforts to get U.S. battery manufacturers to use domestically sourced cathode material.
- Cronin called current supply-side incentives "a good start" but "a drop in the bucket."
- CelLink CEO Kevin Coakley highlighted challenges for his company's next-generation battery-pack interconnection circuits, which reduce about 70 percent of weight and 90 percent of space compared to traditional systems.
- CelLink won a conditional $362 million loan commitment from DOE’s Loan Programs Office to build its first large-scale U.S. manufacturing site in Texas.
- CelLink has already integrated its technology into battery packs used by at least one major EV maker with about 2.5 million vehicles on the road.
- Coakley noted that specifying a domestic value for this part of a battery could help his technology gain traction, but current Treasury guidance appears to discount the innovation and creates ambiguity/loopholes.
- Gore acknowledged policymakers face a chicken-and-egg problem in crafting industrial policy that accommodates current lack of U.S. battery-materials capacity while encouraging domestic production.
- South Korea announced it would make $5.3 billion in government loans and guarantees to South Korean companies to expand production in the United States.
- Republicans in Congress joined Manchin in attacking Biden administration EV supply-chain policies, claiming they benefit Chinese companies at the expense of U.S. competitors.
- DOE canceled a $200 million grant to Texas-based battery manufacturer Microvast following Republican attacks based on its inclusion in a 2022 SEC list of companies suspected of intellectual-property violations in China.
- Ford’s joint venture with Chinese lithium-ion battery giant CATL to build batteries in Michigan has also come under scrutiny.
- The Treasury Department has yet to issue guidance on barring products/materials passing through China at any stage from 30D tax credits.
- Sam Jaffe, vice president of battery storage solutions at E Source, noted that barring such materials could immediately cut out a large proportion of currently eligible EVs and drive demand for non-Chinese sources.
- Eric Stopka, CEO of Anovion (formed from Amsted Graphite Materials and Pyrotek’s battery materials division), won a $117 million DOE grant in October to support graphite production for EV batteries.
- Anovion's Georgia facility will be capable of producing 40,000 metric tons of synthetic graphite per year starting in 2025, scaling up to 150,000 metric tons a year eventually.
- It takes about 1,200 metric tons of graphite to make a gigawatt-hour’s worth of lithium-ion batteries.
- North America will need 1.2 million metric tons per year to supply a forecasted annual battery manufacturing capacity of 1,000 gigawatt-hours by 2030.
- China currently controls more than 90 percent of global graphite production capacity with virtually no production in North America.
- Stopka said Anovion will rely on 45X tax credits to bolster cost-competitiveness over the coming decade.
- Stopka emphasized the need to deploy capital and realize earnings without Chinese competitors undercutting prices to eliminate U.S. competition.
- Stopka acknowledged the complexity of creating policies that both get people to buy EVs and support a North American supply base, worrying that yet-to-be-released Treasury rules on foreign entities may fail to clearly establish qualifying supply chains for 30D credits.
